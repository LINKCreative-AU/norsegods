At Ægir's ale-feast, where enchanted horns fill themselves and the peace is sacred, Loki killed a servant for being praised, was thrown out, and came back in — invoking an old blood-oath with Odin that no ale be poured unless poured for both. Seated by right, he began at one end of the hall and worked down: Bragi is a bench-ornament afraid of battle; Odin practised seiðr 'like a witch' — perversion; Frigg took her husband's brothers to bed; Freyja has slept with every god and elf in the room including her brother; Týr can't hold a shield since the wolf took his hand — 'and my son Fenrir did it.' Goddess after god rises to object and is handed their own police file.

The horror engine of Lokasenna is accuracy: nearly every slander is attested elsewhere in the mythology. Loki is the pantheon's memory weaponised, the guest who says what everyone in the room knows. Only one name works: when Sif is reached, thunder arrives, and Thor's four verses — each ending 'Hush, unmanly creature' with hammer raised — accomplish what no argument could. Loki leaves with a parting shot of pure prophecy: Ægir will never brew again; fire will take everything.

The prose coda gets him caught: a salmon in a waterfall, netted by his own invention, bound with his son's entrails under dripping venom. The feast's seating chart, in other words, becomes Ragnarök's order of battle.

## What is a flyting?

*Senna* — the word in the poem's title — is Old Norse for a formal quarrel of words: a structured, public exchange of accusations and insults, governed by rules almost like a duel's. English borrowed the Scots term *flyting* for the genre. It was a real practice with real stakes: in a society run on honor, a well-aimed, *unanswerable* accusation did damage no sword could, and the worst charges — cowardice, unmanliness (*ergi*), sexual deviance — were serious enough that Icelandic law made certain insults legally killable offenses. Lokasenna is the genre's masterpiece precisely because its slanderer has perfect information. [Loki](/gods/loki) does not invent; he *files*. Nearly every charge he makes is corroborated somewhere else in the surviving mythology — which turns the poem into something stranger than comedy: a hostile index to the whole tradition, recited in the victims' faces.

## The feast and the gatecrash

The setting is the sea-giant **Ægir**'s hall — the great feast the gods extorted a mile-deep brewing kettle to hold, back in [Hymiskviða](/events/thors-fishing-trip). It is a marvel of a venue: the ale serves itself, the hall glows with shining gold in place of firelight, and the place is a declared sanctuary — *grið*, sacred truce. All the gods are there except [Thor](/gods/thor), away east killing trolls; the poem needs him offstage, saving the hammer for the finale.

Loki's entrance is a double crime. Hearing Ægir's two servants praised for their skill, he kills one of them, Fimafengr, on the spot — envy answering excellence — and the outraged gods drive him into the woods. Then he comes back. At the door he extracts from the watchman a review of what waits inside, then plays his trump on [Odin](/gods/odin) directly: *remember, Odin, when in bygone days we blended our blood together? You said you would never drink ale unless it were brought for both of us.* The blood-brotherhood of Odin and Loki — attested nowhere else so explicitly, and one of the poem's most tantalizing revelations — makes ejecting him impossible. Odin orders a place made: better to seat the wolf's father, he reasons, than be slandered from the doorway. It is the evening's first miscalculation.

## Down the benches

What follows is systematic. Loki toasts the assembled gods — pointedly excluding [Bragi](/gods/bragi) — and then works the room, and every defense anyone raises becomes the next exhibit:

- **Bragi**, god of poetry, offers a horse and a sword to buy peace; Loki calls him a bench-ornament, brave only when seated. When Bragi's wife **Iðunn** soothes her husband, she is told she embraced her brother's slayer.
- **Gefjun** intervenes; Loki has a story about her and a jewel. When **Odin** rebukes him — Gefjun knows fate as well as I do, be silent — Loki turns on the Allfather: he gives victory unjustly, to the worse man. Odin retorts that Loki spent eight winters beneath the earth as a milking cow and a woman, and bore children there — *argr* behavior. Loki answers with the counter-file: and *you*, Odin, practiced seiðr on Samsø, beat drums like a witch, went among men in wizard's shape — *that* is perversion. The two blood-brothers, in front of everyone, have just confirmed each other's worst secrets. **[Frigg](/gods/frigg)** tries to close the subject — fates should never be told in company — and is told hers: she took Odin's brothers Vili and Vé to her embrace. When she wishes aloud for a son to avenge the insult, Loki produces the poem's cruelest line: *I am the reason you will never again see [Baldr](/gods/baldr) ride to the halls.* It is as close to a confession of the mistletoe as the tradition ever gives us.
- **[Freyja](/gods/freyja)** defends Frigg and receives the most famous charge in the poem: of all Æsir and elves in this hall, each one has been your lover — including, [Njörðr](/gods/njord) is soon told, matters within the family. Njörðr shrugs it off with dignity — a mild scandal, that a woman have lovers — and is answered with his own dossier: sent east as a hostage, and used as a chamber-pot by Hymir's daughters. **[Týr](/gods/tyr)** stands up for [Freyr](/gods/freyr) and is met with the wolf: you cannot carry a shield since Fenrir tore off your hand — *my son*, Loki adds, in case anyone missed the paternity. Týr's reply is the poem's one moment of unanswerable stoicism: I lack a hand, you lack the wolf; each of us misses his own. **Freyr** threatens Loki with binding and is reminded he *bought* his bride Gerðr with his sword — and will rue its absence when Muspell's sons ride. Even the servants take their turn; **Heimdallr** is mocked for his eternal watch, a mud-stiffened back; **[Skaði](/giants/skadi)** trades threats with the killer of her father and is told he was first and foremost at that killing — she promises him her frost-cold counsels, and the poem knows she will keep the promise. **Sif** herself brings Loki a crystal cup, pleading her own blamelessness — and Loki claims to have been her one lover.

Note the engine turning: every single interruption meant to *stop* the flyting supplies its next victim. The gods cannot shut him up, because he is not lying — the mythology's own stories, told elsewhere on this site, back nearly every count. The hall is full of gods discovering that a sanctuary protects the guest who is destroying them.

## Thunder at the door

Beyla, a serving-woman, hears it first: the mountains are shaking — Thor is coming home. The Thunderer arrives to find the feast in ruins and answers Loki the only way that works, four times, in a refrain: *Þegi þú, rög vættr* — "Silence, unmanly creature" — or my hammer will close your mouth, knock you into the east, send you the way of your father Laufey. Loki, magnificent to the last, needles even this — *why so fierce, Thor? You'll be less bold against the wolf when he swallows your father* — and mocks the famous [glove-crouch and knotted bag](/events/journey-to-utgard) of the Skrýmir journey. But after the fourth threat he yields, with the poem's most honest exit line: he has spoken before Æsir and elves what his mind urged him, *but for you alone I shall go out, for I know that you strike.* Argument loses to the one god who never argues.

His parting words to Ægir are prophecy, not insult: you will never brew a feast again; fire will play over everything you own. It is a glimpse straight down the road to [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — where Loki and the gods he catalogued tonight will meet with weapons, the seating chart become an order of battle.

## In the primary sources

Lokasenna survives only in the Codex Regius of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), framed by prose. The prose header sets the feast and the murder of Fimafengr; the famous prose coda tells what came after: Loki hiding as a salmon in the waterfall of Fránangr, caught by the gods, and bound with the entrails of his son Nari while [Skaði](/giants/skadi) fixed the venom-dripping serpent over his face and Sigyn stayed with the bowl — the story told fully at [The Binding of Loki](/events/binding-of-loki), and by Snorri in Gylfaginning as the sequel to [Baldr's death](/events/death-of-baldr). Whether the flyting or the murder of Baldr is the "real" cause of the binding is a genuine seam in the tradition: the poem's coda and Snorri's narrative stitch the same punishment to different crimes. Scholars also debate the poem itself — is it a pagan text unafraid of its gods' flaws, or a late, even Christian-era satire on a discredited pantheon? The poem's deep fluency in the mythology (it is one of our best *sources* for the very scandals it weaponizes) argues that whoever wrote it, and whenever, they wrote from inside the tradition, not against it. A god who tells the truth horribly is not an outsider's joke; he is the mythology auditing itself.

## FAQ

**What does "Lokasenna" mean?**
"Loki's senna" — Loki's quarrel or flyting, a formal Norse exchange of insults. In the poem, Loki crashes the sea-giant Ægir's sacred feast and insults every god and goddess present in turn.

**Are Loki's insults in Lokasenna true?**
Mostly, yes — that is the poem's horror. Odin's seiðr, Freyja's lovers, Frigg and Odin's brothers, Týr's lost hand, Freyr's bartered sword, Loki's own role in Baldr's death: nearly every charge is attested elsewhere in the mythology. Loki wins the flyting because he is armed with the pantheon's own record.

**Why couldn't the gods just throw Loki out?**
He invoked an ancient blood-brotherhood with Odin — a sworn pledge that no ale be served to Odin unless served to Loki too — and the feast itself was a declared sanctuary. Bound by oath and sacred truce, the gods had to seat him and endure him.

**Who finally silenced Loki?**
Thor, arriving late from the east. Four times he answered Loki's taunts with the same refrain — "Silence, unmanly creature" — and the raised hammer. Loki yielded to Thor alone, saying he knew that Thor, unlike the others, actually strikes.

**What happened to Loki after the feast?**
According to the poem's prose ending, he hid as a salmon in the Fránangr falls, was caught by the gods, and was bound in a cave with his son's entrails beneath a serpent dripping venom — where his wife Sigyn holds a bowl over his face, and where he lies until Ragnarök.

**Is Lokasenna a pagan or a Christian poem?**
Scholars disagree. Some read its merciless mockery of the gods as a Christian-era satire; others note that flyting was a respected native genre, that the poem is one of our richest sources of genuine myth, and that Norse religion could evidently tolerate laughter at its gods. Its date and intent remain open questions.
