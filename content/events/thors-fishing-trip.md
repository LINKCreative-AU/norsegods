Guesting with the giant Hymir and eating him out of house and home, Thor was told to earn his supper: they would fish. He 'borrowed' bait by tearing the head off Hymir's largest ox, then rowed them so far past the whale-grounds that the giant went pale — he knew what lives out there.

Thor lined the ox-head onto a hook and dropped it, 'and the Midgard Serpent gaped at the bait.' What followed is the mythology's supreme freeze-frame: the god hauling, feet braced so hard they went through the boat's bottom to the sea-floor; the serpent's venom-spitting head breaking the surface; god and world-monster eye to eye — 'and it has been said, no one has seen a more terrifying sight' — the hammer rising for a blow that would have ended the Ragnarök problem centuries early. Hymir, in terror, cut the line. The serpent sank; Thor knocked the giant overboard (says Snorri) and waded home.

The scene was the ancient world's favourite: it survives carved on stones in Sweden, Denmark, and England — the Altuna stone even includes Thor's foot through the hull. Theologians of the conversion era loved it too: God baiting a hook for the world-serpent was an image both religions could argue about.

## Why Thor went fishing at all

The two main tellings frame the trip differently. In **Hymiskviða**, the whole adventure is about a kettle: the gods want to feast at the sea-giant Ægir's hall, and Ægir slyly demands a brewing-cauldron big enough for all of them — which only the giant **Hymir**, out at heaven's edge, possesses. So [Thor](/gods/thor) and [Týr](/gods/tyr) (whose father, in this poem, Hymir is said to be — one of the loose threads scholars still tug at) travel to Hymir's hall to win a kettle "a league deep." The fishing is an episode inside that quest. In **Gylfaginning**, Snorri strips the frame away and gives the trip a darker spring: Thor set out from Midgard in a young man's shape, expressly to settle accounts with the [Midgard Serpent](/creatures/jormungandr) after his humiliation in [Útgarða-Loki's hall](/events/journey-to-utgard), where the "cat" he failed to lift had been the Serpent all along. In the poem, Thor hooks the apocalypse almost incidentally; in Snorri, he goes looking for it.

Either way, the visit starts with hospitality strained to cracking. Hymir's hall is a place of monstrous in-laws — a nine-hundred-headed grandmother, a gold-decked mother kind enough to hide the guests behind a pillar — and the giant's welcome-glance shatters the pillar and the roof-beam. At table, Thor eats **two whole oxen** by himself. Hymir announces that if they mean to eat again, they will have to catch it. The fishing trip is, at bottom, a grocery run.

## The ox-head and the deep water

Asked for bait, Hymir tells Thor to get his own. Thor's solution sets the tone: he walks to the giant's herd and twists the head off **Himinhrjótr**, "Heaven-bellower," the biggest ox in the field. From that moment the trip is a contest of nerves between rower and boat-owner. Thor rows them out to Hymir's usual grounds, where the giant hauls up two whales at once and is ready to stop. Thor keeps rowing — hard, far past the whale-banks — and Hymir begins to warn, then to plead: it is dangerous to sit farther out, *because of the Midgard Serpent*. The giant is not being timid; he is being accurate. Out there lies the serpent that [Odin threw into the sea](/creatures/jormungandr) at the beginning, and that grew until it circled the whole world and bit its own tail. Every Norse listener knew what waited under that particular water. Thor rows two strokes more.

## The freeze-frame

Then the god of thunder rigs a line "not weak," fixes the ox-head on a hook, and lets it sink — and the poem and Snorri converge on the same magnificent sentence: *the Midgard Serpent gaped at the bait.* The hook set in the roof of its mouth, and the serpent jerked so hard that both Thor's fists slammed against the gunwale.

What follows is the single most-depicted moment in all of Norse art. Thor, furious, takes on his divine strength — the *ásmegin* — and braces so hard that **his feet go through the bottom of the boat and he stands on the sea-floor**, hauling the serpent up hand over hand to the gunwale. The head breaks the surface, spitting venom; the god's eyes and the monster's meet; and Snorri steps outside his own narrative to say it plainly: *no one has seen a more terrifying sight than Thor sharpening his eyes at the serpent, and the serpent glaring back from below and blowing poison.* Hymiskviða's poet reaches for cosmic accompaniment: the sea-wolf shrieked, the ancient earth resounded — the whole world feeling the tug on the thing that holds it together. Thor raises [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir).

And Hymir panics. The giant — pale, watching the sea run in and out of a hulled boat with the world-snake alongside — takes his bait-knife and **cuts Thor's line at the gunwale**. The serpent sinks back into the sea. Thor hurls the hammer after it, and Snorri passes on a rumor he clearly relishes — "people say that it struck off its head by the sea-bed" — before overruling it himself: *I think the truth is that the Midgard Serpent lives still and lies in the encircling sea.* Then Thor, wasting no more words, clouts Hymir overboard with his fist and **wades ashore** — a god so heavy with rage the boat is no longer worth using.

Hymiskviða, with its kettle-plot to finish, lets Hymir live a little longer: they row back, and the giant sets Thor two more tests — smashing a magic goblet (solved, on the mother's whispered advice, by throwing it at Hymir's skull, the one thing in the hall harder than the cup) and carrying off the mile-deep cauldron, which Thor wears away on his head like an enormous hat, his heels breaking through the floor. The giants pursue; the hammer settles it; the gods get their brewing-kettle, and Ægir's feast — the one where [Loki will ruin everything](/events/flyting-of-loki) — is provisioned.

## Why this scene mattered so much

Something about this image gripped the Viking Age like nothing else in the mythology. It survives in stone in at least three countries: the **Altuna runestone** in Sweden — Thor alone in the boat, hammer up, ox-head on the line, and *his foot poking through the hull*, proof the carver knew the story in the same detail we do; the **Hørdum stone** in Denmark, with two figures in the boat and the foot through the bottom again; and the **Gosforth "fishing stone"** in England, a carved slab in a Cumbrian churchyard showing two fishermen, the ox-head bait, and the coiling monster below. A fragment from Ardre, Gotland, may show the walk to the boat with the ox. Skaldic poets loved it just as long: verses by Bragi Boddason and Úlfr Uggason — among the oldest Norse poetry we possess — already describe the glaring match between god and serpent, which means the myth was fully formed by the ninth century.

The conversion era gave the image a second life. Christian writers had their own picture of a god angling for a world-serpent — medieval theology described Christ's crucifixion as a baited hook by which God caught Leviathan — and the Gosforth stone sits in a churchyard beside an outright cross for a reason. Whether the carvers meant rivalry, translation, or truce between the two stories, the fishing trip became the place where the old religion and the new could look at each other, eye to eye, like the god and the serpent.

## In the primary sources

**Hymiskviða** ([Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda)) embeds the fishing in the kettle-quest, has Týr along, keeps Hymir alive for further tests, and is told in a dense, kenning-crusted style. **Gylfaginning** ([Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda)) isolates the fishing as Thor's revenge trip after Útgarðr, omits Týr and the kettle, ends with Hymir knocked overboard — and preserves the greatest details: the feet through the hull, the mutual glare, the cut line. The skaldic fragments and picture stones are older than both and agree on the core scene, though at least one early verse has been read as implying Thor actually killed the serpent — the version Snorri politely reports and rejects. The disagreement matters: the serpent must survive, because Thor and Jörmungandr have an appointment at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), where the fight this trip postponed is finished — the god kills the snake and dies nine steps later of its venom. The fishing trip is the dress rehearsal the mythology could not allow to become the performance.

## FAQ

**What did Thor use as bait to catch the Midgard Serpent?**
The head of Hymir's biggest ox, called Himinhrjótr ("Heaven-bellower"), which Thor tore off himself when the giant told him to find his own bait.

**Did Thor actually catch Jörmungandr?**
He hooked it and hauled its venom-spitting head up to the gunwale, bracing so hard his feet went through the boat to the sea-floor. But just as he raised Mjölnir, the terrified Hymir cut the line and the serpent sank. Snorri mentions a tradition that the thrown hammer killed it, but rejects it: the serpent still lies in the sea, waiting for Ragnarök.

**Why did Hymir cut the fishing line?**
Terror — and arguably good sense. He knew what lived beyond the whale-grounds and had warned Thor against rowing so far; with the boat hulled and the world-serpent thrashing alongside, he chose survival over his guest's glory.

**Are Thor's fishing trip and the Hymir kettle story the same myth?**
They are two framings of one adventure. The Eddic poem Hymiskviða makes the fishing an episode in Thor's quest to win Hymir's giant brewing-cauldron for Ægir's feast; Snorri's Gylfaginning tells the fishing alone, as Thor's revenge on the serpent after his humiliation at Útgarðr.

**Where can you see Viking-Age pictures of this myth?**
On the Altuna runestone in Sweden (complete with Thor's foot through the hull), the Hørdum stone in Denmark, and the Gosforth fishing-stone in England — plus probable fragments elsewhere. It is the most widely depicted scene in Norse art.

**What is the connection to Ragnarök?**
Thor and Jörmungandr are fated opponents. The fishing trip is their near-miss: had the line held, Ragnarök's central duel would have happened early. Instead they part, and meet again at the end of the world, where Thor kills the serpent and dies of its venom nine steps later.
