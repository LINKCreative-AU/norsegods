Baldr dreamed his own death, and Asgard took it seriously: Odin rode Sleipnir to Hel to wake a dead seeress, who told him the hall was already decked for his son. Frigg took oaths from fire and water, iron and every metal, stones, earth, trees, sicknesses, beasts, birds, venom, and serpents — everything except one young sprig of mistletoe, west of Valhalla, too young, she thought, to swear.

The invulnerability became a game: the gods threw weapons at Baldr and laughed as they glanced off. Loki, in a woman's shape, teased the gap out of Frigg, cut the mistletoe, and brought it to blind Höðr, standing idle at the edge of the sport — 'do him honour as the others do; I will guide your hand.' The dart went through Baldr and he fell dead, and the gods' grief was so great none could speak, and none could take vengeance on the spot, for the place was sanctuary.

Hermóðr rode nine nights down to Hel, who set her price: let all things weep for Baldr, and he goes back. All things wept — people, beasts, earth, stones, metals ('as you see when they come out of frost into heat') — except one giantess in a cave, Þökk, who said Hel should keep what she held. Þökk, all agree, was Loki. Baldr's ship-pyre burned with his wife Nanna beside him, dead of grief, and Odin bent to whisper something in his dead son's ear that no myth reveals. The gods hunted Loki down and bound him under dripping venom until Ragnarök — where Baldr's death is avenged by the world's ending, and answered by his return in the world reborn.

## The most beloved of the gods

To feel the weight of this story you have to know who died. [Baldr](/gods/baldr) is the one god the sources describe almost entirely in superlatives: the fairest, the wisest-spoken, the most merciful, so bright that light shines from him — Snorri says the whitest of flowers is named "Baldr's brow" after his face. He is the son of [Odin](/gods/odin) and [Frigg](/gods/frigg), husband of Nanna, father of Forseti the judge, master of the hall Breiðablik where nothing unclean may be. He has one flaw, and Snorri notes it with a straight face: none of his judgments hold. Beauty, goodness, and no power to make anything stick — Baldr is the mythology's picture of everything precious and everything fragile in the same person, which is exactly why the story chooses him.

## The dreams, and the ride to the dead

It begins with nightmares. Baldr dreams great dreams boding peril to his life, and when he tells the Æsir, they take counsel — this is a pantheon that treats a prophetic dream as actionable intelligence. The Eddic poem **Baldrs draumar** ("Baldr's Dreams") narrates what Odin did next: he saddled [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir) and rode down the long road to Niflhel, past the blood-dabbled hound that bayed at him, to a seeress's grave on the east side of Hel's gate. With necromantic chants he woke the dead woman, who complained of the snow and rain that had fallen on her through the long years, and — calling himself "Vegtamr," Way-tamer — asked for whom Hel's benches were strewn with rings, the mead brewed, the hall decked. For Baldr, she answered. Höðr would be his slayer; Váli, a son not yet born, would avenge him at one night old. When Odin's questions gave him away, the seeress dismissed him with a taunt and told him to ride home proud — no one would wake her again until Loki slipped his bonds and [the doom of the gods](/events/ragnarok) came. Even the appointment to grieve, in this mythology, is scheduled next to the end of the world.

## Frigg's oaths and the game of throwing things at Baldr

Frigg's response was breathtaking in scope: she extracted oaths from everything. Fire and water swore; iron and all ores of metal; stones and the earth itself; trees; sicknesses; all beasts and birds; venoms and serpents. The world was made contractually harmless to her son.

What happened next tells you everything about the Æsir. They made it entertainment. At their assembly, Baldr stood while the gods shot at him, hewed at him, threw stones — and everything glanced off, and everyone laughed, and it seemed to them a great honor. There is no better image in the mythology of the gods' peculiar blindness: they turned their deepest fear into a party game and played it in public, in front of Loki.

[Loki](/gods/loki) watched, and it displeased him. Taking the shape of a woman, he went to Frigg at Fensalir, chatted about the assembly's sport, and asked the one question that mattered: had *everything* really sworn? And Frigg, to a stranger, gave up the secret she should have died holding: a little bush growing west of Valhalla, called mistletoe, "too young for me to demand its oath." The mythology never lets a spoken word be harmless. Loki went straight to the plant, pulled it up, and carried it to the assembly.

## Why did Loki kill Baldr?

The sources give no motive — and the silence is famous. Snorri says only that Loki was displeased that Baldr took no hurt. Lokasenna, set before or after depending on your chronology, shows a Loki already at knives with the whole pantheon, and there he boasts to Frigg that he is the reason Baldr will never ride home again. Readers have filled the gap for centuries: envy of the one being everyone loved; the trickster's compulsion to find the flaw in any perfect thing; a role scripted by fate itself, since the seeress had already seen the mistletoe flying. Perhaps the most honest reading is structural: the mythology needed its own heart broken for the end to begin, and Loki is the god whose job is doing what cannot be undone.

His method was crueler than the deed. He did not throw the dart himself. He found [Höðr](/gods/hodr), Baldr's blind brother, standing at the ring's edge — excluded from the game because he could not see and had nothing to throw — and offered him inclusion: *you should do Baldr honor like the others; I'll show you where he stands.* The blind god threw, with a guide's hand on his aim, and the mistletoe went through Baldr, "and this was the greatest misfortune ever worked among gods and men."

## The grief, the funeral, and the whisper

The gods' first response was silence — grief so total that speech failed, and no one could even lift the body. They could not take vengeance either: the assembly-ground was a sanctuary, a place of peace, and even now the law held. Snorri says Odin suffered most, because he understood best what the gods had truly lost — not a son only, but the proof that the doom could be delayed.

The funeral is one of the great set-pieces of Norse literature. Baldr's ship Hringhorni, the greatest of all ships, would not move from its rollers until the gods sent to [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim) for the giantess Hyrrokkin, who arrived riding a wolf bridled with vipers and shoved the ship so hard that fire burst from the rollers and the earth shook. Nanna, Baldr's wife, saw the pyre and died of grief on the spot; she was laid beside him. Thor hallowed the pyre with [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) — and kicked a dwarf named Litr into the fire in passing, grief making even the hallowing violent. All the worlds attended: Odin with his ravens and valkyries, [Freyr](/gods/freyr) in his boar-drawn chariot, [Freyja](/gods/freyja) with her cats, [Heimdall](/gods/heimdall) on his horse, and a great host of frost-giants and mountain-giants, mourning a god even his enemies loved. Odin laid on the pyre the ring [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir), which afterward dripped eight gold rings of its weight every ninth night — treasure sent into death, and multiplying there.

And Odin bent over his son and whispered something in his ear before the fire took him. No source reveals the words. The mythology keeps this secret deliberately: in the wisdom-contest of Vafþrúðnismál, Odin defeats the wisest giant with exactly this question — *what did Odin whisper in Baldr's ear before he was borne to the pyre?* — the one question in the cosmos only he can answer.

## The ride to Hel, and the price of all tears

Frigg asked who among the Æsir would earn all her love by riding to [Hel](/realms/hel) to offer a ransom. Hermóðr, called the Bold, Odin's son, took Sleipnir and rode nine nights through valleys so dark and deep he saw nothing, over the river Gjöll on its gold-thatched bridge — where the maiden Móðguðr remarked that he made more noise alone than five troops of dead men the day before, and that he had the wrong color for a corpse — and leapt Sleipnir clean over Hel's gate.

He found Baldr enthroned in the hall of the dead, sat with him through the night, and in the morning put the plea: all the gods weeping, would Hel let him ride home? Her answer is one of the mythology's coldest and fairest lines: it should now be *tested*, she said, whether Baldr was as loved as everyone claimed. If everything in the world, alive and dead, would weep for him, he could return. If anything spoke against him or refused to weep, he would stay.

Messengers went out over the whole world, and the whole world wept — men and beasts, earth and stones, trees and every metal, "as you have seen these things weep when they come out of frost into heat." Baldr's return was, for one shining moment, arithmetic: every tear counted, and every tear fell. Except one. In a cave sat a giantess who gave her name as Þökk — "Thanks" — and her verse is the door of the trap clicking shut: *Þökk will weep dry tears for Baldr's burial; alive or dead, the old man's son gave me no joy. Let Hel keep what she has.* And it is generally thought, Snorri adds, with the whole tradition nodding behind him, that this was Loki, who had done the harm and now made sure it could not be mended.

## In the primary sources

**Gylfaginning** (in Snorri's [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda)) carries the full narrative — the oaths, the game, the mistletoe, Hermóðr's ride, Þökk — and nearly every detail above that has quotation marks around it is Snorri's. **Völuspá** confirms the story is old: the seeress sees Baldr's fate, the mistletoe "slender and fair" made into a deadly shaft, Höðr shooting, Váli born to avenge, and Frigg weeping in Fensalir — all in a few compressed stanzas that assume the audience knows the rest. **Baldrs draumar** supplies Odin's ride to the dead seeress.

Then there is **Saxo Grammaticus**, whose Latin *Gesta Danorum* (c. 1200) tells a wildly different version: his Balderus is a lustful demigod who wars against the human hero Høtherus (Höðr) for the hand of the princess Nanna; Høtherus kills him with a magic sword, not mistletoe, and Odin fathers Bous (Váli) to take revenge. No Loki, no oaths, no universal weeping. Saxo's telling is a standing caution that the myth circulated in versions Snorri did not choose to tell — and evidence that Höðr was once a warrior in his own right, not merely a blind hand. The tenth-century skald whose verses on the weeping world Snorri could quote, and place-names in Scandinavia, suggest the core story is genuinely pre-Christian; how much of its polish is Snorri's, scholars still argue. The resemblance of a dying, universally mourned, returning god to Christ was noticed early and is debated still — against it stands the fact that Baldr does not save anyone, and returns only after everything is lost.

## What happens after

Vengeance came twice. Odin begot Váli — as Baldrs draumar had prophesied — who at one night old, unwashed and uncombed, killed Höðr: the blind instrument paid the instrument's price, while the guiding hand ran free a while longer. Then the gods took their fuller vengeance on Loki, hunting him into a salmon's shape and binding him in a cave with his own son's entrails under a serpent's dripping venom — [a story with its own page](/events/binding-of-loki), and no pretense of being justice.

But the deep consequence is the one the whole mythology bends around: with Baldr dead and held in Hel, Ragnarök could no longer be averted, only awaited. And on the far side of the fire, the promise is kept in full: Baldr and Höðr come back together from the dead, reconciled, and sit in the new grass of the reborn world. The mythology kills its best beloved and then refuses to let death have the last word — the tears were not wasted, only delayed.

## FAQ

**How did Baldr die?**
He was killed by a dart of mistletoe — the one thing in the world his mother Frigg had not sworn to harmlessness — thrown by his blind brother Höðr, whose aim was guided by Loki. The gods had been throwing weapons at the invulnerable Baldr for sport when the mistletoe went through him.

**Why did Loki kill Baldr?**
No source gives an explicit motive. Snorri says only that Loki was vexed that Baldr took no hurt; in Lokasenna, Loki boasts of the deed to Frigg. Envy, malice, and the pull of fate — the seeress had already foreseen the death — are the traditional explanations readers supply.

**Why didn't Baldr come back from Hel?**
Hel agreed to release him if every thing in creation, living and dead, wept for him. Everything did — except a giantess in a cave calling herself Þökk, who refused, saying "let Hel keep what she has." The sources agree Þökk was Loki in disguise, completing his own crime.

**What did Odin whisper to the dead Baldr?**
Nobody knows — deliberately. It is the mythology's sealed secret, and Odin uses it as an unanswerable question to win the wisdom contest in Vafþrúðnismál. Whatever was said, only Odin and the dead have heard it.

**Does Baldr return after Ragnarök?**
Yes. Both Völuspá and Snorri say that after the world burns and rises green again, Baldr and Höðr return together from Hel, reconciled, and dwell with the surviving gods where Asgard once stood.

**Is the Baldr story influenced by Christianity?**
Scholars have debated it for two centuries. A beautiful, innocent god who dies, is universally mourned, and returns after a world-ending invites the comparison — but Baldr redeems no one, Saxo preserves a thoroughly un-Christlike warrior version, and skaldic verse and place-names indicate the cult and story are genuinely pre-Christian. Most scholars see an old myth, possibly retold with some Christian coloring.
