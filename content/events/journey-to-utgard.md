It begins with goats and a farm-boy's marrow-theft, collects Þjálfi and Röskva as Thor's servants, and detours through a night in a 'hall' that dawn reveals as the thumb of a giant's glove — the giant, Skrýmir, snores like earthquakes, ties the provision-bag with wire no god can undo, and receives three of Thor's best hammer-blows as 'a leaf,' 'an acorn,' and 'some bird droppings' falling on him. The party arrives at Útgarðr already thoroughly gaslit.

The giant-king's rule: no one stays who cannot beat someone at something. Loki loses an eating race to Logi; Þjálfi is outrun three heats by Hugi; Thor fails to drain a drinking horn in three world-record pulls, cannot lift the king's grey cat past one paw, and is wrestled to a knee by the crone Elli. The gods leave humiliated — and at the gates, Útgarða-Loki, suddenly respectful, decrypts the scoreboard: Logi was wildfire, Hugi was thought, the horn's far end was the ocean (behold the tides you made), the cat was Jörmungandr (all Midgard trembled when you lifted it), and Elli was Old Age, who takes everyone. The three hammer-blows? Look at those three square valleys in yonder mountain. Thor swung — and castle, king, and plain were gone.

It is the one myth where Thor's problem cannot be hit, and the poets clearly loved that: strength meets epistemology and loses on points. Every later story of rigged carnival games against unbeatable abstractions is playing in this castle.

## The goats, the boy, and the bag

The journey opens with the mythology's oddest piece of domestic economy. [Thor](/gods/thor) and [Loki](/gods/loki), driving the goat-chariot toward [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim), lodge overnight with a peasant family — and Thor provides dinner by slaughtering his own goats, Tanngrisnir and Tanngnjóstr, with instructions that all bones be thrown unbroken onto the skins. The farmer's son, **Þjálfi**, splits a thigh-bone for the marrow. At dawn Thor hallows the skins with [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) and the goats stand up alive — one lame in a hind leg. The god's brows come down; the family nearly dies of the look; and the settlement is two servants: Þjálfi and his sister **Röskva**, who follow Thor ever after. The story thus begins with a hidden breach and a resurrection — a quiet overture, since the whole tale ahead is about damage you can't see and things that aren't what they seem.

Walking east beyond the sea, the party spends a night in a strangely shaped hall: five rooms off a wide main chamber, door as wide as the building. Earthquakes keep waking them. Dawn explains everything at once: the earthquake is the snoring of a giant of genuinely unreasonable size, and the "hall" is his **glove** — they have been sleeping in the thumb. The giant names himself **Skrýmir** ("Big-looking"), invites himself along, takes their provisions into his own bag, and ties it with a knot that Thor — the strongest of the Æsir, starving at bedtime — cannot loosen. What follows is the most patient humiliation in the mythology: Thor hits the sleeping giant in the head with Mjölnir three times through the night, blows that should crack mountains, and Skrýmir stirs each time to ask whether a **leaf** fell on him, then an **acorn**, then whether **birds roosting in the tree** have dropped something. In the morning the giant points them toward the castle, advises them sincerely not to act up there — the men of Útgarðr don't put up with squirts — and walks off. By now, god and audience alike have lost their grip on scale itself, which is precisely the point.

## The games at Útgarða-Loki's hall

**Útgarðr** — "the outer enclosure," everything beyond the fences of gods and men — is a castle so tall you have to bend your neck back to your spine to see over it. The travelers squeeze between the bars of its locked gate (strength failing again; slipperiness working). Inside, the king **Útgarða-Loki** barely glances at them: is this little fellow really Öku-Þórr? The house rule follows: no one stays who does not surpass someone at some art or skill.

The games proceed like a beautifully rigged carnival:

- **Loki** volunteers that no one can eat faster than he. A trencher of meat is set between him and one **Logi**; they meet in the middle — but Loki has eaten the meat, while Logi has eaten meat, bones, *and the trough*. Loss.
- **Þjálfi**, the fastest of men, runs three heats against a slip of a lad called **Hugi**, and loses ground in every one — by the third, Hugi finishes and doubles back before Þjálfi reaches the end.
- **Thor** chooses drinking. The horn brought out is long but not impossibly wide; a good drinker empties it in one pull, and *no one is so wretched he cannot manage it in three*. Thor drinks three enormous, lung-bursting draughts — and the level barely falls.
- Útgarða-Loki, all mock kindness, offers a game for youngsters: lift the household **cat**. The grey cat arches as Thor strains beneath it; at his utmost the god raises **one paw** off the floor.
- Furious now, Thor calls for a wrestling match, and is given the king's old foster-mother, the crone **Elli**. The harder Thor grips, the firmer she stands; when she tries her holds, he is brought down **to one knee**, and the king stops the bout.

The company is hosted generously overnight — Útgarða-Loki is never once rude; that is part of the horror — and escorted out in the morning.

## The debrief at the gate

Outside the walls, the king asks how Thor thinks it went, and then, since they are now safely out of his stronghold, tells the truth — and Snorri's reveal is one of the great pages of medieval literature. Had he known Thor's strength beforehand, he would never have let him in; everything inside was *sjónhverfingar*, deceptions of sight. **He himself was Skrýmir**: the wire on the bag was iron cunning, and the three hammer-blows — each of which *would have killed him* — were caught on a mountain slipped between hammer and head; Thor may inspect the three square valleys in it on his way home. **Logi** was wildfire, which devours trough as happily as meat. **Hugi** was thought, and nothing outruns it. **The horn's far end stood in the sea**: Thor drank so deeply he made the ebb-tide — go and look at what the ocean is missing. **The cat** was [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), the Midgard Serpent that rings the world, and when Thor raised its "paw" the serpent's back scraped the sky and everyone inside went pale. And **Elli** was Old Age herself, who has thrown, or will throw, every being that lives: to be forced down only to one knee was a marvel no one had ever seen.

Every defeat, decoded, is a triumph; every triumph was administered by fraud. Thor, understanding at last, swings Mjölnir — at nothing. Castle, king, and plain are gone. There is no target. It is the one myth where the thunder-god's problem cannot be hit, and the mythology lets him stand there, hammer raised over empty grass, as its most honest portrait of strength meeting the limits of strength.

## In the primary sources

This tale exists in connected form only in **Gylfaginning**, in Snorri's [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) — the longest single narrative in the book, and by common consent its storytelling masterpiece. No Eddic poem tells it, though the tradition around it is demonstrably old: the poem Lokasenna has Loki taunt Thor with cowering "in the thumb of a glove" and Hárbarðsljóð sneers at the fear-crouch in Skrýmir's glove and the impossible knots on the bag, proving the Skrýmir episode was famous enough to insult a god with. Þórsdrápa, a difficult tenth-century skaldic poem, sends Thor on a different journey with Þjálfi through giant-country. And in Saxo Grammaticus's Gesta Danorum, a Danish adventurer visits a loathsome bound giant called **Utgarthilocus** ("Útgarða-Loki") in a cave of snakes — the same name attached to a wholly different, darker tradition, a standing reminder that medieval Scandinavia knew versions we have lost. How much of Gylfaginning's wit — the leaf, the acorn, the debrief — is ancient and how much is Snorri at his desk in thirteenth-century Iceland, no one can say; the architecture of illusion-contests has folktale parallels across the world, and Snorri built the cathedral version.

## What happens after

The mythology gives Thor two rematches. Snorri says Thor set out for [his fishing trip](/events/thors-fishing-trip) expressly because of Útgarðr — determined to meet the "cat" again without the lens of illusion, and this time he hauls the actual serpent to the gunwale. And at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) all the veils come off: Thor and Jörmungandr finish their business for real, and no one puts a mountain in the way. Against Elli, of course, there is no rematch; the king of Útgarðr said so himself, and it is the one thing in his castle that was never an illusion.

## FAQ

**Who is Útgarða-Loki? Is he the same as Loki?**
No — the shared name is coincidence (or giant mockery). Útgarða-Loki is the giant king of Útgarðr, "the outer world," a master of illusions who also appears, monstrously transformed, as the bound giant Utgarthilocus in Saxo's Gesta Danorum. Thor's companion Loki competes in the games like everyone else — and loses to wildfire.

**What were the contests at Útgarðr really?**
Every opponent was a disguised abstraction: Loki's eating rival Logi was wildfire; Þjálfi's racing opponent Hugi was thought; Thor's drinking horn was connected to the ocean; the grey cat was the Midgard Serpent; and the old woman Elli who wrestled Thor down to one knee was Old Age itself.

**Did Thor really lose?**
By the scoreboard, yes; by the debrief, no. His three drinks visibly lowered the sea and created the tides; lifting the "cat's" paw dragged the World Serpent's back against the sky; and merely staggering against Old Age was a feat no other being had managed. The games were unwinnable by design.

**Who is Skrýmir?**
The colossal giant who joins Thor's party on the road — in whose empty glove the travelers unknowingly spend a night, and who shrugs off three full-strength hammer-blows as a falling leaf, an acorn, and bird droppings. At the gate, Útgarða-Loki reveals that Skrýmir was himself, and that a hidden mountain took the blows: three square valleys prove it.

**Where is this story written down?**
Only Snorri's Gylfaginning in the Prose Edda tells it in full. But Eddic poems (Lokasenna, Hárbarðsljóð) mock Thor with the glove and knot episodes, showing the story was old and widely known before Snorri wrote.

**Who are Þjálfi and Röskva?**
Farm children who became Thor's servants as compensation after Þjálfi lamed one of Thor's resurrecting goats by splitting a bone for its marrow. Þjálfi, fastest of men, runs the races at Útgarðr — and later fells the clay giant in the duel with Hrungnir.
