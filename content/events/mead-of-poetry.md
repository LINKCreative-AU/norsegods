Kvasir, born of the gods' mingled spittle at the Æsir–Vanir peace, was so wise no question could defeat him. Two dwarves, Fjalar and Galar, murdered him and brewed his blood with honey into a mead that makes whoever drinks it a poet or a scholar — telling the gods that Kvasir had 'suffocated in his own intelligence.' Through further murders the dwarves forfeited the mead to the giant Suttungr, who sealed it in three vats inside the mountain Hnitbjörg, his daughter Gunnlöð as its guard.

Odin came for it as a farmhand called Bölverkr ('Evil-worker'): he engineered the death of nine mowers, worked their labour for a summer, and demanded his wage — a sip. Refused, he bored through the mountain with an auger, slid through as a snake, and spent three nights with Gunnlöð, receiving three sips: in three swallows he drained all three vats. Then he became an eagle and flew for Asgard with Suttungr's eagle-shape behind him; the gods set out vats, and Odin spat the mead into them — though a little squirted backwards under pursuit, and that, the myth grins, is the share of bad poets.

The Mead is the Norse theory of inspiration: poetry is stolen property, wisdom fermented from a murdered god, won by oath-breaking and paid for in someone else's grief — which is why skalds call poetry 'Kvasir's blood' and 'Óðrerir's sea,' and why it belongs to Odin.

## Born from a peace treaty, killed by envy

The mead's pedigree begins at the end of [the first war in the world](/events/aesir-vanir-war). When the Æsir and the Vanir made peace, they sealed it in the old way: both sides went to a vat and spat into it. Rather than let the token of the truce be lost, the gods shaped from it a man — **Kvasir**, whose very name is probably cognate with words for crushed fruit and fermented drink; he is brewage walking. Kvasir was so wise that no one could ask him a question he could not answer, and he did what wisdom in this mythology almost never does: he gave it away free, travelling widely through the world teaching whoever asked.

That could not last. Two dwarves, **Fjalar and Galar**, invited him to a private talk and murdered him, running his blood into three vessels whose names the myth preserves like relics: the two vats **Són** and **Boðn**, and the kettle **Óðrerir** — "the one that stirs *óðr*," the untranslatable Norse word for mind, frenzy, inspiration, the root of [Odin](/gods/odin)'s own name. They blended the blood with honey and it fermented into the mead that makes anyone who drinks it a *skáld* or a *fræðamaðr* — a poet or a man of learning. When the Æsir came asking after the wisest of beings, the dwarves offered the mythology's most quoted excuse: Kvasir had *suffocated in his own intelligence*, there being no one educated enough to drain the surplus off with questions. Snorri lets the lie stand there, gleaming, the first recorded instance of academic satire.

## From the dwarves to the giant: how murder compounds

The dwarves were not done killing. They invited the giant Gillingr out in a boat and capsized it; he could not swim, and drowned. Home again, they dropped a millstone on his grieving widow's head, because — Fjalar explained — her weeping was tiresome. But Gillingr had a son, **Suttungr**, and Suttungr's grief did arithmetic. He seized the dwarves and set them on a skerry that the tide would cover, and let them talk. They offered the precious mead as compensation for his father — *weregild*, the blood-price, the same legal instrument that drives [The Otter's Ransom](/events/otters-ransom). Suttungr took it, carried the three vessels home, and hid them in the mountain **Hnitbjörg**, setting his daughter **Gunnlöð** inside as guardian.

Notice what the mead has become by this point in the story: it began as the embodiment of a peace, was converted by murder into a commodity, and has now been converted by more murder into a legal settlement. Every hand it passes through is bloodier than the last — and it has not yet made a single poem. Snorri pauses here to note that this chain of custody is why poetry is called "Kvasir's blood," "dwarves' drink," "Suttungr's mead," and "the liquid of Óðrerir": the kennings of skaldic verse are, quite literally, this story's receipt trail.

## Bölverkr's summer: the long con

Odin wanted it — of course he wanted it; the god who [gave an eye for a drink of wisdom](/gods/odin) and [hanged himself for the runes](/events/odins-sacrifice) was never going to leave the drink of inspiration in a mountain. What follows is his most elaborate recorded operation.

He travelled as a wanderer and came upon nine thralls mowing hay — the slaves of **Baugi**, Suttungr's brother. He offered to whet their scythes; the hone worked such wonders that they all wanted to buy it; and Odin threw it into the air. In the scramble to catch it, the nine mowers cut each other's throats with their own scythes. The god walked on and took lodging that night with Baugi, who complained that his nine workmen had killed each other and he had no labor. The stranger — calling himself **Bölverkr**, "Evil-worker," a piece of nominative honesty nobody at the farm examined — offered to do nine men's work through the summer. His wage: one drink of Suttungr's mead.

Baugi said the mead was not his to give, but agreed to help Bölverkr get it. All summer the disguised god did the work of nine; when winter came he asked for his pay, and the brothers-in-law went to Suttungr — who refused a single drop. Whatever Bölverkr had planned for, it was this. He produced an auger named **Rati** and set Baugi to bore through the mountain wall. Baugi drilled, declared the hole through; Odin blew into it, and the chips flew back at his face — the first lie. He made Baugi drill again; this time the chips flew inward; and as Odin shrank into a snake and slid into the hole, Baugi stabbed after him with the auger and missed. Both sides of that partnership, the myth notes without comment, betrayed it in the same minute.

## Three nights with Gunnlöð

Inside the mountain sat Gunnlöð on her golden seat, guarding what her father valued most. Odin went to her — Snorri says simply that he lay with her three nights, and she then permitted him three drinks of the mead. In the first draught he emptied Óðrerir; in the second, Boðn; in the third, Són — three sips as promised, technically, and the entire hoard in fact. The letter of the bargain kept, its spirit gutted: pure Odin. Then he shifted into eagle shape and flew for home as hard as he could.

But **Hávamál**, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), remembers the inside of the mountain differently, and its stanzas are the oldest and strangest witness to this myth. There Odin speaks in his own voice, and what he describes sounds less like a heist than a wedding: Gunnlöð gave him the drink *on her golden seat*; he speaks of rings and oaths sworn — an oath on the sacred ring, the most binding kind — and then, in one of the most startling moral passages in Eddic poetry, he reflects on what he did: an ill reward he gave her for her whole heart, her grieved spirit. Elsewhere in the poem he asks how anyone can trust the word of Odin, who cheated Suttungr of his mead *and left Gunnlöð weeping*. The god of poetry convicts himself in verse — which is, after all, the most poetic possible confession. Hávamál also preserves details Snorri lacks: that Odin trembled between the "high" giant-halls, that Rati gnawed the passage, that he risked his head — and a cryptic scene where "Bölverkr" is sworn for on the ring while frost-giants come asking after him in the High One's hall the next day.

## The eagle chase and the share of bad poets

Suttungr discovered the theft, took his own eagle shape, and flew in pursuit. The chase was close enough that the Æsir, seeing the pair from Asgard's walls, rushed their vats out into the courtyard. Odin cleared the wall and spat the mead out — but Suttungr was so near that some of it went out backwards, the other way. That splatter fell outside the walls, unclaimed, free to anyone: **the rhymester's share** (*skáldfífla hlutr* — the portion of poetasters), Snorri calls it, the mythology's permanent explanation for bad verse. Real poetry is the mead Odin carried; doggerel is what leaked out the back of a fleeing eagle. No literary culture has ever produced a crueler or more cheerful theory of talent.

The mead Odin kept he gives out as he pleases — to the Æsir and to those humans who can compose. This is why poetic skill in the North was Odin's gift, why skalds were his men, and why the kennings for poetry pile up around this one story: "Kvasir's blood," "the dwarves' ship" (they bought their lives with it), "Suttungr's mead," "Óðrerir's sea," "Odin's booty," "the prize Odin bore off in his beak." Every skald reciting in a king's hall was drinking, at one remove, from a stolen vat.

## In the primary sources

**Skáldskaparmál**, the poetics handbook of Snorri's [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), carries the complete connected story — Kvasir, the dwarves, Gillingr, Suttungr, Bölverkr's summer, the eagle chase — told expressly to explain the kennings a working poet needed. **Hávamál** (stanzas about Gunnlöð, Rati, and Óðrerir, in the so-called wisdom sections) is the older poetic witness: fragmentary, allusive, morally raw where Snorri is smooth, with the oath-breaking and Gunnlöð's grief at the center. The two versions genuinely differ — in Hávamál, Óðrerir seems to be the *mead* itself rather than the kettle, and the affair with Gunnlöð has the weight of a betrayed marriage rather than a three-night trick. Skaldic poetry from the ninth and tenth centuries already uses the kennings, proving the myth was current in the Viking Age. Scholars have also long compared the whole structure — a divine being's fluid of inspiration stolen by a shape-shifting god in bird form — with the Indian myth of the eagle stealing *soma*, suggesting roots deep in Indo-European antiquity.

## What happens after

The mead settles in Asgard and drips down into human history: every poem in Old Norse — including, in a fine loop, the very Eddas that tell this story — is understood as a sip of it, regifted. Gunnlöð's weeping stays in Hávamál like a stone in a shoe; Suttungr gets no revenge (giants seeking payback for relatives fill several other myths, but his line falls silent); and Odin adds "mead-thief" to a résumé that already included oath-breaker and gallows-rider. The Norse knew exactly what they were saying about art: that inspiration is real, divine, and fermented from someone's blood; that it was not given to humanity but *smuggled* to it; and that every poet since owes royalties to a murdered god, a robbed giant, and a woman crying alone in a mountain.

## FAQ

**What is the Mead of Poetry?**
A magical mead brewed by two dwarves from the blood of Kvasir, the wisest of beings, mixed with honey. Whoever drinks it becomes a poet or a scholar. After passing to the giant Suttungr as blood-price, it was stolen by Odin, who dispenses poetic inspiration from it to gods and men.

**Who was Kvasir?**
A being created from the mingled spittle of the Æsir and Vanir when they sealed their peace treaty, so wise that no question could defeat him. He traveled the world teaching until the dwarves Fjalar and Galar murdered him and brewed his blood into the mead — telling the gods he had "suffocated in his own intelligence."

**How did Odin steal the Mead of Poetry?**
Disguised as a laborer named Bölverkr, he worked a summer for the giant's brother Baugi, had a hole drilled through the mountain Hnitbjörg, entered as a snake, spent three nights with the guardian Gunnlöð, drained all three vessels in three drinks, and escaped as an eagle with the giant Suttungr in pursuit.

**What is the "bad poets' share"?**
As Odin fled in eagle form with Suttungr close behind, some of the mead was lost backwards out of the eagle. Snorri says that portion fell outside Asgard's walls, free for anyone — and it is the source of bad verse, the "rhymester's share."

**What are Óðrerir, Boðn, and Són?**
The three vessels holding the mead: Óðrerir (the kettle, "stirrer of inspiration") and the vats Boðn and Són. In Hávamál, Óðrerir appears to name the mead itself rather than the container — one of several differences between the poetic and prose versions.

**Where is the story told?**
Fully in Skáldskaparmál in Snorri's Prose Edda, where it explains the kennings for poetry ("Kvasir's blood," "Suttungr's mead"). Hávamál in the Poetic Edda preserves an older, darker fragmentary version centered on Odin's broken oath and Gunnlöð's grief.
