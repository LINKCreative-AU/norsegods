Ragnarök ('fate of the powers') is not a possibility in Norse myth but a schedule. The seeress of Völuspá recites it to Odin as accomplished fact in the future tense, and the gods live their whole mythology in its shadow — binding the wolf, gathering the battle-dead, asking riddles about the end over a giant's table.

It begins with the Fimbulvetr, three winters with no summer between them, while brothers kill brothers and 'an axe-age, a sword-age' breaks the bonds of kinship. The wolves Sköll and Hati catch and swallow the sun and moon. Heimdallr blows the Gjallarhorn. Fenrir breaks Gleipnir; Jörmungandr comes ashore in a wall of poisoned sea; Naglfar, the ship of dead men's nails, sails with Loki at the helm; Surtr rides from Múspellsheimr and Bifröst shatters. On the plain of Vígríðr, a hundred leagues on every side, the pairs meet: Odin is swallowed by Fenrir and avenged by Víðarr; Thor kills the Serpent and dies of its venom nine steps later; Freyr falls to Surtr; Týr and Garmr, Heimdallr and Loki, kill each other. Then Surtr flings fire over everything, and the stars fall into the steaming sea.

And then — the part popular culture forgets — the earth rises again, green, from the water. Baldr and Höðr come back from Hel reconciled; Víðarr, Váli, Magni, and Móði survive with Thor's hammer; two humans, Líf and Lífþrasir, emerge from shelter in the world-tree to repeople the earth; and the gods find the old golden chess-pieces glinting in the new grass. Ragnarök is an ending with a morning after.

## What does "Ragnarök" actually mean?

The Old Norse word is a compound: *ragna*, the genitive plural of *regin*, "the ruling powers, the gods"; and *rök*, "fate, destiny, origin, final cause." So the older and better-attested form, *ragnarök*, means something like "the fate of the powers" or "the doom of the gods" — the word the Völuspá poet and the riddling giant of Vafþrúðnismál both use.

The famous "twilight" belongs to a variant. Some Old Norse texts, including passages of Snorri's Prose Edda, use *ragnarøkkr* — with *røkkr*, "twilight, darkening" — and it was this form Richard Wagner rendered into German as *Götterdämmerung*, "Twilight of the Gods," fixing the more poetic (and probably secondary) reading in the modern imagination. Scholars generally treat *ragnarøkkr* as a later reinterpretation or a play on the older word: a beautiful pun, but a pun. The event itself is not a slow dimming. It is an appointment.

That is the strangest and most powerful thing about Ragnarök: the gods know. [Odin](/gods/odin) has heard the whole sequence from the dead seeress; he rides to the wisest of giants in Vafþrúðnismál and stakes his head on questions about it; he collects slain warriors into Valhalla for no other reason than to have an army on the day. Norse religion is, at its core, the worship of gods who have read their own obituary and get up every morning anyway.

## The omens: how the end announces itself

Ragnarök does not arrive unheralded. The sources give it a long fuse, and the first spark is a death inside Asgard itself.

**Baldr's death.** The killing of [Baldr](/gods/baldr) — the shining, beloved son of Odin, brought down by a mistletoe dart in blind [Höðr](/gods/hodr)'s hand, with [Loki](/gods/loki) guiding it — is the point after which the end can no longer be averted. Völuspá places Baldr's fate at the hinge of its prophecy; once he has gone down to Hel and stayed there, the seeress's vision runs downhill without a brake. The gods answer the murder by hunting Loki and binding him under a venom-dripping serpent, and in doing so they load the final spring: the bound god will lie there, fed on hatred, until the day he breaks loose. The full story is told in [The Death of Baldr](/events/death-of-baldr) and [The Binding of Loki](/events/binding-of-loki).

**Fimbulvetr, the terrible winter.** The nearer omens begin with weather. Three winters come with no summer between them — the *fimbulvetr*, "mighty winter" — with driving snow from every quarter, biting frost and keen winds, and a sun that gives no warmth. Vafþrúðnismál is the poem that names it; Snorri's Gylfaginning spells out its length. Before or alongside it comes a moral winter to match: Völuspá's famous stanza foretells an age when brothers fight and kill each other, kin defile kinship, "an axe-age, a sword-age — shields are riven — a wind-age, a wolf-age, before the world goes headlong." No man will spare another. The world's bonds of loyalty rot before its physical bonds snap.

**The devouring of sun and moon.** The wolves who have chased the sky's lights since the beginning finally catch them. Sköll swallows the sun and Hati the moon (Snorri assigns the sun to a wolf he elsewhere calls Fenrir's kin; the poems speak of a wolf swallowing the sun outright), the stars vanish from heaven, and the earth shakes so violently that trees are torn up and mountains fall.

**The breaking of bonds.** With that shaking, every fetter in the cosmos fails at once. [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) snaps [Gleipnir](/artifacts/gleipnir), the impossible ribbon that cost [Týr](/gods/tyr) his hand — the debt incurred at [The Binding of Fenrir](/events/binding-of-fenrir) falling due. Loki tears free of the iron entrails that held him to the three stones. The hound Garmr, bound before the cave Gnipahellir, breaks loose baying. Even the dead will not stay put: the sources say Naglfar loosens from its moorings. Everything the gods ever tied down gets up.

Three cocks crow the armies awake — golden-combed Gullinkambi rousing the heroes in Odin's hall, a second crowing in the gallows-wood of the giants, a third, soot-red, in the halls of Hel. And then [Heimdall](/gods/heimdall), the watchman who can hear grass growing, sets the [Gjallarhorn](/artifacts/gjallarhorn) to his lips and blows a blast heard through all the worlds. The gods wake and meet in council; Odin rides one last time to Mímir's well for counsel; the world-ash Yggdrasil shudders from root to crown, and everything that lives is afraid.

## The gathering of the armies

The hosts of the end converge from every compass point, and the poets took visible pleasure in the muster.

From the east, over the poisoned flood, comes **Jörmungandr**, the [Midgard Serpent](/creatures/jormungandr), heaving itself out of the sea in giant-wrath. Its writhing drives the ocean over the land, and it sprays venom across sky and sea as it comes.

On that flood-tide sails **Naglfar**, the most macabre ship in any mythology: a vessel [made of dead men's nails](/artifacts/naglfar), which the giants have been building since time began out of the unclipped fingernails of the carelessly buried dead. (Snorri pauses here for the mythology's only piece of funerary hygiene advice: cut the nails of the dead, and you delay the launching.) In Völuspá the ship comes from the east with the giant Hrymr at the helm and "Muspell's people" aboard; Snorri gives the steering of a ship of the dead to Loki, who leads the hosts of [Hel](/realms/helheim). The bound god sails to war crewed by everyone the gods ever failed.

From the south rides **Surtr**, the [fire-giant](/giants/surtr) who has stood at the border of [Muspelheim](/realms/muspelheim) since before the world was made, "with fire before him and after him," his sword shining brighter than the sun. Behind him ride the sons of Muspell in glittering array, and when they cross the rainbow bridge Bifröst, it breaks beneath them — the bridge the gods always knew was too beautiful to be strong.

**Fenrir** advances with his upper jaw against the sky and his lower against the earth — he would gape wider, Snorri says, if there were room — with fire burning from his eyes and nostrils. And against them all come the [Æsir](/realms/asgard): Odin riding first in his gold helmet and shining mail, [Gungnir](/artifacts/gungnir) in his fist, with the einherjar — every warrior who ever died in battle and was carried to Valhalla — arming and marching out through the hall's five hundred and forty doors, eight hundred abreast from each. The whole point of Valhalla, the entire economy of the valkyries and the battle-dead, was always this single morning.

They meet on **Vígríðr**, the plain appointed for it, a hundred leagues on every side. Vafþrúðnismál names the field centuries in advance, as calmly as a road-sign.

## The last battle: who fights whom

The final battle is not a melee but a series of duels, each one the payoff of an old story. The mythology has spent its whole length arranging these pairings, and it settles them one by one.

**Odin and Fenrir.** The Allfather rides straight at the wolf — the monster he ordered bound, the son of the blood-brother he wronged — and the wolf swallows him whole. There is no euphemism in the sources: the highest of the gods, with all his wisdom, his runes, his dead seeresses and pickled counsel-heads, goes down the throat of the thing he saw coming. Völuspá calls it the grief of grief: "then falls Frigg's second sorrow."

**Víðarr's vengeance.** It lasts moments. [Víðarr](/gods/vidar), Odin's silent son, steps forward and plants his foot on the wolf's lower jaw — a foot wearing the famous shoe assembled through all of time from the leather scraps men cut from the toes and heels of their shoes (throw the scraps away, Snorri advises, if you wish to help the gods). He seizes the upper jaw and tears the wolf apart; Völuspá gives the variant that he drives his sword into the wolf's heart through the gaping mouth. Either way, the father is avenged before his body is cold, and the silent god's one deed in all the mythology is this one.

**Thor and Jörmungandr.** [Thor](/gods/thor) cannot help Odin, because he is fighting the thing he was always going to fight: the World Serpent he once hauled from the sea on a fishing line and once lifted as a grey cat in a giant's hall. This time no one cuts the line. [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) crushes the serpent's head — and then the serpent's venom, sprayed over the god through the whole struggle, does its work. Thor walks nine steps from the corpse and falls dead. The defender of Midgard dies having, one last time, done exactly his job.

**Freyr and Surtr.** [Freyr](/gods/freyr), the bright lord of harvest and peace, meets the fire-giant — and fights him without his sword, the good blade that fought by itself, because he gave it away long ago to win the giantess Gerðr. Snorri says plainly that the missing sword is the death of him. Freyr falls, and the cost of an old bride-price comes due at the world's end.

**Týr and Garmr.** The one-handed god of oaths and the howling hound of Gnipahellir kill each other. There is a rough symmetry to it: the god who lost his hand binding one monster dies destroying another. (Some scholars suspect Garmr is simply Fenrir under another name in the older poetry, in which case Snorri, ever the systematizer, has given Týr an opponent so that no god goes unpaired.)

**Heimdall and Loki.** The watchman and the trickster — enemies of old, who once fought as seals over Freyja's necklace — meet last and kill each other. The god who guards every boundary and the god who exists to cross them cancel each other out, and no one is left at the bridge, and no one is left to lie.

Then **Surtr flings fire over the whole world.** The sun goes black, the earth sinks into the sea, steam surges against the stars, and flame plays against heaven itself. Völuspá's stanza on the burning is one of the most quoted in Old Norse; it reads less like a battle-report than like a memory.

## Who survives Ragnarök?

More than the popular versions admit. The sources agree on a small, careful list:

- **Víðarr and Váli**, Odin's sons — the avenger of Odin and the avenger of Baldr — survive the fire, and neither flame nor flood harms them. They dwell where Asgard once stood, on the plain of Iðavöllr.
- **Magni and Móði**, Thor's sons ("Strength" and "Courage"), survive and carry Mjölnir between them — the hammer outlives its owner, and the world's protection passes to the next generation.
- **Baldr and Höðr** return from the dead. Hel must finally give Baldr back, and he comes up with his blind brother beside him, killer and killed reconciled, and they sit down together in the grass and talk over what happened.
- **Hœnir** appears in the renewed world in Völuspá, handling the lot-twigs of prophecy — the old hostage god given, at last, something to say.
- **Two humans, Líf and Lífþrasir** ("Life" and "Striver-after-life" or "Life's eager one"), survive by hiding in Hoddmímis holt — understood by most readers as the wood of the world-tree, Yggdrasil itself. They live on morning-dew through the fire, and from them all humankind descends again. Vafþrúðnismál is the source: the giant tells Odin about them as calmly as everything else.
- **A daughter of the sun**, no less bright than her mother, rides the old road across the sky. Even the light is reborn with an heir.

## The world after: green earth and golden game-pieces

Völuspá's ending is the most beautiful passage in Norse literature, and the least remembered. The seeress sees the earth rise a second time out of the sea, green anew; the waterfalls pour, the eagle flies over them hunting fish along the fell. The surviving gods meet on Iðavöllr and talk of the mighty World Serpent, of the old dooms and Odin's ancient runes — and there in the grass they find the golden *töflur*, the game-pieces the Æsir had owned in the morning of the world, when they played at tables in the golden age before the first war. The find is the whole theology in one image: what was lost in the beginning is recovered after the end.

Fields grow unsown. A hall stands on Gimlé, fairer than the sun, thatched with gold, where the righteous — Snorri's word — will live in delight forever. And then Völuspá closes on a deliberate shadow: the dark dragon Níðhöggr comes flying from below, bearing corpses in his feathers — and "now she must sink down." Whether the dragon is the last of the old world being carried away, or the first crack in the new one, the poem refuses to say. It ends with the seeress going under, and the question open.

## Völuspá vs. Gylfaginning: how the two tellings differ

Everything above is stitched from two main tellings, and they are not identical.

**Völuspá** ("The Seeress's Prophecy"), the first poem of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), is the older and stranger witness — most scholars date it around the year 1000, on the cusp of Iceland's conversion. It is visionary, compressed, allusive: the seeress speaks in flashes, repeats her refrains ("the hound bays loud before Gnipahellir... would you know yet more?"), and assumes you already know the stories. In Völuspá, Víðarr kills Fenrir with a sword-thrust to the heart; Naglfar comes from the east with Hrymr steering; Þórr's death is told in two dense lines; and the rebirth, Gimlé, and the final dragon are all present.

**Gylfaginning**, in Snorri Sturluson's [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) (written c. 1220, two centuries into the Christian era), is the systematic retelling. Snorri quotes Völuspá extensively but smooths it into orderly prose: he fixes the length of Fimbulvetr, measures Vígríðr, pairs every god with an opponent (adding Týr/Garmr, which no surviving poem attests), puts Loki at a ship's helm, has Víðarr tear the wolf's jaws rather than stab it, and supplies the homely details — the nail-ship etiquette, the leather-scrap shoe. He also sorts the afterlife of the reborn world into layered, rather Christian-looking halls of reward. Snorri is invaluable and unreliable in the same breath: without him we could not assemble the sequence, but wherever he is our only source, we must remember we are reading a thirteenth-century Christian antiquarian imposing order on poems that preferred lightning to order.

**Vafþrúðnismál** supplies a third strand: the wisdom-contest poem in which Odin himself, disguised, quizzes the giant Vafþrúðnir about the end — and learns the names of Vígríðr, Líf and Lífþrasir, the sun's daughter, and the gods who will dwell after the fire. Its Ragnarök is entirely matter-of-fact, which is somehow the most chilling telling of all.

And one witness is not a text at all: the **Gosforth Cross** in Cumbria, England — a tenth-century stone cross carved with scenes most scholars read as Ragnarök, including Víðarr prying open the wolf's jaws and a bound figure with a woman holding a bowl, side by side with Christian imagery. Someone in Viking-age England thought the doom of the old gods and the crucifixion belonged on the same stone.

## What scholars make of it

**Volcanic and climatic memory.** Several scholars have argued that the imagery of Ragnarök — earth-fire, black sun, steam against the stars, a years-long winter — draws on real experience. Icelanders composed and preserved these poems on an island of eruptions, and Völuspá's burning world reads uncannily like an eyewitness account of one. More broadly, some researchers connect the Fimbulvetr tradition to the historical climate catastrophe of 536–540 CE, when volcanic dust-veiling dimmed the sun for years, crops failed across Scandinavia, and archaeology shows massive settlement abandonment. A society that had actually lived through summerless years might well remember them as the beginning of the end of the world.

**Christian influence.** The other long argument concerns the ending. A final battle, a world consumed by fire, a renewed earth, a shining hall for the righteous — the parallels with Christian apocalyptic (and Völuspá's likely composition at the moment of conversion) have led many scholars to see the rebirth, or at least its coloring, as influenced by the new religion, with Baldr's return sometimes read as a Christ-echo. Others push back: world-fire and rebirth have Indo-European cousins, Baldr's story resists a clean Christian mapping, and the poem's grief for the doomed gods is nothing a missionary would write. The honest summary is that Völuspá stands exactly on the threshold between two faiths and can be read facing either way — which may be precisely why it survived.

**The shape of the myth.** However it was assembled, Ragnarök makes Norse mythology structurally unique: it is a mythology with a plot. Greek gods are immortal and their stories circular; the Norse gods are mortal on a known date, and every myth — [the binding of the wolf](/events/binding-of-fenrir), [the building of the wall](/events/building-of-asgards-wall), [the flyting at Ægir's feast](/events/flyting-of-loki) — is a scene in a single tragedy the audience already knows the end of. The gods' courage is not the courage of the invincible. It is the other kind.

## Legacy

Ragnarök has had an afterlife as vigorous as Baldr's. Wagner's *Götterdämmerung* (1876) made the "twilight of the gods" a fixture of European high culture; Tolkien absorbed its "northern courage" — the theory of glory in guaranteed defeat — into the bones of Middle-earth; and the word itself now names comics, films, video games, and at least one metal subgenre's entire emotional register. Almost all of these keep the fire and drop the green earth. The originals did the opposite of despair: in the Eddas, the point of the world's end is that the world comes back — the grass, the gods' children, the two people stepping out of the tree, and the golden game-pieces waiting in the grass to be found.

## FAQ

**What does Ragnarök mean in English?**
It means "the fate (or doom) of the powers," from *regin*, "the ruling gods," and *rök*, "fate, destiny." The translation "Twilight of the Gods" comes from a variant form, *ragnarøkkr* ("darkening of the gods"), popularized by Wagner's *Götterdämmerung*; most scholars consider "fate/doom of the gods" the older meaning.

**Who survives Ragnarök?**
Among the gods: Víðarr and Váli (Odin's sons), Magni and Móði (Thor's sons, who inherit Mjölnir), the returned Baldr and Höðr, and Hœnir. Two humans, Líf and Lífþrasir, survive hidden in the world-tree and repeople the earth, and the sun's daughter takes her mother's road across the sky.

**Does Loki die at Ragnarök?**
Yes. After breaking free of his bonds and steering a ship of the dead to the battlefield, Loki meets Heimdall, his oldest enemy, and the two kill each other on the plain of Vígríðr.

**Who kills Odin, and is he avenged?**
The wolf Fenrir swallows Odin whole. He is avenged almost immediately by his son Víðarr, who pins the wolf's lower jaw with his famous shoe and tears its jaws apart — or, in Völuspá's version, stabs it to the heart through the open mouth.

**Has Ragnarök already happened?**
Within the mythology's own frame, no — the seeress of Völuspá foretells it in the future tense. But some scholars think the imagery preserves memories of real past catastrophes, especially the volcanic "dust-veil" winters of 536–540 CE that devastated Scandinavia, refracted into prophecy.

**Is Ragnarök the end of everything?**
No — and this is the most commonly forgotten part. After the fire and flood, the earth rises green from the sea, surviving gods gather where Asgard stood, fields grow unsown, and humanity begins again from Líf and Lífþrasir. Ragnarök is a destruction and a renewal in one movement, though Völuspá closes on the ambiguous image of the dragon Níðhöggr still flying.
