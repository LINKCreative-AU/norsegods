The first war in the world began, Völuspá says, when the Æsir speared and thrice burned a woman called Gullveig ('Gold-drink') — and thrice she was reborn, walking on as the seiðr-witch Heiðr. Whether she was a Vanir emissary or gold-greed personified, the Vanir demanded redress; Odin answered by hurling Gungnir over their host, the spear-cast that opens every war. The Vanir broke Asgard's wall; the fighting swung both ways; neither side could win.

The peace was Norse to the bone: not a treaty but an exchange of hostages. Njörðr, Freyr, and Freyja crossed to the Æsir — bringing fertility, wealth, and seiðr into Asgard's bloodline — while Hœnir and wise Mímir went to Vanaheimr. That trade soured: handsome Hœnir was mute without Mímir's counsel, and the Vanir, feeling cheated, beheaded Mímir and sent the head to Odin, who preserved it in herbs and consults it still.

The truce itself was sealed by both sides spitting into one vat, and from the mingled spittle the gods shaped Kvasir, the wisest of beings — whose later murder by two dwarves, his blood brewed with honey, became the Mead of Poetry. The myth reads like tribal history remembered as theology: two cults, a war neither could win, and a merger whose by-product was poetry itself.

## Two tribes of gods

Norse mythology has not one divine family but two. The **Æsir** — [Odin](/gods/odin), [Thor](/gods/thor), [Frigg](/gods/frigg), [Týr](/gods/tyr) and their kin — are the gods of sovereignty, war, law, and sky, dwelling in [Asgard](/realms/asgard). The **Vanir** — [Njörðr](/gods/njord) and his children [Freyr](/gods/freyr) and [Freyja](/gods/freyja) — are gods of fertility, wealth, seafaring, and pleasure, at home in Vanaheim. That the mythology remembers a time when these two families met in open war, and explains the mixed pantheon of the historical cult as the outcome of a hostage exchange, is one of its most remarkable features. No other event tells us as much about how the Norse imagined their own religion: not as a seamless whole, but as a merger with a violent prehistory.

## Gullveig: the woman who would not die

Völuspá gives the cause in two of its most disputed stanzas. A woman called **Gullveig** — the name suggests "gold-drink" or "gold-intoxication" — came among the Æsir, and they lifted her on spears and burned her in Odin's hall. Three times they burned her; three times she was reborn. "Again and again, and yet she lives." Under the name **Heiðr** ("Bright," a stock name for wandering seeresses) she went from house to house practicing *seiðr* — the ecstatic, prophetic, fate-bending magic — charming spells, playing with minds, "ever the delight of an evil bride."

Who was she? The poem never says, and the argument is as old as the scholarship. The dominant reading makes her a Vanir figure — many scholars suspect she is Freyja herself under a byname, since Freyja is the mistress of seiðr and, per Snorri, the one who first taught it to the Æsir. On this reading, the Æsir's attempt to destroy a Vanir sorceress (or their corruption by the gold she embodies) was the provocation. Others read her as gold-greed personified, thrice-refined in fire the way smiths refine ore, her indestructibility a parable about wealth: burn it as often as you like, it walks again. What Völuspá says plainly is what came next: the powers all went to their judgment-seats and debated whether the Æsir should pay a tribute-fine, or all gods share the worship — a line that sounds exactly like the negotiation of two cults over sacrifices. Then talk failed.

## The spear-cast and the broken wall

"Odin hurled his spear, shot it over the host: that was war, still, the first in the world." Völuspá's stanza became the very definition of how war begins — casting a spear over the enemy's ranks dedicated them to Odin, a rite Viking-Age warriors actually performed, and here the god performs it first, with [Gungnir](/artifacts/gungnir), against other gods. The poem then gives the Æsir the worse of it: "broken was the plank-wall of the Æsir's stronghold; the Vanir, war-wise, trod the field." Asgard's defenses were breached — the same broken wall that a mysterious builder later offers to rebuild, in [the story of Asgard's wall](/events/building-of-asgards-wall), for the price of Freyja, the sun, and the moon.

Snorri's Ynglinga saga adds the strategic summary the poem skips: each side ravaged the other's land, both sides won victories, and both grew weary — a war of attrition between equals. The gods of battle could not defeat the gods of abundance, and the gods of abundance could not finish the gods of battle. So they did what exhausted Norse chieftains did: they made a settlement.

## Hostages, a beheading, and a head in herbs

The peace was sealed by exchange of hostages — in the medieval sense, honored persons sent to live among former enemies as living guarantees. The Vanir sent their best: **Njörðr** and **Freyr** (with **Freyja**), who came to Asgard, took up priesthoods of the sacrifices, and became so thoroughly Æsir that most people know them as nothing else. With them came seiðr, which Freyja taught — magic the Æsir found powerful and slightly shameful, as Loki does not let Odin forget in [the flyting](/events/flyting-of-loki).

The Æsir's counter-offer was where the trouble lay. They sent **Hœnir** — big, handsome, and chieftain-like — and with him **Mímir**, the wisest of beings, as his counselor. The Vanir made Hœnir a chief at once; and Hœnir did splendidly in every assembly *at which Mímir stood beside him*. Whenever hard questions came and Mímir was absent, Hœnir's answer was always the same: "let others decide." The Vanir concluded they had been swindled in the exchange — sold a figurehead — and, in Ynglinga saga's brutally practical sentence, they seized Mímir, beheaded him, and sent the head to Odin. Odin took the head, preserved it with herbs so it would not rot, and spoke charms over it, so that it talks with him and tells him hidden things. The wisest counsel in the cosmos thereafter lives in a pickled head — the one Odin consults at the well beneath the world-tree, and again on the morning of [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok). It is entirely characteristic of this mythology that its peace treaty produces one atrocity and one oracle, and that Odin profits from both.

## The vat of spittle

The truce itself was ratified by the oldest fellowship-rite imaginable: everyone on both sides walked to a vat and spat into it. Fermented drinks in many traditional cultures were actually started with saliva, so the image is less bizarre than it first appears — the two tribes were, quite literally, brewing something together. And rather than let the peace-token be poured away, the gods shaped it into a person: **Kvasir**, wisest of all beings, who could answer any question. In him the war's two halves were finally one flesh. His later fate — murdered by dwarves, his blood brewed into [the Mead of Poetry](/events/mead-of-poetry) — means that skaldic verse itself is the last distillation of the Æsir–Vanir peace. Poetry, in the Norse accounting, is what two enemy tribes of gods made when they finally stopped fighting.

## In the primary sources

No single source tells the whole story, and the three witnesses pull in different directions. **Völuspá** ([Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda)) gives the oldest, most cryptic account: Gullveig's burnings, the debate over tribute, the spear-cast, the broken wall — and stops there, with no hostages and no ending. **Skáldskaparmál** ([Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda)) skips the war and gives the peace-meeting, the vat, and Kvasir, as backstory to the mead. **Ynglinga saga**, opening Snorri's Heimskringla, tells the fullest connected version — the attrition, the hostages, Hœnir's empty chieftaincy, Mímir's beheading — but tells it euhemerized, with Odin as a mortal chieftain of "Asia-men" and the Vanir as a neighboring people. Assembling one narrative means trusting Snorri to have known more than the poem shows — a standing gamble of Norse scholarship. Older theories read the myth as folk-memory of a real prehistoric collision between an invading sky-cult and a native fertility-cult; most current scholars are warier, noting that a war-then-merger of divine tribes is an inherited Indo-European story-pattern (Rome told one about Romans and Sabines). Either way, the myth's function is clear: it explains why gods of war and gods of harvest shared the North's temples, altars, and toasts.

## FAQ

**What caused the war between the Æsir and the Vanir?**
Völuspá traces it to the Æsir spearing and thrice burning the mysterious Gullveig, who was thrice reborn and practiced seiðr as "Heiðr." Whether she was a Vanir emissary (perhaps Freyja) or an embodiment of gold-lust, negotiations over redress failed, and Odin opened hostilities by hurling his spear over the Vanir host.

**Who won the Æsir–Vanir war?**
Nobody. Völuspá says the Vanir broke Asgard's wall; Snorri says both sides won victories and wearied of the fighting. The war ended in a negotiated peace with an exchange of hostages — which is why both families of gods were worshipped side by side.

**Which gods were exchanged as hostages?**
The Vanir sent Njörðr and Freyr — Freyja came too — to live among the Æsir. The Æsir sent Hœnir and the wise Mímir to Vanaheim, an exchange the Vanir came to regret when Hœnir proved helpless without Mímir's counsel.

**Why was Mímir beheaded?**
The Vanir felt cheated: Hœnir, their new chieftain, could only say "let others decide" whenever Mímir wasn't beside him. In retaliation they beheaded Mímir and sent the head to Odin — who preserved it with herbs and charms, and consults it for hidden knowledge ever after.

**What is the connection between the war and the Mead of Poetry?**
The peace was sealed when both sides spat into a single vat, and from that spittle the gods created Kvasir, the wisest of beings. When the dwarves Fjalar and Galar later murdered Kvasir and brewed his blood with honey, the result was the Mead of Poetry that Odin eventually stole.

**Is the war based on real history?**
Older scholarship read it as a memory of a real clash between an incoming warrior-cult and a native fertility religion. Most modern scholars are more cautious, seeing an inherited Indo-European myth-pattern in which two divine classes fight and then merge. The sources themselves — Völuspá, Skáldskaparmál, and Ynglinga saga — each tell only part of the story.
