Not every giant wants the gods dead. When Loki tricked a hammerless Thor into visiting the murderous giant Geirröðr, the road passed Gríðr's cave, and she talked: Geirröðr means to kill you. Then she armed him from her own kit — her belt of strength, her iron gloves, and her unbreakable staff, Gríðarvölr — the equipment that let Thor survive a rising river of giantess piss and a chair pressed toward the roof to crush him.

She is also the mother, by Odin, of Víðarr — the silent god who avenges the Allfather at Ragnarök. The gods' survival kit and their avenger both come from a giantess's cave: the myths' quiet reminder that the border between Asgard and Jötunheimr runs through bedrooms as often as battlefields.

Gríðr's entire attested biography fits into a night's lodging and a genealogical note, and it is worth being upfront about that thinness: she appears, essentially, in one chapter of Snorri's Skáldskaparmál. Yet her two contributions — the loaned war-gear and the son — sit at load-bearing points of the mythology, one saving [Thor](/gods/thor)'s life in the middle of the story and the other avenging [Odin](/gods/odin)'s at the end of it. Small part, enormous consequences.

## The night at Grid's cave

The context is the [Geirröd](/giants/geirrod) affair. [Loki](/gods/loki), caught in falcon-shape and starved in a chest for three months, bought his freedom with a promise: he would deliver Thor to Geirröðr's courts *without* his hammer, his belt of strength, or his iron gloves — the only recorded plot against a deliberately disarmed Thor. Loki, being Loki, talked Thor into the journey, and Snorri notes the one stroke of luck in it: on the way they lodged with a giantess called Gríðr, "mother of Víðarr the Silent."

What Gríðr did that night was commit treason against her own kind, quietly and completely. She told Thor the truth about his host — that Geirröðr was a cunning giant, "dog-wise," and bad to deal with — and then she armed the god from her own possessions: a belt of strength (*megingjörð*, the same term used for Thor's own), a pair of iron gloves, and her staff, called after her Gríðarvölr, "Gríðr's wand." Every item mattered. Crossing the river Vimur, swollen to Thor's shoulders by Geirröðr's daughter Gjálp straddling it upstream, Thor steadied himself against the current with Gríðarvölr while the borrowed belt gave him the strength to wade. In Geirröðr's hall, when the chair beneath him surged toward the rafters to crush him, he braced the staff against the roof and pushed back, breaking the backs of both giant daughters beneath the seat. And when Geirröðr flung a white-hot iron bolt, the iron gloves caught it out of the air for the return throw that killed the host through his own pillar. Thor walked out of the deadliest ambush of his career wearing, head to toe, a giantess's spare equipment.

Why did she help? Snorri does not say, and the honest answer is that no source tells us Gríðr's motives. The usual inference is family: as Odin's former lover and the mother of his son, she had standing ties to [Asgard](/realms/asgard), and Thor was her son's kinsman. Whatever the reason, the myths give her no reward scene and no further appearances — helpfulness, unlike villainy, apparently generated no sequels.

## Grid, Odin, and Vidar the avenger

The genealogical note is briefer still and heavier. Skáldskaparmál, listing kennings, establishes that Víðarr is the son of Odin and the giantess Gríðr. Víðarr — "the silent god," owner of the thick shoe, nearly Thor's equal in strength — is the god built for one moment: at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), after [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) swallows Odin, Víðarr strides forward, sets his famous shoe on the wolf's lower jaw, seizes the upper, and tears the beast apart (or, in Völuspá's version, drives his blade into its heart). He then survives the world-fire to inherit the new earth. That the Allfather's avenger and heir is half jötunn — Gríðr's boy — is one of the mythology's structural ironies: the gods' future is carried by giant blood, exactly as their past descends from the giantess [Bestla](/giants/bestla). Gríðr thus stands in a small, crucial sorority of giant mothers of gods, alongside Bestla (mother of Odin) and Járnsaxa (mother of Thor's son Magni).

## Why is a giantess helping the gods?

Gríðr is the cleanest example of a figure the sources otherwise show in glimpses: the friendly jötunn woman. Norse myth's frame is a war of gods and giants, but its texture is full of crossings — marriages ([Gerd](/giants/gerd)), hospitality ([Ægir](/giants/aegir)), and here, outright military aid. Scholars have read Gríðr variously as a survival of an older, less polarized mythology; as a narrative necessity (Thor must be armed somehow, and a helper at the threshold is a folktale universal); or simply as evidence that jötnar are a people, not a species of villain, with individuals free to choose sides. One caution for readers chasing her through the sources: the name Gríðr — which may mean something like "vehemence, greed" or, by other etymologies, "peace, truce" (the scholarship genuinely disagrees) — was a productive giantess-name, appearing in the þulur name-lists and attached to an ogress in the legendary saga Illuga saga Gríðarfóstra. That saga's Gríðr, a curse-bound troll-woman with a beautiful daughter, is a different literary creature and no reliable evidence for the mythological figure, though the shared name shows the type — the formidable cave-dwelling woman who tests and then helps a hero — had deep roots.

## In the primary sources

Everything secure about Gríðr comes from the **Prose Edda**: Skáldskaparmál's telling of the Geirröðr expedition (her warning, the belt, the gloves, and Gríðarvölr) and its statements, there and in the kenning-lists, that Víðarr is her son by Odin. The skaldic poem **Þórsdrápa**, which Snorri quotes as his source for the Geirröðr adventure, describes the river-crossing and the staff but tells the story so obliquely that Gríðr herself is a matter of interpretation in its kennings. She appears in the **þulur** among giantess names. No eddic poem narrates her, and no saga preserves the mythological Gríðr — a one-scene character whose props saved the thunder-god and whose son outlives the world.

## FAQ

**Who is Grid in Norse mythology?**
A giantess, friendly to the gods, who warned Thor that the giant Geirröd planned to kill him and lent Thor her belt of strength, iron gloves, and unbreakable staff, Gríðarvölr. She is also the mother of the god Víðarr by Odin.

**What is Gridarvolr?**
"Gríðr's staff" — the pole she lent Thor. He used it to brace against the flooding river Vimur and to push back the chair with which Geirröd's daughters tried to crush him against the roof, breaking both their backs.

**Why did Grid help Thor against her fellow giants?**
No source states her motive. The likeliest reading is loyalty through her son: as mother of Odin's child Víðarr she had family on the gods' side, and Thor arrived at her cave as her son's kinsman walking into a trap.

**Who is Grid's son?**
Víðarr, "the silent god," second in strength only to Thor. At Ragnarök he avenges Odin by killing the wolf Fenrir, and he is among the gods who survive to inherit the renewed world — making Gríðr an ancestress of the next cosmos.

**Is Grid in the Poetic Edda?**
No. Her story survives only in Snorri's Prose Edda (Skáldskaparmál) and, indirectly, in the difficult skaldic poem Þórsdrápa that Snorri used. The name Gríðr also appears in giantess name-lists and, for an unrelated character, in a late legendary saga.
