Freyr saw her from Hliðskjálf, Odin's forbidden high seat: a girl in her father Gymir's courtyard whose arms, as she lifted them, made air and water shine. The lovesickness that followed cost him his appetite, his sleep, and — when his servant Skírnir agreed to woo her in his place — his self-fighting sword.

Skírnismál is the wooing, and it is not romantic: Gerðr refuses Freyr's gold (eleven apples, the ring Draupnir) and his threats, until Skírnir carves curse-runes promising her madness, monstrousness, and endless unwanted desire. She yields to the curse, not the gifts, naming a grove nine nights hence. 'Long is a night,' groans Freyr, 'longer are two — how shall I bear three?' Their union — sky-fertility and frozen earth, thawed by force — reads as an old seasonal myth with its bruises showing; their son Fjölnir begins the Yngling line of Swedish kings.

Gerðr is the most consequential marriage in Norse myth measured by what it cost: a god's sword, and therefore — when [Surtr](/giants/surtr) comes at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) and [Freyr](/gods/freyr) stands weaponless — a god's life. She is also one of the few jötunn women the tradition treats as fully divine afterward: Snorri counts her among the Ásynjur, the goddesses, and a royal dynasty claimed descent from her. Few figures cross the border between [Jötunheim](/realms/jotunheim) and [Asgard](/realms/asgard) so completely, or on worse terms.

## The wooing of Gerd, scene by scene

Skírnismál stages the story as theater — it is almost entirely dialogue, and may have been performed. It opens with the gods worried: Freyr sits apart, silent, and no one dares question him until Skírnir, his page and friend, is sent to try. Freyr's confession is one of the tenderest speeches in the Edda: in Gymir's courts he saw a maiden walking whose arms shone, "and from them all the sea and sky took light"; she is dearer to him than any maiden to any man since the dawn of days, "and none of Æsir or elves will have it that we come together." Skírnir agrees to ride and woo her — asking in return the sword that swings itself against giant-kind, and the horse that can pass the flickering flame around Gymir's courts. Freyr hands both over without a visible flinch. The audience, knowing what waits on the plain of Vígríðr, is meant to flinch for him.

Skírnir rides through darkness and fire, past Gymir's savage hounds, to where Gerðr sits among her women. Her first words on hearing the din at the gates are pure jötunn steel: she suspects it is her brother's slayer outside, but bids the stranger in for the ancient mead anyway. The negotiation that follows runs the whole scale of persuasion. Skírnir offers eleven golden apples — some read them as the apples of youth themselves — for a single "yes" to Freyr. Refused: never, while life lasts, will she and Freyr share a home. He offers Draupnir, [Odin](/gods/odin)'s ring that drips eight matching rings every ninth night, burned on Baldr's pyre. Refused: there is no lack of gold in Gymir's courts. He shows her the naked sword and promises to strike her head from her neck. She is unmoved: coercion will not work, and if Gymir finds him, there will be a fight worth watching.

Then Skírnir stops threatening her body and begins on her existence — and Skírnismál becomes the most chilling stretch of verse in the Edda. With a "taming-wand" and carved runes he unspools a curse: she will sit on the eagle's mound at the world's edge, staring toward Hel; food will disgust her; she will be a spectacle gaped at by all beings; madness, wailing, and unbearable longing will be her portion; she will wither like a dried thistle in the eaves; her husband will be a three-headed þurs in the roots of the forest, or no husband at all; the gods' wrath will pen her in. He begins to carve the final runes — þurs, and three staves more: lewdness, frenzy, and unbearable craving — adding that he can scrape them off again, if there is cause. And Gerðr breaks. "Welcome now rather, lad, and take the icy cup full of ancient mead" — the formal drink of acceptance — "though I never believed I should love one of the Vanir." She names the meeting: the grove Barri, quiet, known to them both, nine nights hence. Skírnir rides home to a Freyr pacing the gate, and delivers the news; the poem ends not with the wedding but with the bridegroom's famous groan at three nights' wait — half a year, he calls it, has often seemed shorter.

## What does the marriage of Freyr and Gerd mean?

Older scholarship read the poem confidently as nature-allegory: the fertility god's emissary — perhaps a sunbeam, since Skírnir means "the shining one" — penetrates the frozen earth (Gerðr's name shares a root with *garðr*, enclosure, the fenced field), which resists the spring until it must yield, and the union in the grove Barri (often connected to barley) is the sown field embracing the sky. It is an attractive reading, and the poem's imagery supports pieces of it. But modern readers rightly notice what the allegory smooths over: Gerðr refuses gold, refuses fear for her life, and yields only when her mind and desire themselves are threatened. The poem knows the difference between wooing and breaking, and stages the breaking in full view. Whether its first audiences heard sacred marriage, royal propaganda for Yngling ancestors, or exactly the coercion we hear, is one of the liveliest debates in eddic studies — the honest summary is that Skírnismál will bear all three readings and endorses none aloud.

What the tradition agrees on is the aftermath. Snorri lists Gerðr among the goddesses; Lokasenna's taunts against Freyr treat the sword-price as common knowledge ("gold you gave for Gymir's daughter — and so gave away your sword; when Múspell's sons ride over Myrkwood, how will you fight then, wretch?"); and Ynglinga saga in Heimskringla makes Fjölnir, son of Freyr and Gerðr, the first of the Yngling kings from whom Sweden's and Norway's royal lines descended. Her mother, named in Hyndluljóð, is Aurboða; her father Gymir is a mountain-giant whose name also drifts, confusingly, onto the sea-giant [Ægir](/giants/aegir) in one prose note — most scholars keep the two Gymirs apart.

## In the primary sources

**Skírnismál** ("For Scírnis," the journey of Skírnir) in the Poetic Edda is the full drama — Freyr's lovesickness, the gifts, the threats, the curse, and Barri. **Gylfaginning** in the Prose Edda retells it compactly, naming Gerðr the most beautiful of all women, explaining Freyr's swordlessness at Ragnarök as its price, and admitting the god will regret the bargain when Múspell's sons ride. **Lokasenna** references the sword-price as a taunt. **Hyndluljóð** supplies her parents, Gymir and Aurboða. **Ynglinga saga** in Heimskringla gives her dynastic afterlife as ancestress of the Ynglings — a giantess at the root of real medieval king-lists.

## FAQ

**Who is Gerd in Norse mythology?**
A jötunn maiden, daughter of the giant Gymir and Aurboða, so beautiful that her raised arms lit sea and sky. The god Freyr saw her from Odin's high seat, fell desperately in love, and won her through his messenger Skírnir — at the cost of his magic sword.

**Did Gerd love Freyr?**
The sources are uncomfortable on purpose. In Skírnismál she rejects gifts and death-threats alike and consents only under a rune-curse threatening madness and monstrous marriage; her last words admit she never expected to love one of the Vanir. Snorri's retelling softens it to a simple match, and later tradition treats them as a settled divine couple.

**Why did Freyr give away his sword for Gerd?**
It was Skírnir's price for undertaking the wooing: the sword that fought giants by itself, plus the horse that could leap the wall of flame. The loss is fatal — at Ragnarök Freyr faces the fire-giant Surtr without it and falls.

**What does Gerd's name mean?**
It is related to *garðr*, "enclosure, fenced yard" — the same element as in Midgard and Asgard. Many read her as the enclosed earth or field itself, wooed by the fertility god: a seasonal myth of spring unlocking frozen ground.

**Did Gerd and Freyr have children?**
Yes — Fjölnir, according to Ynglinga saga, who succeeded Freyr in Uppsala and heads the legendary Yngling dynasty. Through him, medieval Swedish and Norwegian kings traced their blood to a god and a giantess.
