Before earth, sea, or sky there was only Ginnungagap, the yawning void, with icy Niflheimr to the north and burning Múspellsheimr to the south. Where rime met sparks, the melt quickened into Ymir, ancestor of all jötnar. As he slept, a male and female grew from the sweat of his armpit, and one leg begot a son upon the other. He fed on the milk of the primordial cow Auðumbla, who licked the salty ice into the shape of Búri, grandfather of Odin.

Odin and his brothers Vili and Vé slew Ymir, and the blood of his wounds drowned all the frost-giants but two. Then they carried the corpse to the middle of Ginnungagap and made the world of him: his flesh the earth, his blood the sea, his bones the mountains, his teeth the scree, his skull the dome of the sky — held up by four dwarves named North, South, East, and West — and his brains the clouds. From his eyebrows they walled Midgard, the world of men. Norse cosmology thus begins with a murder, and the world is a body.

That double image — first ancestor and first building material — makes Ymir the strangest and most important figure in the mythology. He never speaks, never acts, never wants anything that we are told of. He is simply the condition for everything else: the giants descend from his sweat, the gods descend from the ice-shape his nurse licked free, and the ground under every farm in [Midgard](/realms/midgard) is his flesh. Everything in the Norse cosmos, friend and enemy alike, is family, and everything stands on a corpse.

## The birth of the first being

The Prose Edda's Gylfaginning tells the beginning with unusual care, because Snorri Sturluson is answering the biggest question a mythology can be asked: what was there before anything? His answer, drawing on the poem Völuspá, is a void with a temperature gradient. To the north lay [Niflheim](/realms/niflheim), world of mist and ice, where the spring Hvergelmir fed the venom-cold rivers called Élivágar; to the south blazed [Muspelheim](/realms/muspelheim), where [Surtr](/giants/surtr) already sat with his burning sword. The rivers ran into Ginnungagap and froze, layer on layer; sparks and heat from the south drifted over the rime; and where the two met, the ice dripped and the drops took life. The being that formed was Ymir — though, Snorri adds, the frost-giants call him Aurgelmir.

Ymir's reproduction is as elemental as his birth. He never takes a mate; there is no one to take. Instead, as he sleeps and sweats, a man and a woman grow beneath his left arm, and one of his legs fathers a six-headed son upon the other. From these come the frost-giants, which is why the jötnar are, in the gods' eyes, a crooked and dangerous kin: they descend from a being the sources call evil, born without love or intention, multiplying like frost spreading across a window.

Beside him in the thaw stood the cow Auðumbla, herself formed from the melting rime. Four rivers of milk ran from her udders and fed Ymir. She, in turn, licked the salty ice-blocks, and over three days a figure emerged from the ice: hair the first day, a head the second, a whole man the third. This was Búri, beautiful and strong, whose son Borr married the giantess [Bestla](/giants/bestla), daughter of Bölþorn. Their sons were [Odin](/gods/odin), Vili, and Vé. Note what has happened in three generations: the line of the gods is already half jötunn. The killers of Ymir are his own kin by marriage — great-nephews, more or less, of the being they will dismantle.

## How was Ymir killed?

The sources give no battle scene, no weapon, no last words. Gylfaginning says only that Borr's sons killed Ymir, and that when he fell, so much blood ran from his wounds that the whole race of frost-giants drowned in it — all but one. Vafþrúðnismál preserves the survivor's name: Bergelmir, Ymir's grandson, who escaped with his household upon a *lúðr*. That word has puzzled readers for centuries — it can mean a mill-frame, a wooden box or trough, or by extension something boat-like — and Snorri, perhaps thinking of Noah, turns the scene into an ark-story. However Bergelmir floated, every giant afterward, from [Thrym](/giants/thrym) to [Hrungnir](/giants/hrungnir), descends from him, and the feud between gods and giants begins here: the giants have a drowned world to avenge.

Why the gods killed him, the myths never quite say. Snorri's Gylfaginning offers only that Ymir and all his kind were evil. Scholars have long noticed the shape of the story instead: a young generation of gods destroying the inert first being and making an ordered world from the pieces. It is creation by sacrifice — some point to parallels as far away as the Vedic Purusha, whose dismembered body likewise becomes the world — and it makes Odin's later obsession with sacrifice, including of himself, look like the family trade.

## What was made from Ymir's body?

The fullest inventory comes from Grímnismál, where Odin himself recites it, and from Gylfaginning, which expands the accounting:

- **Flesh** became the earth itself.
- **Blood** became the sea and all waters, laid as a ring around the land.
- **Bones** became the mountains; **teeth and shattered bone** became rocks and scree.
- **Skull** became the dome of the sky, set over the earth with a dwarf under each corner — Norðri, Suðri, Austri, and Vestri: North, South, East, and West.
- **Brains** were thrown into the air and became the clouds.
- **Hair**, in Grímnismál's telling, became the trees.
- **Eyebrows** were bent into the wall around Midgard, to keep the giants out of the world of men.

Even the maggots in the corpse were put to use: by the decree of the gods, the creatures that bred in Ymir's flesh took human-like form and wits and became the dwarves, who dwell in earth and stone to this day — that is, in the body they were born in. Sparks from Muspelheim were caught and set in the skull as sun, moon, and stars. Nothing is wasted; the entire visible world is Ymir, rearranged. Skalds understood this literally: in their verse the sky is "Ymir's skull," the sea "Ymir's blood," the earth "Ymir's flesh."

## Are Ymir and Aurgelmir the same being?

Yes — the two names belong to one figure, seen from two sides of the feud. In Vafþrúðnismál, when the disguised Odin quizzes the wise giant [Vafthrudnir](/giants/vafthrudnir) about the deepest past, the giant speaks of Aurgelmir, formed from the venom-drops of the Élivágar, and traces the line Aurgelmir–Þrúðgelmir–Bergelmir. Snorri, harmonizing the poems, states plainly that Aurgelmir is what the frost-giants call the being the rest of the tradition calls Ymir. The doubled name is a small window into how the myths worked before Snorri tidied them: different poems, different communities, different names for the same enormous fact at the bottom of time.

## Was Ymir good or evil?

Snorri says flatly that Ymir was evil, "as are all his kindred." But it is worth noticing that Ymir never does anything — he is born, eats, sleeps, breeds in his sleep, and is murdered. The judgment against him is the victors' judgment, delivered in a text framed by the gods' own descendants. Modern readers often find the myth more unsettling than Snorri intended: the world of gods and humans is built not just from a corpse but from a killing inside the family, and the drowning of the frost-giants sits in the story like an original sin. When [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) comes and the giants pour in to burn [Asgard](/realms/asgard), the mythology is, in one reading, simply balancing its first ledger.

## In the primary sources

Ymir appears at the foundations of the oldest cosmological poems. **Völuspá** (stanza 3) remembers the age "when Ymir lived," before sand or sea or cool waves, when earth was nowhere and heaven nowhere — the most famous lines of Norse cosmogony. **Vafþrúðnismál** (stanzas 20–35) supplies the fullest poetic account: the making of earth and sky from his flesh and skull, the venom-river origin, the name Aurgelmir, and Bergelmir's escape. **Grímnismál** (stanzas 40–41) gives the body-part inventory in Odin's own voice. In the Prose Edda, **Gylfaginning** (chapters 4–8) weaves these poems into the continuous narrative summarized above, quoting all three. Ymir has no saga appearances — he belongs entirely to the deep time before stories with human beings in them.

## Ymir in modern culture

The name has traveled widely: Marvel's comics and films use Ymir as their archetypal frost giant; the *God of War* games refer to his dismemberment in their world-building; and *Attack on Titan* borrows the name for its own progenitor-figure, Ymir Fritz, whose body likewise becomes the source of a whole race. These borrowings keep the core idea — a first being whose flesh underlies everything — while inventing freely around it; none should be mistaken for the Eddic account.

## FAQ

**Who was Ymir in Norse mythology?**
Ymir was the first living being, a primordial giant formed in the void Ginnungagap where the ice of Niflheim met the heat of Muspelheim. All jötnar descend from him, and the gods Odin, Vili, and Vé later killed him and built the world from his body.

**How was Ymir born?**
He was not born but condensed: melting rime in Ginnungagap quickened into life where frost met sparks. The primordial cow Auðumbla, formed in the same thaw, nourished him with her milk.

**Who killed Ymir and why?**
Odin and his brothers Vili and Vé killed him. The Prose Edda says only that Ymir and his offspring were evil; the deeper narrative purpose is creation — the ordered world is made from his dismembered corpse.

**What did Ymir's body become?**
His flesh became the earth, his blood the sea, his bones the mountains, his skull the sky, his brains the clouds, his hair the trees, and his eyebrows the wall around Midgard. The dwarves arose from the maggots in his flesh.

**Did any giants survive Ymir's death?**
One household did: Bergelmir, Ymir's grandson, escaped the flood of blood on a *lúðr* — a mill-frame, chest, or boat-like vessel, depending on interpretation. Every later giant in the mythology descends from him.
