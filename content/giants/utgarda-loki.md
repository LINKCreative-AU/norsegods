In the best comic myth in the Eddas, Thor, Loki, and the servant Þjálfi travel to the castle of Útgarða-Loki, king of the giants of the Outer Yards. On the way a giant called Skrýmir — so huge the travellers sleep in the thumb of his glove — snores beside them; Thor strikes his head three times with Mjölnir and the giant wakes asking if a leaf fell on him.

At the castle the travellers are set contests and lose them all: Loki loses an eating race to Logi; Þjálfi loses a footrace to Hugi; Thor fails to drain a drinking horn, cannot lift a grey cat's paw off the floor, and is wrestled to one knee by the crone Elli. Only at their departure does the giant-king dissolve the illusions: Logi was wildfire, Hugi was thought, the horn's far end stood in the ocean — and Thor's drinking made the tides — the cat was Jörmungandr, and Elli was Old Age, who fells everyone. Thor turns with his hammer raised, and the castle vanishes. It is the myths' sly admission that even divine strength loses to fire, thought, sea, and time.

The story fills three chapters of Gylfaginning and is Snorri Sturluson's masterpiece of narrative: a road comedy that turns, in its last page, into something close to philosophy. It deserves telling at full length.

## The journey to Útgarðr

It begins with dinner. Driving his goat-drawn chariot toward [Jötunheim](/realms/jotunheim), [Thor](/gods/thor) lodges overnight with a farming family and, as is his custom, slaughters his own goats for the pot — they can be resurrected with [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir), provided the bones are returned unbroken to the skins. The farmer's son Þjálfi splits a thigh-bone for the marrow, and in the morning one goat limps. Thor's knuckles whiten on the hammer-shaft; the terrified family buys peace by giving him their children, Þjálfi and Röskva, as servants forever. So the party that walks east — the goats left behind — is Thor, [Loki](/gods/loki), and two farm children.

Crossing the deep sea and a great forest, they find no lodging until nightfall offers a strange hall with an entrance as wide as the hall itself. At midnight an earthquake sends them huddling into a side-chamber, Thor standing guard at the door. Dawn explains everything: the earthquake was the snoring of a giant so vast that the "hall" was his glove, and the side-room its thumb. The giant names himself Skrýmir — "Big-fellow" — offers to carry their provisions in his bag, and strides ahead. That night Thor cannot open the bag: the knots will not budge, and hungry fury takes him. Three times in the dark he swings Mjölnir full-force into the sleeping giant's skull. Skrýmir stirs each time to ask whether a leaf, an acorn, or some twig-borne bird-dropping has fallen on him, and wonders aloud whether the little travelers slept well. In the morning he points them toward the stronghold of Útgarðr, advises them to turn back, and walks away into the mountains — and Snorri adds that the travelers were not sorry to see him go.

## The contests at the castle

Útgarðr's gates are so tall the travelers slip in between the bars. Inside, the king — Útgarða-Loki, "Loki of the Outer Yards," no relation to their companion, though the shared name has teased readers ever since — looks the newcomers over slowly and asks whether this scrap of a boy can really be Thor of the chariot. No one stays here, he says, without excelling at some art or skill. What follows is the finest rigged game in world myth.

Loki declares no one can eat faster than he and races a certain Logi down a trough of meat: Loki eats all the flesh, but Logi eats flesh, bones, and trough, and wins. Þjálfi, swiftest of men, runs three heats against a slight figure called Hugi and is beaten by widening margins. Then the king has his drinking horn brought for Thor: a good drinker empties it in one draught, and no one is so poor as to need three. Thor drinks three enormous pulls and barely lowers the level. Offered games for children, he is invited to lift the king's grey cat off the floor; heaving with all his strength, arched under the beast as it arches over him, he lifts a single paw. Stung, Thor demands a wrestling match, and the king — with elaborate apology — summons his old foster-mother, the crone [Elli](/giants/elli). The harder Thor grips, the firmer she stands, and after a hard exchange of holds the god of thunder is brought down to one knee before Útgarða-Loki stops the match. That night, at least, the beaten guests are feasted well.

## Did Thor really lose to Utgarda-Loki?

He lost every contest and won a kind of terrified respect, and the king explains why only when the travelers are safely outside the gates in the morning. Everything had been *sjónhverfing* — deluding of sight. Útgarða-Loki himself was Skrýmir on the road; the food-bag was wired shut with trick-iron; and Thor's three hammer-blows, each of which would have killed him, were caught on a mountain secretly interposed — the traveler will pass a flat-topped fell with three square valleys in it, the deepest where the third blow landed. Logi who out-ate Loki was wildfire itself, devouring trough and all. Hugi who outran Þjálfi was the king's thought, and no one outruns thought. The horn's far end stood in the sea, and Thor's three draughts lowered the ocean visibly — the origin, the king says, of the tides. The cat was [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), the Midgard Serpent that rings the world, and every watcher trembled when Thor hauled its back up toward the sky. And Elli was Old Age — and no one, however strong, escapes being brought down by old age at last. One knee, the king admits, was a marvel.

Then comes the ending Snorri times perfectly: never come back, says Útgarða-Loki, for I will defend my castle with tricks like these or better. Thor swings Mjölnir — and there is no king, no gate, no castle: only a wide and beautiful plain. The strongest being in the cosmos stands alone in the grass, holding a hammer with nothing to hit.

## Is Utgarda-Loki the same as Loki?

No — in Snorri's tale they stand in the same room as separate characters, the trickster of [Asgard](/realms/asgard) and the trick-king of the Outer Yards. But the shared name is a genuine puzzle, and the medieval evidence tangles it further. In Saxo Grammaticus's Gesta Danorum (Book 8), written around 1200, the Danish king Gorm sends the traveler Thorkillus on a nightmare voyage beyond the edge of the world to seek a being called **Utgarthilocus** — and finds him not enthroned but chained in a filthy cavern, gigantic, reeking, bound hand and foot with enormous fetters. Thorkillus plucks a hair from his beard as proof, and the stench of it later kills men. Saxo's chained giant in a cave looks far more like the bound Loki of the Eddas — fettered underground until [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — than like Snorri's urbane illusionist, and scholars have long suspected the two figures were once one, or at least entangled: an outer-world demon whom different traditions split into a clever king and a chained monster. The honest answer is that Snorri and Saxo preserve two irreconcilable versions, and both are worth knowing.

## In the primary sources

The full story occupies **Gylfaginning** chapters 44–47 in the Prose Edda — the goat-laming, Skrýmir, the contests, and the revelation — and it is told by a suspiciously well-informed narrator, since Gylfaginning's own frame is an illusion-contest between a traveling king and disguised masters of *sjónhverfing*. Snorri alone preserves the tale in this form; no eddic poem tells it, though Lokasenna and Hárbarðsljóð both needle Thor about cowering in a glove and the strap-knots of Skrýmir's bag — proof the story was old and famous enough to mock, well before Snorri wrote. **Gesta Danorum** Book 8 gives Saxo's grim variant, Utgarthilocus the chained and stinking giant. Beyond these, Útgarða-Loki has no attestations: two texts, two irreconcilable portraits, one unforgettable myth.

## FAQ

**What does the name Útgarða-Loki mean?**
"Loki of the Útgarðar," the Outer Yards — the wild space beyond the fenced human and divine worlds. He is the master of everything outside the walls: a king of the beyond, ruling by illusion rather than strength.

**Is Utgarda-Loki related to Loki?**
The texts treat them as distinct — Loki competes (and loses) in Útgarða-Loki's hall. But the shared name, and Saxo Grammaticus's account of a chained giant called Utgarthilocus who resembles the bound Loki, suggest the two figures were once connected in ways the surviving sources no longer explain.

**What tricks did Utgarda-Loki use against Thor?**
Sight-deceiving magic: an eating contest against wildfire, a footrace against thought, a drinking horn whose end stood in the ocean, a cat that was the World Serpent, and a wrestling match against Old Age. Thor's hammer-blows on the road were diverted into a mountain.

**Did Thor ever get revenge on Utgarda-Loki?**
No. When Thor raised Mjölnir at the final revelation, castle and king had already vanished. Snorri's frame-story does add a sly echo — the tale is told by illusionists who likewise vanish — but no myth records a rematch.

**What is the meaning of the Utgarda-Loki story?**
Beneath the comedy is a sober point: strength has limits. Fire devours more than appetite, thought outruns any foot, the sea cannot be drunk, the World Serpent cannot be lifted, and old age fells everyone. Even Thor's best is only a marvel — one knee, not victory.
