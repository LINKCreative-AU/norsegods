Angrboða ('she who brings grief') appears in few lines of the sources, but those lines carry the whole doom of the gods: by Loki she is mother of the wolf Fenrir, the World Serpent Jörmungandr, and half-dead Hel. When prophecy warned the gods what these children would become, Odin had them seized — the serpent hurled into the sea, Hel cast down to rule the dead, the wolf raised (and then bound) in Asgard.

She is generally identified with the old giantess of Völuspá who sits in Járnviðr, the Ironwood east of Midgard, breeding the wolf-kin from whom will come Sköll and Hati, the wolves that finally devour the sun and moon. Grief, as her name promises, on a cosmic schedule.

Few figures in Norse myth get so much consequence from so little text. Angrboða speaks no recorded word and performs no recorded act except the one that matters: motherhood. Yet trace any thread of [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) backward — the wolf that swallows [Odin](/gods/odin), the serpent that kills [Thor](/gods/thor), the queen who holds Baldr among the dead, the wolves that eat the sun and moon — and it runs to a giantess in a forest east of [Midgard](/realms/midgard). The gods' doom has a mother, and her name is a prophecy: *angr*, grief or sorrow; *boða*, to announce, to bode. She who forebodes sorrow.

## The mother of monsters

Gylfaginning tells the story with the economy of a case file. [Loki](/gods/loki) had yet more children: a giantess in [Jötunheim](/realms/jotunheim) called Angrboða, and by her three offspring — [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) the wolf, [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr) the Midgard Serpent, and Hel. When the gods learned that these three were being raised in Jötunheim, and prophecies warned that great harm would come to them from the siblings — evil to be expected, Snorri says, from their mother's nature, and worse from their father's — Odin sent the gods to fetch the children.

What follows is the mythology's most consequential act of preemption. Odin threw the serpent into the deep sea that rings all lands, where it grew until it circled the world and bit its own tail. Hel he cast down into Niflheim and gave her authority over nine worlds of the dead — the halls where, later, Baldr himself would sit. The wolf the gods kept at home, thinking to tame what they could watch; only [Tyr](/gods/tyr) was brave enough to feed him, and when Fenrir's growth frightened them, they bound him with the dwarf-forged fetter Gleipnir at the cost of Tyr's right hand. Every one of these containments becomes a countdown. The serpent will come ashore at Ragnarök; Hel's hosts will sail with the enemies of the gods; the wolf will break the fetter and swallow Odin whole. The gods' attempt to abort Angrboða's legacy is precisely what schedules it.

The sources do not say what Angrboða thought of losing all three children in a day. She is given no scene, no lament, no revenge. But the mythology may answer in its own oblique way, through the Ironwood.

## Who is the old one in the Ironwood?

Völuspá, in its survey of the gathering doom, pauses on an image: "East sat the old one in Ironwood, and bred there the broods of Fenrir; from them all shall come one of that kind, in troll's shape, the snatcher of the sun." Snorri expands: in the forest called Járnviðr, east of Midgard, dwell troll-women called járnviðjur, and an aged giantess there breeds many giant sons, all in wolf shapes — from whom come Sköll, the wolf that pursues the sun, and Hati Hróðvitnisson, who runs before her hunting the moon. At Ragnarök they catch what they chase.

Neither text names the old one, but the identification with Angrboða is ancient and natural: she is the mother of "Fenrir's kin" by definition, and Hati's patronymic — son of Hróðvitnir, a name for Fenrir — keeps it in the family. If the identification holds, Angrboða's answer to the gods' seizure of her children was simply to breed more of them, generation after generation, in a forest of iron trees, until her grandchildren run down the lights of heaven. It cannot be proven — the sources are too spare — and honest retellings say so. But no other candidate fits so well, and the epithet "Hag of Ironwood" has stuck to her for good reason.

## Angrboða and the burnt heart

One more fragment may belong to her file, and it is the strangest. Hyndluljóð — the section scholars call Völuspá hin skamma, "the Short Völuspá" — says first, plainly, that "Loki got the wolf on Angrboða." A few stanzas later it adds a darker verse: Loki ate a heart he found half-burned among embers, the heart of an evil woman, and from it became pregnant, and "from that come all ogresses on earth." The poem does not name the woman, and it does not connect the two stanzas. But some scholars have wondered whether the burnt-hearted woman is Angrboða herself — killed, perhaps burned, her essence swallowed by her lover and monstrously reborn. It is speculation built on a genuinely eerie verse, nothing more; the honest summary is that the tradition around Loki and Angrboða was once richer than what survives, and this stanza is a splinter of it.

## Is Angrboda in God of War accurate to the myths?

Barely — and the game's writers knew it. *God of War Ragnarök* makes Angrboda a young Ironwood giantess, a painter and guardian of giant souls, and gives her a warm relationship with Atreus/Loki. The sources' Angrboða is Loki's elder counterpart, mother (not companion) of his monstrous children, and no personality survives beyond her ominous name. The game keeps the Ironwood and the connection to Loki's fateful children; everything else is invention, made possible precisely because the Eddas left the page so blank. In Marvel's comics, by contrast, Angrboda barely registers — Hel(a), Fenrir, and the serpent appear without much of their mother.

## In the primary sources

Angrboða's entire direct attestation fits in a handful of lines. In the **Poetic Edda**, Hyndluljóð (in its Völuspá hin skamma section) states that Loki begot the wolf on Angrboða — the only occurrence of her name in the old poetry — and carries the burnt-heart stanza that may or may not concern her; Völuspá describes the unnamed old giantess of the Ironwood breeding Fenrir's kin. In the **Prose Edda**, Gylfaginning names her as the giantess in Jötunheim who bore Loki's three monstrous children and tells of their seizure and disposal; a later chapter repeats Völuspá's Ironwood material. She appears in no saga. Everything else said about her — in retellings, games, and novels — is extrapolation from these fragments.

## At Ragnarök

Angrboða herself is given no role in the last battle; her presence there is total nonetheless. Fenrir devours Odin. Jörmungandr and Thor kill each other. Hel's dead sail on Naglfar with Loki at the helm. And in the sky above the field of Vígríðr, her Ironwood brood finish their long hunt: Sköll swallows the sun, Hati takes the moon, and darkness falls on the world her children unmake. Every doom the völva foresaw for the gods passes through Angrboða. She announced grief, as her name said she would — and delivered it by the simplest means the myths know: children who become what they were always going to become.

## FAQ

**Who is Angrboda in Norse mythology?**
A jötunn woman of Jötunheim, called in Gylfaginning the mother, by Loki, of the wolf Fenrir, the World Serpent Jörmungandr, and Hel, ruler of the dead. Her name means "she who bodes grief."

**Was Angrboda Loki's wife?**
The sources never call her a wife. Loki's wife in Asgard is Sigyn, mother of Narfi and Váli; Angrboða is his lover or consort among the giants. The two households mirror Loki's double life between gods and jötnar.

**What happened to Angrboda's children?**
The gods, warned by prophecy, seized all three: Odin threw the serpent into the world-sea, sent Hel to rule the dead in Niflheim, and had the wolf bound with the magic fetter Gleipnir — the binding that cost Tyr his hand. All three return against the gods at Ragnarök.

**Is Angrboda the Hag of the Ironwood?**
Probably. Völuspá describes an unnamed old giantess in Járnviðr breeding Fenrir's wolf-kin, ancestors of the sun-devouring wolves; since Angrboða is the attested mother of Fenrir's line, she is generally identified with the Ironwood crone — though no source states it outright.

**Does Angrboda appear at Ragnarök?**
Not in person. But the battle is her family reunion: her son kills Odin, her other son kills Thor and dies, her daughter's hosts march with Loki, and her Ironwood descendants swallow the sun and moon.
