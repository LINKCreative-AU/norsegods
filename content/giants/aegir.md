Ægir personifies the ocean at its most generous: not the drowning sea but the feasting sea. Though a jötunn, he is on hospitable terms with the Æsir, brewing ale for all the gods in a cauldron a mile deep — the quest to fetch that cauldron from the giant Hymir is the plot of Hymiskviða. In his undersea hall, gleaming gold serves in place of firelight, and the ale-horns fill themselves.

His wife is Rán, who drags the drowned down in her net; their nine daughters are the waves, named for the sea's moods — and in some tellings these nine sisters are the mothers of Heimdallr. It was at Ægir's feast that Loki burst in to insult every god in turn, the poem Lokasenna. Skalds called the sea 'Ægir's jaws' and gold 'Ægir's fire.'

That marriage of images — the host and the taker under one roof — is the whole Norse sea in miniature. A seafaring people knew the ocean as both larder and grave, and they split it into a married couple: Ægir pours the ale, [Rán](/giants/ran) spreads the net. Uniquely among the jötnar, Ægir is never the gods' enemy. No god raids his hall, no hammer is raised against him. He is the standing proof that the border between [Asgard](/realms/asgard) and [Jötunheim](/realms/jotunheim) could be crossed by a well-set table.

## The brewer of the gods

The story begins, as Hymiskviða tells it, with the gods in the mood for a feast. They shake divination twigs and read the omens, and the answer points to Ægir: he has the makings of ale in abundance. [Thor](/gods/thor), never diplomatic, tells the giant to his face to brew for the gods — and Ægir, nettled at the imperious tone, sets a condition designed to be impossible: bring me a cauldron big enough to brew for all of you at once. It is [Tyr](/gods/tyr) who remembers that his own kinsman [Hymir](/giants/hymir), out beyond the rivers at heaven's edge, owns a kettle a mile deep, and the poem becomes the tale of Thor's most famous fishing trip and the theft of the great cauldron. Once it stands in Ægir's hall, the arrangement becomes permanent: Grímnismál mentions the gods drinking at Ægir's, and the feast recurs in the frame of Lokasenna. The sea-giant becomes the gods' host every winter, his self-filling horns going around a hall where bright gold does the work of firelight — a detail so beloved that "Ægir's fire" became a standard skaldic kenning for gold itself.

## What happened at Ægir's feast?

The most famous night in Ægir's hall is a catastrophe of etiquette. In Lokasenna ("Loki's flyting"), the gods gather at Ægir's for ale — Thor is away in the east — and the hall is declared a great place of peace. [Loki](/gods/loki), unable to bear hearing Ægir's servant Fimafeng praised, kills him, is driven out, and forces his way back in, invoking an old oath of blood-brotherhood with [Odin](/gods/odin) to claim a seat. Then, cup by cup, he insults every god and goddess present — cowardice, incest, infidelity, unmanliness — until Thor arrives and threatens him into the night with [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir). The prose that follows the poem says Loki fled and was afterward hunted down and bound. Ægir's hall, in other words, hosts the last supper of the old order: the peace of his benches is the final peace the gods share before the road to [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) begins in earnest. The poem's prose introduction adds one more thing: Ægir is "also called Gymir" — a puzzle, since Gymir is elsewhere the father of [Gerd](/giants/gerd), and most scholars think two different giants ended up sharing the name.

## Ægir, Hlér, and the sons of Fornjótr

Ægir's other name, Hlér, opens onto an older and stranger genealogy. The legendary prehistory preserved in Orkneyinga saga and the Icelandic þættir makes Hlér one of three sons of the ancient giant Fornjótr — the others being Logi, fire, and Kári, wind. Sea, fire, and wind as three brothers: a family portrait of everything that could kill a Norse traveler. The Danish island of Læsø ("Hlér's island") preserves his name in the real world, and Snorri says in Skáldskaparmál that Ægir dwelt there. Whether Ægir began as a god, a giant, or simply the sea itself given a face, the sources shrug: he is called jötunn, yet he moves among the Æsir as an equal. Skáldskaparmál even opens with him traveling to Asgard as an honored guest, trading lore with Bragi, the god of poetry — the frame within which Snorri unpacks the story of the [Mead of Poetry](/giants/suttungr) and much of the skaldic art.

## Ægir's family: Rán and the nine waves

With Rán, Ægir fathered nine daughters, and their names, preserved in Skáldskaparmál, are a poem in themselves: Himinglæva (heaven-shining), Dúfa (the pitching one), Blóðughadda (bloody-haired — red foam after a battle at sea), Hefring (the lifting one), Uðr (wave), Hrönn (surge), Bylgja (billow), Dröfn (foam-fleck), and Kólga (the cold one). They are the sea's moods with a shared surname. A separate tradition, in Völuspá hin skamma and the lost Heimdalargaldr, makes [Heimdall](/gods/heimdall) the son of nine sisters — and though those poems give the mothers different names, readers ancient and modern have been tempted to identify them with Ægir's daughters, the watcher of the gods born of nine waves. The identification is attractive but not certain, and honest tellings say so.

## In the primary sources

Ægir appears across both Eddas. In the **Poetic Edda**, Hymiskviða turns on his demand for a brewing cauldron; Lokasenna takes place at his feast, and its prose calls him both Ægir and Gymir; Grímnismál mentions the gods drinking at his benches. In the **Prose Edda**, Skáldskaparmál frames its opening dialogue as Ægir's visit to the gods, lists his daughters' names, and records the kennings — the sea as "Ægir's jaws," gold as "Ægir's fire," and Ægir himself as a term for the sea in poetry. The Fornjótr genealogy appears in Orkneyinga saga and the þáttr Hversu Noregr byggðist, where he is Hlér. He is a presence in skaldic verse wherever the sea is: to sail was to travel "on Ægir's back."

## FAQ

**Is Ægir a god or a giant?**
The sources call him a jötunn, but he functions as a friendly divine power: he hosts the gods' feasts, visits Asgard as an honored guest, and is never their enemy. He is best described as the sea personified, standing outside the usual war between Æsir and giants.

**What is Ægir the god of?**
He personifies the open sea, especially its generous face — brewing, feasting, and hospitality. His wife Rán embodies the sea's deadly side, and their nine daughters are the waves themselves.

**Why did the gods feast in Ægir's hall?**
After Thor and Tyr fetched the mile-deep cauldron from the giant Hymir, Ægir brewed ale for all the gods, and his undersea hall — lit by glowing gold, with horns that filled themselves — became their feasting place. The poem Lokasenna is set at one such feast.

**Are Ægir's daughters Heimdall's mothers?**
Possibly. Heimdall is said to be born of nine sisters, and Ægir and Rán have nine daughters, the waves — but the poems that name Heimdall's mothers give them different names. The identification is an old and appealing guess, not a settled fact.

**What does "Ægir's fire" mean?**
It is a skaldic kenning for gold, born of the image of Ægir's hall lit by shining gold instead of flame. Kennings like this let poets call gold "the fire of the sea" in endless variations.
