Gunnlöð ('war-summons') sat on a golden stool inside Hnitbjörg with one job: guard her father's three vats of the Mead of Poetry. Odin bored in as a snake, and for three nights she was his — and for each night she granted him one sip. Three sips, three empty vats: the poets' technicality that swallowed an ocean.

Then he was gone through the rock as an eagle, leaving her to face her father. Remarkably, Hávamál — Odin's own wisdom poem — takes her side: 'Gunnlöð gave me from her golden seat a drink of the precious mead; ill reward she got for her whole heart, her heavy spirit.' The god keeps the mead but files the guilt; poetry remembers the woman it was stolen from.

Among the giantesses of Norse myth, Gunnlöð occupies a place no one else does: she is the one the tradition apologizes to. Giants are killed by the dozen in these stories without a backward glance; Gunnlöð is merely deceived, and the deception echoes through the mythology's greatest wisdom poem like a bad conscience. She is also, arguably, the person from whom all poetry comes — not its maker, but its door.

## The guardian in the mountain

Her situation, as Skáldskaparmál frames it, is starkly simple. Her father [Suttung](/giants/suttungr) had taken the Mead of Poetry — brewed from the blood of the all-wise Kvasir — as blood-price for his murdered parents, and hidden it in three vessels, Óðrerir, Boðn, and Són, deep in the mountain Hnitbjörg. There he "set his daughter Gunnlöð to guard it." That is her entire life as the prose source gives it: a young woman alone inside a rock, watching vats. She is treasure and treasurer at once — and like all guarded treasures in myth, she exists to be reached.

[Odin](/gods/odin) reached her the hard way. After a winter laboring under the alias Bölverkr for her uncle Baugi, and after Suttung refused him even one drink, the god had Baugi bore a hole through the mountainside with the auger Rati and slipped through it in serpent's form. What happened next differs subtly — and importantly — between our two sources. Snorri's version is brisk and transactional: Odin went to where Gunnlöð was, lay with her three nights, and she then gave him leave to drink three draughts of the mead; he emptied all three vessels in three swallows and flew away as an eagle. In Snorri, Gunnlöð makes a bargain and is cheated on the measure. She is a gatekeeper outwitted.

## What does Hávamál say about Gunnlöð?

Hávamál — the "sayings of the High One," a poem in Odin's own voice — tells it warmer and darker. There, Gunnlöð is no toll-keeper but a lover: she "gave me from her golden seat a drink of the precious mead," and the giving is remembered as generosity, not fee. The poem's Odin admits everything: that Rati gnawed him a passage through the rock, that he risked his head among the giants' halls, that he "used" the good woman's help to escape, and that on a sacred ring he swore an oath he did not keep. Then it turns and looks at the wreckage: "Ill reward she got for her whole heart, her heavy spirit" — and, in the next breath, the frost-giants coming to the High One's own hall to ask whether Bölverkr was among the gods or whether Suttung had slaughtered him. The stolen mead makes every poem possible, including the poem in which the thief confesses. Earlier in Hávamál, in its litany of drunkenness and folly, Odin even cites the episode against himself: drunk he was, dead drunk, "at wise Fjalar's" — in the house of Gunnlöð.

The tension between the two accounts is real and worth respecting. Snorri, writing a handbook for poets, needed the heist; Hávamál, meditating on wisdom, trust, and oaths, needed the betrayal. Read together, they leave Gunnlöð as the mythology's sharpest case study in its own sexual politics: an unmarried giantess alone is, to a traveling god, an opportunity — and the tradition knows it was shabby, and says so, and keeps the mead anyway.

## Was Gunnlöð more than a victim?

Some scholars think the surviving fragments hide an older, larger role. Hávamál's furniture is suggestive: Gunnlöð sits on a *golden seat* inside the mountain and gives a marvelous drink to a stranger who becomes, by drinking it, supreme — an image with the shape of a sovereignty rite, the sacred drink-bearer whose cup confers kingship, familiar from Celtic and other traditions. On this reading, later tellers recast an ancient mead-goddess as a duped daughter. The Icelandic novelist Svava Jakobsdóttir developed exactly this argument (and a novel, *Gunnlöth's Tale*, from it), and the idea has real scholarly currency without being provable. Others note a small genealogical bombshell in Snorri's Skáldskaparmál: the god of poetry, Bragi, is listed as a son of Odin — and one old tradition, taken up by many modern readers, makes Gunnlöð his mother, conceived in those three nights. The medieval evidence for Bragi's mother is thin, so honesty requires the hedge: it is an inference the sources permit rather than state. But it would be very like this mythology for poetry itself to be the child of the theft.

## In the primary sources

Gunnlöð appears in exactly two texts, and they disagree in tone. **Hávamál** in the Poetic Edda (stanzas 13–14 and 104–110) gives Odin's first-person confession: the golden seat, the drink, the broken ring-oath, the "ill reward" she got for her whole heart. **Skáldskaparmál** in the Prose Edda gives the connected narrative: her post in Hnitbjörg, the three nights, the three drinks that emptied Óðrerir, Boðn, and Són, and the eagle-flight to [Asgard](/realms/asgard). Kennings preserve her too — poetry can be called "Gunnlöð's embrace"-adjacent formulations and the mead "the drink of Hnitbjörg" — so that skalds invoked her, knowingly or not, whenever they priced their art. What happened to her after the eagle burst out of the mountain, no source says. She faces her father off the page, and the tradition, having apologized, looks away.

## FAQ

**Who was Gunnlöd in Norse mythology?**
The daughter of the giant Suttung, set to guard the Mead of Poetry inside the mountain Hnitbjörg. Odin reached her in serpent form, spent three nights with her, drank all the mead, and escaped as an eagle.

**Did Gunnlöd willingly give Odin the mead?**
The sources split. Snorri says she allowed him three draughts after their three nights — a bargain he exploited by draining everything. Hávamál presents her as giving from the heart and being betrayed, with Odin admitting he broke a sworn oath to her.

**What does Gunnlöd's name mean?**
Old Norse Gunnlöð is usually interpreted as "war-summons" or "battle-invitation" — a conventional giantess- and valkyrie-flavored name whose martial ring contrasts poignantly with her story, which contains no fighting at all.

**Was Gunnlöd the mother of Bragi?**
Possibly. Bragi, god of poetry, is called Odin's son, and a widespread tradition — ancient in inspiration, thin in direct attestation — makes Gunnlöð his mother from the three nights in Hnitbjörg. It fits the myth's logic, but no surviving source states it plainly.

**Why does Odin regret betraying Gunnlöd?**
Hávamál is a poem about wisdom, and its Odin uses his own worst bargains as teaching cases: he won the mead but proved that his oath is worthless — "a ring-oath Odin swore; who shall trust his troth?" Gunnlöð's heavy heart is the price tag the poem leaves attached to poetry.
