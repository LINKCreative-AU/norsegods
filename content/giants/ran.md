Where her husband Ægir is the sea as host, Rán ('Robbery') is the sea as taker. She sweeps a great net through the water and gathers the drowned to her hall; sailors who went down were said to have 'fared to Rán.' Norse seafarers carried gold on voyages so as not to arrive in her hall empty-handed — the drowned who came with gold were well received.

Her net has a history: Loki borrowed it to catch the dwarf Andvari in his fish-shape, the theft that started the cursed gold of the Völsungs on its way. With Ægir she mothered the nine wave-maidens. In Friðþjófs saga, storm-tossed sailors divide their gold so that each man can pay his way in Rán's hall — one of the clearest glimpses of a real Norse belief about death at sea.

Rán is one of the few figures in the mythology who governed something every Norse family actually feared. Battles were for warriors; the sea was for everyone — traders, fishermen, settlers crossing to Iceland with their households aboard. The mythology gave that universal danger a face, a tool, and a household, and then, characteristically, gave the drowned somewhere to go. Not everyone who died at sea was lost to the cold: they had "fared to Rán," and if they came with gold in their belts, they would sit at benches in a hall lit by the sea's own treasure.

## The goddess with the net

The net is Rán's defining possession, and it makes her a fisher of men in the most literal sense. Snorri says in Skáldskaparmál that Rán "has a net in which she catches all men who come into the sea." The image is precise to the experience it explains: drowning in the North Atlantic is sudden, entangling, and inescapable, less like a battle than like being hauled. Her name compounds the menace — *rán* is an ordinary Old Norse legal word meaning robbery, plunder, the open seizure of goods. The sea does not borrow; it robs. And yet the same tradition insists she keeps what she takes: her hall houses the sea-dead as Valhalla houses the battle-dead, a parallel underlined in Friðþjófs saga, where the hero, expecting shipwreck, chops a gold arm-ring apart and shares it out "so no man of ours goes empty-handed to Rán."

## Rán, Loki, and the cursed gold

Rán's net casts a long shadow into heroic legend. In the prose of Reginsmál — retold in Völsunga saga and in Snorri — [Odin](/gods/odin), [Loki](/gods/loki), and Hœnir must pay a ransom of gold for the killing of Ótr, the shape-shifting son of Hreiðmarr. Loki needs to catch the dwarf Andvari, who swims the waters of a falls in pike's shape, so he goes to Rán and borrows her net. With it he drags Andvari from the pool and strips him of his hoard, down to the last ring — Andvaranaut, on which the dwarf lays his famous curse. Everything that follows — the dragon Fáfnir brooding on the gold, Sigurd's rise and murder, the fall of the Völsungs and the burning of heroes — begins with a cast of the drowning-goddess's net. It is a quiet, perfect irony: the tool that gathers the sea's dead is used to launch the most famous chain of deaths on land in Northern legend.

## Rán in the poets' grief

The most human moment in Rán's whole dossier belongs to Egils saga. When Egill Skallagrímsson's beloved son Böðvarr drowns, the old poet shuts himself in his bed-closet to die, and is coaxed back to life only by composing Sonatorrek, "The Wrecking of Sons" — one of the greatest poems of the North. In it he turns on the sea's rulers directly: Rán has robbed him, he says, stripped him bare of his line, and if he could pursue his case with a sword he would fight the ale-brewer's wife herself. That a tenth-century poet reached for Rán by name in the rawest grief of his life shows she was no literary ornament; she was where the drowned went. The Helgi poems of the Poetic Edda use her the same way — ships that survive a storm are said to have slipped "out of Rán's hand," and a giantess destroying seafarers is said to want to offer men to her.

## Was Rán worshipped?

There is no evidence of temples or formal cult — but the gold custom recorded in Friðþjófs saga looks like genuine popular belief, and the idiom "faring to Rán" is embedded across poetry and saga. Scholars generally read her as a mythic personification who received real ritual gestures: gold carried against drowning is functionally an offering. She and [Ægir](/giants/aegir) also make a theological pair unusual in the sources — a married couple sharing one domain split by temperament, the feast above the waves and the net beneath them, with their nine daughters, the [waves](/giants/aegir), embodying everything in between.

## In the primary sources

Rán is attested across poetry and saga rather than in any single myth of her own. In the **Poetic Edda**, Helgakviða Hundingsbana I speaks of ships escaping from Rán's grasp, and Helgakviða Hjörvarðssonar has a giantess who would drive men into her power. In the **Prose Edda**, Skáldskaparmál describes her net, her marriage to Ægir, their nine wave-daughters, and the kennings built on her name — gold as "Rán's fire," the sea as her domain; the Andvari episode with the borrowed net appears in Snorri's telling of the Otter's ransom and in the prose of Reginsmál. **Völsunga saga** carries the net-borrowing into the heroic cycle. **Friðþjófs saga** preserves the gold-for-the-drowned custom. **Egils saga** contains Sonatorrek's accusation against her — the closest thing we have to a Norse man's own words about the goddess who took his son.

## FAQ

**What does Rán's name mean?**
It is the ordinary Old Norse word for robbery or plunder — the open, forcible seizure of goods. The name frames drowning as theft: the sea takes what belongs to the land.

**What happens to people who drown, in Norse belief?**
They go to Rán's hall beneath the sea rather than to Valhalla or Hel. Sources like Friðþjófs saga show sailors carrying gold so as to arrive as guests rather than beggars — the drowned who brought gold were said to be well received.

**Is Rán a goddess or a giantess?**
Both descriptions fit. She is of jötunn kind and married to the sea-jötunn Ægir, but she functions as a goddess with a genuine domain — the sea's dead — and the poets treat her with a god's seriousness.

**What is the story of Rán's net?**
Besides gathering the drowned, it was borrowed once by Loki, who used it to catch the dwarf Andvari in fish-form and seize his gold — the cursed hoard that sets the tragedy of Sigurd and the Völsungs in motion.

**Who are Rán's children?**
With Ægir she has nine daughters, the wave-maidens, whose names describe the sea's moods — from Himinglæva, the light-catching wave, to Kólga, the cold one. Some traditions connect nine sisters like these with the birth of the god Heimdall.
