At the end of the sky sits a giant dressed as an eagle, and his name is 'Corpse-Swallower.' When Hræsvelgr beats his wings, wind crosses the world; that is where weather comes from, Vafþrúðnir the all-wise tells Odin, as an examination answer.

It is one of the mythology's most economical images: meteorology as one enormous, sinister bird. His grisly name is usually read against the old belief that the eagle feeds on the dead — the wind that fills your sail is the wingbeat of something that eats battlefields. Sailors of a drowning-prone civilisation knew exactly what the wind wanted.

Hræsvelgr's entire ancient record is two passages — a single stanza of eddic poetry and Snorri's prose gloss on it — and an entry in the poets' name-lists. That is thin even by the standards of the mythology's outer figures, and this article will not pretend otherwise. But the two passages are choice ones, sitting inside the Norse cosmos's official examination on how the world works, and the image they preserve has proven one of the most durable the North ever coined.

## Where the wind comes from

The stanza belongs to [Vafþrúðnismál](/giants/vafthrudnir), the great wisdom-contest in which a disguised [Odin](/gods/odin) and the all-wise giant Vafþrúðnir quiz each other with their heads as stakes. Odin asks — question twelve in his sequence — where the wind comes from, that crosses the waves yet is never itself seen. The giant answers: "Hræsvelgr he is called, who sits at heaven's end, a jötunn in eagle's shape; from his wings, they say, the wind comes over all men." The question is the kind a sailing culture would genuinely ask — wind is the most consequential invisible thing in a seafarer's life — and the mythology's answer has the compact confidence of all its natural science: day and night are horses, the tides are Thor's drinking, and the wind is feathers.

Snorri Sturluson, systematizing in Gylfaginning, sets the same lore into his cosmic geography and sharpens the mechanism. At the *northern* end of heaven, he writes, sits the giant called Hræsvelgr in eagle's guise, "and when he stretches his wings for flight, winds arise from under them." Gylfaginning's frame even stages the lesson as a question — "whence comes the wind? It is strong, it moves great seas, it whips up fire; but strong as it is, it cannot be seen: therefore it is wondrously made" — before quoting the Vafþrúðnismál stanza as proof-text. The northern placement matters: for Scandinavians the north wind was the killing wind, cold off the ice, and the mythology's other horrors — Niflheim's mists, the corpse-shore Náströnd — cluster in the same quarter. The wind-eagle sits where the worst weather lives.

## Why "Corpse-Swallower"?

The name is the dark heart of the figure, and the sources never explain it — they did not need to. In Norse poetry the eagle is, with the raven and the wolf, a "beast of battle": the creature that arrives after the fighting to feed. Skaldic convention praised a warrior by saying he "fed the eagle" or "reddened the eagle's claws"; slaughter was the eagle's banquet. Name an eagle "Hræsvelgr" — *hræ*, corpse or carrion, plus *svelgr*, swallower, gulper (the same word used of whirlpools that swallow ships) — and every listener understood: this is the eagle of the slain, scaled up to the size of the sky. The resulting theology of weather is quietly terrifying if you sit with it. The wind that dries your hay, fills your sail, or drives your ship under is the stirring of an appetite. Some scholars have connected the image to a broader northern folk-belief in a wind-eagle at the world's edge, and to the corpse-cold wind of the dead; others note the practical poetry of it — wind and carrion birds both arrive at battlefields, both circle, both are announced by a sound of wings. The sources themselves offer no myth in which Hræsvelgr acts, speaks, or eats anyone in particular. He has no stories; he has a function and a name, and the name does the storytelling.

One adjacent figure deserves mention for honesty's sake. An unnamed eagle sits in the branches of the world-tree Yggdrasil, trading insults with the serpent Níðhöggr via the squirrel Ratatoskr, with a hawk perched between its eyes. Readers ancient and modern have wondered whether the tree-top eagle and the wind-eagle at heaven's end are one bird. No source identifies them; Snorri treats them separately, and the þulur list Hræsvelgr simply among giant-names. The safest statement is that Norse cosmology had room for more than one enormous eagle, and only this one is named.

## In the primary sources

**Vafþrúðnismál** (stanzas 36–37) in the Poetic Edda contains the original question-and-answer: the wind's origin in the wingbeats of the eagle-shaped jötunn at heaven's end. **Gylfaginning** in the Prose Edda repeats and localizes the lore — northern rim of the sky, winds from under the stretching wings — and quotes the eddic stanza as authority. The **þulur** (versified name-lists appended to the Prose Edda) include Hræsvelgr among jötnar. That is the complete corpus. He appears in no saga and no other myth; everything beyond these lines is inference or invention. Modern culture has adopted the name enthusiastically — most visibly as the wind-associated dragon Hraesvelgr in *Final Fantasy XIV* and in various games and metal lyrics — borrowings that keep the wind and the wings and discard the carrion.

## FAQ

**What does Hraesvelgr's name mean?**
"Corpse-Swallower" — from *hræ*, carrion or corpse, and *svelgr*, a swallower or gulper. The name draws on the eagle's ancient role as a battlefield scavenger: the bird that swallows the slain, enlarged to cosmic scale.

**What does Hraesvelgr do in Norse mythology?**
One thing: he makes the wind. Sitting at the northern end of the sky in eagle's shape, he raises all the world's winds with the beating and stretching of his wings — the Norse explanation for where weather comes from.

**Is Hraesvelgr the eagle in Yggdrasil?**
Probably not, though no text rules it out. The eagle atop the world-tree is never named, and Hræsvelgr is placed at heaven's rim, not in the tree. Sources treat them as separate figures, and cautious readers should too.

**Is Hraesvelgr a god or a giant?**
A giant — Vafþrúðnismál calls him "a jötunn in eagle's shape." He belongs to the class of jötnar who personify natural forces, like Ægir (the sea) and Logi (fire), rather than to the gods' society.

**Where does Hraesvelgr appear in the sources?**
In exactly two places: a stanza of Vafþrúðnismál in the Poetic Edda, and Snorri's prose restatement in Gylfaginning, plus name-lists. He has no myths of his own — a single unforgettable image rather than a story.
