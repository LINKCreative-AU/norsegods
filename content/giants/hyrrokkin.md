Baldr's ship Hringhorni, greatest of vessels, was to be his pyre — but grief-weakened gods could not budge it down the rollers. So they sent to Jötunheim for strength, and got theatre: Hyrrokkin ("fire-withered") arrived riding a giant wolf with venomous snakes for reins. Odin's four berserkers, assigned her mount, could not hold it until they wrestled it flat.

She walked to the prow and pushed the ship to sea in one heave — fire bursting from the rollers, the whole earth shaking. Thor, humiliated by proxy, gripped his hammer and would have broken her skull had the gods not begged peace on a funeral day. A giantess out-lifting the strength of the gods at their lowest moment: even the enemy came to bury Baldr.

## The funeral the gods couldn't launch

Hyrrokkin's one scene falls at the darkest point in the mythology's narrative arc, the funeral of [Baldr](/gods/baldr), told in full in Snorri's account of the [death of Baldr](/events/death-of-baldr). Baldr, best-loved of the gods, has been killed by a sprig of mistletoe thrown by blind [Höðr](/gods/hodr) at [Loki](/gods/loki)'s prompting, and the gods carry his body to the greatest of ships, [Hringhorni](/artifacts/hringhorni), moored on the shore, meaning to launch it, load it with his belongings, and set it alight as a funeral pyre. But grief has done what no enemy could: the assembled gods, [Odin](/gods/odin) and [Thor](/gods/thor) among them, cannot move the ship an inch down its rollers. Every god in Asgard failed at the one physical task the day required, and the story pauses on that failure before offering its fix — not divine strength, but a summons across the border into enemy territory.

## Hyrrokkin's arrival

The messenger sent to [Jötunheim](/realms/jotunheim) comes back with Hyrrokkin, and Snorri stages her entrance as pure spectacle. She rides a wolf so large and vicious that it takes four of Odin's chosen berserkers, thrown down and pinned, to hold it still once she dismounts; its reins, in place of leather, are living, writhing serpents. She walks to the ship's prow, sets her shoulder or hand to it, and shoves — and Hringhorni, which no god could stir, leaps down the rollers so hard that fire springs from the wood by friction and the whole earth trembles with the force of the launch. It is one of the few moments in the mythology where raw physical power is shown to exceed anything the Æsir themselves can manage, performed not by a god but by a giantess called in as a last resort.

Thor's reaction measures the insult precisely: he seizes [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir), ready to cave in her skull on the spot, and only the other gods' plea — that this is a day for burying the dead, not starting a fight — talks him down. It is a rare glimpse of a Thor forced to swallow his pride at the very moment his strength has just been publicly outdone.

## Baldr's pyre, completed

With the ship afloat, the funeral proceeds in full: Baldr's body is carried aboard, his wife [Nanna](/gods/nanna) dies of grief and is laid beside him on the pyre, Odin lays the ring [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir) on the fire so that it will drip eight equal rings every ninth night thereafter, and Baldr's horse is led onto the ship fully caparisoned to burn with its master. Hyrrokkin, having done the one thing no one else could, disappears from the story exactly as abruptly as she entered it — no farewell, no further word, her single scene closed the moment its purpose is served.

## What does Hyrrokkin's name mean, and was she real to the Norse?

Hyrrokkin's name is generally parsed from Old Norse elements meaning "fire" and a word suggesting shrinking, shriveling, or smoking — commonly rendered "fire-withered" or "fire-smoked," though the exact image intended (scorched by flame? aged like smoked meat? something else) is debated, since the name explains nothing about her actions in the story. Beyond the text, there is a striking possible confirmation in stone: the Hunnestad Monument, a group of Viking Age picture stones from Scania in southern Sweden (now largely lost, known through eighteenth-century drawings), includes a carving of a giantess riding a wolf bridled with what appear to be serpents — an image many scholars have connected to Hyrrokkin specifically, since no other Norse myth pairs a giantess with exactly that mount. The identification cannot be proven, since the stone carries no inscription naming her, but the visual match to Snorri's description is close enough that it is widely treated as the best surviving depiction of any figure from Baldr's funeral.

## In the primary sources

Hyrrokkin's narrative appears in **Gylfaginning** in Snorri's [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), as part of the extended account of Baldr's death and funeral. Snorri's source for the funeral scene appears to include **Húsdrápa**, a skaldic poem by Úlfr Uggason composed around 980 CE describing carvings of mythological scenes on a hall's walls, which Snorri quotes elsewhere in the same episode for details like Heimdallr and Loki's fight over Brísingamen — making it likely, though not stated outright, that Hyrrokkin's launch of the ship was among the images that poem described. No Eddic poem mentions her by name.

## FAQ

**Who is Hyrrokkin in Norse mythology?**
A giantess summoned from Jötunheim to launch Baldr's funeral ship, Hringhorni, after the grieving gods proved unable to move it themselves. She arrived riding a wolf bridled with live snakes and pushed the ship into the sea with a single, earth-shaking heave.

**Why couldn't the gods launch the ship themselves?**
Snorri's account doesn't explain it beyond the fact of their failure — the natural reading is that grief over Baldr's death had sapped their strength, making the moment a striking contrast between divine sorrow and a giantess's undiminished power.

**Why was Thor angry at Hyrrokkin?**
Her single push accomplished what the assembled gods could not, and Thor reached for Mjölnir to kill her on the spot — an act of wounded pride the other gods had to talk him out of, given the solemnity of a funeral.

**What does the name Hyrrokkin mean?**
It is generally translated as "fire-withered" or "fire-smoked," though scholars are not fully certain what image the name was meant to evoke, since nothing in her one recorded scene involves fire directly — except the sparks her launch strikes from the ship's rollers.

**Is Hyrrokkin shown on any surviving artifact?**
Possibly. The Hunnestad Monument, a set of Viking Age picture stones from Sweden, includes a carving of a giantess riding a wolf with serpent reins — widely, though not certainly, identified as Hyrrokkin, since her myth is the only one that pairs a giantess with that exact mount.
