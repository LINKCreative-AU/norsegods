Starkaðr's fate was set at a night-time court where twelve gods judged one man: Odin, who loved him, granted three life-spans, victory in every battle, the best weapons, and the gift of poetry; Thor, who hated his giant-descended line, countered clause by clause — in each life a níðingsverk, an unforgivable crime; never a son; grievous wounds in every battle; and to remember his poems no longer than he takes to speak them. Grant and curse fused into the North's longest sentence: excellence, forever, with mandatory shame.

He served kings across three ages and betrayed the ledger on schedule: hanged his own lord Víkarr in a 'mock' sacrifice that Odin's spear made real — the god collecting his fee through his favourite's hands; fled a battle in his second life; took gold to murder a king he served in his third. Saxo, who gives him a whole biography, adds the ending: old beyond old, carrying his death-money on a chain, Starkaðr paid a young man to behead him, advising him to leap between head and body at the stroke — the corpse's fall, it turned out, would have killed the boy. Even his death had a body count budgeted. He is the heroic ethic examined to destruction: what if you got everything the poems promise, and it never let you die?

## Born under Thor's grudge

Starkad's trouble predates his birth. In Gautreks saga's account, his grandfather — also Starkaðr, a giant with eight arms — carried off a woman whom [Thor](/gods/thor) then rescued in the god's usual style, killing the abductor. The line was giant-tainted from that day, and Thor, the professional exterminator of giant-kind, never let the account close. Some traditions marked the grandson with the taint physically: Saxo reports the tale that Starkad was born with extra arms and that Thor tore the surplus off, pruning him down to a man's shape but not a man's luck. He grew up huge, grey early, and silent, fostered by a man called Hrosshárs-Grani — Horsehair-Grani — who took him from age nine and trained him for years on an island. The name is a transparent mask: Grani is [Odin](/gods/odin), and Starkad's whole upbringing was the Allfather's investment, made under his oldest alias, patience.

## The judgement of the gods

The scene that defines Starkad has no parallel in the North: a man's entire fate drafted, clause and counter-clause, in open court. One night on the island, Grani woke Starkad and rowed him to a grove where eleven figures sat in council; Grani took the twelfth seat and was hailed as Odin. The two gods then bid over the man like advocates settling a contract. Odin granted three life-spans; Thor countered: in each of them he shall commit a níðingsverk — a villainy, an unforgivable act. Odin gave the best weapons and clothing; Thor: he shall never own land. Odin: wealth in movable goods; Thor: he shall never feel he has enough. Odin: victory and prowess in every battle; Thor: in every battle, a grievous wound. Odin: the art of poetry, verse as fast as speech; Thor: he shall not remember what he composes. Odin: favour with the noblest men; Thor: the common people shall hate him. The court adjourned, the sentence fused, and Starkad rowed home carrying the strangest fate in the literature: guaranteed excellence with mandatory shame, three lives long, structurally incapable of contentment. Then Grani named his fee — send me King Víkarr.

## The sacrifice of Víkarr

Starkad was Víkarr's champion and dearest friend; that was the point. Becalmed on a raiding voyage, the fleet cast lots for a sacrifice to Odin to raise a wind, and the lot fell — as it would — on the king. The crew settled on a mock sacrifice to satisfy the omen: Starkad would loop a calf's gut over Víkarr's neck from a sapling branch, and touch him with a reed. Odin had given his man a reed the night before. Starkad set the noose, said "now I give you to Odin," and stabbed — and the reed was [Gungnir](/artifacts/gungnir)'s point, the gut a hard noose, the bent sapling a gallows tree hauling the king into the air. Víkarr died Odin's threefold death — hanged, stabbed, given — and Starkad had committed the first and worst of his crimes: the killing of his own lord, by his own hand, as the god's fee, out of his own fated helplessness. Gautreks saga preserves stanzas of Víkarsbálkr, the poem the saga puts in Starkad's own mouth, remembering it. Norway, Saxo adds, hated him for it ever after; so, on the evidence of the verse, did Starkad.

The other two crimes arrived on Thor's schedule. In his second life, Starkad fled a battlefield — cowardice, in the one man victory was promised. In his third, he took gold to murder the Danish king Hunthiof (in Saxo's account) whom he served — regicide again, this time for pay, and he flung the payment away in disgust afterward. The curse's design is exact: each crime strikes at the heroic code from a different side — loyalty, courage, honour — and each is committed by the age's supreme exemplar of all three.

## Saxo's old monster, and the asking-for-it death

Saxo Grammaticus, who spreads Starkad across books VI to VIII of the [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum) — more space than almost any other figure — loves him the way one loves a terrible grandfather. Saxo's Starkad is the scourge of softness: he arrives at feasts and delivers verse sermons against German cooks, luxury, mimes, and the moral decline of the Danes; he goads King Ingellus to a duty of vengeance the young king would rather have digested away. He fights across Russia, Byzantium, and the whole map of legend. And he grows old three times over, wounds accumulating, until — blind, propped on crutches, and carrying the gold that paid for his last crime in a pouch at his neck — he wanders the roads offering his own death-money to anyone of good enough birth to behead him. He finds Hather, a young man whose father Starkad had killed: perfect. He hands over the gold and the sword, instructs the boy to strike, and offers a final piece of professional advice — leap between the head and the falling body, and you will be proof against weapons. Hather strikes, and prudently declines to jump; the enormous corpse, crashing down, would have crushed him. Starkad's last act on earth was an attempted posthumous homicide, budgeted in advance. Saxo reports that the severed head bit the ground in rage.

## In the primary sources

Gautreks saga (13th century, Iceland) carries the giant ancestry, the fostering by Grani, the judgement of the gods, and the Víkarr sacrifice, with the embedded verses of Víkarsbálkr. Saxo's [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum), books VI–VIII, gives the sprawling Latin biography: the three lifetimes, the moral sermons, the crimes, and the death by appointment. Starkad also appears at the legendary battle of Brávellir (Saxo, book VIII, and the Norse Sögubrot), fighting his last great field, and is name-checked across the fornaldarsögur as the yardstick of the old heroic age. The versions disagree in detail — Saxo rationalises the gods' judgement and reshuffles the crimes — but the design is constant: Odin's gifts, Thor's riders, and a man serving out both.

## FAQ

**Who was Starkad in Norse legend?**
Starkaðr was the great cursed champion of the legendary sagas: descended from giants, fostered by Odin in disguise, granted three lifetimes of victory and poetry by Odin and cursed by Thor with a terrible crime in each life, grievous wounds, and no rest. He served kings across generations and died by paid beheading, at his own request.

**Why did Thor hate Starkad?**
Because of his ancestry: Starkad's giant grandfather had stolen a woman whom Thor rescued and avenged. Thor's enmity toward the giant-descended line played out as the counter-curses he attached to every gift Odin gave — the theological grudge match that structures Starkad's whole life.

**What was Starkad's worst crime?**
The sacrifice of his own king and friend, Víkarr. A staged mock-sacrifice to Odin turned real in Starkad's hands: the reed became a spear, the calf-gut a noose, and the king died Odin's threefold death. It was the fee Odin charged for his gifts, and the first of the three crimes Thor had decreed.

**How did Starkad die?**
Ancient, blind, and unable to die in battle because victory was guaranteed him, he carried his blood-money on a chain and offered it to a worthy man to behead him. Young Hather — whose father Starkad had killed — took the bargain. Starkad advised him to leap between head and falling body "to become weapon-proof"; the corpse's fall would have killed him. Hather wisely stood back.
