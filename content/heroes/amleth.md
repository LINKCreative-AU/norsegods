The plot is familiar because it conquered the world twice: Amleth's uncle Fengi kills his brother — Amleth's father — marries the widow, and rules. The boy's problem is arithmetic: an heir with a grievance is a corpse on schedule. His solution is the North's strangest heroic performance — professional idiocy. He sits in the ashes carving wooden hooks ('barbs for my father's avenger,' he answers, truthfully, when asked; madmen may say anything), crows like a rooster, and rides to meetings facing his horse's tail, while the court runs tests: a girl planted to seduce the act away, a spy under the queen's bedstraw — whom Amleth kills, dismembers, and feeds to the pigs, still in character.

Shipped to England with two escorts and a runic death-warrant, he edits the letter in their sleep: execute the bearers, marry the prince to your daughter. He returns to Denmark mid-funeral — his own — gets the court ceremonially drunk, nets the hall down over them with the hooks he had been carving all along in plain sight, and burns it; Fengi he kills with the man's own sword, having politely swapped it for his sword-nailed-in-sheath. Saxo gives him a triumphant speech and a king's career after; the story travelled through a French collection to a certain playwright, who kept the uncle, the mother, the feigned madness, the spy behind the arras, and the edited letter, and changed mainly the ending's efficiency.

Amlóði surfaces first in a skaldic kenning — the sea as 'Amlóði's mill' grinding sand — suggesting the mad-prince tale is older than any surviving text. The North's contribution to world literature's most famous character: the insight that in a court where telling the truth is fatal, a man who tells it as a joke, from the ashes, in plain sight, is invisible.

## The legend as Saxo tells it

In books III and IV of the [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum), Saxo Grammaticus sets the story in Jutland. Two brothers, Horwendil and Fengi, share the governorship; Horwendil wins glory in single combat, marries Gerutha, daughter of the Danish king, and fathers Amleth. Fengi murders his brother openly, dresses the killing in slander, marries the widow — "adding incest to fratricide," growls Saxo, in a phrase Elizabethan readers would meet again — and rules.

Amleth's calculation is the tale's cold heart: a sane, aggrieved nephew is a corpse on schedule; a witless one is furniture. So he goes down into the filth deliberately — ashes, grime, crowing, nonsense — practising a very specific madness: he never lies. Asked what he is whittling, he answers truthfully: barbs to avenge his father. The court roars; a few sharper men note that the work is oddly careful for a lunatic. Truth worn as camouflage is the tale's great invention, and Saxo underlines it: "there was no lack of those who suspected cunning under the folly."

The tests come in ascending order of danger. First honey: a beautiful girl (his foster-sister; Ophelia's remote ancestor) is planted to seduce the act away; warned by a friend, Amleth carries her off to a hidden spot and secures her silence — she loved him of old, Saxo says, and lied for him. Then steel: a counsellor — the direct ancestor of Polonius — hides under the bedstraw to overhear Amleth with his mother. Amleth plays the rooster, jumps on the straw, feels the lump, and runs it through; he dismembers the body and feeds it to the pigs, then answers enquiries with perfect madman's truth — the man fell into the sewer and the swine ate him. In between, he has scourged his mother's conscience into alliance: the closet scene, complete, four centuries before the play.

## The letter, the funeral, and the fire

Fengi, unable to kill a popular nephew under his wife's eye, subcontracts: Amleth is shipped to England with two escorts carrying a runic letter asking the English king to execute the bearer's charge. Amleth searches their baggage while they sleep, shaves the runes, and recuts the message: put the escorts to death, and marry the prince to your daughter. The wedding duly follows — Saxo's Amleth so impresses the English king with uncanny deductions at table that the marriage seems only prudent. A year to the day, as he had instructed his mother, Amleth walks back into Fengi's hall in the middle of a funeral: his own. Drink takes the court; as the nobles lie sodden he drags down the great woven hanging his mother had made, nets it over the sleepers, pins it fast with the wooden hooks — whittled in plain sight over all the years for exactly this — and fires the hall. Fengi gets a formal reckoning: Amleth swaps the king's sword for his own, which had been nailed into its scabbard, wakes him, announces the debt, and kills him with his own blade as the usurper claws at a sword that will not draw. Saxo then gives the survivor a triumphant oration — not an apology but a prosecution — and the Jutes hail him king.

Saxo's Amleth, unlike his famous descendant, gets a career: a reign, two wives, and death at last in battle against Wiglek, king of the Danes — because, Saxo shrugs, no fortune outwits fate.

## Amleth and Hamlet: what Shakespeare kept

The transmission is unusually traceable: Saxo's Latin, printed in 1514, was retold by François de Belleforest in his Histoires tragiques (1570), and from Belleforest — probably via a lost English play scholars call the Ur-Hamlet — it reached Shakespeare. What he kept is remarkable: the fratricide uncle, the remarried mother, the feigned madness, the planted girl, the spy behind the hangings killed through them, the edited letter turning the death-warrant on its bearers, even the melancholy wit that tells lethal truths as jokes. What he changed is mostly the machinery of doubt: Saxo's murder is public, so Amleth needs no ghost and no proof — his problem is tactical, not metaphysical. And the ending's efficiency: Amleth wins, cleanly, and rules; Hamlet's revenge takes the whole court and himself with it.

## Was Amleth a real person?

No — and here the historians are unusually confident. Saxo's first nine books are legend organised as history, and Amleth's tale is built from folktale materials — the wise fool, the truth-telling trickster, the letter of death — with analogues across many cultures, including Rome's Lucius Junius Brutus, who feigned idiocy to survive a tyrant uncle. But the figure is genuinely old in the North: the tenth-century skald Snæbjörn, quoted by Snorri, uses the kenning "Amlóði's mill" — the sea as a giant quern grinding the sand — which only works if audiences already knew a famous Amlóði. In later Icelandic, amlóði became a common noun for a fool: the mad prince had passed into the dictionary. Old enough for kennings by the 900s, written down around 1200, staged in London around 1600 — three timestamps in the life of one durable Northern idea.

## In the primary sources

Saxo Grammaticus, [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum), books III–IV (c. 1200), is the only full early text. Snæbjörn's skaldic kenning, preserved in Snorri's Skáldskaparmál in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), attests the name Amlóði by the tenth century. A later Icelandic Ambáles saga retells the story in romance style. Belleforest's Histoires tragiques (1570) carried it to the Elizabethans. Robert Eggers's film The Northman (2022), co-written with the Icelandic poet Sjón, returned deliberately to Saxo's Amleth rather than Shakespeare's Hamlet — one short paragraph is all the note it needs: the film's revenge-in-a-burning-hall instincts are the saga's own, even where its plot is new.

## FAQ

**Is Amleth the original Hamlet?**
Yes. Shakespeare's play descends, via Belleforest's French retelling and probably a lost English Ur-Hamlet, from Saxo Grammaticus's Latin account of Amleth. The uncle's fratricide, the remarriage, the feigned madness, the spy killed behind the hangings, and the altered letter are all in Saxo, written around 1200.

**Was Amleth a real Danish prince?**
No. Historians class him as legend: the tale is woven from widespread folktale motifs, and no historical record supports him. But the legend is old — a tenth-century skaldic kenning, "Amlóði's mill," proves the name was already famous in the Viking Age.

**Why did Amleth pretend to be mad?**
Survival arithmetic: a sane heir with a grievance would have been killed at once. By playing the harmless fool — while truthfully announcing his revenge as jokes no one believed — he bought years to prepare, carving the very hooks he would use to pin down the court on the night of the fire.

**How is Saxo's story different from Shakespeare's play?**
In Saxo the murder is public, so there is no ghost and no uncertainty; Amleth's problem is tactics, not conscience. And Amleth wins — he burns the hall, kills the usurper with the man's own sword, justifies himself in a speech, and is made king, dying only years later in battle.
