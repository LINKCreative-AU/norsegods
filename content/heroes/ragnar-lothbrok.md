Ragnarr earned the least glamorous epithet in heroic literature — loðbrók, 'Hairy-Breeches' — by engineering: to win the princess Þóra, penned in by a venomous serpent her father had unwisely let grow, he boiled his trousers in pitch and rolled them in sand, and the snake's strike met aggregate. The sagas then marry him, magnificently, into the deeper legend: his second wife Áslaug is revealed as the daughter of Sigurðr and Brynhildr — the Völsung bloodline smuggled into the Viking Age, and their sons' strange gifts (Sigurðr Snake-in-the-Eye carried the dragon-mark in his iris) explained by pedigree.

Those sons — Ívarr the Boneless, Björn Ironside, Sigurðr, Ubba — outgrew him, which the saga makes his motive: raiding England undermanned rather than be out-famed, Ragnarr was captured by king Ælla of Northumbria and thrown into a snake-pit. His death-song became the genre's summit: identifying himself in riddles, itemising a life of battles, and closing with the most quoted line in Viking literature — 'the little pigs would grunt if they knew the old boar's fate.' They grunted: the Great Heathen Army of 865, history's actual invasion of England, sails in the sagas as his sons' revenge, and Ælla dies under the blood-eagle.

A historical Ragnarr flickers behind the legend — a Reginheri raided Paris in 845 — but the saga figure is a composite the North built as its ideal Viking: cunning over strength, dynasty over survival, and a death scene rehearsed until it doubled as a will. Modern television has made him the most famous Norseman alive, which the old boar would have regarded as no more than his due.

## The legend, in full

Ragnars saga loðbrókar opens with a monster problem of domestic origin. The Gautish jarl Herruðr gave his daughter Þóra a small serpent, which she raised on gold — and, in the best tradition of the dragon [Fáfnir](/creatures/fafnir), the gold grew under it and the serpent grew with the gold, until it ringed her bower and no suitor could approach. Ragnar, a young Danish king's son, prepared for the fight like an engineer rather than a hero: he boiled shaggy trousers and a cloak in pitch, rolled them in sand, and let them freeze. The serpent's venom and fangs met a man armoured in aggregate; his spear did the rest. He won Þóra, the mocking nickname loðbrók, and — when Þóra died young — a grief the saga treats as real.

The second marriage is where the saga plays its master-stroke. Raiding along Norway, Ragnar's crew found, at a poor farmstead, a girl called Kráka ("Crow") of impossible beauty. Ragnar tested her with a riddle — come to me neither clothed nor unclothed, neither fed nor fasting, neither alone nor in company — and she arrived wrapped in a fishnet and her own hair, biting an onion, with a dog at her heel. He married her. Only years later, under pressure to put her aside for a Swedish princess, did she reveal her name and lineage: Áslaug, daughter of [Sigurd](/heroes/sigurd) the dragon-slayer and the valkyrie [Brynhild](/heroes/brynhild), hidden as a baby after her parents' deaths. As proof she foretold that her next son would be born with a serpent circling the pupil of his eye — and Sigurðr ormr-í-auga, Snake-in-the-Eye, arrived on schedule, carrying the Völsung dragon-mark. With one genealogical flourish the saga welds the Viking Age to the [Völsunga saga](/texts/volsunga-saga): Ragnar's dynasty becomes the continuation of the greatest bloodline in Northern legend, with [Odin](/gods/odin)'s stock in its veins.

The sons justified the pedigree. Ívarr the Boneless — carried on shields, unable to walk, and the coldest strategic mind in the saga; Björn Ironside; Hvítserkr; Sigurðr Snake-in-the-Eye; and by other accounts Ubba: they raided wider than their father, and the saga is unusually honest about what that did to him. Ragnar, ageing and out-famed by his own children, resolved to conquer England with just two ships — glory being, in his arithmetic, inversely proportional to the force employed. Wrecked on the Northumbrian coast, he was captured by King Ælla and thrown into a pit of serpents. The pitch-boiled trousers of his youth reportedly resisted the snakes until they were stripped from him; then, dying, he sang. Krákumál, the twelfth-century poem put in his mouth, is twenty-nine stanzas of magnificent memoir — battle after battle, each opening "we hewed with the sword" — closing with the taunt that made his death a detonator: "the little pigs would grunt if they knew the old boar's fate." And in the saga's telling he added the promise directly: word of this will reach my sons.

It did. Ívarr, in the saga, dissembled, took wergild from Ælla in the form of as much land as an ox-hide could cover — cut into thread, it enclosed the site of York — and, when the moment came, the brothers took Northumbria and carved the blood-eagle on Ælla's back. The historical Great Heathen Army that landed in East Anglia in 865 and destroyed three English kingdoms thus enters legend as a family reprisal: history's invasion, refiled as saga vengeance.

## Was Ragnar Lothbrok a real person?

Almost certainly not as the sagas present him — and the honest answer is more interesting than a yes. No contemporary source records a Viking named Ragnarr loðbrók. What the ninth century actually gives us is fragments that later tradition seems to have fused:

A Viking leader called **Reginheri** raided up the Seine and sacked Paris in 845, extorting a famous ransom from Charles the Bald; Frankish sources say his force was struck by plague and that he died soon after — one annal claims he boasted before Danish king Horik and promptly perished. Many historians see this Reginheri as the likeliest seed of the legend. Separately, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle and later English sources attest the *sons* far better than the father: Ívarr (plausibly the Ímar active in Ireland and Northumbria), Halfdan, and Ubba are historical commanders of the Great Heathen Army, and Björn Ironside appears in Frankish sources raiding the Mediterranean in the 850s. The ninth-century chronicle habit of calling notable Vikings "sons of Lothbrok" (the eleventh-century chronicler Adam of Bremen knows "Inguar, son of Lodparchus") suggests the name was famous early — but note the oddity that *Lothbrok* there looks like a standalone name, and a few scholars have even argued the original Lothbrok was a separate figure, possibly a woman, later merged with Ragnar. Ælla of Northumbria is fully historical: he died fighting the Great Army at York in March 867, though no contemporary source mentions a snake-pit, a blood-eagle, or a captured Danish king as his motive.

The scholarly consensus, then: Ragnar Lothbrok is a legendary composite — a Seine raider, a dynastic name, and the very real fathers of the 865 invasion, welded together by three centuries of storytelling into the ideal Viking. The sagas that fix his biography were written in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, four hundred years after the events, and openly serve a genealogical purpose: the kings of Denmark and the great families of Iceland liked descending from him.

## The death-song in the snake-pit

Whatever its history, the death scene is the legend's beating heart, and it is worth seeing what the tradition is doing with it. Ragnar in the pit is composing his own obituary in real time — Krákumál has him look forward past the snakes to the benches of Valhalla, drinking ale from curved horns in Odin's hall, laughing as he dies. (The often-quoted "drinking from the skulls of enemies" is a mistranslation of exactly this line — the horns are the curved trees of skulls, i.e. drinking-horns, not skulls.) The scene deliberately mirrors and inverts the older legend: [Gunnarr](/heroes/gunnar) of the Völsung cycle also died in a snake-pit, harping defiance; Ragnar, married into Gunnarr's legend by way of Áslaug, dies the same death singing. The saga-makers knew precisely what echo they were building.

## In the primary sources

The core texts are Ragnars saga loðbrókar (13th century, transmitted alongside the Völsunga saga in the same manuscript — the genealogical splice made physical), the shorter Ragnarssona þáttr ("Tale of Ragnar's Sons"), and the poem Krákumál. Saxo Grammaticus gives a long, divergent Latin account in book IX of the [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum), where Ragnar is a Danish king with a sprawling career, [Lagertha](/heroes/lagertha) appears as his first wife, and the snake-pit ending is retained. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle documents the sons' invasion of 865–878 without ever naming Ragnar himself. Icelandic tradition also credits Áslaug's back-story to the lost heroic poetry of the Völsungs. The versions contradict each other freely — Saxo's Ragnar and the saga's Ragnar cannot be reconciled into one career, and neither matches the Frankish Reginheri — which is itself the strongest evidence of how the legend grew: every region kept its own Ragnar.

## Ragnar in Vikings and modern culture

The History Channel's Vikings (2013–2020) made Ragnar, via Travis Fimmel's performance, the most famous Norseman in the modern world. The show is avowedly legend, not history: it compresses two centuries (Ragnar farms next door to events of the 790s and fathers the men of the 860s), invents freely, and blends Saxo's material (Lagertha) with the saga's (Aslaug, the sons, the snake-pit). As an adaptation of the *legend*, though, it is often shrewd — the pit, the death-song, and the sons' revenge all come straight from the sources.

## FAQ

**Was Ragnar Lothbrok real?**
Not as a single man. He is a legendary composite: the Seine raider Reginheri (sacked Paris, 845) is the likeliest historical seed, and his "sons" — Ivar, Halfdan, Ubba, Björn Ironside — are genuinely historical Viking commanders. The connected biography exists only in sagas written three to four centuries later.

**What does "Lothbrok" mean?**
Loðbrók means "hairy breeches" (or "shaggy trousers"). The saga explains it by the pitch-boiled, sand-rolled trousers Ragnar wore as armour when he killed the serpent guarding Þóra — protective gear immortalized as a nickname.

**How did Ragnar Lothbrok die?**
In legend, King Ælla of Northumbria threw him into a pit of snakes, where he died singing his death-song and warning that his sons would avenge him. History records no snake-pit; the real Reginheri seems to have died of disease after 845, and Ælla's death fighting the Great Heathen Army in 867 needed no personal motive.

**Who were Ragnar Lothbrok's sons?**
The tradition names Ívarr the Boneless, Björn Ironside, Hvítserkr, Sigurðr Snake-in-the-Eye, and Ubba, among others depending on the source. Ivar, Halfdan, and Ubba led the historical Great Heathen Army that conquered Northumbria, East Anglia, and much of Mercia from 865.

**Was Aslaug real?**
No. Áslaug is a purely legendary figure whose function is genealogical: as the daughter of Sigurd and Brynhild she grafts Ragnar's dynasty onto the Völsung line. Her riddle-wooing (neither clothed nor unclothed) is an international folktale motif, the "clever peasant girl."
