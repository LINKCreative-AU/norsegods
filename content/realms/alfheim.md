Álfheimr is the home of the ljósálfar, the light-elves, whom Snorri describes as 'fairer than the sun to look upon' — while the dark-elves below the earth are 'blacker than pitch.' Grímnismál records that the gods gave Álfheimr to [Freyr](/gods/freyr) as a tannfé, a tooth-gift, when he cut his first tooth: the fertility-lord thus rules the shining elves, and elves and Vanir run close together throughout the sources.

The elves themselves hover between gods and ancestors. They received their own sacrifice — the álfablót, held privately at farms in autumn, strangers turned away at the door — and heroes like Óláfr Geirstaðaálfr were venerated as elves in their burial mounds after death. Álfheimr was also a real place-name: the coastal district between the mouths of the Göta and Glomma rivers, whose people were held to be 'more beautiful than other folk.'

Beyond this the realm is nearly invisible: no myth is set there, no hall of it is named, no poem describes an elf at home. What survives is a scatter of details — a child-god's gift, a skald's slammed door, a king worshipped in his mound — from which a whole world must be read.

## Freyr's tooth-gift

In Grímnismál, [Odin](/gods/odin) — starving between two fires, cataloguing the dwellings of the gods — says the gods gave Álfheimr to Freyr 'in ancient days' as a tooth-gift. The tannfé was a real Norse custom: a present to an infant at the cutting of the first tooth. The image is disarming — a god small enough to be teething, handed a world — and it is also a riddle, because Freyr is no elf. He is of the Vanir, born in [Vanaheim](/realms/vanaheim), son of [Njörðr](/gods/njord), and nothing else shows him ruling there. But the pairing is not random: Freyr and the elves share a portfolio of sunlight, growth, and fruitful land, and scholars have long suspected álfar and Vanir were overlapping categories — perhaps two names for one kind of being.

## Are the elves gods?

The eddic poems come close to saying so. Again and again they pair 'Æsir and álfar' as the whole community of divine beings — Völuspá's seeress asks what is happening 'with the Æsir, with the elves.' In Völundarkviða, the smith-hero Völundr is three times called a prince of elves — the only individual elf the poems name. And elves receive cult like gods: besides the álfablót, Kormáks saga prescribes reddening an elf-mound with bull's blood to heal a wound.

Yet the elves also shade into the honoured dead. Óláfr Geirstaðaálfr, a legendary king of Vestfold remembered in Flateyjarbók, was worshipped in his mound as the 'elf of Geirstaðir' — as if a great man buried in his land could become an álfr and keep blessing it. The elves sit on a threshold: brighter than ancestors, homelier than gods.

## Light-elves and dark-elves

The sorting of elves into kinds is Snorri's work. In Gylfaginning he places Álfheimr in heaven with the light-elves, and sets against them the døkkálfar down in the earth, the two 'unlike in appearance, and far more unlike in nature.' Elsewhere he speaks of svartálfar, 'black-elves,' whose underground world is plainly the dwarves' realm of [Svartalfheim](/realms/svartalfheim). No eddic poem knows these distinctions; the poems have simply álfar. Many scholars therefore suspect the division is Snorri's own systematization — a Christian intellectual tidying native tradition into something like angels above and demons below — and that his dark-elves are the dwarves under another name.

## The álfablót

The realm's cult is attested by an annoyed Christian. Around 1019 the Icelandic skald Sigvatr Þórðarson travelled into Sweden on King Óláfr Haraldsson's business; his Austrfararvísur complain, farm by farm, of doors shut in his face. At one homestead a woman barred his way, saying the household was heathen, that she feared Odin's wrath, and that the álfablót — the sacrifice to the elves — was being held within. The grumbling verses are gold: they show elf-worship alive a generation after Iceland's conversion, conducted at home, in autumn, under the lady of the house, and closed absolutely to outsiders.

## Alfheim on the map

Uniquely among the mythic realms, Álfheimr can be found on a modern map. The legendary sagas know Álfheimar as a real district: the coastlands between the mouths of the Göta and the Glomma, on the present Swedish–Norwegian border, whose people were said to be 'more beautiful than other folk' — kin to the elves, the sagas imply. The overlap shows how thin the wall was between elf-lore and local pride.

## In the primary sources

Grímnismál 5 in [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) is the realm's one poetic attestation, recording the tooth-gift to Freyr. In [the Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), Gylfaginning places Álfheimr in heaven, houses the ljósálfar there, and draws the light-elf/dark-elf contrast, adding that the highest heavens are inhabited 'now' only by light-elves. Sigvatr's Austrfararvísur attests the álfablót; Völundarkviða and Völuspá attest the álfar and their pairing with the Æsir; Flateyjarbók preserves Óláfr Geirstaðaálfr; the legendary sagas supply the Álfheimar district. The realm, in short, is one stanza — everything else is elves.

## At Ragnarök

No source gives Álfheimr or the elves any part in [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok): they fight in no battle-line and are named among neither the destroyed nor the survivors. The only hint is Snorri's remark that the light-elves alone inhabit the highest heavens — where the hall Gimlé stands, which he says will endure when heaven and earth have passed away. Pressed, that quarters the light-elves in the safest real estate in the cosmos; but the honest answer is that when the wolf swallows the sun, the poems simply forget the elves.

## Legacy

Few realms have echoed louder. Snorri's ljósálfar — tall, luminous, fairer than the sun — stand directly behind J.R.R. Tolkien's High Elves, and through them behind virtually every radiant elf in modern fantasy; Tolkien's Light and Dark Elves rework Gylfaginning's very schema. In Iceland the álfar survive as the huldufólk, the hidden people, around whose reputed dwellings roads are still occasionally bent; English kept the creature in the word 'elf' and in names like Alfred, 'elf-counsel.'

## FAQ

**Who rules Alfheim?**
Freyr, the Vanir god of fertility. Grímnismál says the gods gave him the realm as a tannfé — a tooth-gift — when he cut his first tooth. The sources never show him governing it; the gift signals how closely elves and Vanir belonged together.

**What is the difference between light-elves and dark-elves?**
The distinction comes from Snorri's Prose Edda: light-elves live in Álfheimr and are fairer than the sun; dark-elves live in the earth and are blacker than pitch. The older poems know only álfar, undivided, and many scholars think Snorri invented the split, with his dark- and black-elves really being the dwarves.

**What was the álfablót?**
An autumn sacrifice to the elves held privately at individual farms, attested in Sigvatr Þórðarson's Austrfararvísur from around 1019. The Christian skald was turned away from farm after farm in Sweden; at one, a housewife refused him entry because the álfablót was underway. Because outsiders were barred, no description of the rite survives.

**Is Alfheim one of the nine realms?**
Modern lists almost always count it, but the medieval sources never enumerate the nine worlds they mention — the tidy roster is a modern reconstruction. Álfheimr's place among the nine rests on its name, 'elf-world.'
