Vanaheimr is the least-described of the nine worlds: home of the Vanir, the 'other' family of gods associated with fertility, prosperity, the sea, and the fate-magic called seiðr. Its location is given only as west of [Asgard](/realms/asgard), and no poem takes us inside; the Vanir we know — [Njörðr](/gods/njord), [Freyr](/gods/freyr), [Freyja](/gods/freyja) — we know because they left it.

They left as hostages. The first war in the world, between Æsir and Vanir, ended in stalemate and a peace sealed by exchange: Njörðr and his children went to Asgard, while Hœnir and wise Mímir went to Vanaheimr — a trade the Vanir came to regret, beheading Mímir when Hœnir proved useless without him. Vafþrúðnismál adds one quiet detail: at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok)'s end, Njörðr will go home 'to the wise Vanir' — implying Vanaheimr, unlike almost everything else, survives.

That is nearly the whole dossier. Where [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim) gets strongholds and rivers, Vanaheimr gets a name, a family, and a war. Yet the thinness is telling: the realm matters not as a stage but as an origin — proof that the Æsir were never the only divine family in the world.

## What happened in the Æsir–Vanir war?

Völuspá, the great prophecy-poem of [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), remembers how it began. A woman called Gullveig — 'Gold-drunkenness' — came among the Æsir, who speared and burned her three times in [Odin](/gods/odin)'s hall; three times she was reborn, and as the seeress Heiðr she went among people working seiðr. Most scholars read her as a Vanir figure, perhaps Freyja in disguise; the poem never says.

The poem does say that Odin ended the gods' debate — pay a penalty, or let all gods share the sacrifices — by hurling his spear over the enemy host. 'That was still the first war in the world,' the seeress says. The shield-wall of the Æsir's stronghold was broken, and the Vanir, war-wise, held the field. The gods of fertility could fight.

Snorri Sturluson, in euhemerized form in Ynglinga saga, gives the ending: both sides wearied of a war neither could win and made peace by exchange of hostages. The Vanir sent their best — Njörðr and his son Freyr — and the Æsir sent Hœnir, tall and outwardly fit for chieftainship, with wise Mímir as his counsellor. But whenever Mímir was away and a hard question came to the assembly, Hœnir answered only, 'Let others decide.' Concluding they had been cheated, the Vanir cut off Mímir's head and sent it to Odin — who preserved it with herbs and charms so that it spoke to him ever after.

In Skáldskaparmál, Snorri adds a gentler seal on the truce: both sides spat into a vat, and from the mingled spittle the gods made Kvasir, wisest of beings — though Ynglinga saga instead counts Kvasir among the Vanir hostages. Either way, Kvasir's blood later became the Mead of Poetry, so poetry itself descends from the treaty with Vanaheimr.

## Where is Vanaheim?

No source draws a map. The one directional clue is indirect: in Lokasenna, [Loki](/gods/loki) taunts Njörðr that he was 'sent east from here' as a hostage — which, read backwards, puts his homeland west of Asgard. Snorri's pseudo-historical Ynglinga saga sets 'Vanaland or Vanaheimr' by the river Tanais, the Don, with the Æsir east of it. That is learned medieval geography rather than myth, but it preserves the idea: two divine peoples, one border worth fighting over.

## What the sources don't say

It is worth being plain about the silence. No hall in Vanaheimr is ever named. No landscape is described — no counterpart to Jötunheimr's frozen river Ífingr or Asgard's plain of Iðavöllr. No myth is set inside it: even Mímir's beheading happens 'among the Vanir' without scenery. And the familiar roll-call of 'nine realms' that places Vanaheimr on a cosmic diagram is a modern reconstruction; the sources speak of nine worlds around [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil) but never once list them. Vanaheimr's claim rests on its name — heimr, 'world' — and on Vafþrúðnismál's testimony that Njörðr was 'created in Vanaheimr' by wise powers. The realm is real in the sources; its geography is ours.

## Seiðr and the gifts of the Vanir

What Vanaheimr exported changed Asgard. Ynglinga saga says Freyja was a priestess of the sacrifices and first taught the Æsir seiðr, the trance-magic of fate and prophecy, 'as was the custom among the Vanir.' With the hostages came the sea, the harvest, and love and gold — the provinces of Njörðr, Freyr, and Freyja. The mythology's whole economy of plenty is immigrant labour from a realm we never see.

## In the primary sources

Vafþrúðnismál 39 is the realm's single naming in the Poetic Edda: Njörðr was made in Vanaheimr, given to the gods as hostage, and will return to the wise Vanir at the doom of men. Völuspá 21–24 preserves the war — Gullveig's burning, Odin's spear-cast, the broken wall — without naming the realm; Lokasenna 34 remembers the hostage-taking as an insult. In the Prose Edda, Gylfaginning repeats that Njörðr was raised in Vanaheimr, and Skáldskaparmál gives the spittle-born Kvasir. Ynglinga saga, opening Heimskringla, supplies the fullest narrative — the exchange, Hœnir's failure, Mímir's head, Freyja's seiðr — with Vanaheimr set by the Don.

## At Ragnarök

Vanaheimr's most remarkable attestation is about the end. Vafþrúðnismál says that Njörðr will 'come home to the wise Vanir' at the doom of men. Almost everything else burns under [Surtr](/giants/surtr)'s fire or drowns; yet there must be Vanir, and somewhere for them to be wise in, for Njörðr to return to. The poem implies — it never states — that the western realm outlasts the catastrophe that destroys Asgard.

## Legacy

Because the sources say so little, Vanaheimr has become a screen for scholarly projection. An older generation read the Æsir–Vanir war as folk-memory of a real religious collision — a sky-cult overcoming a fertility-cult; recent scholarship is skeptical, some arguing the Æsir/Vanir distinction is largely a literary construction sharpened by Snorri. In modern culture Vanaheim appears in Marvel films and video games as a green counter-Asgard — an image the sources permit but never paint — and the Vanir remain central to modern Heathen practice.

## FAQ

**Who lives in Vanaheim?**
The Vanir, the family of gods associated with fertility, wealth, the sea, and seiðr-magic. Its only named natives are Njörðr, his children Freyr and Freyja, and (in one tradition) Kvasir — all of whom we meet only after they left for Asgard as hostages.

**Is Vanaheim one of the nine realms?**
It is usually counted among them, and its name — 'world of the Vanir' — supports that. But the sources never enumerate the nine worlds they mention; the canonical list in modern books is a reconstruction.

**Does Vanaheim survive Ragnarök?**
Apparently, yes. Vafþrúðnismál says Njörðr will return home 'to the wise Vanir' at the doom of men, implying both the Vanir and their homeland persist. It is one quiet line, but it makes Vanaheimr one of the few corners of the cosmos with a future.

**Why did the Vanir behead Mímir?**
Because they felt swindled. The Æsir sent Hœnir as a hostage fit for lordship, but without Mímir's whispered counsel he only said 'let others decide.' Judging the exchange unequal, the Vanir killed Mímir and sent his head to Odin, who preserved it and consulted it ever after.
