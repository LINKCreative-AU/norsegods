Those who die of sickness, age, or plain bad luck travel the Helvegr, the Hel-road: north and downward for nine nights, through valleys dark and deep, over the river Gjöll on its gold-roofed bridge — where the maiden Móðguðr asks the dead their name and business — to the high gates of Hel. Within rules [Hel](/gods/hel), [Loki](/gods/loki)'s half-corpse daughter, in her hall Éljúðnir. It is not a place of torment but of grey continuation; the punishment shore of Náströnd, where [Níðhöggr](/creatures/nidhogg) chews oath-breakers, lies at its worst edge.

This is where most people went: Valhalla gets the poetry, but the ordinary end of an ordinary Norse life led down the long north road, to the realm of the dead as census, not as verdict.

## What is the difference between Hel and Helheim?

The medieval sources use one word, Hel, for both the ruler and her realm — to "go to Hel" means the place and the person at once. The compound "Helheimr" barely exists in the Old Norse corpus; it is a modern convenience for keeping the queen and her kingdom apart on the page.

Gylfaginning makes the queen the third child of Loki and the giantess Angrboða, sibling to the wolf Fenrir and the world-serpent [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr) — a family of catastrophes, which the gods dealt with one by one: the serpent thrown into the sea, the wolf bound, and the girl cast down into the dark of [Niflheimr](/realms/niflheim), where [Odin](/gods/odin) gave her authority over nine worlds, to allot lodgings to all sent to her: those dead of sickness and of old age. She is half blue-black like a corpse and half flesh-colored, easily known, grim and downcast of face.

Snorri furnishes her estate with the bleakest inventory in Norse literature. Her hall is Éljúðnir, lashed by sleet; her dish is called Hunger and her knife Famine; her man-servant Ganglati and serving-woman Ganglöt are both named for slowness; her threshold is Stumbling-block, her bed is Sickbed, and its hangings are Glimmering-bale. It is a household of decline itself, named for the slow ways people arrive there.

One more blur must be admitted: the poems also speak of Niflhel, "misty Hel," nine worlds down, into which the wicked "die out of Hel" — death beyond death. Where Hel's realm ends and Niflheimr begins is a border no source ever draws.

## Who goes to Helheim?

The rough rule is a division by manner of death: the weapon-dead are gathered to Odin's Valhalla or to [Freyja](/gods/freyja)'s field Fólkvangr, which takes half the slain each day, per Grímnismál. The rest — the sick, the old, the unlucky — go down to Hel. Seafaring tradition adds that the drowned might go instead to Rán. None of this is a moral judgement: Helheimr is an address, not a verdict.

And the system leaks. [Baldr](/gods/baldr), a god, dies by violence — a mistletoe dart thrown in play — yet he goes to Hel, not Valhalla; the mythology's most beloved death lands in its greyest afterlife. The heroine Brynhildr, dead by her own blade, rides the Hel-road in a wagon in Helreið Brynhildar, trading insults with a giantess on the way. The straw-death rule is a tendency the poems themselves cheerfully violate.

Real punishment exists, but it is a district, not the realm. Völuspá describes a hall standing far from the sun on Náströnd, the Corpse-shore: its doors face north, venom drips in through the roof-vents, and its walls are woven of serpents' spines. There wade the perjurers, the murderers, and the seducers of other men's wives, in heavy venom-streams, while Níðhöggr sucks the corpses and the wolf tears men. That shore is Helheimr's worst edge — the vivid exception to the grey rule.

## The ride of Hermóðr

The realm's great story is an almost. When Baldr died, Frigg asked who would ride to Hel to offer a ransom for him. Hermóðr the Bold took the errand, mounted Odin's horse [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir), and rode down into the dark.

Nine nights he rode through valleys so deep and black he saw nothing, until he came to the river Gjöll and its gold-roofed bridge. Its keeper Móðguðr stopped him: the bridge, she said, had thundered less under five battalions of dead men the day before than under him alone — and he had not the color of dead men. He answered that he sought Baldr, and she pointed him on: downward and northward lies the way to Hel.

At the gates he did not knock: he tightened Sleipnir's girth and spurred him, and the horse cleared the gate of Hel without grazing it. Within, Baldr sat in the high seat; Hermóðr stayed the night, and in the morning begged Hel to let his brother ride home, telling her how great the weeping was among the gods. Hel set her test with a ruler's coolness: if Baldr was so loved, let everything in the world, living and dead, weep for him — then he may return. If anything refuses, he stays.

Baldr walked his brother out and sent tokens back into the daylight: the ring [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir) returned to Odin, and from Nanna, dead of grief, a linen robe for Frigg and a gold ring for Fulla. Messengers went out, and the whole world wept — people and beasts, earth and stones, trees and every metal, the way iron weeps when it comes out of frost into heat. Until, in a cave, they found a giantess who called herself Þökk, and she answered in verse: Baldr had never done her any good, living or dead — let Hel hold what she has. Men say, Snorri adds, that this was Loki. One dry eye, and the gates kept their guest.

## Odin at the eastern door

Helheimr had already drawn Odin himself down its road. In Baldrs draumar, when Baldr's sleep turns heavy with evil dreams, Odin saddles Sleipnir and rides down toward Niflhel to find out why. A dog meets him out of Hel, its chest blood-dabbled, and bays long at the father of magic as he passes. He rides on to the high hall and its eastern door, where a völva — a seeress — lies long dead in her grave, and chants corpse-waking spells over her until she rises, complaining of long ages under snow and rain.

Calling himself Vegtamr, "Way-tamer," he asks for whom Hel's benches are strewn with rings, the dais flooded with gold. For Baldr, she answers — the mead stands brewed for him already, a shield over the bright drink. Verse by unwilling verse she gives up the rest: blind Höðr will be the slayer; a son of Odin one night old will avenge him. Then she sees through the traveler — "you are not Vegtamr; you are Odin" — and her last words close the realm like a vault-door: none will wake her again until Loki slips his bonds and the doom of the gods arrives. The dead-land keeps even its prophecies in cold storage until [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok).

## In the primary sources

Gylfaginning in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) carries the fullest account: Hel's parentage and banishment, her authority over those dead of sickness and age, the grim inventory of Éljúðnir, Hermóðr's ride, and the lore of Naglfar's unclipped nails. In the Poetic Edda, Völuspá contributes Náströnd and its waders, the refrain of the hound Garmr howling before the cave Gnipahellir, the ship coming from the east at the world's end, and Baldr's return after it; Baldrs draumar is the Hel-road seen through Odin's own journey; Grímnismál places one of Yggdrasil's three roots over Hel herself; and Helreið Brynhildar sends a Völsung heroine down the same road. Saxo Grammaticus adds a comparison in Gesta Danorum: King Hadding, led beneath the earth through mist, finds sunny fields of growing grain and armies of the slain fighting forever — a greener, neighboring underworld.

## At Ragnarök

Helheimr does not sit out the end. In its harbor lies Naglfar, the ship built of dead men's uncut nails, growing plank by plank through all of history — which is why, Gylfaginning notes, the dead should go to the pyre trimmed: every neglected fingernail is timber for the enemy. When the seas surge over the land as Jörmungandr comes ashore, Naglfar floats free at last.

Who commands it is a genuine conflict in the sources. Snorri gives the helm to the giant Hrymr, with Loki arriving separately at the head of all Hel's people; Völuspá has the ship come from the east bearing Múspell's people, with Loki himself steering. Either way, the grey country's census empties onto the battle-plain and becomes an army. Garmr, howling all the while before Gnipahellir — often taken as Hel's own cave-mouth — breaks his fetters and kills, and is killed by, the god Týr.

The realm's final reversal comes after the fire: when the earth rises green from the sea, Völuspá says Baldr comes back — Baldr and his blind slayer Höðr together, out of Hel, to dwell in the gods' old sanctuaries. The gate Hermóðr could not open swings wide when the world is made new. Death keeps what it has — until it doesn't.

## Legacy

Hel's name is the direct ancestor of the English word "hell," from a Germanic root meaning "the hidden, covered place." Christian preaching poured fire into that old cold word, so that the modern "hell" of flames is almost the opposite of the sleet-grey original. Marvel's Hela has made the goddess one of the most recognizable Norse figures in popular culture; the realm appears in games from God of War to Assassin's Creed Valhalla; and the Norwegian group Wardruna's song "Helvegen" — "the way to Hel" — has carried the old road's name into modern funerals.

## FAQ

**Is Helheim a place of punishment?**

No — for most of its dead it is a grey continuation, not a torment; Baldr is even given the high seat there. The exception is Náströnd, where Völuspá puts perjurers, murderers, and seducers wading venom while Níðhöggr chews them. Punishment is a district of Hel, not its purpose.

**Is Hel evil?**

The sources never call her evil; they make her grim, half-corpse, and scrupulously fair. Odin himself appointed her over the dead, and in the Baldr story she sets a lawful condition — universal weeping — and abides by its result exactly.

**Where is Helheim located?**

North and down: nine nights' ride through deep dark valleys and over the river Gjöll, per Gylfaginning. Grímnismál sets Hel beneath one of Yggdrasil's roots, and the poems blur her realm into Niflheimr and Niflhel — the sources agree on the direction, never on the map.

**Did all Vikings expect to go to Helheim?**

It was the default, but not the only door: the battle-slain hoped for Valhalla or Freyja's Fólkvangr, and saga tradition also imagines the dead living on inside their burial mounds. Helheim was simply the widest of many roads.

**Can anyone leave Helheim?**

Almost no one. Hermóðr, alive, jumped the gate and rode out again, but Baldr's release failed on one giantess's refusal. The dead leave twice in the sources: as an army on Naglfar at Ragnarök, and — the exception that ends the story — Baldr and Höðr walking home to the renewed world.
