Múspellsheimr is the fire that was burning before anything else existed: the southern primordial world, "bright and hot, flaming and burning," impassable to any not born to it. Its sparks, thrown into the void, met the ice of [Niflheimr](/realms/niflheim) and quickened the first living being, the giant [Ymir](/giants/ymir); and the gods later caught drifting embers of Múspell and set them in the sky as the sun, the moon, and the stars. The cosmos is lit by borrowed fire.

At its frontier sits [Surtr](/giants/surtr) with his burning sword, waiting. He has waited since before the world was made, and when he finally rides, everything ends. Múspellsheimr is the only realm whose whole story is a single, deferred event — the world's first cause and its last.

## Fire before the gods

Snorri's Gylfaginning gives the realm absolute priority: "first of all there was, in the southern region, the world called Múspell." It is light and hot, so flaming and burning that it cannot be endured by those who are foreigners there and have no ancestral lands in it. That is the entire geography we are given — heat, light, and a border no outsider survives.

And no story ever crosses it: no god, giant, or hero sets foot in Múspellsheimr, and no poem is set inside it. We know the fire-world only by its exports: the sparks that made life possible, the lights that hang in the sky, and the army that will one day come out of it. In a mythology built on border-crossings, Múspellsheimr is the one border that only opens outward.

Its role in creation is the counterpart of Niflheimr's. Into the yawning void of Ginnungagap the northern world sent rivers of venomous ice; the southern world sent sparks and glowing particles streaming from its heat. The middle of the void was mild as a windless sky, and there the rime met the warm blasts, melted, and dripped — and life quickened in the drops, taking the shape of the first giant, Ymir. Frost alone is sterile and fire alone devours; the Norse creation happens only at the seam where they temper each other. Every living thing descends from that first thaw — and so, in part, from Múspell's heat.

## Where did the sun, moon, and stars come from?

When Odin and his brothers had slain Ymir and built the world from his body, the sky above it was dark. So, Gylfaginning says, they took the sparks and glowing embers that were flying loose out of Múspellsheimr and set them in the midst of the firmament, above and below, to light heaven and earth. To some they gave fixed stations; others they set to move on appointed paths through the sky. Only then could days be counted and years reckoned.

Völuspá remembers the disorder that came before: the sun did not know where her hall was, the stars did not know their places, the moon did not know his power — until the gods went to their judgement-seats and gave names to night and its phases, to morning and midday, afternoon and evening. Ordered time itself, in other words, is Múspell's fire tamed and put on a schedule: every sunrise is a captured spark from the world that will one day burn everything, shining on a leash.

## Who is Surtr?

Surtr's name means "the black one," the swarthy or charred one — a figure the color of burnt land. Gylfaginning places him at Múspellsheimr's land's-end as its guardian: he sits at the frontier to defend the land, he bears a flaming sword, and at the end of the world he will go forth, make war on the gods and defeat them, and burn the whole world with fire. He is not a marauder but a sentinel; his violence is scheduled, and the gods know the schedule.

Völuspá gives him his unforgettable entrance: Surtr fares from the south with the harm of branches — fire itself — and sunlight flashes from the sword of the slaughter-gods. Cliffs crash together, troll-women are abroad, men tread the road to Hel, and heaven itself splits apart. Vafþrúðnismál names the field where "Surtr and the sweet gods" will meet: Vígríðr, a hundred leagues on every side. The poems even name his fire as a thing in itself — Surtalogi, "Surtr's flame": Vafþrúðnismál asks which of the gods will still walk when Surtalogi at last burns out.

His appointed opponent is [Freyr](/gods/freyr) — and that duel is one of the mythology's cruelest ironies. Freyr gave away his good sword, the one that fought by itself, as the price of winning the giantess Gerðr; in Lokasenna, [Loki](/gods/loki) taunts him with exactly this, jeering that when Múspell's sons ride over the dark wood Myrkviðr, Freyr will not know what to fight with. Snorri confirms the outcome: at the last battle Freyr falls to Surtr, because the good sword is not in his hand. The god of harvests is cut down by the fire that ripens harvests.

## What does Múspell actually mean?

Honesty requires a step back here, because "Múspellsheimr" as a realm is largely Snorri's word. In the Eddic poems, Múspell appears only in possessives: Völuspá's "Múspell's people" crossing the sea by ship, Lokasenna's "Múspell's sons" riding over Myrkviðr. The poems never say what — or who — Múspell is: it reads less like a place-name than the name of a being, or of the doom itself, whose "sons" are the agents of the world's burning. It is the Prose Edda that turns Múspell into a country with a coastline and a border guard.

The word is older and wider than Scandinavia. A ninth-century Old High German poem is known as the Muspilli after its own term for the fiery end of the world, and the Old Saxon Heliand uses mutspelli the same way. The etymology is disputed, but the distribution suggests a common Germanic word for the world-consuming fire, older than any surviving Norse text. Múspellsheimr, then, may be a country built out of an apocalypse: Snorri giving the end of the world a homeland.

## In the primary sources

The [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda)'s Gylfaginning carries the realm's whole description: its primacy in the south, its unendurable heat, Surtr the frontier-guard, the sparks that quickened Ymir and became the heavenly lights, and the full choreography of Ragnarök. In the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Völuspá contributes Surtr's ride from the south, the splitting heaven, the ship bearing Múspell's people with Loki at the helm, and the world-fire itself; Lokasenna adds Múspell's sons riding over Myrkviðr and Freyr's missing sword; Vafþrúðnismál names the battle-plain Vígríðr and the fire Surtalogi, and counts the survivors. Fáfnismál preserves a variant tradition: there the gods and Surtr meet at an island called Óskópnir, and the bridge breaks beneath the riders as they cross — the same catastrophe with different names on the map.

One conflict is worth flagging plainly. In Völuspá, Múspell's people come by sea, aboard a ship steered by Loki. In Snorri, the sons of Múspell ride against [Asgard](/realms/asgard), shattering the bridge Bifröst beneath them, while the ship Naglfar is steered by the giant Hrymr. The traditions agree that fire's army arrives and disagree about the itinerary — a reminder that we are reading the ruins of many tellings, not one canon.

## At Ragnarök

When the end comes, the sky splits open and the sons of Múspell ride out of the breach, Surtr foremost, fire burning before him and behind him, his sword outshining the sun. As Snorri had foretold, strong as it is, Bifröst breaks beneath their riding — the burning rainbow that [Heimdallr](/gods/heimdall) watched for ages fails at the first true test, and the sons of Múspell ride on over its wreckage to the plain of Vígríðr. There, alone among the armies of the end, they form their own shining battle-line, drawn up apart in their brightness.

Surtr meets Freyr, and Freyr falls. And when the great duels are finished — [Odin](/gods/odin) swallowed, the wolf torn, [Thor](/gods/thor) nine steps dead from the serpent's venom — Surtr performs the act he was posted for before time began: he flings fire over all things. Völuspá watches it happen: the sun turns black, the earth sinks into the sea, the hot stars whirl down from heaven, and steam and flame surge up to play against the sky itself. The world that began with Múspell's sparks ends in Múspell's flame — the loan called in, with interest.

Yet Völuspá does not end there. The earth rises from the sea a second time, green, its fields growing unsown. Of Múspellsheimr's own fate after [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) the sources say nothing; the fire made the world, unmade it, and passes out of the story as unvisited as it entered.

## Legacy

The fire-world has kept its grip on real landscapes. Icelanders have connected Surtr with their island's volcanism since the settlement age — the great lava cave Surtshellir bears his name in medieval sources — and when a new volcanic island boiled up out of the North Atlantic in 1963, Iceland named it Surtsey, "Surtr's island." The Old High German Muspilli preserves the word's continental cousin in a Christian vision of world-fire. And modern culture keeps the realm burning: Surtur looms over Marvel's Ragnarok, Múspellsheimr appears in God of War, and every "fire giant" in modern fantasy descends from Vígríðr's shining battle-line.

## FAQ

**Is Muspelheim older than the gods?**

Yes. Gylfaginning makes Múspellsheimr the first world of all, existing in the south before earth, sea, or sky — and long before Odin's family, only Niflheimr sharing its antiquity. The gods themselves descend from beings quickened where the two realms' ice and fire met.

**Who lives in Muspelheim?**

The sources are sparing: the realm is passable only to those born to it, Surtr guards its frontier, and at Ragnarök the "sons of Múspell" ride out — an otherwise unnamed host of fire. "Fire giants" is a modern label; the texts describe no one beyond Surtr, usually reckoned among the jötnar.

**Did Surtr create Muspelheim?**

No. Surtr is its guardian, not its maker — Gylfaginning says he sits at the land's end to defend it. No source describes Múspellsheimr being created at all; it is simply there first, and no maker is ever named for either primordial world.

**Is Loki from Muspelheim?**

No. Loki is of jötunn stock, son of Fárbauti and Laufey, though Völuspá does put him at the helm of the ship carrying Múspell's people at Ragnarök. The confusion is helped along by Logi, "flame" personified, who out-ate Loki in Útgarða-Loki's contest — similar names, different beings.

**What is Surtalogi?**

Surtalogi, "Surtr's fire," is the world-consuming flame Surtr flings over the earth at Ragnarök. Vafþrúðnismál uses the word when Odin asks which gods will survive "when Surtr's fire is slaked" — the answer includes Víðarr and Váli, and Thor's sons Móði and Magni, who inherit Mjölnir in the renewed world.
