Niflheimr ("Mist-world") is one of the two primordial worlds, the cold pole of the Norse cosmos, made many ages before the earth. At its heart boils the spring Hvergelmir, mother of the eleven rivers called the Élivágar, whose venom-cold waters flowed out into the void Ginnungagap, froze layer on layer, and — meeting the sparks of [Múspellsheimr](/realms/muspelheim) — dripped into the first life, the giant [Ymir](/giants/ymir). All existence is the child of this frost meeting that fire.

Nor did the mist-world retire once its work was done. When the cosmos was ordered, one of [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil)'s three great roots was laid over Niflheimr, with Hvergelmir seething beneath it and the dragon [Níðhöggr](/creatures/nidhogg) gnawing the root from below. And into this same darkness [Odin](/gods/odin) cast down Hel, daughter of Loki, and gave her the realm of the dead — hence "Niflhel," the misty hell into which the wicked dead die out of Hel itself, nine worlds down. Niflheim is thus doubly fundamental: everything begins in its ice, and the worst of everything sinks back into its dark.

## Before the world: Ginnungagap and the eleven rivers

Snorri Sturluson's Gylfaginning is blunt about the mist-world's seniority: Niflheim was made many ages before the earth. In the beginning there was no sand, no sea, no cool waves — only the yawning emptiness of Ginnungagap, with Niflheim's cold and gloom filling its northern reaches and Múspellsheimr's glare burning far to the south.

In the middle of Niflheim lies the spring Hvergelmir, the "roaring kettle," and from it pour the rivers the sources call the Élivágar, the "ice-waves." Snorri names them: Svöl, Gunnþrá, Fjörm, Fimbulþul, Slíðr and Hríð, Sylgr and Ylgr, Víð, Leiptr — and Gjöll, "which is nearest the gates of Hel." Eleven rivers, and the last of them already pointing toward the dead.

These waters were not merely cold. They ran with a yeasty venom, and as they flowed farther and farther from their source, the venom hardened like the slag that runs from a furnace, and turned to ice. Where the rivers halted, the ice stood still; the drizzle that rose off the venom froze into rime, and rime piled upon rime, layer over layer, until frost filled all the northern half of Ginnungagap. The southern half glittered with sparks and molten particles flying out of Múspellsheimr. And between them, says Snorri, the void was as mild as a windless sky.

## How was the world created from ice and fire?

Where the rime met the breath of heat, it melted and dripped — and in those falling drops life quickened, by the power of the force that sent the heat, and took the likeness of a man. This was Ymir, whom the frost-giants call Aurgelmir, the ancestor of all their kind. The [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) confirms the story independently: in Vafþrúðnismál, the wise giant tells Odin that venom-drops sprang from the Élivágar and grew until a jötunn came of them, and that every frost-giant lineage descends from that beginning — "that is why we are all so terrible."

From the same melting rime came the cow Auðumla, whose four rivers of milk fed Ymir, and whose tongue, licking the salty rime-stones, freed the first of the gods' line: Búri, father of Borr, father of Odin and his brothers Vili and Vé. Those three slew Ymir, drowned nearly all the frost-giants in his blood, and built the world from his body — his flesh the earth, his blood the sea, his bones the mountains, his skull the dome of the sky over [Midgard](/realms/midgard).

The Norse creation, note, is not a making from nothing; it is a chemistry of extremes, life happening at the seam where ice and fire temper one another. Niflheim alone is sterile. Niflheim plus its opposite is everything.

## What is the difference between Niflheim, Niflhel, and Hel?

Here honesty matters more than tidiness, because the sources blur these names and no diagram can be drawn that they all agree on.

The word Niflheimr itself appears only in the Prose Edda and texts under its influence. The Eddic poems instead speak of Niflhel, "misty Hel." In Vafþrúðnismál, the giant boasts that he has travelled through nine worlds, down below Niflhel, "where men die out of Hel" — death beyond death, a second dying for those already dead. In Baldrs draumar, Odin rides down toward Niflhel on his way to the hall of the dead. Snorri, for his part, says Odin threw Hel into Niflheim and gave her authority over nine worlds; elsewhere in Gylfaginning he says wicked men go to Hel and thence to Niflhel, "which is down in the ninth world." Even the manuscripts waver, some reading Niflheimr where others read Niflhel in the same passage.

The most honest picture is this: Niflheim is the primordial ice-world in the north; Hel — the realm treated fully at [Helheim](/realms/helheim) — is the land of the dead that lies in or beneath that same northern darkness, under Hel's rule; and Niflhel is the deepest, worst layer of the whole cold complex. Whether these are one region, two, or three is a question the medieval sources never settle, because they were never trying to draw a map.

A related caution: the familiar canon of "Nine Realms" is a modern reconstruction. The poems speak of "nine worlds" but no source ever lists them, and whether Niflheim and Hel count as one world or two on that unwritten list is exactly the kind of question the tradition leaves open.

## Hvergelmir and the root of Yggdrasil

In the ordered cosmos, Niflheim keeps its station under the world-tree. Gylfaginning describes Yggdrasil's three roots: one among the Æsir, one among the frost-giants "where Ginnungagap once was," and the third stretching over Niflheim — with Hvergelmir beneath it, and Níðhöggr gnawing the root from below. The tree that holds up all the worlds is being eaten, endlessly, at its coldest foundation.

Grímnismál adds a detail that binds the top of the cosmos to its bottom: on the roof of Odin's hall stands the stag Eikþyrnir, and from his antlers drips a stream that falls all the way down into Hvergelmir — and from Hvergelmir, the poem says, all rivers rise. The spring in the mist-world is the source and the drain of the world's waters, its deep circulation. The same poem warns that more serpents lie beneath Yggdrasil "than any fool imagines," coiled with Níðhöggr about that northern root.

## In the primary sources

The [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda)'s Gylfaginning carries nearly the whole weight of Niflheim's description: its antiquity, Hvergelmir and the named Élivágar, the venom-ice filling Ginnungagap, the quickening of Ymir, Hel's banishment into its dark, the root and the dragon, and the realm's grim function after the world's renewal. Snorri is our systematizer, and it is fair to say that Niflheim as a tidy "world" with borders is largely his construction.

The Poetic Edda supports the essentials without the system. Vafþrúðnismál attests the Élivágar and the venom-drops that became Aurgelmir, and gives us Niflhel with its nine worlds beneath. Baldrs draumar sends Odin riding down toward Niflhel to wake a dead seeress. Völuspá never names Niflheim, but it knows Ginnungagap, the corpse-shore Náströnd, and the final image of the dark dragon rising from the Niðafjöll, the "dark mountains" — the cold underside of the cosmos that Snorri's Niflheim organizes into a realm.

## At Ragnarök

Niflheim sends no army to the last battle. The fire, the wolf, the serpent, and the dead all march — but the mist-world stays home. Its contribution to the end is atmospheric and absolute: the Fimbulvetr, the "mighty winter" that heralds [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — three winters together with no summer between, snow driving from all directions, hard frost and biting winds — is the whole world turning, for a while, into Niflheim.

And when the fire has come and gone and the earth rises green from the sea, the region keeps its cold work. Snorri says that in the renewed world Hvergelmir is the worst place of all, where Níðhöggr torments the bodies of the dead; and Völuspá closes — after all its images of renewal — with the dark dragon flying up from the Niðafjöll, corpses borne in his wings, before he sinks from sight. The reborn world still has a basement. Even paradise, in the Norse imagination, is built over the old ice.

## Legacy

The element nifl- is cousin to German Nebel and Latin nebula, "mist" and "cloud" — the same word-family drifts through the name of the Niflungar, the doomed treasure-holding dynasty of the Völsung legend, whom German tradition calls the Nibelungen. Wagner drew on both when he named the dwarves' subterranean realm Nibelheim in Das Rheingold. Scholars have long suspected that the whole fire-and-ice cosmogony owes something to Iceland itself, where glaciers and volcanoes grind against each other in a single landscape. And Niflheim endures wherever modern fantasy needs a frozen underworld, from Marvel to God of War.

## FAQ

**Is Niflheim the same as Helheim?**

Not quite, though the sources tangle them badly. Niflheim is the primordial world of ice and mist, older than the earth; Hel's realm of the dead was established inside or beneath that darkness when Odin cast Hel down into it. The poems also speak of Niflhel, a deepest hell into which the wicked "die out of Hel." The three names shade into one another, and no source draws the border cleanly.

**Who lives in Niflheim?**

Almost nothing — that is the point. It has no native population like the jötnar of Jötunheimr; it holds a spring, eleven poisonous rivers, rime, and darkness. Its most famous residents are Níðhöggr and the serpents beneath Yggdrasil's root, and the dead in the adjoining realm of Hel. Life came out of Niflheim's ice, but nothing is said to live happily in it.

**Is Niflheim one of the Nine Realms?**

Modern lists almost always include it, but the "Nine Realms" as a fixed roster is a modern reconstruction. The Eddas speak of nine worlds without ever naming them, so whether Niflheim, Niflhel, and Hel count as one world or several is unanswerable from the sources.

**What is Hvergelmir?**

Hvergelmir, the "roaring kettle," is the spring at the heart of Niflheim: source of the Élivágar rivers that froze into the first ice, the well beneath Yggdrasil's northern root, and — per Grímnismál — the origin of all rivers. After Ragnarök, Snorri names it the worst of all places, where Níðhöggr torments the dead.

**What does Niflheim mean?**

"Mist-world" or "mist-home," from nifl-, a Germanic word for fog and darkness related to German Nebel and Latin nebula, plus heimr, "world, home" — the same element as in Jötunheimr and Múspellsheimr.
