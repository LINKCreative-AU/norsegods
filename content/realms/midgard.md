Midgard is the middle of everything: the human world, built by [Odin](/gods/odin) and his brothers from the slain giant [Ymir](/giants/ymir) and fenced with his eyebrows against the jötnar outside. Beyond its coasts runs the world-ocean, and in that ocean lies [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), the Midgard Serpent, wrapped around the world with its tail in its mouth — the horizon itself as a sleeping monster.

The name means 'Middle Enclosure', and both halves matter. *Mið-* places it at the center of the cosmos, between the gods' [Asgard](/realms/asgard) and the giants' [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim), between the primordial fire of the south and the primordial ice of the north. *-garðr* makes it a defended space: like a Norse farmstead, the human world is a clearing with a fence around it, and everything human — law, kinship, harvest, memory — happens inside the fence. Outside is *útgarðr*, the outer yards, where the giants live and the rules do not hold. Midgard is not merely a location; it is a claim about what a world is.

## A world built from a body

Before Midgard there was Ymir, the first being, congealed alive where the venom-rivers of [Niflheimr](/realms/niflheim) met the sparks of [Múspellsheimr](/realms/muspelheim) in the void Ginnungagap. Odin and his brothers Vili and Vé killed him, and so much blood ran from his wounds that it drowned nearly all his frost-giant descendants. Then, in one of mythology's most complete acts of recycling, they carried the corpse into the middle of the void and made the world from it. His flesh became the earth, his blood the encircling sea, his bones the mountains, his teeth and shattered bone-fragments the scree and stones. His skull they raised as the dome of the sky, set at its four corners under four dwarves — Norðri, Suðri, Austri, Vestri, the compass points holding up heaven — and his brains they threw aloft as the clouds. Last of all they took Ymir's eyelashes (or eyebrows; the word wavers) and built with them the fortification around the world's middle, and that ringed and sheltered ground they gave to humankind and called Midgard.

The Grímnismál stanzas that preserve this making are among the oldest cosmology in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), and the picture is consistent: the human world is giant-flesh, fenced with giant-hair, against giants. We live inside the body of the enemy, protected by a rampart made of him.

## Askr and Embla: the first humans

Midgard was a world before it had a people. Walking the shore — Völuspá says three gods, Odin, Hœnir, and Lóðurr; Snorri says Odin, Vili, and Vé — the gods found two trees, or two pieces of driftwood: Askr, 'Ash', and Embla, whose name may mean 'Elm' or 'Vine'. They were *örlöglausir* — fateless, powerless, without destiny. The gods gave gifts, each his own: breath and life from Odin, wit and movement from the second god, warmth, speech, hearing, and good color from the third. From those two salvaged trunks descends every human lineage in the Norse world; humanity is driftwood that the gods bothered to finish. It is worth noticing what the story assumes: people are made on Midgard's beach, from Midgard's own washed-up timber, native to the middle world in a way even the gods are not.

## Where is Midgard in the Nine Realms?

At the center — but the tidy list of "Nine Realms" you will find in modern books is a reconstruction. The Eddas speak of nine worlds without ever enumerating them; the roster (Asgard, Midgard, Jötunheimr, [Vanaheimr](/realms/vanaheim), [Álfheimr](/realms/alfheim), [Svartálfaheimr](/realms/svartalfheim), Niflheimr, Múspellsheimr, [Helheim](/realms/helheim)) is stitched together from scattered names. What the sources do consistently give Midgard is its relational position: linked upward to Asgard by the rainbow bridge Bifröst, ringed by the sea where the serpent lies, with the giants east across the rivers and the dead below and to the north. Every direction from Midgard leads somewhere dangerous, which is the point: the middle world is defined by what surrounds it.

## The serpent at the edge

Jörmungandr, Midgard's namesake monster, is [Loki](/gods/loki)'s child by the giantess Angrboða. Odin threw the young serpent into the deep sea that rings all lands, and there it grew until it circled the entire world and bit its own tail. The serpent is Midgard's boundary made animate — and its doom deferred. [Thor](/gods/thor), Midgard's great defender, meets it three times: once in Útgarða-Loki's hall, where the "cat" he strained to lift was the serpent in disguise and his lifting bent the world's edge; once fishing with the giant Hymir, when he hooked Jörmungandr on an ox-head and had the monster's venom-blowing head at the gunwale before the terrified giant cut the line; and finally at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok). The fishing episode was a favorite of Viking Age artists — picture-stones from Altuna in Sweden and Gosforth in England still show the god, the ox-head bait, and the coiling line.

## In the primary sources

Midgard's making from Ymir is given in Völuspá and, in question-and-answer form, in Vafþrúðnismál and Grímnismál; Snorri assembles the full narrative in Gylfaginning in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), including the eyebrow-wall and the four dwarves under the sky. The creation of Askr and Embla appears in Völuspá 17–18 and Gylfaginning, with the two texts naming different divine triads — a genuine discrepancy the sources never resolve. The word *miðgarðr* itself is older than any of these texts: cognates appear in Gothic (*midjungards*) and Old English (*middangeard*, the word Beowulf's poet uses for the world of men), showing that the "middle enclosure" was common Germanic inheritance long before the Eddas wrote it down.

## At Ragnarök

Midgard's end begins at its edge: Jörmungandr releases its tail and comes ashore, whipping the sea into the flood that floats the death-ship Naglfar, and the drowned coasts are only the start. The serpent sprays venom over sky and sea; Thor kills it and walks nine steps before its poison kills him — the middle world's protector and its boundary-monster canceling each other out. Then [Surtr](/giants/surtr)'s fire burns heaven and earth, and the sun goes black, and Midgard sinks into the sea. But Völuspá does not end there, and neither does Midgard: the völva sees the earth rise a second time out of the water, green forever, with fields that grow unsown, eagles hunting fish on the mountainside, and a human pair — Líf and Lífþrasir, sheltered through the fire — to begin the lineages again. Midgard is the one realm explicitly shown reborn with its people.

## Legacy

The word has had the longest afterlife of any Norse realm-name. Old English *middangeard* passed into Middle English as *middellærd*, and J.R.R. Tolkien — who knew the philology professionally — revived it as Middle-earth, a direct calque of Miðgarðr; every fantasy "midworld" since is downstream. The Midgard Serpent's tail-in-mouth ring became a Northern cousin of the ouroboros, and modern astronomy, games, and fiction reach for "Midgard" whenever they need a name for the human world seen from outside. The deeper legacy is the frame itself: the intuition that the human world is a lit clearing in a dark forest, held by a fence that someone has to keep defending.

## FAQ

**What does Midgard mean?**
'Middle Enclosure' or 'Middle Yard' — the fenced world of humans at the center of the cosmos. The same word appears across the Germanic languages, including Old English *middangeard*, which is the ancestor (via Tolkien's deliberate revival) of "Middle-earth."

**Who created Midgard?**
Odin and his brothers Vili and Vé, who killed the primordial giant Ymir and built the world from his corpse: flesh into earth, blood into sea, bones into mountains, skull into sky, and eyebrows into the wall that fences Midgard against the giants.

**Is Midgard just Earth?**
Functionally yes — it is the world where humans live, the Norse name for the inhabited earth. But it is Earth understood mythically: flat-centered, ringed by an ocean with a world-serpent in it, joined to the gods' realm by a rainbow bridge, and bordered by giants.

**What is the Midgard Serpent?**
Jörmungandr, Loki's monstrous child, thrown into the world-ocean by Odin, where it grew to encircle all lands and grip its own tail. It is Thor's destined enemy: he hooks it on a fishing trip in Hymiskviða, and at Ragnarök they kill each other.

**Does Midgard survive Ragnarök?**
It drowns and burns — the serpent's flood, then Surtr's fire. But Völuspá sees it rise again from the sea, green and self-sowing, and two humans, Líf and Lífþrasir, survive to repeople it. Of all the realms, Midgard has the clearest promised renewal.
