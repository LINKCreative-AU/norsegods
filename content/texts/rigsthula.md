A god calling himself Ríg walks the green roads of the young world and lodges at three houses in ascending order of prosperity. At each he shares the couple's bed for three nights, and nine months later the households are enriched: Great-grandmother bears Þræll (Thrall), black-haired and lumpy-knuckled, whose children's names are a catalogue of insults (Stumpy, Stinker, Lump) and whose lot is dung-spreading and goat-herding; Grandmother bears Karl (Freeman), the red-cheeked farmer whose kin build houses and drive ploughs; Mother bears Jarl (Earl), blond and snake-eyed, who from childhood knows shield, spear, and — when Ríg returns to claim him — runes and inheritance.

The prose header identifies Ríg as Heimdallr, which lights up Völuspá's opening address to 'the sons of Heimdallr' — all humanity, high and low, as the watchman's children. The poem ends mid-story with Jarl's son Konr ungr — 'young Konr,' a pun on konungr, king — out-riddling his father in rune-lore as a crow incites him to conquest; the page breaks off there, monarchy arriving just as the manuscript fails. Read straight, it is class ideology with divine warranty; read slant, it is the North explaining, with a completely straight face, that aristocracy is what happens when a god sleeps over.

## What is Rígsþula?

Rígsþula — "The List (þula) of Ríg" — is an Eddic-style poem of some 48 surviving stanzas in fornyrðislag, and one of the strays: it appears in no [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) manuscript proper, surviving instead on a leaf of the Codex Wormianus, a fourteenth-century manuscript of [Snorri's Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda). It is also incomplete — the text breaks off mid-scene, and whatever ending it had is gone with the following pages. What remains is unique in the corpus: the only sustained Norse myth of social origins, an account not of how the world was made but of how it was stratified. Nothing else in the tradition explains where thralls, farmers, and aristocrats come from; this poem does, in loving, damning, anthropological detail.

## The story: three houses, three nights each

The frame is folktale-simple and executed with a sociologist's eye. A god — the prose header says it is [Heimdall](/gods/heimdall), calling himself Ríg — walks the green roads of a young world and comes to three dwellings in ascending order.

**The first house** is a hut with the door on the latch; inside, Ái and Edda — Great-grandfather and Great-grandmother — by the fire. The bread is heavy, thick, packed with husks; the broth is in a bowl. Ríg gives counsel, then lies between them in the marriage bed for three nights and travels on. Nine months later Edda bears a son: swarthy, and they sprinkle him with water and call him Þræll — Thrall. He grows strong and ugly by the poem's lights — wrinkled skin, lumpy knuckles, thick fingers, bent back — and marries a walk-in bride, Þír ("bondwoman"), with crooked legs and dirty feet. Their children's names are a catalogue of contempt: Fjósnir (Cattle-man), Klúrr (Coarse), Kleggi (Horsefly), Kefsir, Fúlnir (Stinker), Drumbr (Log), Digraldi (Fatty), Dröttr (Sluggard), Lútr (Stooped), Leggjaldi, and daughters to match — Drumba, Kumba (Stumpy), Ökkvinkálfa (Fat-calves), Arinnefja (Hearth-nose). Their work: fencing, dunging fields, herding goats and swine, digging peat. "From them come the generations of thralls."

**The second house** is a hall, its door ajar. Afi and Amma — Grandfather and Grandmother — are at work, he shaping wood for a loom-beam, beard trimmed, shirt snug; she spinning, with a headdress and a kerchief. The same three nights; nine months later Amma bears Karl — Freeman, Farmer — red-haired and ruddy, with lively eyes. He tames oxen, forges tools, builds houses and barns, makes carts, drives the plough. He marries properly — Snör, the daughter-in-law, brought home with keys at her belt in a goatskin kirtle; a wedding, rings exchanged, a household founded. Their sons are called Man and Smith, Yeoman, Householder, Sharp-beard; their daughters Sensible, Wife, Bride, Capable. "From them come the generations of karls" — the free farmers who were the actual spine of the Norse world.

**The third house** is a hall facing south, its door standing open, a ring on the doorpost, the floor strewn. Faðir and Móðir — Father and Mother — have leisure: he twists bowstrings and shafts; she admires her own arms, smooths her linen, pins her headdress; her dress trails, her brooches shine, her brow is brighter, her breast more radiant, her neck whiter than new-fallen snow. The table gets a patterned cloth, white wheat bread, silver dishes, game-birds and pork, wine in mounted cups. Three nights; nine months; and Móðir bears Jarl — Earl. He is blond and bright-cheeked, and his eyes are grim as a young serpent's. Jarl's childhood is a weapons catalogue: shield-shaking, bow-bending, spear-brandishing, hound-training, horse-riding, sword-swimming. And to Jarl alone the god returns. Ríg comes back, teaches him [runes](/concepts/runes), names him his son and heir, and tells him to take the odal lands — the ancestral estates. Jarl obliges by war: darkening the land with spear-play, taking eighteen homesteads, dealing out treasure. He marries Erna, slender-fingered and wise, daughter of a lord; their sons are called Heir, Child, Noble, Kinsman — and the youngest is Konr.

**And then the pun that ends everything.** Konr ungr — "young Konr" — is a name and a destiny in one, because konungr is the Old Norse word for king. Young Konr knows runes better than his father — life-runes and aeon-runes; he knows bird-speech, can blunt blades and calm seas, and has the strength of eight. As he rides fowling, a crow speaks to him from a branch: why hush the birds, when you might ride against men? — Danr and Danpr have costlier halls than yours... The manuscript breaks off there, mid-incitement: monarchy arriving on the page at the exact moment the page fails. Whether the lost ending crowned Konr, connected him to the legendary Danish kings the crow names, or did something stranger, no one will ever know.

## Who is Ríg — and why Heimdall?

The name Ríg is generally derived from Old Irish rí (genitive ríg), "king" — one of several hints of Celtic-Norse contact that have led some scholars to imagine the poem composed in the mixed milieux of the British Isles or by a poet who knew them. The prose header's identification of Ríg with Heimdall has been debated forever: Heimdall is nowhere else a wandering begetter, and some scholars suspect the header guesses, or that the traveller was originally [Odin](/gods/odin), the corpus's habitual wandering guest. But the identification has one mighty ally: [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) opens by calling on all humanity as "greater and lesser sons of Heimdallr," a phrase that makes perfect sense if the watchman of the gods fathered every class of mankind — and very little sense otherwise. The two texts light each other up, which is either corroboration or the source of the header-writer's guess. Scholarship holds both candles.

## Ideology with a straight face

The poem's date and purpose are genuinely open questions. Estimates have ranged from the tenth century to the thirteenth, and its politics read differently at every date: an old sacral myth legitimating Viking Age kingship; court poetry flattering some historical dynasty; or a late antiquarian construction. What is not in question is what the poem does: it presents social hierarchy as begotten by a god — not assigned, not earned, but bred, visible in bodies (black hair and bent backs below, white necks and serpent eyes above), diets (husky bread to wine in silver), and even names. Read straight, it is the frankest piece of class ideology the medieval North produced, aristocracy with a divine pedigree. Read slant — and the poem's deadpan precision invites it — it is an almost anthropological document: the North explaining its own three-tier society (thrall, free farmer, lord — the same structure the laws assume) by telling, with a completely straight face, a story in which the difference between a slave and a king is which bed a god happened to visit. Modern readers wince at the thrall-stanzas' cruelty, and should; the poem is evidence, among other things, of exactly how Norse slavery was seen by those who profited from it.

## How reliable is it?

Single manuscript, no parallel text, incomplete ending, disputed god, undatable: Rígsþula demands more caution than almost any other Eddic poem. Its social portrait, however, checks out remarkably well against law-codes, sagas, and archaeology — the three-order society, the free farmer's household economy, the keys at the wife's belt, the odal inheritance, the rune-lore of elites — which is why historians of Norse society quote it constantly even while mythologists argue over its header. Treat the sociology as observed and the theology as contested, and the poem gives full value.

## Reading it today

Rígsþula is included among the additional poems in complete editions of the Poetic Edda — Larrington's and Crawford's translations both carry it, as do Hollander and Bellows. It is short, vivid, and unlike anything else in the corpus: read it after Völuspá, and the "sons of Heimdallr" in that poem's first stanza will never sound generic again.

## FAQ

**What is Rígsþula about?**
A god calling himself Ríg visits three households — poor, comfortable, and noble — sleeping three nights in each marriage bed. The resulting sons, Þræll (Thrall), Karl (Freeman), and Jarl (Earl), become the ancestors of the three Norse social classes, and Jarl's son Konr ungr embodies the coming of kingship.

**Is Ríg really Heimdall?**
The medieval prose introduction says so, and Völuspá's address to humanity as "sons of Heimdallr" supports it — but the poem itself never names Heimdall, and some scholars think the wanderer was originally Odin or that the header is a copyist's guess. The question remains open.

**What does the ending of Rígsþula mean?**
We only have part of it: the poem breaks off as a crow incites young Konr — whose name puns on konungr, "king" — to seek conquest like the Danish lords Danr and Danpr. The manuscript's final pages are lost, so the poem's conclusion, presumably the establishment of kingship, is gone.

**Where is Rígsþula preserved?**
On a leaf of the Codex Wormianus, a fourteenth-century manuscript of Snorri's Prose Edda — not in the Codex Regius of the Poetic Edda. It survives incomplete and in that single copy.

**Is Rígsþula an accurate picture of Viking society?**
Its mythology is ideology, but its sociology is well corroborated: the three-tier structure of thrall, free farmer, and aristocrat matches Norse law and archaeology, down to details like household keys and odal land. Historians use it carefully as evidence for how the social order was imagined — especially how brutally slavery was viewed from above.
