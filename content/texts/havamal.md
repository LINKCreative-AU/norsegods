Hávamál — 'Sayings of the High One' — is the North's wisdom literature with Odin as speaker. Its famous opening movement is startlingly practical: check the doorways for enemies before entering; drink your share but talk sense or be silent; the fool who knows nothing lies awake all night worrying and wakes exhausted with everything as bad as before. It is the ethics of a cold, hospitable, dangerous world — be wary, be generous, be remembered.

Its most quoted stanza is the Norse creed in eight lines: 'Cattle die, kinsmen die, you yourself will die; but the fame of a dead man never dies, for the one who earns it well.' Later movements turn personal — Odin's rueful confessions of love gone wrong with Billingr's girl and Gunnlöð, whom he seduced and robbed for the Mead of Poetry — and then vertiginous: the Rúnatal, where the god describes hanging 'nine long nights, wounded with a spear, given to Odin, myself to myself, on that tree of which no man knows where its roots run,' screaming as he seized the runes. The poem ends with a catalogue of eighteen spells he will not fully teach.

Pragmatic table-manners and self-crucifixion for secret knowledge, in one poem, in one voice: nothing else states so completely what kind of god Odin is.

## What is Hávamál?

Hávamál sits second in the Codex Regius, immediately after [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) — the compiler's one-two punch: first the shape of the cosmos, then how to live in it. At 164 stanzas it is by far the longest poem in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), and it is almost certainly not one poem at all but several, of different ages and characters, stitched together at some stage of transmission under the unifying voice of Hávi, "the High One" — [Odin](/gods/odin). The stitching shows, and scholars have mapped the seams for generations; but the compilation has its own logic, moving from the wisdom anyone needs, through the wisdom experience costs, to the wisdom only a god can seize.

Most of the poem runs in ljóðaháttr, the chanting "song metre" of Norse wisdom and magic — an asymmetrical stanza that lands each thought with a short, heavily stressed final line, built for memorability. Hávamál was made to be carried in heads, and it was: no other Eddic poem is so widely quoted, tattooed, and lived by a thousand years later.

## The movements of the poem

**The Gestaþáttr, or "guest's section" (roughly stanzas 1–80).** The famous part: proverbial wisdom for a traveller in a world of open halls and hidden knives. Look through every doorway before you enter. A guest needs water, a towel, and a welcome. Drink your ale, but know that "the more a man drinks, the less he knows." Don't cling to the mead-bench telling everyone your troubles. A coward may hope to live forever if he avoids every fight, but old age gives him no truce. Be a friend to your friend and repay gifts with gifts; laugh with the false-hearted friend and pay him lie for lie. The wisdom is unheroic, canny, weather-eyed — closer to a farmer's almanac than a warrior's code — and that is its fascination: this is the day-to-day morality of the Viking Age speaking in its own voice, without a Christian filter and without romance.

Threaded through it are the stanzas everyone eventually meets. On worry: the fool lies awake all night and wakes worn out, "and everything is as bad as it was." On moderation of wisdom itself: know enough, but not too much, for "a wise man's heart is seldom glad." On the middle way of possessions: better a living man than a corpse — "the halt can ride a horse, the handless drive herds; the deaf can fight and win; better blind than burned: a dead man is of no use to anyone." And the creed: cattle die, kinsmen die, you yourself will die; only the dómr — the judgment, the renown — of a dead man endures. Two consecutive stanzas hammer it twice, the second naming what never dies as the reputation each one earns. In a religion without a promised heaven for ordinary people, memory is the afterlife, and these eight lines are its scripture.

**Odin's love-lessons (roughly stanzas 84–110).** The voice becomes personal and rueful. Trust no maiden's words, the speaker warns — and then, with disarming fairness, admits men lie to women just as smoothly. He tells two stories on himself: how Billingr's girl outwitted him utterly, leaving the god of victories mocked by a whole household with a bitch tied to the bedpost where the woman should have been; and how he bored through the mountain, seduced [Gunnlöð](/giants/gunnlod) in her father's hall, and stole the Mead of Poetry from Suttungr — repaying her love with betrayal, "he beguiled the girl and left her weeping." The stanzas are extraordinary: a god confessing that wisdom about love is bought only with disgrace, his and others'.

**Loddfáfnismál (roughly stanzas 111–137).** A run of counsels addressed to one Loddfáfnir, each opening with the same incantatory formula — "I advise you, Loddfáfnir, take this advice; it will help you if you heed it." The advice is homely and humane: don't rise at night except to guard or to relieve yourself; cherish your friends and visit them often, for a path unwalked grows over with brushwood and high grass; never mock a guest or a wanderer; be kind to the ones you trust.

**Rúnatal (stanzas 138–145).** Then the floor drops. Without warning the poem opens onto the tradition's most vertiginous image: "I know that I hung on the windswept tree nine long nights, wounded with a spear, given to Odin — myself to myself — on that tree of which no one knows where its roots run. They gave me no bread nor drink from the horn; I peered downward; I took up the runes — screaming I took them — and fell back from there." The tree is understood to be [Yggdrasil](/realms/yggdrasil); the sacrifice is the god hanged and spear-marked in his own cult's manner, offered to himself, dying into knowledge. This is how Odin won the [runes](/concepts/runes) — not letters merely, but the carved powers under reality. The resemblance to the Crucifixion has been noticed since scholarship began, and argued both ways: influence, or independent image of the sacrificed god. The poem does not care; it moves straight on to practicalities — do you know how to carve? how to read? how to stain, test, ask, offer?

**Ljóðatal (stanzas 146–163).** The finale is a numbered catalogue of eighteen spells Odin knows and will not fully share: a charm for help in grief and sorrow; one to blunt enemies' blades; one to break fetters; one to catch an arrow in flight; one to still storms; one to make a hanged man walk and talk with him; charms against fire, hatred, and witch-riders; and last, one "I will never tell to maid or man's wife" — the poem ending, like its god, on a secret kept. A closing stanza seals the whole: now the sayings of the High One are spoken in the High One's hall — "hail to the one who spoke them, hail to the one who knows them."

## What does Hávamál actually teach?

Read whole, the poem's ethic is coherent and bracing. It assumes a dangerous world and refuses both despair and bravado. Its cardinal virtues are watchfulness, moderation, loyalty, generosity, and the long game of reputation; its cardinal sins are folly, faithlessness, and the babbling of fools. It is strikingly unmystical about happiness — a fire, a friend, a full stomach, "a house of your own, however small" — and utterly unsentimental about death, which it answers not with comfort but with the counter-asset of fame. And then, in the Rúnatal, it shows the price of the deeper wisdom: everything. The same poem that tells you to check the doorway tells you the god of wisdom hanged himself on the world-tree to learn what is behind doorways. That double register — kitchen-table and gallows-tree — is Hávamál's signature, and Odin's.

## Reliability and caveats

Hávamál survives complete in exactly one manuscript: the Codex Regius, written c. 1270 by Christian Icelanders. The gnomic stanzas feel old — much of the Gestaþáttr could plausibly be Viking Age — but individual parts cannot be securely dated, and the compilation as we have it may owe its shape to the thirteenth century. The counsel it preserves is proverbial wisdom of a broadly heathen society, but readers should be wary of treating any single stanza as "the Viking code": this is an anthology with seams, filtered through generations of oral carriers and at least one Christian pen. That said, nothing about its worldview has been Christianized in any obvious way — its silence about sin, salvation, and mercy is one of the best arguments for its genuine antiquity.

## Reading it today

Hávamál is included in every complete Poetic Edda — Carolyne Larrington's and Jackson Crawford's translations are the most accessible modern versions (Crawford has also published a popular stand-alone rendering), with Hollander, Bellows, and Orchard offering other complete texts. It is the ideal first Eddic poem for readers who want the Norse mind rather than the Norse plot: no background required, and at least a dozen stanzas will follow you home.

## FAQ

**What does "Hávamál" mean?**
"Sayings of the High One" — Hávi, "the High One," is a name of Odin. The poem presents itself as Odin's own spoken wisdom, delivered in his hall.

**What is the most famous quote from Hávamál?**
Stanzas 76–77: "Cattle die, kinsmen die, you yourself will die; but the fame of a dead man never dies, for the one who earns it well." It is the most quoted statement of the Norse belief that reputation is the only immortality.

**Is Hávamál really the words of Odin?**
It is a poem voiced as Odin, composed and transmitted by human poets — not scripture in any historical sense. The Odin persona unifies what is almost certainly a compilation of several originally separate wisdom poems.

**What is the Rúnatal?**
Stanzas 138–145 of Hávamál, in which Odin describes hanging nine nights on the windswept tree, wounded by a spear and sacrificed "myself to myself," to seize the runes. It is the central myth of how runic knowledge entered the world, and one of the most discussed passages in Norse literature.

**Was Hávamál influenced by Christianity?**
The question is most alive around the Rúnatal, whose hanged, spear-pierced god inevitably recalls the Crucifixion; scholars have argued both for Christian influence and for an independent pagan image tied to Odin's historical cult of hanged and spear-marked sacrifices. The practical wisdom sections, by contrast, show no obvious Christian coloring at all.
