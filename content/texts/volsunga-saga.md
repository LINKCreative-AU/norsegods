Völsunga saga stitches the whole heroic matter of the North into one prose arc: Odin seeding the Völsung line, Sigmundr and the sword from the tree, Sigurðr and the dragon, Brynhildr and the fire-wall, the murder, the burnings, Guðrún's marriages and revenges, down to the last doomed sons. Its unknown Icelandic author worked around 1260, paraphrasing heroic poems of the Edda — including some from pages of the Codex Regius now physically lost, making the saga our only witness to their contents.

Its reach is absurd for an anonymous book: Wagner built the Ring cycle largely from it; William Morris called his translation 'the great story of the North, which should be to all our race what the Tale of Troy was to the Greeks'; and Tolkien — who wrote his own version in verse — mined it for the broken sword reforged, the dragon on the hoard, the ring that ruins its owners. Anyone who has consumed fantasy in the last century has been downstream of this saga, usually without knowing the river's name.

## What is Völsunga saga?

Völsunga saga belongs to the genre Icelanders called fornaldarsögur — "sagas of ancient times," set in the legendary past before Iceland's settlement. But it stands apart from its genre-mates in ambition: its anonymous author set out to convert the entire heroic half of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) — a jumble of magnificent, fragmentary, mutually contradictory poems — into one continuous prose epic spanning five generations. The method is visible on every page: the author paraphrases the old lays, quotes stanzas outright when the poetry is too good to lose, smooths contradictions between variant versions, and bridges gaps with connecting narrative. The result is the closest thing the North produced to a prose Iliad: the full tragic cycle of the Völsungs and the Gjúkungs (Niflungs), from divine origin to annihilation.

The story it tells was already ancient. Its deepest layer reaches back to the fifth-century wreck of Migration Age Europe — Attila the Hun (Atli), the Burgundian king Gundahari (Gunnarr) destroyed by Huns in 437, the shadowy fame of Sigurd the dragon-slayer — history smelted into legend and carried north, where it fused with native myth until gods and Rhine-gold and valkyries were all one story. The same matter, developed separately in Germany, became the Nibelungenlied; the saga is its northern sibling, wilder, older in feel, and with the gods still in it.

## The story, movement by movement

**The Völsungs.** [Odin](/gods/odin) seeds the line and haunts it. The dynasty's founder Völsungr builds his hall around a living tree, and at a wedding-feast a one-eyed stranger in a hooded cloak strides in and drives a sword into the trunk: whoever draws it may keep it. Only Sigmundr can. The gift is the usual Odinic gift — glory with ruin in its sheath. Betrayal by his sister Signý's husband destroys Völsungr and his sons; Sigmundr survives chained in the forest, and with Signý's terrible collusion (she trades shapes with a witch to beget, by her own brother, a child hard enough for revenge) raises Sinfjötli, werewolf-seasons in the woods and all, to burn the betrayer in his hall. Late in life Sigmundr falls in battle when the one-eyed stranger returns and shatters the wonder-sword with his spear: Odin collecting what he lent. Sigmundr dies refusing healing, and asks that the shards be kept for the son in his wife's womb.

**Sigurd and the dragon.** That son is [Sigurd](/heroes/sigurd), raised at a foreign court with the malicious, brilliant smith Reginn as his foster-father. Reginn tells him the tale of the gold: how Odin, [Loki](/gods/loki), and Hœnir killed an otter that was Reginn's shape-shifted brother; how the ransom was paid in gold extorted from the dwarf Andvari, who cursed the hoard — and above all the ring [Andvaranaut](/artifacts/andvaranaut) — to destroy every owner; and how Reginn's other brother Fáfnir murdered their father for it and became a dragon on a heath to brood over the treasure. Reginn reforges the shards of Sigmundr's sword into Gramr, so keen it cleaves an anvil, and Sigurd — after first avenging his father, priorities being priorities — digs a pit in the dragon's track and drives the blade home from below. Dying, [Fáfnir](/creatures/fafnir) interrogates his killer with cold courtesy and promises the gold will be his death. Roasting the dragon's heart for Reginn, Sigurd burns his thumb, tastes the blood, and understands the speech of birds — who tell him Reginn is planning treachery. Sigurd beheads the smith, eats of the heart, loads the hoard on his horse Grani (of [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir)'s line, chosen with a disguised Odin's help), and rides away as the most famous man in the North.

**Brynhild.** On Hindarfjall, Sigurd finds a fortress of shields ringed with fire, and inside it an armored sleeper — [Brynhild](/heroes/brynhild), a valkyrie pierced with a sleep-thorn by Odin for giving victory against his decree. He cuts the mail from her; she wakes, teaches him rune-wisdom and counsel, and they swear to marry. It is the still point of the whole cycle, and it lasts a page. At the court of the Gjúkungs on the Rhine, queen Grímhildr serves Sigurd an ale of forgetting; Brynhild vanishes from his mind; he marries [Gudrun](/heroes/gudrun), swears blood-brotherhood with her brothers Gunnarr and Högni, and then — the tradition's cruelest scene — rides through Brynhild's fire in Gunnarr's shape, wooing his own beloved for another man, and lies three nights beside her with a drawn sword between them. The two queens quarrel in the river over precedence; Gudrun, stung, reveals the trick and shows the ring as proof. Brynhild's grief curdles into something annihilating: she goads Gunnarr until the brothers arrange Sigurd's murder — stabbed in his bed (in the saga's version) by their youngest brother, who was never sworn. Brynhild laughs once at Gudrun's wail, then tells the truth at last, prophesies everything to come, and runs herself through to burn on Sigurd's pyre, the drawn sword laid between them again.

**Gudrun's revenges.** The cursed gold passes to her brothers, and the saga's last movement grinds the survivors. Gudrun, dosed again with forgetting, is married to Atli the Hun, who invites her brothers to his court for the hoard's sake. They sink the gold in the Rhine before riding, knowingly, into the trap. Högni's heart is cut out; Gunnarr, asked again for the treasure's hiding place, answers that now only he knows it, and dies in the snake-pit playing a harp with his toes. Gudrun's vengeance on Atli is the saga at its most pitiless: she kills their two sons, serves them to their father at the funeral feast, tells him, and burns the hall over him. Even then the doom is not spent — her daughter Svanhildr is trampled by horses on a slandered charge, and Gudrun's last sons die avenging her, stoned at Jörmunrekkr's court when iron will not bite them, taunted at the end by a one-eyed old man who advises the stoning. Odin opens the saga and Odin closes it.

## Manuscript and sources

The saga survives complete in exactly one medieval manuscript: NKS 1824 b 4to in Copenhagen, written in Iceland around 1400, where it runs directly into Ragnars saga loðbrókar — the legendary Viking Ragnarr "Shaggy-breeches" being married, in tradition, to Áslaug, a daughter of Sigurd and Brynhild, so that the two sagas form one dynastic continuum. The composition itself is dated on internal and literary grounds to the mid-thirteenth century, around 1260, by an unknown Icelander.

Its supreme documentary value is an accident of damage. The Codex Regius of the Poetic Edda is missing a gathering of eight leaves — the "great lacuna" — which contained the poems covering Sigurd's youth with Brynhild and the engineering of the betrayal. The saga's author read those pages before they were lost and paraphrased them. For that stretch of the cycle, Völsunga saga is not a retelling of surviving poems but the sole surviving witness to vanished ones: a prose photograph of a burned original.

## The afterlife: Wagner, Morris, Tolkien

No anonymous medieval book has a louder modern echo. Richard Wagner drew the architecture of Der Ring des Nibelungen chiefly from Völsunga saga and the Eddas (far more than from the German Nibelungenlied): the cursed ring, the god's doomed scheme, the fearless hero, the fire-ringed sleeper. William Morris, with the Icelandic scholar Eiríkr Magnússon, translated the saga in 1870 and then re-poeticized it as the epic Sigurd the Volsung, calling the story "the great story of the North, which should be to all our race what the Tale of Troy was to the Greeks." And J.R.R. Tolkien taught the saga, wrote his own Eddic-style verse rendering (published posthumously as The Legend of Sigurd and Gudrún), and let its deep patterns — the broken sword reforged for the heir, the dragon coiled on cursed treasure, the golden ring that corrodes every bearer — become load-bearing beams of Middle-earth. Modern fantasy's dragon-and-ring furniture is Völsung furniture; most of its users have never read the source.

## How historical is any of it?

The kernel is real: Gunnarr is the historical Burgundian king Gundahari, destroyed with his kingdom by Hunnish forces in 437; Atli is Attila the Hun, who died in 453 (after a wedding night, tradition insisted — legend made his bride the avenging sister); Jörmunrekkr is the fourth-century Gothic king Ermanaric. Sigurd himself has no secure historical anchor, though candidates have been proposed for over a century. But the saga is not history and does not behave like it: it is myth about how glory, gold, and oath-breaking devour a family, told by an author working eight centuries after the events with poems, not archives, as sources. Readers should also remember the usual filter — a Christian Icelander of the 1260s shaping pagan-age material, compressing variant poems that disagreed (the Edda itself preserves conflicting accounts of where Sigurd was killed), and occasionally rationalizing what the verse left strange.

## Reading it today

Völsunga saga is short — an evening or two — and reads with startling speed. Jesse Byock's translation (The Saga of the Volsungs) is the standard modern entry point; R.G. Finch's edition-with-translation serves scholars; and the Morris–Magnússon Victorian version is freely available and has its own antique music. Read it before or alongside the heroic poems of the Poetic Edda: the saga supplies the map, the poems the lightning. And if you come from Wagner or Tolkien, prepare for the shock of the original's pace — what the Ring cycle does in fifteen hours, the saga does in four pages, twice.

## FAQ

**Who wrote Völsunga saga?**
No one knows. It was composed by an anonymous Icelander around 1260, working from older heroic poems — many preserved in the Poetic Edda, some now lost. Like most sagas, it survives without any author's name attached.

**Is Völsunga saga the same story as the Nibelungenlied?**
They are two branches of the same legend. The Middle High German Nibelungenlied (c. 1200) tells the Siegfried–Kriemhild version in a courtly, Christianized register with the gods removed; the Icelandic saga keeps the mythological frame — Odin, the valkyrie, the cursed ring — and the older, harsher ethics.

**Did Tolkien base The Lord of the Rings on Völsunga saga?**
Not as adaptation, but as deep inheritance. Tolkien knew the saga professionally, wrote his own verse version of its story, and drew on its core motifs — the reforged sword, the dragon's hoard, the ring that destroys its owners — while insisting his ring was his own. The family resemblance is unmistakable and well documented.

**Is Sigurd based on a real person?**
Unknown. The saga's political frame is genuinely historical — Gunnarr, Atli, and Jörmunrekkr correspond to the historical Gundahari, Attila, and Ermanaric — but Sigurd himself cannot be securely identified with any documented figure, despite many attempts.

**What is the curse in Völsunga saga?**
The dwarf Andvari, robbed of his gold by Loki to pay a blood-ransom, cursed the hoard and especially the ring Andvaranaut to be the death of every owner. The curse works through the whole saga: Fáfnir, Reginn, Sigurd, Brynhild, the Gjúkung brothers, and Atli all die in its train.
