Freyr has seen Gerðr from the forbidden high seat and stopped eating. His servant Skírnir ('the Shining') agrees to woo by proxy, taking the god's horse (fire-wall rated) and, disastrously for later events, the god's self-fighting sword as his fee. In Gymir's courts, the negotiation escalates like a textbook: eleven golden apples — refused; the ring Draupnir — refused, 'I lack no gold in my father's house'; the sword's edge — 'I will not be forced.'

Then Skírnir carves runes, and the poem becomes one of the most chilling passages in the Edda: a detailed curse of invisibility-madness-monstrousness — she will sit on the eagle's mound staring at Hel, food loathsome, wed to a three-headed thurs or no one, itching with unfulfillable desire, 'a spectacle to gods and men.' Gerðr yields: nine nights hence, the grove Barri. Skírnir rides home to a god who cannot bear the arithmetic ('long is one night; how shall I endure three?'), and the poem ends before the wedding. Whether it records a sacred marriage of sky and earth, a meditation on coercion wearing courtship's clothes, or both at once, is the oldest argument in Eddic scholarship — the poem, disquietingly, works either way.

## What is Skírnismál?

Skírnismál — in the Codex Regius it carries the title För Skírnis, "The Journey of Skírnir" — is one of the mythological poems of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), about 42 stanzas of ljóðaháttr with brief prose links, preserved in the Codex Regius and (partially) in AM 748 I a 4to. It is the tradition's one full-length love story involving a major god, and it is nothing like a love story. Structurally it is almost a play: short scenes, rapid dialogue, four speaking roles (five counting a herdsman and a serving-maid), and no narrator to tell you how to feel. Scholars have in fact long noted that Skírnismál is the most theatrical of all Eddic poems, and some have proposed it was performed — a script for a ritual drama — though this remains speculation.

The story it dramatizes was famous enough that [Snorri](/texts/prose-edda) retells it in Gylfaginning, in a gentler, abbreviated form — gifts and threats omitted, the curse gone entirely. Snorri's version is a courtship; the poem is something else.

## The story, scene by scene

**The lovesick god.** [Freyr](/gods/freyr), lord of harvests and the best of the Vanir, climbs one day into Hliðskjálf — [Odin](/gods/odin)'s high seat, from which all the worlds are visible, and by strong implication not his to use. Looking north into Jötunheimr, he sees a girl walk from her father's hall to her bower, and as she raises her arms, air and sea catch light from them. She is Gerðr, daughter of the giant Gymir. Freyr comes down sick with longing — the poem's frame reads the sight as punishment for the stolen view — and stops eating, stops drinking, stops speaking. His worried parents, [Njörðr](/gods/njord) and Skaði, send his servant and friend Skírnir ("the Shining One") to get the trouble out of him. Freyr confesses: the girl is dearer to him than any young man's beloved has ever been, and no one, god or elf, will permit the match.

**The fee and the journey.** Skírnir agrees to woo her by proxy — for a price. He asks for the horse that can carry him through the flickering wall of fire ringing Gymir's courts, and for Freyr's sword: the famous blade that fights by itself, of its own accord, if the one who wields it is wise. Freyr hands both over without a recorded flicker of hesitation. It is the costliest impulse purchase in the mythology: the sources remember that at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), Freyr will face the fire-giant Surtr weaponless and fall for the lack of that sword. The poem knows its audience knows this; the price tag hangs over every later stanza.

**The negotiation.** Past chained hounds and a laconic herdsman, through Gerðr's hall doors, and the bargaining begins with the poise of a saga heiress. Skírnir offers eleven apples of gold (whether ordinary treasure or the apples of youth is an old crux). Refused: her love cannot be bought, and while their lives last, she and Freyr will never be together. He offers [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir), the ring that drips eight equal rings every ninth night — and names its pedigree: it was burned with young [Baldr](/gods/baldr) on the pyre. Refused, with the great line of self-possession: "I lack no gold in my father's house." He shows the bright sword and threatens to cut her head from her neck. Refused: "I will not be forced" — she will not endure coercion for any man's love, and if it is battle Skírnir wants, her father will oblige him.

**The curse.** Gifts have failed and force has failed; Skírnir reaches for the third instrument, and the poem becomes the darkest sustained passage in the Edda. He will strike her with a taming-wand; he begins to carve [runes](/concepts/runes); and stanza by stanza he builds the life she will have if she refuses: set apart on the eagle's mound at the world's edge, staring toward [Hel](/realms/helheim); food more loathsome to her than the shining serpent is to men; a spectacle when she comes out, gaped at by all beings; madness, weeping, torment doubling; three-headed thurs for a husband, or no man at all; withering like a thistle at harvest's end; and desire itself turned to punishment — endless, unfulfillable craving among the frost-giants. He names the powers he invokes and the rune he cuts — "thurs" — and three staves more: lewdness, frenzy, unbearable need. And then, the sales-clause that completes the horror with courtesy: I can scrape off what I scraped on, if there is need.

**The yield, and the arithmetic of longing.** Gerðr breaks. She welcomes him — with a crystal cup of ancient mead, hospitality resumed at the exact moment resistance ends — and names the terms: nine nights hence, in the windless grove Barri, she will grant Freyr's desire. Skírnir rides home. Freyr, standing outside, cannot wait for the report, and on hearing it delivers the poem's last, strange, unforgettable stanza: one night is long; two are longer; how shall I bear three? A month has often seemed shorter than half such a wedding-eve. The poem ends there — before Barri, before the wedding, on a god counting nights. Whether that ending is romantic or appalling depends on everything you have just read, which is precisely the poem's design.

## Sacred marriage, or coercion in courtship's clothes?

The interpretive war over Skírnismál is the oldest in Eddic studies, and both camps hold real ground. The mythic-ritual reading sees a hierogamy — a sacred marriage: Freyr the fertility god of sun and rain united with Gerðr, whose name echoes garðr, the enclosed field; the frozen, resistant earth won for the sky in spring; the nine nights and the grove pointing to seasonal ritual. On this reading the "curse" is winter itself — barrenness described — and the poem preserves genuine cult material for the god whose worship at Uppsala Snorri's [Heimskringla](/texts/heimskringla) remembers. The literal reading takes the text at its word: a woman says no three times, is threatened with a sword, and is broken by a curse of madness, monstrous marriage, and weaponized desire; her final hospitality is the compliance of the defeated; and the poem's coldest touch is that the lovesick god never appears in Jötunheimr at all — the violence is subcontracted. Modern scholarship increasingly declines to choose, noting that the poem supports both registers simultaneously and may always have: fertility myth and coercion narrative, played on the same strings. The poem, disquietingly, works either way — and it works because of Gerðr, who for three magnificent stanzas is the most self-possessed voice in the mythology.

## How reliable is it?

The usual Eddic caveats apply, with extras. The poem's date is unknown — arguments have ranged from the tenth century to the Christian era, with no consensus — and the prose frame is likely the compiler's. Snorri's softened retelling shows the story existed in more than one register. And the ritual-drama theory, attractive as it is, rests on the poem's form rather than on any external evidence of performance. What is not in doubt is the poem's place in the larger structure: the loss of Freyr's sword is remembered against him at Ragnarök in both Eddas, making Skírnismál one of the few myths whose price is explicitly invoiced at the end of the world.

## Reading it today

Skírnismál is included in every complete Poetic Edda; Larrington's and Crawford's translations are the most accessible, with Orchard, Hollander, and Bellows also available. It reads in fifteen minutes and is best read twice in a row — once for the fairy-tale, once for what the fairy-tale is standing on.

## FAQ

**What is Skírnismál about?**
The god Freyr sees the giantess Gerðr from Odin's high seat and falls into lovesick despair; his servant Skírnir rides to Jötunheimr to woo her by proxy, offering golden apples and the ring Draupnir, then threatening her with a sword, and finally breaking her resistance with a rune-curse. She agrees to meet Freyr after nine nights in the grove Barri.

**Why did Freyr give away his sword?**
It was Skírnir's fee for undertaking the dangerous wooing — a blade that fought by itself, handed over in the fog of lovesickness. The loss is permanent and fatal: at Ragnarök Freyr must face the fire-giant Surtr without it, and falls.

**Is Skírnismál a love story?**
Only on its surface. Gerðr refuses gifts and defies threats, yielding only under a detailed curse of madness, monstrousness, and unfulfillable desire — which is why scholars have argued for two centuries over whether the poem is a sacred-marriage fertility myth, a cold study of coercion, or deliberately both.

**Who is Skírnir?**
Freyr's servant, messenger, and friend — his name means "the Shining One," and some scholars have read him as an aspect or emanation of the god himself. Elsewhere in the tradition he also runs errands for the Æsir, such as fetching the fetter Gleipnir from the dwarfs to bind Fenrir.

**What does Gerðr's name mean?**
It is usually connected to garðr, "enclosure, field, yard" — one pillar of the interpretation that reads her as the fenced, frozen earth wooed by the fertility god. The etymology is suggestive rather than conclusive, like most things about this poem.
