The Poetic Edda is not a book anyone wrote but a net that caught the old poems: some thirty-plus anonymous works in alliterative verse, composed across centuries of oral tradition and written down in Christian Iceland. Its core manuscript, the Codex Regius ('King's Book', GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270), spent centuries in obscurity before reaching the Danish king in 1662, and is now one of Iceland's national treasures.

The mythological half opens with Völuspá — the seeress's history of everything — and runs through Hávamál (Odin's wisdom and his rune-sacrifice), the riddle-duel Vafþrúðnismál, the cosmic gazetteer Grímnismál, the comedy of Þrymskviða, and the sacred roast-battle of Lokasenna. The heroic half carries the doom of the Völsungs and Niflungs: Sigurðr, Brynhildr, Guðrún, and the cursed gold — the matter that became the Nibelungenlied and, much later, Wagner and Tolkien's raw material.

Nearly everything anyone knows about Norse myth traces to this one manuscript and its relatives; Snorri's Prose Edda is largely a commentary on poems of this kind. If the Codex Regius had burned, Odin would today be a name with a spear and little else.

## What is the Poetic Edda?

Strictly speaking, "the Poetic Edda" is a modern editorial label for a body of anonymous Old Norse poems, most of them gathered in the Codex Regius, with a handful of related poems — [Baldrs draumar](/texts/baldrs-draumar), [Rígsþula](/texts/rigsthula), and others — surviving in other manuscripts and usually printed alongside them. The poems themselves are far older than the parchment. They circulated orally, recited and reshaped across Scandinavia and the Norse settlements, some perhaps for centuries before an Icelandic scribe finally fixed them in ink around 1270. Dating any individual poem is notoriously hard: scholars argue from metre, vocabulary, loanwords, and worldview, and estimates for a single poem can range from the ninth century to the thirteenth. What is certain is that the collection preserves pre-Christian mythology as poetry — dense, allusive, sometimes funny, sometimes terrifying — rather than as the tidy prose narrative most modern readers meet first.

The poems come in two great metres. Fornyrðislag, "old story metre," is the narrative workhorse — short alliterating half-lines marching in stanzas, the same family of verse as Beowulf. Ljóðaháttr, "song metre" or "chant metre," has an incantatory, asymmetrical swing and is the metre of wisdom and dialogue: [Hávamál](/texts/havamal), [Vafþrúðnismál](/texts/vafthrudnismal), [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna), and [Skírnismál](/texts/skirnismal) all use it. The metres are part of the meaning: when a poem shifts into ljóðaháttr, something is being taught, argued, or cursed.

## The Codex Regius: a manuscript history

The physical object at the centre of all this is a modest, darkened vellum book of 45 leaves, written by a single unknown Icelandic scribe around 1270, almost certainly copying from earlier written exemplars now lost. Nothing is known of its whereabouts for nearly four centuries. In 1643 it surfaced in the hands of Brynjólfur Sveinsson, bishop of Skálholt, who — following the scholarly fashion of his day — attributed the poems to the eleventh-century Icelandic priest Sæmundr the Wise. The attribution was wrong, but it stuck long enough that older editions call the collection "Sæmundar Edda." In 1662 the bishop sent the book to King Frederik III of Denmark, which is how it earned the name Codex Regius, "King's Book," and its shelfmark in the Royal Library in Copenhagen: GKS 2365 4to.

There it stayed for three centuries, surviving (unlike much else) the Copenhagen fire of 1728. In 1971 Denmark returned it to Iceland, where its arrival by naval vessel was a national event, broadcast live; it now rests in Reykjavík as one of the founding documents of Icelandic identity.

The manuscript carries one famous wound: the "great lacuna," a missing gathering of eight leaves in the heroic section, torn out or lost at some unknown date. Those pages contained the heart of the Sigurðr story — his time with Brynhildr and the betrayal that follows. Their content survives only because the anonymous author of [Völsunga saga](/texts/volsunga-saga) had read them and paraphrased them into prose around 1260. It is the clearest possible demonstration of how thin the thread is: one lost quire, and a central stretch of the North's greatest story exists only second-hand.

Two other manuscripts matter. AM 748 I a 4to, a fragment from the early fourteenth century, preserves several mythological poems including Baldrs draumar, which the Codex Regius lacks entirely. And Hauksbók, a learned Icelander's compilation from around 1300, contains its own significantly different version of [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) — proof that even the most famous poem existed in more than one shape.

## What's actually in it

The Codex Regius arranges its poems with visible editorial intelligence: mythology first, heroes second, and within each half a rough order of dignity and chronology.

**The mythological poems** begin with Völuspá, the seeress's prophecy spanning creation to [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) and beyond — placed first because it is the frame everything else fits inside. Then come the Odin poems: Hávamál, the great wisdom collection; Vafþrúðnismál, the head-wagering quiz with a giant; [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal), the tortured god's catalogue of the cosmos. Skírnismál tells the coercive wooing of Gerðr for [Freyr](/gods/freyr); Hárbarðsljóð pits [Thor](/gods/thor) against a jeering ferryman who is Odin in disguise; [Hymiskviða](/texts/hymiskvida) sends Thor fishing for the World Serpent; Lokasenna lets [Loki](/gods/loki) indict the whole pantheon at a feast; [Þrymskviða](/texts/thrymskvida) puts Thor in a bridal veil to recover his stolen hammer. Alvíssmál, in which Thor stalls a dwarf with questions until sunrise turns him to stone, closes the mythological sequence with a poem that is half riddle-game, half glossary of poetic synonyms.

**The heroic poems** open with the Helgi lays — valkyrie-loved warrior kings, love across death — and then commit to the long doom of the Völsungs: young [Sigurd](/heroes/sigurd) avenging his father, winning wisdom from the dragon's blood, waking the valkyrie on the fire-ringed mountain; then (after the lacuna) the murder of Sigurd, [Brynhild](/heroes/brynhild)'s terrifying laughter and suicide, and the long aftermath in which [Gudrun](/heroes/gudrun) is married off to Atli (Attila the Hun of legend), watches her brothers die in his snake-pit and hall, and takes a revenge that out-horrors everything before it. The final poems, Atlakviða among the oldest and hardest in the book, and the Greenlandic Atlamál, retell that catastrophe twice; Guðrúnarhvöt and Hamðismál follow the story down to its last doomed generation. Grief, gold, and obligation grind through these poems like ice.

## The most famous passages

Certain stanzas of the collection have escaped it entirely and live loose in world culture. Völuspá's vision of the end — "brothers will fight and kill each other... an axe-age, a sword-age, shields are riven, a wind-age, a wolf-age, before the world goes headlong" — is the most quoted Old Norse ever written. Hávamál's "Cattle die, kinsmen die, you yourself will die; but the fame of a dead man never dies" is the nearest thing the pagan North left to a creed, and its account of [Odin](/gods/odin) hanging nine nights on the windswept tree, "myself to myself," wounded with a spear to seize the [runes](/concepts/runes), is the religion's deepest image. Þrymskviða's cross-dressed Thor devouring eight salmon at his own bridal feast is its funniest. And the heroic poems gave European literature the dragon-slayer, the cursed treasure, and the ring that ruins whoever holds it.

## What is the difference between the Poetic Edda and the Prose Edda?

They are two different kinds of book that share a confusing name. The Poetic Edda is an anthology: anonymous traditional poems, raw and often obscure, preserved without explanation. The [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) is an authored work — Snorri Sturluson's handbook of mythology and poetics, written around 1220, which retells the myths in lucid prose and quotes poems of the Eddic kind as evidence. Snorri's book actually came first in name: "Edda" originally belonged to his treatise (its meaning is still debated — "great-grandmother," "poetics," and a derivation from Oddi, where Snorri was raised, all have defenders). When the Codex Regius surfaced in 1643 and turned out to contain the very poems Snorri quoted, scholars called it an Edda too. A practical rule: the Prose Edda tells you the stories; the Poetic Edda lets you hear the voices.

## How reliable is it?

Honestly: it is the best evidence there is, and it still demands care. Every poem passed through generations of oral transmission and was written down by Christians, two to three centuries after Iceland's conversion in the year 1000. Some poems (Völuspá above all) may already reflect the meeting of pagan and Christian imaginations; others may be late compositions in an antique style. The compiler selected, ordered, and added connecting prose. None of this makes the poems inauthentic — it makes them medieval documents about an older faith, not transcripts of it. Scholars therefore read the Edda alongside Snorri, [Saxo's Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum), skaldic verse, place-names, and archaeology, watching for where the witnesses agree.

## Which Edda should I read first?

Most readers do best starting with the Prose Edda's Gylfaginning, which delivers the mythology as connected story, then moving to the Poetic Edda with that map in hand. Within the Poetic Edda itself, Þrymskviða, Völuspá, and the first ninety stanzas of Hávamál are the friendliest doors in. The heroic poems reward a first pass through Völsunga saga, which supplies the plot the fragmented lays assume you already know.

## Reading it today

The Poetic Edda is widely available in strong modern English versions: Carolyne Larrington's translation is the standard readable complete text; Jackson Crawford's aims at plain contemporary English; Andy Orchard offers another complete scholarly rendering; and older versions by Henry Adams Bellows and Lee M. Hollander remain in print, Hollander's preserving much of the alliterative texture. Any of them will put the whole net of poems in your hands in an evening — the same net that caught the gods.

## FAQ

**Who wrote the Poetic Edda?**
No one knows, and in a sense no one did. The poems are anonymous products of oral tradition, composed by many poets across several centuries, and written down by an unnamed Icelandic scribe around 1270. The old attribution to Sæmundr the Wise is a seventeenth-century mistake.

**How old is the Poetic Edda?**
The manuscript dates to about 1270, but the poems are older — how much older varies poem by poem and is heavily debated. Some, like Völuspá, are commonly placed around the year 1000; others may reach back further in oral form or may be considerably later literary compositions.

**What does "Edda" mean?**
The honest answer is that nobody is certain. The name originally attached to Snorri Sturluson's prose treatise, and proposed explanations include an Old Norse word for "great-grandmother," a derivation from óðr ("poetry"), and a link to Oddi, the estate where Snorri was educated.

**Is the Poetic Edda a religious text?**
Not in the sense of scripture. It was never a canon or liturgy; it is poetry about the gods and heroes, preserved by Christians for its literary and antiquarian value. It is, however, the closest surviving window onto pre-Christian Norse belief.

**Where is the Codex Regius now?**
In Reykjavík, Iceland. Denmark returned the manuscript in 1971 after three centuries in the Royal Library in Copenhagen, and it is kept today as one of Iceland's greatest national treasures.
