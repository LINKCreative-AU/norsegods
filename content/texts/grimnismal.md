The frame is a wager between married gods: Odin and Frigg each fostered a king's son, and Frigg, losing, engineered a slander — her husband's protégé, king Geirröðr (not the giant), tortures guests. Odin went to check, as 'Grímnir,' the Masked One, and was seized and set between two fires for eight nights without food or drink. On the ninth, a boy's act of kindness — a full horn — opens the god's mouth, and what pours out is the mythology's most systematic download.

Grímnismál is why we can draw maps: it names the twelve halls of the gods and their owners, Valhalla's 540 doors and roof of shields, the goat and hart on its roof, Yggdrasil's harts and serpents and suffering, the rivers, the horses of day and night — the Norse cosmos as an inventory recited by its owner while he cooks. The poem ends with Odin unmasking through a cascade of his own names, and Geirröðr, leaping up too late, falling on his own sword. Snorri quarried the poem relentlessly; without these eight fire-lit nights, half of Gylfaginning would have nothing to cite.

## The frame story

The poem opens, unusually, with a substantial prose introduction — likely added by the compiler to make sense of a monologue that begins mid-ordeal. [Odin](/gods/odin) and [Frigg](/gods/frigg) once fostered two brothers, sons of a king: Frigg took Agnarr, Odin took Geirröðr, and Odin arranged (by a bit of quiet sabotage involving a boat and a shove offshore) for his boy to take the kingdom while Agnarr ended up in a cave with a giantess. Years later, gloating from the high seat Hliðskjálf, Odin needles his wife: my fosterling is a king; yours breeds children with a troll-woman. Frigg's counterstrike is elegant — she tells her husband that his beloved Geirröðr is a miser who tortures his guests, a slander so far as we know, and then sends her handmaid ahead to warn the king that a dangerous wizard is coming, recognizable because no dog will attack him. So when a blue-cloaked stranger calling himself Grímnir, "the Masked One," arrives and the dogs shy away, Geirröðr does exactly what his slandered reputation says: he seizes the guest and sets him between two fires to make him talk.

Eight nights the disguised god sits in the heat, given nothing. On the ninth, the king's ten-year-old son — named Agnarr, after the displaced uncle — brings the prisoner a full horn to drink, saying his father does wrong. That single act of hospitality is the hinge of the poem. The fire has crept close enough to singe the cloak on Odin's back; he speaks; and what comes out is a torrent.

## The download: what the poem contains

The body of Grímnismál — some fifty-plus stanzas in ljóðaháttr — is the mythology's great gazetteer, delivered by the being who owns the real estate. Grímnir begins with blessing for young Agnarr, then unrolls the sacred geography: the lands and halls of the gods, traditionally counted as twelve — Þrúðheimr where [Thor](/gods/thor) dwells; Ýdalir of Ullr; Valaskjálf; Sökkvabekkr where Odin and Sága drink from golden cups as the waves sound over; Glaðsheimr, where [Valhalla](/realms/valhalla) rises gold-bright; Þrymheimr, the mountain hall of the giant Þjazi now held by his daughter Skaði; Breiðablik, [Baldr](/gods/baldr)'s blessed ground where nothing unclean lies; Himinbjörg, where [Heimdall](/gods/heimdall) drinks good mead as watchman; Fólkvangr, where [Freyja](/gods/freyja) chooses half the slain each day — the other half to Odin; shining silver-roofed Glitnir, hall of the judge Forseti; Nóatún, [Njörðr](/gods/njord)'s harbor; and the brushwood wilds where Víðarr the avenger waits for his one appointed deed.

Then Valhalla in loving close-up: easy to recognize for those who come to Odin — a hall raftered with spears, roofed with shields, mail-coats strewn on the benches, a wolf hanging before the west door and an eagle above. Five hundred and forty doors, and through each eight hundred warriors will march abreast when they go to fight the wolf. The cook Andhrímnir boils the boar Sæhrímnir, who is whole again each evening; the goat Heiðrún on the roof fills the mead-vats; the hart Eikþyrnir drips from his antlers into the source of all rivers — and the rivers are then named, dozens of them, a cataract of names. The einherjar's daily economy, the valkyries who bear the ale-horns, the horses of day and night with dew and foam on their bits, the wolves that chase sun and moon, the making of the world from Ymir's flesh and skull and lashes — it is all here, stanza after stanza.

And [Yggdrasil](/realms/yggdrasil), in the tradition's most detailed portrait: three roots over [Hel](/realms/hel), the frost-giants, and mankind; the squirrel Ratatoskr running slander up and down between the eagle above and Níðhöggr below; four harts gnawing the shoots; more serpents beneath than any fool imagines — and the summarizing line that generations have loved: "the ash Yggdrasil suffers hardship more than men know."

## The unmasking

The final movement is one of the great scenes in Norse literature. The voice by the fires begins to name itself: Grímr and Gangleri, Herjan and Hjálmberi, Þekkr and Þriði, Óski, Ómi, Bileygr, Báleygr — name after name pouring out, the god's biography told entirely in aliases, each one a story compressed to a word (Masked, Wanderer, One-Eyed, Flame-Eyed, Spear-Lord). "By a single name I have never been known since I went among the peoples." Then the turn, straight at his host: you are drunk, Geirröðr; you have lost much when you lost my favor. I am Odin. Now, if you can — approach me. The prose coda finishes it in four lines: the king leaps up to pull the god from the fires, his sword slips from his lap, he stumbles, and the blade runs him through. Odin vanishes. Agnarr the boy rules long as king — the kindness of one full horn repaid with a kingdom, and the god's own fosterling discarded for failing the one test that matters in Odin's world: how you treat the stranger at your fire, because the stranger is sometimes the god.

## Why Grímnismál matters

Without this poem, the Norse cosmos would have almost no floor plan. It is the primary source for the gods' dwellings, for Valhalla's architecture and economy, for the fauna of Yggdrasil, and for dozens of Odin's names — and [Snorri](/texts/prose-edda) mined it relentlessly, quoting it more than almost any other poem when he built Gylfaginning's world-picture. It is also a theological statement disguised as a list: knowledge of sacred names and places is presented as power, revealed under ordeal, between fires, by a god who suffers to speak — a miniature of Odin's whole character, the same figure who hangs on the tree in [Hávamál](/texts/havamal) to seize the runes. Some scholars have read the frame as reflecting real initiation or sacrificial patterns; others see a purely literary device. Either way, the poem's information is not neutral data — it is revelation, staged as such.

## How reliable is it?

Grímnismál survives in the Codex Regius and the fragmentary AM 748 I a 4to, written down in the later thirteenth century; the poem itself is generally thought older, though — as with all Eddic verse — a firm date is impossible and estimates vary widely. Its lists were probably shaped partly by the needs of poets (catalogues of names and places were working reference material for skalds), which means some entries may be poetic elaboration rather than common belief; the tidy count of twelve divine dwellings looks suspiciously schematic to many scholars. And the prose frame is likely a later addition stitched around the monologue. None of this diminishes its standing: it remains the closest thing we have to the mythology's atlas, in the owner's voice.

## Reading it today

Grímnismál is included in every complete translation of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) — Larrington and Crawford are the usual modern choices, Hollander and Bellows the older standbys. Read it after [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) and alongside Gylfaginning, and the experience changes: you start recognizing exactly which stanzas Snorri had open in front of him.

## FAQ

**What does "Grímnismál" mean?**
"The Sayings of Grímnir" — Grímnir meaning "the Masked (or Hooded) One," one of Odin's many disguise-names. The poem is his monologue, delivered under torture while incognito.

**Why is Odin tortured in Grímnismál?**
Because of a wager between Odin and Frigg over their rival fosterlings. Frigg slanders Odin's protégé King Geirröðr as a torturer of guests, then warns the king that a sinister wizard is coming — so Geirröðr seizes the disguised Odin and sets him between two fires for eight nights to force him to speak.

**What information does Grímnismál give about Valhalla?**
More than any other source: a hall raftered with spears and roofed with shields, 540 doors each wide enough for 800 warriors, the boar Sæhrímnir cooked and renewed daily, the mead-goat Heiðrún on the roof, and the einherjar who will march out through those doors to fight the wolf at Ragnarök.

**How does Grímnismál end?**
Odin unmasks through a long cascade of his own names and tells Geirröðr he has lost the god's favor. The king leaps up to free him, drops his sword, stumbles, and dies on his own blade; the boy Agnarr, who showed the prisoner kindness, becomes king.

**Why is Grímnismál important for Norse mythology?**
It is the principal source for the cosmos's layout — the gods' twelve dwellings, Valhalla's design, Yggdrasil's creatures, the rivers, and dozens of Odin's names. Snorri Sturluson quoted it heavily in the Prose Edda; much of the standard picture of the Norse world comes from this single poem.
