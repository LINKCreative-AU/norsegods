Baldr has been dreaming his death, and the gods convene — then Odin saddles Sleipnir and rides down the Hel-road alone, past the blood-chested hound, to a seeress's grave east of the door. The poem is fourteen stanzas of necromancy procedural: he chants the corpse-waking spells, and the völva rises complaining — 'snowed on with snow, beaten with rain, soaked with dew; long was I dead' — and answers because compelled, each answer ending 'unwilling I told you; now I will be silent.'

The answers are the Baldr myth in brutal miniature: the benches of Hel are decked and the mead brewed — for Baldr; Höðr will throw; Váli, unborn, will avenge at one night old. Then the poem's famous ending: Odin asks one question too strange — about maidens weeping and throwing veils to the sky — and the seeress recognises her interrogator: 'You are not the man you claimed; you are Odin.' His retort names her mother of three monsters; she closes the interview with the last word of the dead everywhere: ride home proud, for no one will wake me again 'until Loki slips his bonds.' Foreknowledge, the poem's cold gift: Odin gets everything he asked for, and it helps nothing.

## What is Baldrs draumar?

Baldrs draumar — "Baldr's Dreams" — is the shortest of the major Eddic mythological poems: fourteen stanzas of fornyrðislag, a single scene, one journey and one interview. It is not in the Codex Regius at all; it survives solely in AM 748 I a 4to, the early fourteenth-century fragment that is the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda)'s most important supplementary manuscript. Editors print it among the Eddic poems because it is one in every sense that matters — metre, matter, and manner — and because it forms an obvious diptych with [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa): the same dramatic situation (Odin extracting fate-knowledge from a völva who would rather be left alone), compressed from cosmic panorama to a single graveside transaction. Several lines of the two poems echo each other closely, and scholars have long debated the direction of the borrowing.

Its position in the mythology is precise: it is the prologue to the tradition's central catastrophe. [Baldr](/gods/baldr), the shining son, has been having ominous dreams — heavy dreams, dreams about his own life — and in the Norse world dreams are data. The Æsir and Ásynjur meet in assembly to ask why. Then, characteristically, the collective deliberation ends with one god acting alone: [Odin](/gods/odin) saddles [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir) and rides down.

## The ride and the raising

The poem's middle is the tradition's best procedural account of necromancy — and of the geography of death. Odin rides the Hel-road down to Niflhel, and the poem gives us the checkpoint: a hound comes toward him out of [Hel](/realms/hel), its chest bloody, and bays long at the father of spells — the dog most readers identify with Garmr, the underworld's watchbeast. The god rides on until he reaches the high hall of Hel, then turns east of the door, to where he knows a völva's grave lies. There he chants the valgaldr — the corpse-waking spells, "the spells of the dead" — and looks toward the grave, compelling, until the seeress rises.

Her first stanza is one of the great complaints in Norse poetry, the voice of the disturbed dead everywhere: what man is this, unknown to me, who has made me travel this weary road? "I was snowed on with snow, beaten with rain, soaked with dew; long was I dead." The interview that follows runs on compulsion, not courtesy. Odin — calling himself Vegtamr, "Way-tamer" or "Wanderer," son of Valtamr — asks; she answers because the spells bind her to; and each answer slams shut with the poem's refrain: "unwilling I told you; now I will be silent." Each time, he forces one more question through the closing door.

## The interview: the Baldr myth in miniature

The questions and answers, in order, are the whole catastrophe pre-enacted. For whom are the benches strewn with rings, the platforms fairly flooded with gold? For Baldr, she answers — the mead is brewed, bright drink, a shield laid over the vat, and the Æsir are in despair. Hel is decked for a homecoming: the underworld has already set the table, which means the death is not a possibility but a scheduled event. Who will kill him? Höðr will carry the high glory-tree hither — he will be the bane of Baldr and take the life from Odin's son. Who will avenge? Rindr will bear Váli in the western halls; one night old, that son of Odin will fight — he will not wash his hands nor comb his hair until Baldr's slayer is borne to the pyre. The vengeance is answered before the crime has happened: prophecy has flattened the whole sequence — murder, mourning, retribution — into a single already-written page.

Then Odin asks one question too many, and the poem turns. Who are the maidens who will weep, and throw the corners of their veils to the sky? The question is deliberately riddling — its exact meaning is still argued (waves as the weeping daughters of the sea-giant is the most cited guess) — and it is not the kind of thing a mortal traveller asks. The völva's recognition is instant and cold: "You are not Vegtamr, as I thought; you are Odin, the ancient sacrifice-lord" — and his counter-strike matches her: you are no völva, no wise woman, but the mother of three monsters. (The taunt gestures toward the monstrous brood — the readers of [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna) will think of Loki's children, wolf and serpent and death-queen, though the poem leaves the identification open.) The mutual unmasking dissolves the séance, and she pronounces the closing formula over it, the last word of the dead in every tradition: ride home, Odin, and be proud of yourself; no one will come to visit me again — "until Loki slips free of his bonds and the doom of the gods, all-destroying, comes."

## Foreknowledge, the cold gift

The poem's power is in what it withholds: any suggestion that knowing will help. Odin performs the mythology's most extreme epistemic act — raising the dead at Hel's own doorstep — and receives complete, accurate answers: the victim, the killer, the avenger, the underworld's readiness. And none of it is usable. Höðr will throw regardless; Váli will avenge on schedule; the mead stays brewed. Norse fate does not trade in warnings, only in previews, and Odin's entire character — the eye given, the hanging endured, the dead compelled — is the pursuit of previews he cannot act on. Baldrs draumar states that tragedy in its purest, briefest form, which is why the little poem punches so far above its fourteen stanzas. It is also our clearest witness that the Baldr story's shape — beloved son, blind instrument, infant avenger — was fixed tradition, independent of [Snorri](/texts/prose-edda)'s famous prose telling with its mistletoe and weeping world; the poem never mentions the mistletoe at all, and never needs to.

## How reliable is it?

The single-manuscript survival (AM 748 I a 4to, c. 1300–1325) means no second witness checks the text. The poem's date is unknown; its close verbal relationship with Völuspá guarantees some dependency but not its direction — an old poem Völuspá drew on, or a later poem quarrying Völuspá, and scholarship has argued both. A few of its riddles (the veiled maidens, the völva's monstrous sons) may have had answers obvious to the original audience that are simply lost to us. As usual with Eddic verse: composed in an oral pagan tradition, preserved by Christian pens, and read best in triangulation — here with Völuspá, with Snorri's Gylfaginning, and with the Baldr material in [Saxo](/texts/gesta-danorum), whose radically different version proves how many shapes this myth lived in.

## Reading it today

Baldrs draumar is included in the standard complete translations of the Poetic Edda — Larrington, Crawford, Orchard, Hollander, Bellows — usually placed after the Codex Regius poems with the other manuscript strays. It is five minutes' reading and an ideal first taste of Eddic style: one ride, one raising, one recognition, and a door closing that stays closed until the end of the world.

## FAQ

**What is Baldrs draumar about?**
The god Baldr suffers ominous dreams of his own death, so Odin rides Sleipnir down to the gates of Hel and magically wakes a dead seeress. Compelled to answer, she reveals that Hel's hall is already decked for Baldr, that Höðr will kill him, and that a son of Odin named Váli, one night old, will avenge him — then recognizes her questioner as Odin and refuses all future visits until Ragnarök.

**Why isn't Baldrs draumar in the Codex Regius?**
Unknown — the Codex Regius compiler either lacked the poem or chose not to include it. It survives only in the fragment AM 748 I a 4to, the one medieval manuscript besides the Codex Regius to preserve a collection of Eddic mythological poems.

**How does the seeress recognize Odin?**
His final question — about mysterious maidens who weep and throw their veils to the sky — is too strange and too knowing for the mortal wanderer he claims to be. The riddling question betrays the riddle-master; she answers only, "You are not Vegtamr, as I thought; you are Odin."

**Does Baldrs draumar mention the mistletoe?**
No. The poem names Höðr as the slayer and Váli as the avenger but says nothing of mistletoe or of Loki's guidance of the throw — details familiar from Snorri's Prose Edda. That silence makes the poem valuable independent evidence for the older shape of the Baldr myth.

**What is the relationship between Baldrs draumar and Völuspá?**
The two poems share the same dramatic engine — Odin extracting prophecy from a reluctant völva — and several closely similar lines, so a direct connection is certain. Which poem borrowed from which remains an open scholarly question.
