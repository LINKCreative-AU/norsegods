Formally, Lokasenna is a senna — a flyting, the North's institutionalised insult-duel — staged at Ægir's sacred feast and conducted by the one guest who has read everyone's file. The poem's engine is that Loki fights fair, horribly: nearly every charge he levels (Odin's unmanly seiðr, Freyja's brother, Frigg's brothers-in-law, Njörðr's history as a hostage and worse, Týr's hand — 'my son Fenrir took it') is corroborated somewhere else in the corpus. The gods answer with status; he answers with facts; only Thor's arriving hammer ends the proceedings, and even then Loki leaves quoting the fire that will end everything.

What the poem is doing has been argued for two centuries: a pagan poet's satire? A Christian-era demolition of the old gods from inside? Or — the reading that has aged best — the mythology's own built-in tribunal, letting the pantheon's crimes be read into the record by its prosecutor before the sentence (his binding, in the prose coda) and the verdict (Ragnarök) arrive. No text does more damage to the gods, and none was more carefully preserved by the monks copying it. Everyone, in every century, wants to hear the roast.

## What is Lokasenna?

Lokasenna — "Loki's Quarrel" or "Loki's Flyting" — is a mythological poem of about 65 stanzas in ljóðaháttr, preserved only in the Codex Regius of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), framed by prose that sets the scene and, crucially, finishes the story. The senna it stages was a real cultural institution: the formalized exchange of insults, governed by convention, in which reputations were staked on wit and on the truth-value of the charges — related to the flyting traditions found across Germanic literature. Lokasenna is the form's supreme example precisely because it is played at the highest possible table: the accused are the gods, the venue is sacred, and the accuser is the one being who knows where every body is buried, having buried several of them himself.

## The setting

The sea-giant Ægir — brewer to the gods, whose hall the previous poem in the manuscript, [Hymiskviða](/texts/hymiskvida), spent its whole plot equipping with a cauldron — is hosting the Æsir at a feast. The hall is a place of declared peace (griðastaðr, a sanctuary), lit by shining gold instead of fire, with ale that serves itself. The prose header lands the first blow before a stanza is spoken: the gods praised Ægir's two servants, Fimafengr and Eldir — so [Loki](/gods/loki) killed Fimafengr on the spot. Driven out into the woods, he comes back. At the door he extracts from the servant Eldir what the gods are discussing inside (their weapons, their war-glory — and none of them has a friendly word for Loki), and then he walks in with a lawyer's opening: he is a thirsty traveller come a long way; will the gods grant him a seat and a drink, or send him away? He invokes an old blood-brotherhood oath — [Odin](/gods/odin) once swore never to drink unless Loki was served too. Odin, bound by his own word, orders a place made. It is the poem's first demonstration of its method: Loki's power here is not violence but the gods' own commitments, quoted back at them.

## The flyting, god by god

What follows is a systematic circuit of the hall, and its engine — the thing that makes the poem terrifying rather than merely scurrilous — is that Loki fights fair, horribly. The gods answer him with status ("you are mad," "be silent"); he answers them with citations, and the rest of the corpus notarizes his footnotes.

Bragi, god of poetry, calls him unwelcome; Loki calls him a bench-ornament, brave only in his seat. Iðunn, soothing, is told she embraced her brother's slayer. Gefjun is silenced with a tale of favors sold; when Odin warns that Gefjun knows fate, Loki turns on the Allfather himself: you have given victory unjustly, and you practiced [seiðr](/concepts/seidr) on Samsey, beating a drum like a witch, "in the likeness of a wizardess — and that I call the mark of an argr man." Odin retorts that Loki spent eight winters beneath the earth as a milking cow and a woman, and bore children there. Both charges are unmanliness; both, the tradition confirms — Odin's shameful magic is attested elsewhere, and Loki genuinely bore [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir) as a mare. [Frigg](/gods/frigg) intervenes: what you two did in old days should stay unspoken. Loki informs the company that Frigg took her husband's brothers Vili and Vé to her embrace — and when she wishes aloud for a son to avenge the insult, he delivers the cruelest line in the poem: "I am the reason you will never again see [Baldr](/gods/baldr) ride to the halls." It is the first open confession in the corpus of Loki's role in Baldr's death.

[Freyja](/gods/freyja) is charged with having been the lover of every god and elf in the hall, and — worse, per [Njörðr](/gods/njord)'s attempted defense — with her own brother, "when the gods caught you." Njörðr shrugs at women's love-lives and is reminded that he was sent east as a hostage, and that giantesses used him as a chamber-pot, and that he begot his splendid son on his own sister. "That was my reward when I was a hostage," Njörðr answers with strange dignity — the hostage-history is exactly what [Heimskringla](/texts/heimskringla) records. [Týr](/gods/tyr) rises to defend [Freyr](/gods/freyr) and is met with the poem's most economical brutality: you could never carry justice between two men — and your right hand, "my son [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) took it." Freyr himself, best of the bold riders, is asked where his sword went — sold for Gymir's daughter, as [Skírnismál](/texts/skirnismal) documents — and how exactly he plans to fight when Múspell's sons ride. Byggvir, Freyr's tiny servant, squeaks; Loki flicks him aside. [Heimdall](/gods/heimdall) is mocked for his fate — a wet back, standing watch forever. Skaði is reminded who led the killing of her father Þjazi; she promises him her sanctuaries' cold counsel, and he recalls her warmer hospitality once, "and that must be mentioned, if we are counting our shames." Sif brings him a cup, calls herself blameless; Loki names her one lover: himself. Every stanza is a deposition, and nearly every deposition can be checked.

## Thor arrives

Then the mountains rattle. Beyla, the last small voice, announces [Thor](/gods/thor), and the poem's final movement is a four-round exchange between the trickster and the thunderer that Loki cannot win and refuses to lose. Four times Thor threatens — "be silent, or my hammer will close your mouth," each threat escalating (your head off your neck; hurled east among the giants; every bone broken) — and four times Loki answers, needling him with his least heroic hours in the east: crouched in the thumb of a giant's glove, hero, and hardly like Thor then; the straps of Skrýmir's food-bag you could not open, starving. But the fourth threat lands differently, and Loki's exit concedes it with perfect pitch: I have spoken before the Æsir what my spirit urged; for you alone I will go — "because I know you strike." One prophecy over the shoulder as he goes: Ægir will never brew feast again; fire will play over everything you own. The word is Ragnarök's fire, and he means it as an appointment.

## The sentence

The prose coda finishes the story with saga-cold economy. Loki hides in a waterfall as a salmon; the gods take him; his sons Váli and Nari (Narfi) are set against each other, one turned to a wolf that tears the other, and Loki is bound with his own son's guts, which become iron. Skaði — whose father he boasted of killing, at whose threats he laughed — hangs the venom-serpent over his face. His wife Sigyn holds the basin; when she empties it, the venom drips, and his writhing is earthquakes; "and there he lies until [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok)." Trial, verdict, sentence: the poem plus its coda is the mythology's own judicial proceeding, with the punishment attached directly to the speech.

## What is the poem doing?

Two centuries of argument have produced three main answers. A late-pagan satire, teasing gods the poet still half-owned, in a tradition that had always allowed laughter at the divine? A Christian-era demolition — the old pantheon dismantled from inside, its scandals read aloud by a poet who no longer feared it? Or the reading that has aged best: the mythology's built-in tribunal, in which the tradition itself, through its licensed prosecutor, enters the gods' crimes into the record — the oath-breakings, the unmanly magic, the incests, the seductions — before the sentence (Loki's binding) and the final verdict (Ragnarök) arrive. On this reading the poem is not an attack on the mythology but an organ of it: the system auditing itself, out loud, at a feast. The dating is correspondingly open — arguments run from the tenth century to the thirteenth — and the poem works, as its best readers have noticed, in every one of those frames.

## Reading it today

Lokasenna is in every complete Poetic Edda; Larrington's and Crawford's versions carry the insult-rhythms well, and Orchard, Hollander, and Bellows are also available. Read [Þrymskviða](/texts/thrymskvida) before it for the tradition's affectionate comedy, then this for the comedy with teeth — and keep a finger in the other poems, because half the pleasure is checking Loki's receipts.

## FAQ

**What is Lokasenna about?**
Loki crashes a sacred feast held by the sea-giant Ægir and insults the assembled gods one by one — Odin, Frigg, Freyja, Njörðr, Týr, Freyr, Heimdall, Skaði, Sif — until Thor arrives and threatens him with Mjölnir. A prose epilogue describes Loki's capture and binding with a serpent dripping venom over his face until Ragnarök.

**Are Loki's insults in Lokasenna true?**
That is the poem's engine: nearly every charge is corroborated somewhere else in Norse literature — Odin's shameful seiðr-magic, Loki bearing Sleipnir, Njörðr's hostage history, Freyr's bartered sword, Týr's hand lost to Fenrir. The gods answer with threats and appeals to status; Loki answers with facts.

**What is a senna or flyting?**
A formalized insult-contest — a recognized verbal duel in Norse and wider Germanic culture, with conventions about challenge, boast, and accusation, in which the sting of a charge depended partly on its truth. Lokasenna is the most complete literary example, staged with the entire pantheon as targets.

**Does Loki confess to killing Baldr in Lokasenna?**
Effectively, yes — he tells Frigg, "I am the reason you will never again see Baldr ride to the halls." It is the tradition's most direct admission of his responsibility, and the prose coda's account of his binding follows the same punishment the Baldr myth assigns him.

**Why does Loki leave when Thor arrives?**
Loki himself says why, in his exit stanza: he has said everything his spirit urged and will yield "for you alone, because I know you strike." Words rule the senna, and Loki wins it on words; Thor's hammer simply belongs to a different game — and Loki leaves prophesying the fire of Ragnarök as his last word.
