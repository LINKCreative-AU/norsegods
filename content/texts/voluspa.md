Völuspá is the mythology's spine in sixty-odd stanzas. A völva — a seeress, speaking from beyond death — commands the hearing of 'all holy kindreds' and addresses Odin directly, unrolling everything: the yawning void, the building of the world, the first golden age and the three maidens who ended it, the first war, the death of Baldr, the bound wolf — and then, refrain by tolling refrain ('the fetter will break and the wolf run free'), the full sequence of Ragnarök, through fire and falling stars to the earth rising green from the sea a second time.

Twice she taunts the god who woke her: 'Do you know yet, or what?' — the Allfather paying for a preview of his own death. The poem was likely composed around 1000, by a poet who knew paganism from inside as Christianity arrived; some hear conversion-era resonance in its final stanzas ('the mighty one comes down from above'), and the argument has run for two centuries.

Snorri quotes it as his master-source for Gylfaginning. Its stanzas — 'brothers will fight and kill each other... an axe-age, a sword-age, shields are riven, a wind-age, a wolf-age, before the world goes headlong' — remain the most quoted lines in all Old Norse.

## What is Völuspá about?

The title means "the prophecy (spá) of the völva," and the poem is exactly that: a single unbroken visionary monologue, spoken by a seeress whom [Odin](/gods/odin) has raised — or paid, or compelled; the poem lets all three shimmer — to tell him what she knows. What she knows is everything. No other text in the tradition attempts what Völuspá attempts: total history, from before the first grain of sand to after the last battle, in about sixty stanzas of fornyrðislag so compressed that whole myths flash past in a half-line. It stands first in the Codex Regius because the compiler understood what it was: the frame within which every other poem in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) happens.

## The shape of the vision

The prophecy moves in great arcs, and its architecture rewards attention.

**Creation.** The seeress remembers giants who reared her "in former days," then reaches back further still: "there was neither sand nor sea nor cool waves; earth was nowhere, nor heaven above; a gap was there of gaping voids, and grass nowhere." Out of Ginnungagap, the sons of Burr lift the lands; sun, moon, and stars are assigned their paths; the gods meet in assembly to name the times of day. The dwarfs are catalogued in a long list-passage (the Dvergatal — the stanza-roll from which Tolkien lifted the names of his dwarves, and Gandalf's too). Then three gods find two trees on the shore, "capable of little," and breathe into them breath, spirit, and blood: Ask and Embla, the first humans.

**The golden age and its end.** The young gods play chess-like games with golden pieces in the meadows, lacking nothing — "until three came, giant-maidens, mighty in power, out of Jötunheim." The line is famously cryptic; whoever the maidens are, the world's innocence ends with them, and fate enters the machinery.

**The first war and the first death.** The seeress remembers the war between the [Æsir and the Vanir](/events/aesir-vanir-war) — the spearcast of Odin that opened it, the broken wall of [Asgard](/realms/asgard) — and the corruptions that followed: the burning of the witch Gullveig, oaths broken over the builder of the wall. She sees [Yggdrasil](/realms/yggdrasil), the ash over Urðr's well, and the three Norns who carve fate. She knows where Heimdall's hearing is hidden and where Odin's eye went; she has his pledge in her keeping. And she sees the wound at the myth's heart: the death of [Baldr](/gods/baldr) by the mistletoe, the vengeance, and [Loki](/gods/loki) bound beneath the earth with his wife holding the basin over his face.

**Ragnarök.** Then the drumbeat begins. An eagle-playing giantess in the Iron Wood; the hounds of Hel; the crowing of three cocks; and the refrain that tolls through the second half — "the fetter will break and the wolf run free." The seeress names the stations of the end in order: Heimdall's horn, Yggdrasil shuddering, the ship of the dead loosed, the fire-giant Surtr from the south, [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) swallowing Odin, Víðarr's vengeance, [Thor](/gods/thor) and the [World Serpent](/creatures/jormungandr) killing each other, the sun black, the stars gone, steam against heaven itself. The stanza before the catastrophe is the most famous in Old Norse: brothers killing brothers, kin-bonds broken, "an axe-age, a sword-age... a wind-age, a wolf-age, before the world goes headlong; no man will spare another."

**And after.** The poem does not end in the fire. The seeress sees the earth rise from the sea a second time, green; the surviving gods gather on the plain and find the golden game-pieces in the grass; fields grow unsown; Baldr comes back. A hall stands thatched with gold at Gimlé where the worthy will live in joy. Then — in the Codex Regius text — one last image out of nowhere: the dark dragon Níðhöggr flying up from below with corpses in his wings. "Now she must sink." The vision snaps shut on its own ambiguity.

## Who is speaking, and to whom?

The völva is one of the most arresting narrators in world literature: a professional seeress of the kind Viking Age society really employed, but dead, ancient beyond the gods themselves, and entirely unimpressed by her interrogator. She addresses Odin by his masks ("Valföðr"), reveals that she knows his most private forfeit — the eye in Mímir's well — and twice punctuates her revelations with the taunting refrain "vituð ér enn, eða hvat?" — "do you know yet, or what?" The dramatic situation mirrors [Baldrs draumar](/texts/baldrs-draumar), where Odin necromantically raises a seeress by Hel's gate, and the two poems are clearly related; in both, the god of wisdom must go begging to a dead woman for the one thing his wisdom cannot reach — the exact shape of the end.

## When was it composed?

The scholarly consensus, never unanimous, points to around the year 1000 — precisely the moment Iceland formally converted to Christianity. The poet commands the pagan material with an insider's fluency no later antiquarian could fake, yet the poem's architecture — a fall, a judgment, a purged and renewed world, "the mighty one who comes down from above" in a stanza found in Hauksbók — strikes many readers as shaped by at least an awareness of Christian eschatology. Every position has been argued for two centuries: a purely pagan poem; a pagan poem lightly retouched by Christian copyists; a conversion-era masterpiece written deliberately on the threshold, using each faith's light to see the other. The poem's power is bound up with that unresolvable doubleness.

## One poem, three versions

Völuspá survives in three witnesses that do not fully agree. The Codex Regius (c. 1270) gives the standard text of about 62 stanzas. Hauksbók (c. 1300) preserves a version with stanzas in a different order, some material missing, and some found nowhere else — including the explicitly messianic-sounding stanza about the mighty ruler coming from above. And Snorri's [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) quotes roughly half the poem piecemeal as the load-bearing evidence for Gylfaginning, sometimes with its own variants. Editors weave "the" Völuspá out of all three, which means the poem most people read is, at the margins, a modern reconstruction — a fact worth knowing, and a small lesson in how all Eddic texts reach us.

## Why does Völuspá matter so much?

Because it is the skeleton. Without Völuspá there is no ordered sequence from creation to [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — only scattered episodes. Snorri built Gylfaginning on it; every modern retelling builds on Snorri. Its images have colonized the wider culture: the Dvergatal's dwarf-names populate Middle-earth; its axe-age, sword-age stanza is quoted wherever civilizational dread needs a voice; its green world rising from the sea gives Norse myth the strange, un-Greek shape of an apocalypse with a morning after. And it preserves, better than any other single text, the tradition's emotional core: gods who know they are doomed, and a wisdom that consists of hearing the doom clearly.

## Reading it today

Völuspá opens every complete translation of the Poetic Edda — Carolyne Larrington's and Jackson Crawford's are the most widely read modern versions, with Andy Orchard, Lee M. Hollander, and Henry Adams Bellows also in circulation; Ursula Dronke's scholarly edition offers the deepest commentary for those who want to go further. It is short enough to read in twenty minutes and dense enough to repay twenty years; most readers find it gains enormously on a second pass, once the allusions have faces.

## FAQ

**What does "Völuspá" mean?**
"The prophecy of the völva" — a völva being a Norse seeress, a woman who practiced professional divination. The title compounds völva with spá, "prophecy."

**Is Völuspá pagan or Christian?**
Probably both, in the sense that it was most likely composed around the year 1000 by a poet steeped in paganism at the moment Christianity arrived in Iceland. Scholars have debated for two centuries how much of its fall-judgment-renewal shape and its "mighty one from above" stanza reflect Christian influence; there is no consensus, and the ambiguity may be original.

**What happens at the end of Völuspá?**
After Ragnarök destroys gods, sun, and stars, the seeress sees the earth rise green from the sea again: surviving gods return, Baldr comes back from the dead, fields grow unsown, and a golden hall awaits the righteous. The Codex Regius text then closes on the dragon Níðhöggr flying up with corpses — a deliberately unsettling final note.

**How long is Völuspá?**
About 62 stanzas in the Codex Regius version — roughly the length of a short modern poem sequence, readable in one sitting. The Hauksbók manuscript preserves a variant version with a different stanza count and order.

**Why does Odin consult the völva if he is the god of wisdom?**
Because in Norse myth even Odin's wisdom has limits, and knowledge of fate belongs especially to female seers and the Norns. Odin's whole character is the pursuit of exactly this forbidden knowledge — he trades an eye, hangs on the tree, and raises the dead to get it — and Völuspá shows him paying to hear the prophecy of his own death.
