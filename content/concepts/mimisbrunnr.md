Under the root of Yggdrasil that reaches toward the frost-giants lies the well where 'wisdom and intelligence are hidden.' Its keeper Mímir drinks from it every morning out of the Gjallarhorn, and is the wisest of beings because of it. Knowledge, in this cosmology, is a natural resource with one wellhead and one licensee.

Odin came to drink and was quoted a price: an eye. He paid — and Völuspá's seeress twists the knife by telling him she knows exactly where it is: 'in the famous well of Mímir, where Mímir drinks mead every morning from Valföðr's pledge.' The god of wisdom watches the world with one eye because the other is submerged in wisdom's source — depth perception traded, as it were, for depth.

## The well where wisdom is kept

Of [Yggdrasil's](/concepts/yggdrasil) three roots, Snorri sends one toward the frost-giants — 'where [Ginnungagap](/concepts/ginnungagap) once was' — and under that root lies Mímisbrunnr. The placement is the first lesson. Fate's well, [Urðarbrunnr](/concepts/urdarbrunnr), sits among the gods; the primal spring [Hvergelmir](/concepts/hvergelmir) boils in [Niflheim](/realms/niflheim); but *wisdom* is stored out toward [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim), in the direction of the giants — the old beings who were there before the gods and remember more than they do. In Norse myth the deepest knowledge is always located outside [Asgard](/realms/asgard): with dead seeresses, with ancient giants like Vafþrúðnir, with the head of Mímir. The gods hold power; memory lives at the frontier, and anyone who wants it must travel.

Snorri's description is compact and total: in the well 'wisdom and intelligence are hidden, and Mímir is the name of the well's owner. He is full of learning because he drinks from the well out of the horn Gjallarhorn.' Every morning, a drink of mead from wisdom's source — and the keeper is therefore the wisest of all beings. Note the vessel: the Gjallarhorn is elsewhere [Heimdallr's](/gods/heimdall) horn, the one whose blast will announce [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok); either two horns share the name or the tradition has tangled two functions of one great horn. Either way the detail gleams: the horn of the last warning doubles as wisdom's morning cup.

## What did Odin sacrifice for wisdom?

An eye — and the myth is the cleanest statement of the rule that governs everything [Odin](/gods/odin) does. Snorri tells it in one breath: 'Allfather came there and asked for a single drink from the well, but he did not get it until he laid his eye as a pledge.' No haggling is recorded, no hesitation. The greediest intelligence in the cosmos was quoted a price by a well-keeper, and paid it on the spot.

Völuspá delivers the poetic version with a seeress's cruelty. Speaking to Odin himself — who has come, as ever, asking to know things — she says: 'I know it all, Odin, where you hid your eye: in the famous well of Mímir. Mímir drinks mead every morning from Valföðr's pledge. Do you understand yet, or what?' The eye is not merely gone; it is *in use*. It lies at the bottom of the well, part of wisdom's stock, and the keeper drinks over it daily — 'Valföðr's pledge' has become a feature of the landscape. Some readers go further: an eye submerged in the seeing-water, watching from below, Odin's own down-payment now part of what makes the well wise.

The trade defines the god. One eye for one drink is a terrible exchange rate unless you understand what was bought: not facts but *sight of a different kind* — the wisdom that reads fate. Odin thereafter is 'the one-eyed,' Hárr, and every image of him — the brimmed hat shadowing the missing side, the single burning gaze that unsettles kings in the sagas — carries the receipt. He paid an eye here, a hanging on the tree for the [runes](/concepts/runes), his honour for [seiðr](/concepts/seidr); wisdom in the North is never given, only sold, and the seller sets the price. Depth perception traded for depth.

## Mímir: the head that kept advising

The well's keeper has one of the mythology's strangest careers. Mímir — his name related to memory itself — appears in Snorri's Ynglinga saga as the Æsir's wisest counsellor, sent to the Vanir as a hostage after the gods' war, together with the handsome but empty-headed Hœnir. When the Vanir discovered that Hœnir could answer nothing without Mímir at his side — 'let others decide,' he would say, whenever Mímir was absent — they felt cheated, and they beheaded Mímir and sent the head to Odin. Odin, characteristically, refused to waste the asset: he preserved the head with herbs so it would not rot, and 'spoke charms over it and gave it such power that it spoke to him, and told him many hidden things.'

Thereafter Mímir's head is Odin's standing oracle — and at the very edge of doom it is still on the payroll. When Heimdallr's horn sounds and the armies converge on [Vígríðr](/concepts/vigrid), Völuspá shows Odin in the final hour: he 'speaks with Mímir's head' before riding to face the wolf. The last act of the wisest of the gods, before the battle he has always known he loses, is one more consultation — intelligence-gathering to the literal end. Whether the head sits by the well, whether 'Mímir's well' and the beheaded hostage were originally one figure or two traditions fused, the sources do not settle; the poems also name Mímir's grove, Hoddmímir's wood, where the two human survivors of Ragnarök shelter. Around the name Mímir, everything is memory, storage, and survival.

## In the primary sources

**Völuspá** (Poetic Edda) carries the two great moments: the seeress's taunt about the hidden eye drunk over each morning (st. 28), and Odin's last consultation with Mímir's head as Ragnarök breaks (st. 46). Snorri's **Gylfaginning** (Prose Edda) locates the well under the giant-ward root, names Mímir as keeper, describes the morning drink from Gjallarhorn, and tells the eye-pledge plainly. **Ynglinga saga** (Heimskringla) preserves the hostage-exchange and beheading, and Sigrdrífumál alludes to the head's speech. The traditions do not perfectly align — a living well-keeper and a preserved talking head make an awkward single biography — and scholars generally treat the figure as a deep, partly submerged myth of memory personified, of which the surviving texts give us three overlapping fragments.

## FAQ

**What is Mímisbrunnr?**
The Well of Wisdom in Norse mythology, lying beneath the root of Yggdrasil that reaches toward the frost-giants. Wisdom and intelligence are hidden in its water; its keeper Mímir drinks from it every morning and is the wisest of beings.

**Why did Odin give up his eye?**
As the pledge Mímir demanded for a single drink from the well. Odin paid without recorded hesitation, and the eye remains in the well — Völuspá says Mímir drinks mead each morning 'from Valföðr's pledge.' The sacrifice is the model of the Norse rule that wisdom must always be bought.

**Which eye did Odin lose?**
The sources never say. Art and film choose freely; the Eddas record only that he laid 'his eye' in the well and was one-eyed ever after.

**Who is Mímir?**
The well's keeper and the wisest of beings — and, in Ynglinga saga, a hostage beheaded by the Vanir, whose head Odin preserved with herbs and charms so it could go on advising him. At the onset of Ragnarök, Odin's last act before riding to battle is to consult Mímir's head.

**Is Mímir a god or a giant?**
Unclear. He is counted among the Æsir's circle and sent as their hostage, but his name, his antiquity, and his placement toward Jötunheimr have led many scholars to see an older, giant-kin being of memory. The sources preserve fragments rather than a biography.
