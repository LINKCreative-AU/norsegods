The einherjar ('those who fight alone', or 'the unique warriors') are the elect of the Norse afterlife: warriors picked from the slain by the valkyries and mustered in Valhalla. Their routine never varies — arm at dawn, hew each other down in the courtyard, rise unwounded at dusk, ride home together, and drink the night through on pork from the deathless boar Sæhrímnir and mead from the goat Heiðrún. Death has already happened to them; what remains is practice.

Vafþrúðnismál calls them out plainly: they are training for the day the wolf comes. At Ragnarök they march out of Valhalla's five hundred and forty doors, eight hundred abreast through each, and fight beside the gods on Vígríðr — and lose, because the prophecy says so, and they know it, and they go anyway. The einherjar are the purest expression of the Norse ethic: the certainty of defeat is not a reason to stay home. Skaldic funeral poems like Eiríksmál show Odin explaining why he lets great kings die young: he needs them mustered, 'for none can know when the grey wolf comes.'

## Death as a career change

To become one of the einherjar, you first had to die well — in battle — and then be selected. The [valkyries](/concepts/valkyries), 'choosers of the slain,' rode the field on [Odin's](/gods/odin) behalf and picked, and their criteria were the Allfather's, not the fallen man's: he wanted the best, and he wanted them early. This is the mythology's cold explanation for the deaths of great warriors in their prime — Odin, the sagas complain, is faithless with victory, because from his perspective a brilliant king is worth more inside [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) than out. Nor did Odin get everyone: [Freyja](/gods/freyja) chose half the slain first, for her own field [Fólkvangr](/concepts/folkvangr), a division the einherjar legend tends to eclipse.

Once chosen, the dead man's existence simplifies into two activities, both excellent. Snorri describes the days: every morning, after they dress, the einherjar put on their war-gear, go out into the courtyard, and fight and fell each other — 'that is their sport.' When mealtime nears, the slain stand up, whole, and all ride back to the hall together and sit down to drink. No grudges survive the drill; men who hacked each other apart at noon pass the cup at night. Vafþrúðnismál confirms the picture in verse: the einherjar fight in Odin's courts every day, choose the slain, then 'sit reconciled together.' Practice, resurrection, fellowship — for men whose living trade was violence with permanent consequences, the removal of consequence is the paradise part.

## The provisioning of an immortal army

An army that trains forever must eat forever, and Grímnismál handles the logistics with visible pleasure. The cook Andhrímnir boils the boar **Sæhrímnir** in the cauldron Eldhrímnir, and each evening the boar is whole again — the best of meats, and inexhaustible. Mead comes from the goat **Heiðrún**, who stands on Valhalla's roof cropping the foliage of the tree Læraðr — most likely [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil) — and fills a vat so large that all the einherjar drink their fill. When the disguised Gylfi, in Snorri's telling, wonders whether Odin's host must be enormous, High agrees cheerfully: the host is vast, 'yet it will seem too few when the wolf comes.'

That sentence is the einherjar's whole existence in miniature. The numbers Grímnismál gives are staggering — 540 doors, 800 warriors through each abreast — and explicitly insufficient. Odin, who sits at the high seat giving his food to his wolves and drinking only wine, is not running a reward program. He is running a mobilization, and he knows the audit already: at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) the einherjar and the gods take the field on [Vígríðr](/concepts/vigrid) against [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir), [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), [Loki](/gods/loki) with the dead of Hel, and [Surtr's](/giants/surtr) fire-host — and the prophecy has published the result. The wolf still swallows Odin. The army assembled across all of human history buys, at most, a good death in good company.

## Why fight a battle you know is lost?

Because the alternative — not fighting it — was, to the Norse mind, the only real defeat. The einherjar embody the ethic that runs through everything from the [norns'](/concepts/norns) unappealable verdicts to the sagas' calm death-scenes: fate (ørlög) fixes the outcome, but the manner of meeting it belongs entirely to you. Eiríksmál, the funeral poem for Eiríkr Bloodaxe, stages the doctrine as theatre. A commotion approaches Valhalla; Odin orders the benches readied. The dead hero Sigmundr asks why Odin robbed such a great king of victory and life. Odin's answer is the whole theology in one line: 'Because none can know for certain when the grey wolf will come against the seat of the gods.' Great men are harvested young because the muster cannot wait. Hákonarmál, composed for Hákon the Good, plays the same scene with a wary king who keeps his armour on even inside the hall — a nice touch: trust in Odin was never the point; readiness was.

## In the primary sources

**Vafþrúðnismál** (Poetic Edda) states the einherjar's daily fighting and reconciliation as one of the lore-contest's answers. **Grímnismál** supplies the quartermaster's detail: Sæhrímnir and Andhrímnir, Heiðrún's mead, and the 540 doors with 800 abreast at the wolf-time. Snorri's **Gylfaginning** (Prose Edda) knits it into the connected account of the daily sport and the feast, and glosses the name: the 'einherjar' are Odin's adopted sons of battle. The skaldic memorial poems **Eiríksmál** and **Hákonarmál** (10th century) show the belief in ritual use — a dead king's arrival at the hall imagined as the supreme honour, with Odin's grim recruiting logic spoken aloud. The word itself likely means something like 'those who fight alone' or 'the singular warriors,' and it never refers to living men: einherjar are made only by death.

## FAQ

**What does 'einherjar' mean?**
The name is usually rendered 'those who fight alone' or 'the unique/singular warriors' — battle-dead elevated into a class of their own. It applies only to the slain warriors chosen for Valhalla, never to living fighters.

**What do the einherjar do all day?**
They fight. Every morning they arm and battle each other in Valhalla's courtyard, hewing one another down; at evening the slain rise unharmed and all ride back to feast together on the ever-renewed boar Sæhrímnir and mead from the goat Heiðrún. It is both sport and training.

**How many einherjar are there?**
Grímnismál implies an enormous host: Valhalla has 540 doors, and 800 warriors will march out of each abreast at Ragnarök. Snorri adds the essential caveat — however many there are, it will seem too few when the wolf comes.

**Do the einherjar win at Ragnarök?**
No, and they know it. They march out with the gods onto the plain Vígríðr and fall in the mutual destruction of the final battle — Odin himself is swallowed by Fenrir. Their willingness to fight a foreknown defeat is precisely what the Norse admired in them.

**Did all dead warriors become einherjar?**
No. The valkyries chose only some of the battle-slain for Odin, and Grímnismál says Freyja takes half the slain to her field Fólkvangr — choosing before Odin. The rest of the dead went to Hel or to other afterlives.
