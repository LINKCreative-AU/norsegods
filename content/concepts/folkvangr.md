Valhalla's fame has buried the mythology's most startling personnel fact: Odin gets seconds. Grímnismál is explicit — Fólkvangr is the ninth hall, 'and there Freyja arranges the choosing of seats in the hall; half the slain she chooses every day, and half has Odin.' The goddess of love picks first from the battlefield.

Her hall there is Sessrúmnir, 'seat-roomy.' What her half of the dead do — whether they fight and feast like the einherjar, or something gentler — no source says, and the silence has bred theories: a warrior's paradise for those who fought for love of home rather than glory, say some; an older war-goddess's due, say scholars. One saga heroine, refusing to outlive her husband, declares she will 'sup with Freyja tonight' — for at least some of the North, the desirable afterlife wore Brísingamen.

## The half the legend forgot

Everyone knows where dead Vikings go. That is precisely the problem: the [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) legend is so loud that it has all but erased the source that contradicts it — and the source is not obscure. Grímnismál, the same Poetic Edda poem that gives us Valhalla's spear-rafters and 540 doors, works through the dwellings of the gods one by one, and its ninth stanza belongs to [Freyja](/gods/freyja): 'Fólkvangr is the ninth, and there Freyja directs the seating in the hall. Half the slain she chooses every day, and half has Odin.'

Read it slowly, because every clause carries weight. Half the slain — not the leftovers, not the women, not some special category: half of the battle-dead, the same population the [valkyries](/concepts/valkyries) harvest. She chooses — the verb of agency the valkyries' own name is built on; Freyja is a chooser of the slain in her own right. Every day — this is standing policy, not a one-time grant. And in Snorri's retelling in Gylfaginning, an ordering appears that sharpens it further: wherever Freyja rides to battle, 'she has half the slain, and Odin half.' Snorri elsewhere makes the sequence explicit: she gets the first pick. The [Allfather's](/gods/odin) celebrated army of [einherjar](/concepts/einherjar) is assembled from the half of the fallen that the goddess of love did not take.

The name itself is martial: *Fólkvangr* means 'field of the host' or 'field of the people/army' — *fólk* in Old Norse leaning toward 'war-band' — so this is no meadow of repose by etymology. Her hall on that field is **Sessrúmnir**, 'seat-roomy' or 'rich in seats': a hall built, like Valhalla, to hold multitudes.

## Why does Freyja take the slain?

Because Freyja was never only the goddess of love — that is the modern flattening. The Freyja of the sources drives a chariot pulled by cats, weeps tears of gold, owns the necklace Brísingamen, and taught the Æsir [seiðr](/concepts/seidr), the sorcery of fate itself. She belongs to the [Vanir](/gods/freyr), the older family of powers over fertility, wealth, and — evidently — death. Love and war sharing one goddess is not a Norse eccentricity; it is one of the oldest patterns in religious history (Inanna and Ishtar ran the same portfolio millennia earlier), and many scholars read Fólkvangr as the surviving trace of Freyja's full ancient stature: a great goddess who received the dead, of whom the medieval sources preserve only fragments.

What her chosen dead *do* in Sessrúmnir, no text says — and the silence is genuinely total. No training regimen, no boar-feast, no muster at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok); the sources simply never follow the dead through Freyja's door. Into that silence, interpreters have poured theories: that Fólkvangr held the warriors who fought for love of home rather than glory; that it received women; that Freyja's half were those death-marked by love. All of these are modern guesswork — attractive, unfalsifiable, and unattested. One scholarly conjecture is worth noting for its charm: since *Sessrúmnir* ('roomy-benched') would also make a fine ship-name, some have wondered whether Freyja's hall was imagined as a ship in a field — resonant with Scandinavia's ship-burials and stone ship-settings, though this too is speculation.

## 'I shall sup with Freyja tonight'

One saga passage shows the belief alive in a human mouth, and it is a woman's. In Egils saga, the poet's daughter Þorgerðr, grieving and furious after her brother's death at sea, announces she will neither eat nor drink 'until I sup with Freyja' — declaring, in effect, that she means to die, and naming where death will take her. She is not a warrior and not battle-slain; she is a proud woman claiming Freyja as the hostess of her dead. The line is our best evidence that, for at least some of the North — and perhaps especially for women — the desirable afterlife was not Odin's barracks but Freyja's hall. (The saga, it should be said, ends the scene in life, not death: her vow is part of a stratagem to draw her father Egill out of his own death-fast and into composing his greatest poem. But the theology in her sentence is casual and confident, which is exactly what makes it valuable.)

## In the primary sources

The entire dossier is small enough to hold in one hand, which is itself the story. **Grímnismál** stanza 14 (Poetic Edda) gives Fólkvangr, Freyja's seat-arranging, and the daily half-and-half division. Snorri's **Gylfaginning** (Prose Edda) repeats and lightly systematizes: Freyja is the most glorious of the goddesses ('Ásynjur'), has her dwelling Fólkvangr, rides to battle and takes half the slain, and owns the hall Sessrúmnir. **Egils saga** contributes Þorgerðr's vow to sup with Freyja. That is essentially all — set against dozens of passages elaborating Valhalla. Whether the imbalance reflects Viking Age belief or the interests of the poets and of Snorri (whose sources ran heavily to Odin-centred court poetry) is a real question; more than a few scholars suspect Freyja's death-realm was once far more prominent than the surviving record lets us see.

## FAQ

**What is Fólkvangr?**
Fólkvangr ('field of the host') is the goddess Freyja's realm of the dead. According to Grímnismál and the Prose Edda, she chooses half of all those slain in battle every day — Odin receives only the other half, who become the einherjar of Valhalla. Her hall there is called Sessrúmnir, 'the roomy-seated.'

**Who goes to Fólkvangr instead of Valhalla?**
The sources never say how the split is decided — only that Freyja chooses her half first. Ideas that Fólkvangr received women, lovers, or defenders of home are modern speculation. The one personal claim in the sagas comes from a woman: Egill's daughter Þorgerðr vows to 'sup with Freyja' when she intends to die.

**What do the dead do in Fólkvangr?**
No surviving source describes it. Unlike Valhalla, with its daily battles and nightly feasts, Fólkvangr's interior life is a complete blank in the record — one of Norse mythology's most tantalizing silences.

**Why is Valhalla famous and Fólkvangr forgotten?**
The surviving poetry was composed largely for kings and warriors in Odin's honour, and later retellings followed the sources' emphasis. Fólkvangr rests on a single Eddic stanza plus Snorri's paraphrase — yet that stanza's claim is enormous: the goddess of love holds equal, and prior, rights over the battle-dead.
