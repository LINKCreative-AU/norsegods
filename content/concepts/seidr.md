Seiðr is the old magic of seeing and steering fate: entering trance to learn what is coming, and reaching into the weave to snag an enemy's luck, madden his mind, or still the sea. It came from the Vanir — Freyja taught it to the Æsir — and its human practitioners were above all the völur, staff-carrying seeresses who travelled between farms; the völva of Eiríks saga rauða is described down to her catskin gloves, brass-bound staff, and the high platform she prophesied from, with a choir singing the spirit-songs around her.

Its scandal is at the heart of the pantheon: seiðr was ergi — unmanly, shameful for men to practise — and Odin practises it anyway. Loki flings it at him in Lokasenna ('you beat on drums like a witch... that I call perversion'), and Saxo has Odin exiled for it. That the Allfather trades honour for power is perfectly in character: he traded an eye for wisdom and hung on a tree for runes; seiðr is the same bargain with shame as the currency. Archaeology keeps finding the völur: rich female graves across Scandinavia holding iron and bronze staffs, amulets, and henbane seeds.

## What is seiðr?

If the [runes](/concepts/runes) are the Norse magic of carving — fixed signs cut into things — seiðr is the magic of the loose thread. The Norse imagined fate, [ørlög](/concepts/orlog), as something laid down and woven; seiðr is the craft of reading that weave in trance, and, more dangerously, of reaching into it. The etymology is disputed (suggestions include 'binding' and connections to words for snares and cords), but the practice as the sources show it has two branches. The first is **divination**: the practitioner sits on a raised platform (*seiðhjallr*), assistants sing the spirit-songs (*varðlokur*), and in the trance that follows, spirits arrive and disclose what is coming — the fates of individuals, the end of a famine, the luck of a voyage. The second is **operative magic**, and its list in Snorri's Ynglinga saga is chilling in its practicality: by seiðr one could know men's fates and things not yet happened, but also deal out death, bad luck, and sickness, and take wit or strength from one man and give it to another.

The sagas dramatize the whole range. Seiðr raises storms against sailing enemies; it makes a hunted man's watchers sleepy; it gives a warrior's flesh proof against iron; it snares a young man's mind with lust or madness; it turns the land's spirits against an invader. It overlaps with other named crafts in the sources — *spá* (prophecy proper), *galdr* (chanted spells, sung in a distinct high shriek), and *útiseta*, 'sitting out' at night on mounds or crossroads to wake knowledge — but seiðr is the most powerful of them and the most feared, because its target is the fate-thread itself. Weapons attack the body; seiðr attacks your luck.

## Freyja, Odin, and the price of the craft

Seiðr's divine pedigree is precise, and it matters. Snorri says plainly that [Freyja](/gods/freyja) — daughter of [Njörðr](/gods/njord), of the [Vanir](/gods/freyr) — first taught the Æsir seiðr, 'as was the custom among the Vanir.' The magic of fate belongs originally to the fertility powers, the gods of growth, wealth, and the body, and it entered [Asgard](/realms/asgard) as Vanir technology after the war between the two divine families. Freyja, who takes half the slain to [Fólkvangr](/concepts/folkvangr) and weeps gold, is also the mythology's first sorcery teacher — love-goddess and fate-witch in one figure, which the Norse evidently found no contradiction at all.

Her star pupil is the scandal. Ynglinga saga says [Odin](/gods/odin) practised seiðr and got from it all its powers — and then adds the sentence on which the whole topic turns: 'this sorcery is attended by such great ergi that men thought it shameful to practise, and so the craft was taught to priestesses.' *Ergi* is the strongest insult Old Norse possessed — unmanliness, effeminacy, sexual shamefulness; calling a man *argr* was legal grounds for killing. Why seiðr carried this charge is debated: because the practitioner's body was entered and used by spirits; because trance and distaff-work were women's spheres; because steering fate by song rather than facing it with steel inverted the whole masculine ethic. Probably all of these at once.

And Odin does it anyway. In **Lokasenna**, [Loki](/gods/loki) airs it before all the gods: 'But you, they say, practised seiðr on Samsey, and beat on the drum like the völur do; in the likeness of a witch you went among mankind — and that I call the mark of ergi.' Saxo Grammaticus tells a version in which Odin's disgrace sees him driven from his seat. Yet the mythology never suggests he should have abstained. Odin pawned an eye at [Mímir's well](/concepts/mimisbrunnr); he hanged himself nine nights on [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil) for the runes; for seiðr the price was honour, and he paid that too. In the Norse economy of knowledge, everything true costs something you would rather keep — and the Allfather is defined by never once refusing the price.

## The völva: Norse seeresses at work

Because respectable men could not practise it, seiðr's human face is female: the **völva** (plural *völur*), 'staff-bearer,' the professional seeress. The single best portrait in all Norse literature is in **Eiríks saga rauða**, set in the Greenland colony during a famine around the year 1000. A seeress named Þorbjörg lítilvölva ('little-völva'), last survivor of nine sister seeresses, travels between farms prophesying. The saga dresses her for us with astonishing care: a blue cloak set with stones to the hem; a black lambskin hood lined with white catskin; catskin gloves; calfskin shoes; a pouch of charms at her belt; and in her hand the staff with a brass-bound knob set with stones. She is seated on a cushion stuffed with hens' feathers, fed a porridge of kid's milk and a dish of the hearts of every kind of animal on the farm, and the next evening mounts the high platform. A ring of women forms; a Christian woman, Guðríðr, reluctantly sings the varðlokur so beautifully that the spirits crowd in; and Þorbjörg tells the famine's end and the fates of those present, 'and little of what she said went unfulfilled.'

The scene shows the völva's real social position: honoured, paid, formally welcomed — and slightly dreaded, an outsider by profession. Other sources show the fear outweighing the honour: the sagas also record seiðr-workers, male and female, put to death by drowning or stoning when their craft was judged hostile, and Christian law codes later banned the whole complex — sitting out, prophecy, staff-magic — by name.

Archaeology has given the völur back their bodies. Across Scandinavia, a series of rich female graves — Fyrkat grave 4 in Denmark, the Oseberg ship burial's possible staff, finds from Birka and beyond — contain iron or bronze staffs of a distinctive form, along with amulets, unusual animal remains, and in the Fyrkat case a pouch of henbane seeds, a potent hallucinogen. The woman buried at Fyrkat, in the fortress of a king, was laid out with lavish goods and her staff: whatever the neighbours whispered, someone buried her like a person who mattered. The seeress of Völuspá — whom Odin himself raises and questions, and who speaks the whole history of the world from creation to [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — is the profession's mythic apotheosis: the greatest poem in the tradition is framed as a seiðr-session, with the Allfather as the paying client.

## In the primary sources

**Völuspá** (Poetic Edda) is spoken by a völva and names Heiðr, a seeress who did seiðr 'wherever she could' and was ever the delight of evil women — our oldest glimpse of the craft's ambivalence; the poem also shows Odin's relationship to seeresses as questioner and client. **Lokasenna** preserves the accusation of Odin's drum-beating seiðr and its ergi. Snorri's **Ynglinga saga** (Heimskringla) gives the fullest catalogue of seiðr's powers, Freyja's role as teacher, and the ergi-stigma. **Eiríks saga rauða** contains the great Greenland séance. Further saga evidence is everywhere: Gísla saga, Laxdæla saga, Egils saga, and Vatnsdœla saga all feature seiðr-workers, storms raised, minds bent, and sorcerers executed. **Saxo Grammaticus** adds the Latin tradition of Odin's exile. Alongside the texts stands the staff-grave archaeology — the field's consensus being that these burials are the völur of the written sources, found in the ground.

## Seiðr today: history versus revival

Modern Heathen and Neopagan movements have rebuilt practices under the name seiðr — trance journeys, high-seat oracular sessions modelled on Eiríks saga rauða — often influenced by 20th-century shamanic studies. Scholars themselves debate how far historical seiðr resembled circumpolar shamanism (the Sámi, the Norse's neighbours, had a documented drum-trance tradition, and contact is plausible). But the modern practice is a reconstruction, not a survival: no unbroken lineage connects any contemporary practitioner to a Viking Age völva, and the historical craft's darkest tools — cursing luck, unseating minds — are precisely the parts revivals leave out. The historical seiðr belonged to a world where fate was real, malice was actionable, and the woman with the brass-knobbed staff was received with honour and watched very carefully.

## FAQ

**What is seiðr in simple terms?**
Seiðr is the Norse magic of fate: trance-based prophecy to learn what is coming, and active sorcery to bend it — cursing an enemy's luck, clouding minds, raising storms, granting protection. It was taught to the gods by Freyja and practised among humans chiefly by travelling seeresses called völur.

**Why was seiðr shameful for men?**
Practising it made a man *argr* — the Old Norse concept of ergi, unmanliness, the culture's gravest insult. The sources connect the shame to the practice's passive trance states and its association with women's craft. Odin practised it regardless, and Loki publicly taunts him for it in Lokasenna.

**What is a völva?**
A professional seeress — literally 'staff-bearer.' Völur travelled between communities performing prophecy from a raised platform, aided by sung charms. Eiríks saga rauða describes one in extraordinary detail, and archaeologists have identified likely völva graves across Scandinavia containing metal staffs, amulets, and even hallucinogenic henbane seeds.

**Is seiðr the same as rune magic?**
No. Rune magic works by carving charged signs into objects; seiðr works through trance, song, and spirit-contact, targeting fate and minds directly. Odin masters both — paying with a wound and near-death for the runes, and with his honour for seiðr.

**Is modern seiðr authentic?**
Modern oracular seiðr is a respectful reconstruction, largely modelled on the séance in Eiríks saga rauða and informed by shamanic studies. There is no unbroken tradition from the Viking Age, and the historical practice — which included cursing and mind-harm, and carried real social danger — differed considerably from its modern revivals.
