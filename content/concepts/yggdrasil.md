Yggdrasil is the axis of everything: an evergreen ash whose branches spread over all worlds and reach above heaven. Three roots anchor it — one among the gods by the well of Urðr, where the norns water the tree with white clay and holy water and the gods hold their daily court; one among the frost-giants by Mímir's well of wisdom; one over Niflheimr, where the spring Hvergelmir boils and Níðhöggr gnaws from below.

The tree is an ecosystem of the cosmos's tensions: an eagle at the crown, the dragon at the root, the squirrel Ratatoskr running insults between them; four harts biting the new shoots; more serpents beneath than any fool imagines, says Grímnismál — 'the ash of Yggdrasil suffers hardship more than men know.' It holds the worlds together while everything chews on it, which is as good a summary of Norse cosmology as exists.

Its name means 'Yggr's horse' — Yggr, 'the Terrible One,' is Odin, and the horse is the gallows he rode: nine nights hanged, wounded with his own spear, himself to himself, to seize the runes. At Ragnarök the ash shudders and groans but is never said to fall — and inside its wood the two humans Líf and Lífþrasir shelter through the fire to repeople the new world. The tree survives its gods.

## The tree at the centre of the Norse cosmos

Every mythology needs a way to hold its geography together, and the Norse answer was a living thing. The nine worlds — [Asgard](/realms/asgard) of the gods, [Midgard](/realms/midgard) of humans, [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim) of the giants, misty [Niflheim](/realms/niflheim), fiery [Muspelheim](/realms/muspelheim), and the rest — are not stacked on shelves or drawn on a map. They hang in the branches and cluster around the roots of a single ash tree, so that the whole cosmos is one organism with sap running through it.

This matters for how the myths feel. A cosmos built on a tree is alive, which means it can be wounded, and it can grow, and one day it can wither. The [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) has the disguised king Gylfi ask where the chief centre or holy place of the gods is, and High answers without hesitation: 'It is at the ash Yggdrasil. There the gods must hold their courts each day.' Not a palace, not a mountain — a tree. The supreme court of the universe convenes under leaves.

Snorri and the Eddic poems describe the three roots with the precision of surveyors. The first runs to the [Æsir](/gods/odin) — in Snorri's account, into the sky, to the well of [Urðr](/concepts/urdarbrunnr), where the norns live. The second runs to the frost-giants, 'where Ginnungagap once was,' to [Mímir's well](/concepts/mimisbrunnr), in which wisdom itself is stored and where Odin left his eye as the price of a drink. The third stands over Niflheim, above the roaring spring [Hvergelmir](/concepts/hvergelmir), and this is the root the dragon chews. Fate, wisdom, and primal chaos: the tree drinks from all three, which is why it can stand for everything.

## What does Yggdrasil mean?

The name is one of the mythology's grimmest jokes. *Yggdrasill* breaks apart into *Yggr* — 'the Terrible One,' one of [Odin's](/gods/odin) many names — and *drasill*, a poetic word for horse. Yggdrasil is 'the Terrible One's horse.' But no one rides a tree. In Old Norse poetry, the gallows was called the horse of the hanged: the condemned man 'rides' it. The world-tree is named for the most famous hanging in the mythology — the one Odin performed on himself.

Hávamál, the great wisdom poem of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), gives it to us in Odin's own voice: 'I know that I hung on a windy tree nine long nights, wounded with a spear, dedicated to Odin, myself to myself, on that tree of which no man knows from where its roots run.' No bread, no drinking horn. At the end of the ordeal he peered down, took up the [runes](/concepts/runes) screaming, and fell back. The cosmos's central pillar is also its most sacred instrument of torture, and the name never lets you forget it. Some scholars note the poem never actually names the windy tree — but Norse tradition, and the name Yggdrasil itself, made the identification for us.

An older or alternative name may survive too: Völuspá calls the tree *mjötviðr*, 'the measuring tree' or 'tree of fate,' and elsewhere the poems speak of Léraðr or Læraðr, the tree whose leaves the goat Heiðrún crops on [Valhalla's](/concepts/valhalla) roof — usually taken to be Yggdrasil's crown seen from Asgard's angle.

## The creatures that live in and eat the world-tree

Grímnismál, where a tortured Odin recites cosmology between two fires, spends more lines on Yggdrasil's fauna than on many gods, and the picture is not peaceful. At the top sits an eagle, wise and unnamed, with a hawk called Veðrfölnir perched between its eyes — a detail so odd it must be old. At the bottom coils [Níðhöggr](/creatures/nidhogg), 'Malice-Striker,' gnawing the root from beneath. And running the trunk between them goes the squirrel Ratatoskr, carrying the eagle's insults down and the dragon's insults up, forever. The cosmos, in other words, contains a built-in mechanism for keeping hostility alive between its heights and its depths — and it looks like a squirrel.

Four harts — Dáinn, Dvalinn, Duneyrr, and Duraþrór — browse the highest shoots, biting off the new growth. The trunk rots on one side. Below, in Hvergelmir, lie 'more serpents than any fool can imagine,' and Grímnismál names a sampling of them like a weary landlord: Góinn and Móinn, Grábakr and Grafvölluðr, Ófnir and Sváfnir, 'who must forever, I think, gnaw the tree's twigs.' The poem's summary line is the bleakest sentence in Norse cosmology: 'The ash Yggdrasil suffers hardship more than men know.'

Against all this stands one maintenance crew: the three [norns](/concepts/norns), who each day draw water from Urðr's well and daub it, mixed with the well's white clay, onto the tree so that its limbs will not rot. The dew that falls from its branches is honeydew, food of bees; the well's water bleaches everything it touches white as an eggshell's inner film. Creation is not a finished work in the Norse imagination. It is a patient, endless act of nursing something that everything else is trying to eat.

## Yggdrasil and Odin's sacrifice

The hanging deserves its own telling, because it is the closest thing Norse myth has to a central mystery. Odin already knew that wisdom must be bought — he had pawned an eye at [Mímir's well](/concepts/mimisbrunnr) for a single drink. But the runes were something else: not knowledge but the secret shapes underlying things, and they lay in the deep, among the dead and the roots. So the god of the hanged became one of them. He speared himself — dedicating the victim to Odin, as sacrificers did — and hung nine nights (nine being the mythology's charged number: nine worlds, nine paces Thor takes before falling at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok)).

He was given no bread and no horn of drink. He hung 'on that tree of which no man knows from where its roots run' — and this line lands differently once you know Grímnismál has just told us, exactly, where the roots run. The poem means something more vertiginous: that even the god at the centre of things, dying on the axis of the world, cannot see to the bottom of it. Then he took up the runes, screaming, and fell — and the falling is part of it. Initiation, in this tradition, is not enlightenment. It is a death you survive with something clutched in your hand.

## What happens to Yggdrasil at Ragnarök?

Völuspá gives the tree one of the great lines of the poem: as the end begins, 'the old tree groans, and the giant slips free' — Yggdrasil shudders through its whole height, and everything in heaven and earth is afraid. The ash that suffered quietly for ages finally cries out. Heimdallr blows the Gjallarhorn, Odin consults Mímir's head, and the armies move toward [Vígríðr](/concepts/vigrid).

But here is the thing the poem never says: it never says the tree falls. Surtr's fire engulfs the world, the sun blackens, the earth sinks into the sea — and the tree that groaned is not listed among the casualties. On the contrary: Vafþrúðnismál reveals that two humans, Líf and Lífþrasir ('Life' and 'Stubborn Will to Live'), hide through the catastrophe in 'Hoddmímir's wood' — which most scholars take to be Yggdrasil itself, the timber of Mímir's tree — living on the morning dew. When the earth rises green from the sea and the surviving young gods gather, it is this pair who repeople the world. The tree outlives the pantheon that held court beneath it. Odin named it as his gallows, but it was never his; it was there before the gods organized the cosmos around it, and it shelters what comes after them.

## In the primary sources

Yggdrasil's portrait is assembled from three main texts. **Völuspá** (Poetic Edda) gives the frame: the seeress remembers 'nine worlds, nine wood-ogresses, the famous measuring-tree beneath the earth'; describes the ash standing evergreen over Urðr's well, white with wet clay; and sounds its groan at Ragnarök. **Grímnismál** (Poetic Edda) supplies the natural history — the three roots and what lies under each, the eagle, hawk, squirrel, harts, and serpents, and the tree's suffering. **Hávamál** (Poetic Edda) contains the rune-winning on the windy tree. Snorri Sturluson's **Gylfaginning** in the Prose Edda synthesizes all of this into orderly prose, adding the norns' clay-daubing, the honeydew, and the swans on the well — though readers should know Snorri occasionally smooths contradictions (his placement of one root 'in the sky' among the Æsir sits awkwardly with a tree whose roots ought to go down). The name also flickers through skaldic verse and in *askr Yggdrasils*, 'the ash of Yggdrasil,' as if 'Yggdrasil' were properly the name of something the ash merely embodies.

## FAQ

**What is Yggdrasil in Norse mythology?**
Yggdrasil is the immense evergreen ash tree at the centre of the Norse cosmos, connecting and sheltering the nine worlds. Its three roots reach the well of fate (Urðarbrunnr), the well of wisdom (Mímisbrunnr), and the primal spring Hvergelmir in Niflheim. The gods hold their daily court beneath it, and the norns tend it to keep it from rotting.

**What does the name Yggdrasil mean?**
It means 'Yggr's horse' — Yggr ('the Terrible One') is Odin, and 'horse' is a poetic term for the gallows, which the hanged man was said to ride. The tree is named for Odin's self-sacrifice, when he hung on it for nine nights, wounded by his own spear, to win the runes.

**Is Yggdrasil destroyed at Ragnarök?**
No source says it falls. Völuspá says the tree shakes and groans as Ragnarök begins, but it is never named among the destroyed. Two humans, Líf and Lífþrasir, shelter in its wood through the fire and repopulate the renewed world — so the tree apparently survives the gods themselves.

**What animals live in Yggdrasil?**
An unnamed eagle sits in the crown with the hawk Veðrfölnir between its eyes; the dragon Níðhöggr gnaws the deepest root; the squirrel Ratatoskr runs up and down carrying insults between eagle and dragon. Four harts browse its shoots, countless serpents lie beneath it, and the goat Heiðrún and hart Eikþyrnir graze its foliage from Valhalla's roof.

**Is Yggdrasil an ash or a yew?**
The sources call it an ash (askr), and that is the standard reading. But because the poems also call it evergreen and 'needle-ash' is an old name for yew, some scholars have argued it was originally imagined as a yew tree. The texts as we have them say ash.
