Modern horror has a ghost and a zombie for a reason: it inherited both ideas from a single Norse figure that refused to separate them. A draugr — also called aptrgangr, "again-walker" — is not a spirit haunting the living from beyond the grave. It is the corpse itself, up and moving, unrotted or grotesquely swollen, blue-black or corpse-pale in color, carrying its full physical strength and then some, and actively, personally malicious toward the living it left behind. Killing a draugr means fighting a body, not exorcising a soul, which is precisely what makes the saga accounts of doing so so unnervingly physical.

## What is a draugr, and how is it different from a ghost?

Ordinary Norse dead were expected simply to continue on quietly in [Hel](/gods/hel)'s realm of [Helheim](/realms/helheim) — no reward, no punishment, just a grey continuation. The draugr is the glaring exception to that quiet rule: a corpse that, for reasons the sagas rarely explain, refuses to stay settled and instead comes back as an active menace to the living, distinct even from the specific oath-breakers and murderers condemned to wade the venom rivers of [Náströnd](/concepts/nastrond). The distinction matters because it shapes everything else about the creature. A draugr retains its own memory, personality, and grudges from life — often intensified into pure malice — and it retains its own dead flesh, now grown monstrously heavy and strong. Sagas describe the difficulty of even moving a slain draugr's body: men can barely drag it, and it takes real force to subdue one in a wrestling match, because the fight is with muscle and bone, not with an intangible presence. It smells of the grave, and its eyes are described as open, aware, and terrible to meet.

Draugar are strongly associated with burial mounds, where they guard the treasure interred with them — the original template for every mound-guarding undead in later fantasy fiction, a role distinct from but sometimes confused with monsters like [Fáfnir](/creatures/fafnir), whose hoard-guarding dragon-shape came from a transformed living being rather than a walking corpse. But they do not stay put. A restless draugr leaves its grave at night to torment the living nearby: riding on the roofs of houses, where the sound of something heavy dragging itself across the roof-beams overhead is one of the sagas' most effective pieces of horror; killing livestock; and attacking people directly, sometimes breaking their bones or backs with its bare hands. The worst cases are described as emptying whole valleys of their population, as neighbors abandon farms rather than face what has started walking at night.

## How do you kill a draugr?

Because the draugr's problem is a body, the solution is physical and specific, not spiritual. The standard remedy across the sagas is decapitation, followed by burning the corpse completely, with the ashes then gathered and disposed of somewhere the dead cannot return from — typically thrown into the sea, so there is nothing left on land to reanimate. Some accounts add a further precaution: the head is placed at the corpse's thigh, or the body is burned facing away from land, specifically to keep it from finding its way back.

Prevention took its own physical form as well. When burying someone thought likely to walk again, the coffin might be carried out of the house through a specially cut hole in the wall, which was then bricked or boarded up immediately afterward — the logic being that the dead can only find their way back out through the door they remember leaving by, so removing that door entirely traps them. These are not symbolic gestures; the sagas present them as practical engineering against a physical threat, the same way one might board up a chimney against an intruder.

## Historical belief versus modern pop culture

The clearest saga treatment of the type is Grettis saga's account of the hero Grettir's fight with Glámr, a shepherd turned draugr whose reign of terror empties a valley before Grettir wrestles him to a standstill inside a farmhouse. Killing Glámr does not end the story's damage: with his dying breath, the draugr curses Grettir, promising that his strength will never grow further and that he will live out his life haunted by fear of the dark — a curse the saga insists genuinely comes true. Grettir's wrestling match belongs to the same saga tradition of heroic monster-slaying that produced [Sigurðr](/heroes/sigurd)'s fight with Fáfnir, though the draugr's menace is domestic and haunting rather than epic and treasure-bound. It is the passage most responsible for how modern readers picture the draugr — and it is worth noting how much interpretation sits between the medieval text and the modern one: the sagas were written centuries after the events they describe, blending oral tradition, Christian moralizing, and storytelling craft, so how literally thirteenth-century Icelanders believed in walking corpses remains genuinely debated among historians, even as the narrative details are consistently and vividly attested.

What is not in doubt is the draugr's afterlife in fiction. Every modern zombie guarding a dungeon, every fantasy revenant nursing unfinished business, every video-game barrow-wight is walking, whether the creators know it or not, in the exact footprints Glámr left in the snow: physical, strong, aware, and killed only by fire and dismemberment rather than prayer. Tolkien's barrow-wights and modern tabletop-game "wights" or "revenants" draw directly on this saga template, and the recent wave of Viking-themed film and television has revived the draugr as a stock antagonist, usually stripped of the moral weight the sagas gave it — Glámr's curse is a psychological ruin, not just a monster fight, and that part rarely survives the adaptation.

## In the primary sources

Grettis saga contains the single most famous and fully developed draugr episode in the corpus, the fight with Glámr, along with an earlier mound-battle against the draugr Kárr the Old. Eyrbyggja saga describes a whole household plagued by a chain of hauntings at Fróðá, resolved only through a formal legal proceeding — a "door-court" — held to expel the dead, one of the strangest and most procedural exorcisms in medieval literature. Laxdæla saga includes further accounts of the restless dead disturbing the living, rounding out the type across Iceland's saga tradition.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between a draugr and a ghost?**
A draugr is the physical corpse itself, reanimated with its full strength and awareness, not a disembodied spirit. Norse sources treat it as something you fight and physically destroy, unlike a ghost, which typically has no body to attack.

**How do you kill a draugr in Norse mythology?**
The standard method is decapitation followed by complete burning of the corpse, with the ashes disposed of at sea so nothing remains to reanimate. Some accounts add placing the severed head at the thigh or burning the body facing away from land as extra precautions.

**What is the most famous draugr story?**
Grettis saga's account of the hero Grettir wrestling the shepherd-turned-draugr Glámr is the classic account. Even in defeat, Glámr curses Grettir with permanent bad luck and fear of the dark, a curse the saga treats as genuinely shaping the rest of the hero's life.

**Are draugar the same as zombies?**
They share the core idea of an animate corpse, but draugar in the sagas retain full intelligence, speech, and personal grudges from life, and are often specifically tied to guarding burial mounds and treasure. Modern zombie fiction owes a real debt to the draugr, though it usually strips away the personal malice and curses that make the saga versions so unsettling.
