Norse religion had no scripture, no priesthood in the churchly sense, and almost no fixed theology to argue over. What it had was blót — "sacrifice," a word cognate with blood — and blót is where nearly everything a modern reader would call Norse religious practice actually happened. It was not private devotion but a communal event: a gathering, at a temple, chieftain's hall, or holy grove, that combined killing, feasting, and drinking into a single structured ritual meant to keep the gods, and the community, in good standing with one another.

## What was a blót sacrifice?

The core of a blót was straightforward and physical. Animals — most often horses, boars, or cattle — were killed, and their blood, called hlaut, was caught in bowls. Using twigs or a sprig bound for the purpose, the blood was sprinkled on the altar, on the temple walls, and on the assembled worshippers themselves, marking everyone present as part of the offering. The meat that followed was not wasted or burned away entirely as in some other ancient traditions; it was boiled in cauldrons over fires lit inside the hall and eaten by the whole community as a shared feast, the sacrifice and the meal being one continuous act rather than two separate ones.

Drinking gave the evening its shape. Toasts were raised in a fixed order, each one a small act of devotion aimed at a purpose: a cup to [Odin](/gods/odin) for victory in war and for the health of the king, cups to [Njörðr](/gods/njord) and [Freyr](/gods/freyr) for good seasons and peace — the phrase til árs ok friðar, "for good seasons and peace," recurs across the sources as something close to a fixed liturgical formula — and finally the minni, memorial toasts drunk in honor of dead kinsmen, folding ancestor-remembrance into the same ritual as worship of the gods.

## When and why did the Norse hold a blót?

Blót followed the agricultural and seasonal calendar rather than a single annual holy day: sources describe an autumn blót tied to the harvest, a midwinter Yule blót marking the turn of the year, and a summer blót connected to the opening of the raiding and trading season, sometimes called a victory blót. These were not optional civic ceremonies that a ruler could quietly let lapse. The sagas are blunt about how much popular pressure enforced them — Icelandic and Norwegian sources describe kings who tried to skip or minimize the sacrifice running into open resistance from farmers who refused to plant or fight without the proper rites performed first, since the whole community's luck for the year was understood to be riding on it.

Blót and [seiðr](/concepts/seidr) were the two poles of Norse ritual life — one a public, communal sacrifice performed in daylight for the whole settlement's benefit, the other a private, trance-based sorcery taught by [Freyja](/gods/freyja) and practiced by specialists like the völur. Both aimed at the same goal, luck and favor from powers beyond human control, by opposite means.

The most infamous blót on record is the one Adam of Bremen, an eleventh-century German chronicler, describes at the great temple of Uppsala in Sweden: a sacrifice held every ninth year in which, by his shocked account, nine males of every living species — including men — were hanged in the sacred grove beside the temple. Adam wrote as a hostile outsider relaying secondhand report, and scholars treat the most extreme details of his account with real caution, but archaeological evidence from Scandinavian sites does confirm large-scale animal sacrifice, and occasional human remains in ritual contexts keep the possibility of the darker version from being dismissed outright.

## Historical practice versus modern revival

What we know of blót comes almost entirely through a double filter: it survives in sagas and histories written down generations after conversion, by Christian authors — chiefly Snorri Sturluson in Heimskringla, describing King Hákon the Good's uncomfortable participation in a blót he privately despised, and the anonymous author of Eyrbyggja saga, describing temple ritual in Iceland — plus one outsider's scandalized foreign report in Adam of Bremen. None of these is a participant's own account of what the ritual meant from the inside, and the reconstructions built from them, however careful, remain reconstructions.

That gap between historical attestation and living practice is worth keeping visible, because blót did not simply vanish with conversion. The structure proved durable enough to survive baptism nearly intact under a new name: Yule kept its calendar slot and much of its feasting and toasting shape even as its religious content changed. In the modern era, practitioners of Heathenry and Ásatrú — a legally recognized religion in Iceland, Norway, and elsewhere — hold blóts as their central communal rite, adapted for law and modern sensibility: livestock sacrifice is rare or symbolic today, often replaced by offerings of mead or produce, while the core shape of gathering, toast, and shared feast is deliberately preserved. Popular culture, meanwhile, tends to flatten blót into a single dramatic image — a bloody altar in a television series or video game — that owes more to Adam of Bremen's most sensational paragraph than to the ordinary, food-centered communal feast the word actually described for most Norse families in most years.

## In the primary sources

[Heimskringla](/texts/heimskringla), Snorri Sturluson's history of the Norwegian kings, gives the single most detailed narrative account of blót ritual in Hákonar saga góða, describing the toasts, the blood-sprinkling, and the political friction between a Christian king and his still-pagan subjects. Adam of Bremen's Latin history of the archbishops of Hamburg-Bremen supplies the outsider's account of the Uppsala temple and its nine-year sacrifice. Eyrbyggja saga, an Icelandic family saga, describes temple structure and sacrificial practice in a domestic Icelandic setting, complementing the royal, public blót Snorri describes in Norway.

## FAQ

**What does the word blót mean?**
Blót means "sacrifice" and is linguistically related to blóð, "blood." It refers to the entire ritual of killing an animal, sprinkling its blood, and sharing its cooked meat in a communal feast, not to the killing alone.

**What happened at a blót?**
Animals, typically horses, boars, or cattle, were slaughtered and their blood caught and sprinkled on the altar, walls, and participants. The meat was then boiled and eaten as a shared meal, accompanied by a fixed sequence of ritual toasts to Odin, to Njörðr and Freyr, and to dead kinsmen.

**Is the Uppsala sacrifice described by Adam of Bremen true?**
Adam of Bremen, writing as a Christian outsider, describes a nine-yearly sacrifice at Uppsala that included humans among the offerings. Historians treat his account with caution since he never witnessed it himself, though archaeology confirms large-scale animal sacrifice occurred at major Scandinavian cult sites.

**Do modern pagans still practice blót?**
Yes. Practitioners of Ásatrú and other modern Heathen movements hold blóts as their central communal ritual, typically using symbolic offerings of mead, food, or drink rather than animal sacrifice, while preserving the traditional structure of gathering, toast, and shared feast.
