Hvergelmir ('roaring kettle') is the cosmos's water main: the boiling spring in Niflheimr from which the eleven rivers called Élivágar first flowed into the void to freeze into the stuff of creation — and from which, Grímnismál says, all rivers still run. The hart Eikþyrnir stands on Valhalla's roof, and the drip from his antlers falls all the way down into Hvergelmir: the water cycle, routed through the gods' attic.

It is also the worst address in the cosmology. Beneath the root that arches over it, Níðhöggr and 'more serpents than any fool can imagine' — Grímnismál names them like a landlord reading a tenant list — gnaw the tree from below, and dead bodies are theirs. First spring, last drain: everything begins and rots in the same water.

## The spring at the bottom of everything

Every cosmology needs a source, and the Norse one is audible before it is visible: *Hvergelmir* means something like 'roaring kettle' or 'bubbling cauldron' — a spring you hear from a long way off. Snorri places it at the heart of [Niflheim](/realms/niflheim), the world of freezing mist that existed ages before the earth was made, and makes it the first cause of everything that followed. Out of Hvergelmir flowed the rivers called the **Élivágar** — Snorri names eleven, with names like Svöl ('Cool'), Fjörm, and Gjöll ('Loud'), the river that later runs closest to the gates of the dead. As those rivers ran farther and farther from their source into [Ginnungagap](/concepts/ginnungagap), their yeasty venom-cold hardened like slag from a furnace and froze, layer upon layer — and it was this rime, thawed by the heat drifting from [Muspelheim](/realms/muspelheim), that dripped into life as [Ymir](/giants/ymir), the first being. Trace any pedigree in the mythology far enough back — gods, giants, the world itself — and you arrive at water from this one spring, gone cold in the dark.

Nor did creation close the account. Grímnismál insists the spring is still the world's water main: from Eikþyrnir's drip into Hvergelmir 'all rivers have their flowing' — and the poem then names dozens of them, rivers of gods and men and the rivers of [Hel](/realms/helheim), in a torrent of stanzas that is itself a kind of river-catalogue. Every stream in every world is, hydrologically speaking, a tributary of the roaring kettle in the mist.

## The stag on the roof and the strangest water cycle in mythology

The mechanism by which the spring is fed is one of the most gloriously odd images in the Eddas. On the roof of [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) stands the hart **Eikþyrnir** ('Oak-thorny,' for his antlers), browsing the foliage of the tree Læraðr — almost certainly [Yggdrasil's](/concepts/yggdrasil) crown seen from the hall. From his antlers, moisture drips: down past the hall of the [einherjar](/concepts/einherjar), down the whole height of the cosmos, into Hvergelmir at the bottom — whence all rivers flow out again, water rising through the world-tree's tissues back to the leaves the stag is eating. It is a complete water cycle, routed through the gods' attic: the tree drinks from the spring, the stag eats the tree, the drip refills the spring. Beside Eikþyrnir the goat Heiðrún crops the same foliage and yields the einherjar's mead — the same circulation, tapped for drink. The Norse cosmos runs on one closed loop of sacred plumbing, and its pump is a stag's antlers.

## The serpent-pit under the third root

But Hvergelmir is a double address, and the second listing is grim. Of Yggdrasil's three roots, the third arches over Niflheim, directly above the spring — and Snorri says plainly that **[Níðhöggr](/creatures/nidhogg)** gnaws that root from beneath. Grímnismál supplies the census: under the ash lie 'more serpents than any witless fool can imagine' — and then, deadpan, it names them anyway, like a landlord reading a tenant list: Góinn and Móinn (sons of Grafvitnir, 'Grave-wolf'), Grábakr ('Grey-back'), Grafvölluðr ('Field-burrower'), Ófnir and Sváfnir — 'who must forever, I think, gnaw the tree's twigs.' The poem's summary follows at once: the ash Yggdrasil suffers more hardship than men know — the hart bites above, the trunk rots, and Níðhöggr tears beneath.

Snorri adds the darkest line in the file: in Hvergelmir the torment is worst of all, 'and there Níðhöggr torments the bodies of the dead.' This binds the spring to [Náströnd](/concepts/nastrond), the Corpse-Shore nearby in the cosmology's cellar, where the venom-woven hall holds oath-breakers and murderers and the same dragon sucks the corpses. First spring and last drain: the water that once dripped into life at creation now receives the dead at the bottom of the world, and the same serpent presides over both books of the ledger. The Norse cosmos does not separate its sources and its sewers. Everything begins, and everything rots, in the same roaring water — which is as honest as cosmology gets.

## In the primary sources

**Grímnismál** (Poetic Edda) is the richest source: Eikþyrnir on the hall-roof dripping into Hvergelmir, 'from which all rivers run,' followed by the great river-catalogue; and the serpent-census beneath the ash with Níðhöggr gnawing below. Snorri's **Gylfaginning** (Prose Edda) supplies the creation-role — Hvergelmir in the middle of Niflheim, wellspring of the Élivágar whose frozen venom became the first matter — the placement of the third root over the spring, and the torment of the dead there; his account of [Ragnarök's](/events/ragnarok) aftermath also lodges corpse-wading horrors in this quarter of the cosmos. The name and the river-lists appear with small variations across manuscripts, and Snorri, as usual, has tidied a wetter, wilder poetic tradition into a system — but on Hvergelmir the sources agree with unusual unanimity: at the very bottom of everything, in the cold and the dark, there is a spring, and it is loud.

## FAQ

**What is Hvergelmir?**
The great spring at the heart of Niflheim, the world of primordial mist — its name means roughly 'roaring kettle.' From it flowed the Élivágar, the rivers whose frozen spray became the raw material of creation, and from it, the Eddas say, all rivers in all worlds still flow.

**Where is Hvergelmir located?**
In Niflheim, beneath the third root of Yggdrasil — the root that Snorri says the dragon Níðhöggr gnaws from below. It is the lowest of the three wells under the world-tree, counterpart to Urðarbrunnr (fate) and Mímisbrunnr (wisdom).

**How is Hvergelmir connected to Valhalla?**
By the mythology's strangest water cycle: the stag Eikþyrnir stands on Valhalla's roof eating the world-tree's foliage, and the drip from his antlers falls all the way down into Hvergelmir, keeping the source of all rivers filled.

**What lives in and around Hvergelmir?**
Serpents beyond counting — Grímnismál says more than any fool can imagine, then names several — and Níðhöggr, who gnaws Yggdrasil's root and, Snorri adds, torments the bodies of the dead there. The spring is both creation's source and the cosmos's corpse-pit.
