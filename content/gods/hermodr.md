When [Baldr](/gods/baldr) lay dead and [Frigg](/gods/frigg) asked who would ride to [Hel](/gods/hel) to buy him back, one volunteer stepped forward: Hermóðr, called "the bold," in most tellings a son of [Odin](/gods/odin). He took Sleipnir — only the Allfather's horse can make that trip — and rode nine nights through valleys so dark he saw nothing, over the river Gjöll, and cleared Hel's gate without breaking stride.

He found Baldr enthroned in the hall of the dead, stayed the night, and won from Hel her conditional yes: all things must weep. His mission failed by one dry-eyed giantess, but no one else in the mythology out-rode death and returned to tell it. He is the messenger the worst news gets given to.

## The ride to Hel

The Helreið — the Hel-ride — is told in Gylfaginning, and it is one of the most cinematic sequences Snorri ever wrote. Asgard is paralysed by Baldr's death; the gods stand weeping, unable even to speak. It is Frigg who acts, asking who among the Æsir would earn "all her love and favour" by riding the Hel-road to offer Hel a ransom. Hermóðr inn hvati — "the Bold" — volunteers. Snorri calls him sveinn Óðins, "Odin's boy," which most readers take as "son of Odin," though the word can also mean a servant or page; the ambiguity has kept scholars busy, and Snorri elsewhere lists him among Odin's sons.

Sleipnir is led out — the eight-legged horse that [Loki](/gods/loki) himself bore, the only steed that travels between the worlds — and Hermóðr rides. Nine nights he goes through valleys "dark and deep," seeing nothing at all, until he reaches the river Gjöll and its bridge, Gjallarbrú, roofed with shining gold. There the maiden Móðguðr keeps the bridge, and her challenge gives the journey its eeriest touch: yesterday, she says, five troops of dead men rode across, "but the bridge echoes no less under you alone — and you have not the colour of dead men. Why do you ride the Hel-way?" A living man makes more noise crossing into death than five battalions of ghosts.

He answers honestly — he seeks Baldr — and she directs him: downward and northward lies the road to Hel. At the barred gate Hermóðr does not parley. He dismounts, tightens Sleipnir's girth, remounts, spurs — and the horse leaps so high over the gate that it comes nowhere near touching. It is the great athletic feat of the myth, and the reason every retelling keeps it: death's door is closed, and one rider simply jumps it.

## The bargain with Hel

Inside the hall Hermóðr finds Baldr seated in the high seat, with his wife [Nanna](/gods/nanna) — who died of grief at his funeral — beside him. He stays the night; the dead, apparently, keep hospitality. In the morning he puts his plea to Hel: the gods cannot endure the loss; all the world weeps; let Baldr ride home. Hel's answer is a masterpiece of cold justice: it shall be tested, she says, whether Baldr is as beloved as claimed. If everything in the world, living and dead, weeps for him, he shall return to the Æsir — but if any one thing refuses or will not weep, "then he stays with Hel."

Hermóðr rides back with the terms and with gifts from the dead: Baldr returns the ring Draupnir to Odin — the arm-ring the Allfather had laid on his son's pyre — and Nanna sends a linen robe to Frigg and a gold ring to [Fulla](/gods/fulla). The gods dispatch messengers over all the world, and everything weeps — people, beasts, earth, stones, trees, every metal, "as you have seen these things weep when they come out of frost into heat." Everything but one giantess in a cave, calling herself Þökk ("Thanks"), who answers in verse that Baldr never did her any good, alive or dead — "let Hel hold what she has." It was, everyone understood, Loki. The mission fails by that single refusal, and the failure is total. Hermóðr did everything a messenger could do; the flaw was never in the riding.

## Was Hermóðr a god or a hero?

There is a genuine puzzle here. Outside Snorri's Hel-ride, the name Hermóðr belongs to figures who look human. In the Old English poem Beowulf, a Heremod is a failed Danish king, held up as a cautionary example. In Hyndluljóð, Odin gives "helmet and mail-coat to Hermóðr" — phrasing that suits a favoured mortal warrior, as Odin arms his human champions. And in the tenth-century Hákonarmál, Hermóðr appears with [Bragi](/gods/bragi) in Valhalla, sent by Odin to welcome the slain King Hákon — a role given elsewhere to dead heroes as well as gods. Some scholars therefore think Hermóðr was originally a legendary hero whom Snorri (or his sources) promoted into Odin's family to fill the messenger's role in the Baldr story; others accept him as a genuine minor god whose other myths are lost. The texts allow both. What no one disputes is the shape of his one great story: he is the mythology's psychopomp-in-reverse, the only figure who rides into death on the living side of the ledger and comes back.

## In the primary sources

Hermóðr's dossier: the Prose Edda (Gylfaginning: the entire Hel-ride; Skáldskaparmál names him among the Æsir and among Odin's sons in some lists); the Poetic Edda (Hyndluljóð's arming-stanza); the skaldic Hákonarmál (greeting the dead king in Valhalla); and, more distantly, the Heremod of Beowulf and related Anglo-Saxon genealogies, if the identification holds. The Hel-ride itself survives only in Snorri — no Eddic poem tells it — which makes Gylfaginning our single thread to the mythology's most famous journey into death.

## Legacy

Hermóðr never had a weekday or a cult, but his ride became the template every later "journey to the land of the dead" in Scandinavian literature answers to, and modern retellings of the Baldr myth — novels, children's books, comics — invariably keep him, because the story does not work without a rider. In the comparative study of myth he stands with Orpheus: the envoy who nearly wins back the beloved dead and fails through a single flaw at the last moment. Orpheus looked back; Hermóðr's world could not produce one more tear.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is Hermóðr in Norse mythology?**
Hermóðr, called "the Bold," is the messenger of the gods, in most tellings a son of Odin. His single great myth is riding Odin's eight-legged horse Sleipnir to Hel's realm to ask for the dead god Baldr's release.

**Q: How long did Hermóðr's ride to Hel take?**
Nine nights, according to Gylfaginning, through valleys so dark and deep that he saw nothing at all, until he crossed the golden-roofed bridge over the river Gjöll and leapt Sleipnir clean over Hel's gate.

**Q: Did Hermóðr succeed in bringing Baldr back?**
No — but only just. Hel agreed to release Baldr if every thing in the world wept for him. Everything did, except a giantess called Þökk, universally understood to be Loki in disguise. That one refusal kept Baldr among the dead until after Ragnarök.

**Q: Is Hermóðr a son of Odin?**
Snorri calls him "Odin's boy" (sveinn Óðins) and elsewhere counts him among Odin's sons, so most retellings make him Baldr's brother. But because figures named Hermóðr appear as human heroes in Beowulf and other poems, some scholars think he was originally a legendary warrior later drawn into the divine family.

**Q: What did Hermóðr bring back from Hel?**
Hel's conditional promise, and gifts from the dead: Baldr sent the gold ring Draupnir back to Odin, and Nanna sent a linen robe to Frigg and a gold ring to Fulla — the dead returning treasures to the living, reversing the gifts laid on the funeral pyre.
