Forseti ("President" — literally "the one who sits in front") is the pantheon's judge. His hall Glitnir ("Shining") stands on pillars of gold under a roof of silver, and Grímnismál says of it that all who come with hard cases leave reconciled — the best court of gods and men.

He is the son of [Baldr](/gods/baldr) and [Nanna](/gods/nanna), the gentlest pair in the mythology, and his justice is theirs continued: not punishment but settlement. Iceland's modern word for president, forseti, keeps his name in every news broadcast.

## The god who ends quarrels

What little the sources say about Forseti is strikingly consistent, and strikingly unlike the rest of the pantheon. The Norse gods are, on the whole, litigants rather than judges: they break oaths to the giant who built Asgard's wall, they bind the wolf Fenrir with a pledge they never intend to honour — a pledge [Týr](/gods/tyr) pays for with his hand, they kill and pay and feud like the saga-Icelanders who told their stories. Forseti is the counterweight. Grímnismál, in [Odin](/gods/odin)'s catalogue of the divine dwellings, gives him the tenth hall:

Glitnir is the tenth; it is propped with gold and likewise roofed with silver; and there Forseti dwells most days and settles all suits.

Snorri expands on this in Gylfaginning: all who come to Forseti with hard cases in law go away reconciled — "that is the best court among gods and men." The emphasis matters. Not the harshest court, not the most feared: the best, because everyone leaves satisfied. In a legal culture where a failed settlement meant feud, and feud meant generations of killing, a judge from whom all parties "go away reconciled" was not a mild fantasy but the highest imaginable good. The medieval Icelanders who preserved these texts governed themselves through assemblies and arbitration; Forseti is that ideal projected into Asgard, presiding — as his name says — the way a lawspeaker sits before the assembly.

## Who are Forseti's parents?

Forseti is the son of Baldr, the shining god, and Nanna, who died of grief at his funeral — which makes the judge of the gods an orphan of the mythology's central tragedy. The sources never dwell on this, but the genealogy does quiet work. Baldr is the god so beautiful that light shines from him, of whom Snorri says no judgement of his may be altered; Nanna is devotion itself. Their son inherits the peaceable core of both. Where Odin begot [Váli](/gods/vali) to answer Baldr's death with vengeance, Baldr's own line answers it with law: killing produces an avenger, but the beloved dead god's true heir is reconciliation. Nothing is recorded of Forseti's own deeds, marriages, or fate — no myth survives in which he acts. He exists as a function perfected: the seat, the shining hall, the settled suit.

## Forseti and the Frisian god Fosite

Forseti may be older and more widely worshipped than his slim Norse record suggests. Medieval Latin sources — Alcuin's Life of St Willibrord and related texts from around 800 — describe a sacred island on the North Sea coast, "Fositesland" (usually identified with Heligoland), dedicated to a god the Frisians called Fosite. The island's springs, cattle, and shrines were so holy that no one dared harm an animal or draw water except in silence; the missionary Willibrord courted martyrdom by baptising converts in the sacred spring and slaughtering the sacred cattle. Most scholars connect Fosite with Forseti, though the direction of borrowing — Frisian to Norse, Norse to Frisian, or common inheritance — remains debated, and the vowels do not correspond as neatly as one would like. A much later Frisian legend tells of twelve lawspeakers lost at sea who were joined by a thirteenth figure bearing an axe, who steered them to land, struck a spring from the earth, and taught them law; the tale is late and its link to Fosite uncertain, but it shows how naturally a god of this name gathered legal legend around him. If the identification holds, the quiet judge of Glitnir is one of the few Norse gods with a documented cult site outside Scandinavia — and one holy enough that Christian missionaries treated desecrating it as a headline act.

## In the primary sources

Forseti's entire Norse attestation fits in a few lines: the Glitnir stanza of Grímnismál in the Poetic Edda; Snorri's notice in Gylfaginning, where he is listed among the twelve Æsir and identified as son of Baldr and Nanna; and a passing mention in Skáldskaparmál, which allows "Forseti's father" as a kenning for Baldr. He appears in no surviving narrative myth. Outside the Norse world, the eighth-century accounts of Fositesland and the god Fosite of the Frisians are usually — though not universally — taken as evidence of the same deity. Honesty requires saying plainly: everything else written about Forseti in modern books is inference from these fragments.

## At Ragnarök

No source assigns Forseti any role at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — he is neither listed among the fighters nor among the named survivors. Some readers like to imagine that a god of settlement has no place in the war that ends all settlements; the texts simply say nothing. Whether his hall of gold and silver burns with the rest, the poems do not tell.

## Legacy

Forseti's name is spoken daily in one modern country: forseti is the Icelandic word for "president," so the head of state of Iceland formally bears the old god's title, and every news bulletin from Reykjavík keeps it alive. A few Norwegian place-names (such as Forsetlund by the Oslofjord) may preserve traces of his cult, and if Heligoland is Fositesland, an entire North Sea island once belonged to him. For a god with no myths, he has kept remarkable tenure on the bench.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Forseti the god of?**
Forseti is the Norse god of justice, arbitration, and reconciliation. According to Grímnismál and Snorri's Gylfaginning, he sits in his hall Glitnir and settles every dispute brought before him, so that all parties leave reconciled — the best court of gods and men.

**Q: Who are Forseti's parents?**
He is the son of Baldr, the god of light and beauty, and Nanna, who died of grief at Baldr's funeral. His peaceable justice is often read as the continuation of his parents' gentleness.

**Q: What does the name Forseti mean?**
It means "the one who sits in front" — a presider or chairman, exactly the posture of a lawspeaker before an assembly. Modern Icelandic still uses forseti as its word for "president."

**Q: What is Glitnir?**
Glitnir ("Shining") is Forseti's hall, described in Grímnismál as standing on pillars of gold under a roof of silver. It functions as the supreme court of the Norse cosmos: those who come with hard cases leave with their suits settled.

**Q: Is Forseti the same as the Frisian god Fosite?**
Probably, though not certainly. Eighth-century sources describe a holy island of the god Fosite on the Frisian coast, usually identified with Heligoland, and most scholars connect Fosite with Forseti. The linguistic details are debated, so the identification remains a strong hypothesis rather than a settled fact.
