Promised as much Swedish land as she could plough in a day and a night by king Gylfi, Gefjon fetched her four sons by a giant, turned them into oxen, and tore loose a chunk of Sweden so vast that its absence filled with water — the lake Mälaren — and its presence became the Danish island of Zealand. Copenhagen's Gefion Fountain still shows her driving the team.

Snorri says all who die unmarried go to her hall, making the virgin ploughmaker a psychopomp of the single. [Loki](/gods/loki), inevitably, accuses her of trading herself for a necklace; [Odin](/gods/odin) answers that Gefjon sees all fates as clearly as he does — high praise buried in a quarrel.

## The ploughing of Zealand

The story stands at the very front of Snorri's Prose Edda — Gylfaginning opens with it — and it is one of the mythology's great feats of landscape-engineering. Gylfi, king of Sweden, rewarded a wandering woman who had entertained him with as much land as four oxen could plough in a day and a night. The woman was Gefjon, "of the race of the Æsir." She went north to Jötunheimr, to a giant by whom she had four sons, and turned the sons into oxen. Yoked to the plough, they cut so hard and deep that the land itself tore loose from Sweden, and Gefjon dragged it westward across the sea and set it down where it remains: the island of Zealand (Sjælland), the largest of the Danish isles, where Copenhagen now stands. The hole left behind filled with water and became a lake — Snorri says Lögrinn, Lake Mälaren, and points out that the inlets of the lake match the headlands of Zealand, a lovely piece of mythic geography (in truth Zealand's shape better matches Lake Vänern, and Ynglinga saga's version says Mälaren; the medieval writers were already improvising).

Snorri seals the tale with a stanza from Ragnarsdrápa by [Bragi](/gods/bragi) Boddason, the oldest skald whose verse survives — Gefjon "glad, dragged from Gylfi the deep-ring of land... the oxen steamed with sweat" — which means the myth was already old in the ninth century. In Ynglinga saga, Snorri retells it with a political frame: Odin, migrating north, sent Gefjon ahead to scout for land; after the ploughing she married Skjöldr, Odin's son and the ancestor of the Danish royal house, and they dwelt at Lejre, the ancient seat of Danish kings. The plough-goddess thus becomes literal founding mother of Denmark — its soil imported, its dynasty her marriage.

## The goddess of unmarried women

In his catalogue of goddesses in Gylfaginning, Snorri gives Gefjon a startling second office: "she is a maiden, and those who die unmarried serve her." One sentence, no elaboration — and a small paradox, since the ploughing story gives the "maiden" four sons by a giant. Perhaps the virgin goddess and the ox-mother were originally different figures merged under one name; perhaps the Norse simply tolerated the contradiction, as they tolerated many. Either way the office is unique: the Norse afterlife sorts the dead by manner of death — battle-dead to Valhalla and [Freyja](/gods/freyja)'s Fólkvangr, the drowned to Rán, the sick and old to [Hel](/gods/hel) — and Gefjon's hall receives those who die unwed. In a society where marriage defined a woman's status, the mythology quietly reserved a goddess, and a destination, for everyone the system left out.

## What did Loki accuse Gefjon of?

In Lokasenna, the poem where Loki systematically insults every god at Ægir's feast, Gefjon speaks third — she tries to calm the quarrel — and gets the standard treatment: Loki declares that a "white boy" gave her a jewel, and she laid her thigh over him. The accusation echoes the story told of Freyja and the necklace Brísingamen closely enough that scholars have long wondered whether Gefjon is a doublet of Freyja — or of [Frigg](/gods/frigg), whose name, like Gefjon's, has been connected to giving. Her name derives from the verb gefa, "to give"; she is the Giver, kin in name to the goddess-title Gefn, which is itself one of Freyja's bynames.

What matters most in the exchange is Odin's reply. He warns Loki off: "Mad you are, Loki, to make her your enemy, for I believe she knows all the fates of men, as fully as I myself." That is a staggering credential — spoken by the god who gave an eye for wisdom — and it lifts Gefjon, in one stanza, from plough-heroine to seeress of Odin's own rank. A goddess who moves the land, keeps the unmarried dead, and sees all fates: the fragments hint at a figure once far larger than her surviving myths.

## In the primary sources

Gefjon appears in the Prose Edda (the ploughing at the opening of Gylfaginning, quoting Bragi's ninth-century Ragnarsdrápa; her place among the Ásynjur later in the same book), in the Poetic Edda (Lokasenna), and in Heimskringla's Ynglinga saga (the ploughing retold, with her marriage to Skjöldr and their seat at Lejre). A "Gefjun" also swears an oath in the Christian-era Völsa þáttr, and her name became a common noun for "goddess" or appears in oaths in Old Norse texts. Danish tradition placed her memory at Lejre; place-names such as Gävle in Sweden have been connected to her, though uncertainly.

## Legacy

Gefjon has one of the most visible legacies of any minor Norse deity: the Gefion Fountain (Gefionspringvandet) beside the harbour in Copenhagen, completed in 1908 by Anders Bundgaard, shows the goddess mid-furrow, whip raised, her four ox-sons straining in bronze — Denmark's founding myth as public monument, a short walk from the Little Mermaid. Her name is borne by Danish ships, a Copenhagen square, and an asteroid (1272 Gefion, which has given its name to a whole family of asteroids). Every map of Zealand and Mälaren is, mythically speaking, her signature.

## FAQ

**Q: What did Gefjon do in Norse mythology?**
Her great deed was ploughing the island of Zealand out of Sweden. Promised by King Gylfi as much land as four oxen could plough in a day and a night, she transformed her four giant-born sons into oxen and tore the land loose, dragging it into the sea to become Denmark's largest island; the hole became a Swedish lake.

**Q: Who serves Gefjon after death?**
According to Snorri's Gylfaginning, Gefjon is a maiden, and women who die unmarried go to serve her. She is the only Norse deity specifically assigned the unwed dead — a unique niche in an afterlife otherwise sorted by how one died.

**Q: Is Gefjon the same as Freyja or Frigg?**
Unclear, and scholars still argue. Her name means "Giver," close to Freyja's byname Gefn; Loki's accusation against her in Lokasenna mirrors the Brísingamen story told of Freyja; and Odin credits her with Frigg-like knowledge of all fates. She may be an independent old goddess, or a splinter of one of the great goddesses.

**Q: What is the Gefion Fountain?**
A monumental bronze fountain in Copenhagen, completed in 1908 by sculptor Anders Bundgaard, depicting Gefjon driving her four ox-sons at the plough. It stands near the harbour and dramatises Denmark's founding myth — the creation of Zealand.

**Q: Did Gefjon marry?**
In Snorri's Ynglinga saga she married Skjöldr, son of Odin and ancestor of the Danish royal line of Skjöldungs, and settled at Lejre. This sits oddly beside Gylfaginning's statement that she is a maiden served by the unmarried dead — one of several unresolved contradictions in her record.
