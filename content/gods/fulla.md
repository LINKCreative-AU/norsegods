Fulla walks one step behind the queen of Asgard: a virgin goddess with flowing loose hair bound by a gold band, who carries [Frigg](/gods/frigg)'s ashen coffer, keeps her footwear, and — the telling detail — "knows her secret counsel." In a pantheon where Frigg famously knows all fates and says nothing, the person she does talk to matters.

Dead [Nanna](/gods/nanna) sent Fulla a gold ring back from [Hel](/gods/hel), a gift implying rank. And she is one of the very few Norse deities attested outside Scandinavia a thousand years early: the Old High German Merseburg Charm names "Volla" beside Frija, working horse-healing magic — the same handmaiden, in the same lady's service, three centuries and a language away.

## What the sources actually say

For a figure this minor, it is worth laying the evidence on the table, because there is so little of it and every piece is interesting. Snorri's Gylfaginning, cataloguing the Ásynjur, makes Fulla the fifth: "she too is a maiden and goes with loose hair and a gold band around her head. She carries Frigg's eski" — an ashen box or coffer — "and looks after her shoes, and knows her secret counsel." That is the core attestation: an appearance, a job, and an intimacy.

The loose hair is not a throwaway detail. In the medieval North, loose, uncovered hair marked the unmarried woman — wives bound and covered theirs — so Fulla's flowing hair under its gold band is a status badge: virgin, but gold-crowned; servant, but adorned like treasure. Skaldic poets took the hint and made her headband a standard kenning: gold is "Fulla's snood" or "the falling sun of the plain of Fulla's eyelashes" — her brow itself became a poetic word for gold. Her name means "full" or "bountiful," and many scholars suspect she began as an abundance-goddess — a "Lady Plenty" — whose independent character had faded by Snorri's time into the queen's lady-in-waiting.

## Frigg's keeper of secrets

Twice the sources show what Fulla's intimacy with Frigg is worth. The first is in the prose introduction to Grímnismál in the Poetic Edda. [Odin](/gods/odin) and Frigg, quarrelling over their rival foster-sons, lay a wager, and Odin sets off to test king Geirröðr's hospitality. Frigg does not sit still: she sends Fulla to Geirröðr with a lying warning — beware a travelling wizard, recognisable because no dog is fierce enough to attack him. The king, otherwise a generous man, seizes the disguised Odin and tortures him between two fires; the god's revenge destroys Geirröðr. Fulla is thus the instrument of the one recorded occasion on which Frigg outmanoeuvres Odin — the queen's secret counsel, carried on foot.

The second is a gift. When [Hermóðr](/gods/hermodr) rode to Hel to ransom [Baldr](/gods/baldr), the dead Nanna sent presents back with him: a linen robe for Frigg — and for Fulla, a gold finger-ring. In a gift-economy, that ring is a statement: from within the halls of the dead, Baldr's wife ranks Fulla worth remembering by name, alongside the queen of heaven herself. Snorri also uses Fulla's name in Skáldskaparmál when Frigg is called "queen... of Fulla" — mistress of her, as of the sky and the goddesses.

## Volla of the Merseburg Charm

Fulla's most remarkable attestation is not Norse at all. The Second Merseburg Charm — one of only two surviving pagan spells in Old High German, written down in the tenth century but far older — tells how the horse of Balder (or of Phol) sprained its foot as the gods rode to the wood. The divine ladies charm it: "then Sinthgunt sang over it, and Sunna her sister; then Friia sang over it, and Volla her sister; then Wodan sang over it, as he well could..."

Volla beside Friia; Fulla beside Frigg. The same pair, in the same order of precedence, appear in Germany centuries before the Eddas — except that the charm calls them sisters, where Snorri makes Fulla a handmaiden. Either the relationship was demoted over time, or "sister" is loose usage, or the North and the South remembered the pairing differently; there is no way to decide. What the charm proves is precious enough: Fulla is not a late literary invention but part of the common Germanic inheritance, a goddess whom speakers of Old High German and Old Norse both knew, in the same lady's company, doing practical magic when a horse went lame.

## In the primary sources

Fulla's complete dossier: Gylfaginning (her description among the Ásynjur; the ring from Nanna in the Baldr narrative); the prose header of Grímnismál (her errand to Geirröðr); Skáldskaparmál (kennings — gold as her headband; Frigg as her queen; her name in lists of goddesses); and the Second Merseburg Charm (as Volla). She appears in no other narrative, has no known cult sites or place-names, and no myth of her own survives. Everything else said about her — including the guess that she was once a goddess of plenty — is scholarly inference from her name and her gold.

## Legacy

Fulla's legacy is a footnote, but a glittering one: through the skalds' kennings her golden headband became a word for gold itself, so that for centuries Icelandic poetry paid her a compliment every time it mentioned treasure. For historians of religion she is a key exhibit in arguments about the deep age of the Norse goddesses, thanks to Merseburg. Her modern commemorations are otherwise modest — she appears in retellings of Frigg's court, and in the ongoing scholarly debate about the lost goddesses of plenty that she quietly anchors.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is Fulla in Norse mythology?**
Fulla is a virgin goddess who serves as Frigg's closest attendant and confidante. Snorri describes her with loose hair bound by a gold band; she carries Frigg's coffer, keeps her shoes, and shares her secret counsel.

**Q: What is Fulla's connection to the Merseburg Charms?**
The Second Merseburg Charm, an Old High German healing spell, names "Volla" as the sister of Friia (Frigg) working magic to heal a horse's sprained leg. Most scholars identify Volla with Fulla, making her one of the very few Norse deities independently attested in early medieval Germany.

**Q: Is Fulla Frigg's sister or servant?**
The sources disagree. Snorri's Gylfaginning presents her as Frigg's handmaiden who carries her coffer, while the Merseburg Charm calls Volla the sister of Friia. Whether her status changed over the centuries or the two traditions simply differ cannot be determined.

**Q: What did Nanna send Fulla from Hel?**
A gold finger-ring. When Hermóðr rode to Hel to plead for Baldr's release, Baldr's dead wife Nanna sent gifts back to Asgard — a linen robe for Frigg and a ring for Fulla — a detail that signals Fulla's high standing among the goddesses.

**Q: What does the name Fulla mean?**
It derives from the word for "full," suggesting "bountiful one" or "plenty." Many scholars therefore suspect Fulla was originally a goddess of abundance whose wider role had faded by the time the Eddas were written, leaving her as the gold-crowned keeper of Frigg's secrets.
