Bragi is the skald of the gods: wisest in the ways of words, with runes said to be carved on his tongue. In [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) he greets the arriving battle-slain with verse. He is married to [Iðunn](/gods/idun), keeper of the apples of youth — poetry wed to immortality, as the skalds liked it.

He may descend from a real ninth-century poet, Bragi Boddason, deified by later tradition — or the poet may have been named for the god; scholars still argue. In [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna), [Loki](/gods/loki) needles him as the least warlike of the Æsir, "wariest of war and shyest of shots" — Bragi's weapon was never the sword.

## The court poet of Asgard

Snorri introduces him in Gylfaginning with unreserved praise: Bragi is renowned for wisdom and above all for eloquence and command of language; he knows most about skaldcraft, and from him poetry itself — bragr — takes its name, so that an outstanding man or woman is called a bragr of their kind. Whether the god named the art or the art named the god is, characteristically, uncertain even at the level of the dictionary. His long beard was proverbial enough that Snorri records "the bearded one" among his bynames, and the image of runes carved on his tongue — recorded in the later Icelandic tradition — makes him almost an allegory walking: language itself, inscribed on the organ of speech.

His station is [Odin](/gods/odin)'s hall. In the tenth-century poem Eiríksmál, composed for the death of the Norwegian king Eirík Bloodaxe, Odin hears a thunder of approaching hosts and asks Bragi what it is; Bragi guesses it might be [Baldr](/gods/baldr) returning, and Odin corrects him — it is Eirík, and Bragi is to go out and welcome the king into Valhalla. The parallel poem Hákonarmál gives the same office to Bragi and [Hermóðr](/gods/hermodr) together, sent to greet King Hákon the Good. This is Bragi's job in the divine economy: when the [einherjar](/concepts/einherjar) arrive dead and disoriented at Odin's door, the first voice they hear is the poet's, making their death mean something in well-chosen words.

Skáldskaparmál — Snorri's whole treatise on poetics — is framed as Bragi's after-dinner conversation, the god of poetry explaining to the sea-giant [Ægir](/giants/aegir), guest at a feast of the gods, where the art comes from. It is Bragi's voice that tells the murder of [Kvasir](/gods/kvasir) and the theft of the [Mead of Poetry](/events/mead-of-poetry), Bragi who unfolds the system of [kennings](/concepts/kenning) that ran skaldic verse. The mythology's user manual for poetry is placed, fittingly, in the poet's mouth.

## Bragi and Loki

Bragi's one dramatic scene is a bruising. In Lokasenna, when Loki forces his way into Ægir's feast, Bragi is the first god to speak against seating him — and therefore the first target. Loki works down the benches insulting everyone, but Bragi he dismisses with special contempt: of all the Æsir and elves in the hall, Bragi is "wariest of war and shyest of shots." Bragi answers with a striking offer — a horse, a sword, and a ring from his own goods, compensation to buy peace before Loki earns the gods' hatred — the poet trying to settle a feud the way feuds were lawfully settled, with payment instead of blood. Loki takes it as cowardice and dares him to fight; Bragi says that were they outside Ægir's hallowed hall, he would carry Loki's head in his hand. Iðunn calms her husband, and the storm moves on to the next bench. Readers have argued ever since about who wins the exchange: Loki lands the insult, but Bragi is the only god in the poem who tries a poet's solution — words and gifts — before anyone reaches for the hammer.

## Was Bragi a real person?

Uniquely among the Norse gods, Bragi may have a birth certificate. Bragi Boddason "the Old," a Norwegian skald of the early ninth century, is the earliest poet whose verses survive — his Ragnarsdrápa describes mythological scenes, including [Thor](/gods/thor)'s fishing for [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), painted on a shield. He was famous enough that later tradition imagined him among the honoured dead: Eiríksmál's Bragi in Valhalla could be read as either the god or the deified first skald, and at least one saga anecdote has Bragi Boddason talking his way out of a death sentence with a poem, exactly as his divine namesake would.

So which came first? Some scholars hold that the historical skald was so towering that he was promoted into the pantheon within a couple of centuries — which would make Bragi the only Norse god with a documented human original. Others argue the reverse: bragr, "poetry, the foremost," was an old divine name or title (perhaps even a byname of Odin, who is himself called "god of poets"), and the ninth-century poet was named after it. The evidence genuinely underdetermines the answer, and both positions have been defended for over a century. What is clear is that by the time [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal) lists him — Odin, in that poem, names Bragi best of skalds as Yggdrasil is best of trees and Sleipnir best of horses — he is fully a god, whatever he had been before.

## In the primary sources

Bragi appears in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) in Lokasenna and in Grímnismál's catalogue of bests, and in Sigrdrífumál's rune-lore, which mentions runes carved "on Bragi's tongue" among many holy places runes were scored. In the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), Gylfaginning sketches his character, and Skáldskaparmál makes him narrator of the great poetry-myths and source of kennings — poetry is "Bragi's speech." The memorial poems Eiríksmál and Hákonarmál station him in Valhalla among Odin's chosen. He has no known cult sites, no place-names, no temples: the god of poetry lives entirely in poems, which is either an irony or exactly as it should be.

## Legacy

Bragi's name soaked into the vocabulary of honour: the bragarfull, the "chieftain's cup" or "toast of the foremost," was drunk at feasts with solemn vows, and though the custom's link to the god is debated, medieval writers made the connection. Modern Scandinavian and Icelandic usage keeps bragur alive as a word for verse and melody, and "brag" in English — tempting as the link sounds — is unrelated, a folk-etymology that refuses to die. Wherever Norse myth is retold, Bragi remains the patron figure of the teller: the one whose battlefield is the bench, and whose sword is on his tongue.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Bragi the god of?**
Poetry, eloquence, and the skald's craft. Snorri says he is famed for wisdom and skill with language, that poetry (bragr) is named from him, and that he knows most of skaldcraft; in Valhalla he welcomes the slain with verse.

**Q: Was Bragi a real historical person?**
Possibly. Bragi Boddason, the earliest skald whose poetry survives (ninth century), may have been deified by later tradition — or he may have been named for an already-existing god. Scholars have argued both ways for generations, and the sources do not settle it.

**Q: Who is Bragi's wife?**
Iðunn, the goddess who keeps the apples of youth. The pairing of poetry and everlasting freshness was a favourite of the skalds, and in Lokasenna she stands beside him at the feast, restraining his anger at Loki.

**Q: Why does Loki mock Bragi?**
In Lokasenna Loki brands him the least warlike of the gods — "wariest of war and shyest of shots." Bragi had offered Loki a horse, a ring, and a sword as peace-payment, a poet's lawful settlement, which Loki chose to read as cowardice. Bragi's realm was words, not weapons.
