Ullr glides through the surviving myths mostly as a name: stepson of [Thor](/gods/thor), son of [Sif](/gods/sif), dweller in Ýdalir ("Yew-dales" — yew being the bowyer's wood), beautiful, warlike, the one to invoke before a duel. Oaths were sworn on his ring, and one poem calls a shield "Ullr's ship", hinting at a lost tale of the god crossing water on his shield — or skiing on it.

The archaeology of names tells a bigger story than the texts: Ullr place-names (Ulleråker, Ullevi, Ullern) blanket central Sweden and southeast Norway, marking him as a major god of an older stratum whose myths were already fading when the Eddas were written down. Modern skiers have re-adopted him as their unofficial patron.

## What the texts remember

Snorri's Gylfaginning gives Ullr one packed paragraph: son of Sif, stepson of Thor; so excellent a bowman and ski-runner that none may contend with him; beautiful to look at besides, and with a warrior's accomplishments — and good to call on in single combat. Every clause is suggestive and none is developed. His father is never named anywhere, a blank space beside his mother's famous golden hair. [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal) assigns him the first-listed of the gods' dwellings: Ýdalir, "Yew-dales," they call the place where Ullr has made his halls — and the address is a character sketch in itself, since yew was the wood of the great bows, and the poem's catalogue begins with him as if by old habit of precedence.

Two stray gleams suggest lost importance. In Grímnismál, the tormented Odin says that whoever pulls him from between the fires will have the favour "of Ullr and of all the gods" — a formula that pairs Ullr alone with the whole pantheon, as one names the chief god first. And in [Atlakviða](/texts/poetic-edda), one of the very oldest heroic poems, Guðrún reminds Atli of oaths sworn "by the ring of Ullr" — the god of the sacred oath-ring, an office of real cultic weight. Skáldskaparmál rounds out the file: poets may call him ski-god, bow-god, hunting-god, shield-god — and the shield itself is "Ullr's ship," a kenning pointing to a story, now utterly lost, of the god crossing water on his shield or gliding on it like a ski or sled. That is nearly everything. Ullr appears in no surviving narrative myth at all; he is a portrait without a biography.

## The god the map remembers

What the manuscripts forgot, Scandinavian geography kept. Place-names built on Ullr — and its variant Ullinn — lie thick across central Sweden and southeastern Norway: Ulleråker ("Ullr's field") and Ullevi ("Ullr's sanctuary," a name now attached to Gothenburg's famous stadium) in Sweden; Ullern, Ullensaker, and Ullensvang in Norway. Many are compounded with words for temple, grove, and holy field — the vocabulary of organised cult. Their density and distribution mark Ullr as a major god of an older religious stratum, worshipped intensively in exactly the regions where the compounds cluster, at a period well before the Viking Age literature that barely mentions him. The mismatch between his textual poverty and his toponymic wealth is the standard textbook example of how incomplete the Eddas are as a record of Norse religion: gods rose and faded over the centuries, and Ullr seems to have been sinking toward honourable obscurity just as the poems were being fixed.

Saxo Grammaticus supplies the one narrative scrap, in Latin dress: his Ollerus was a cunning wizard who took over the rule — and even the name — of Othinus (Odin) for a time while the true god was in exile, until Odin returned and drove him out; Ollerus, Saxo adds, could cross the sea on a bone marked with spells, faster than a ship. A bone that carries you over water sounds much like a skate or ski, and the tale of a god who once held Odin's place chimes with the hints of former precedence in the poems. How much genuine tradition lies under Saxo's rationalising is, as ever with him, uncertain — but it is the only story anyone recorded about Ullr, and it is a story about lost supremacy.

## Winter's patron

Ullr's portfolio hangs together beautifully once seen whole: skis and bow are the equipment of the northern winter hunter, yew the wood that serves both (skis and bows alike were made from it), and the duel and the oath-ring belong to the same world of formalised combat in which a god "good to call on in single combat" earns his keep. He pairs naturally with [Skaði](/gods/skadi), the ski-goddess and bow-huntress of the mountains — the sources never connect them beyond the shared craft, but together they show how central winter travel and winter hunting were to Scandinavian life: important enough to need gods on both the Æsir and jötunn sides. Where Thor guarded the farm and [Freyr](/gods/freyr) the harvest, Ullr governed the half of the year when the fields slept and survival moved on skis.

He has no recorded part in [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — no station, no opponent, no death. Whether his ending was never told or simply never written down, the god of winter is absent from the world's last winter, the [Fimbulvetr](/concepts/fimbulvetr), and that silence is of a piece with everything else about him.

## In the primary sources

Grímnismál (Ýdalir; the "favour of Ullr" formula) and Atlakviða (the oath-ring) carry him in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda); Gylfaginning and Skáldskaparmál in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) give the portrait, the kennings, and "Ullr's ship" for the shield. Saxo's [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum) tells the Ollerus usurpation. Beyond the texts stand the place-names of Sweden and Norway and a striking small find: the third-century Thorsberg chape bears a runic inscription often read as owlþuþewaz, "servant of Ullr" — if that reading holds, he is among the earliest Germanic gods attested by name.

## Legacy

Ullr's sanctuaries became farm and parish names that Scandinavians still live in — and one became a football stadium. In the twentieth century, alpine ski culture adopted him wholeheartedly: "Ullr fests" pray for snow in resort towns from Norway to Colorado, and Ullr medallions dangle from ski jackets as the unofficial patron saint of powder. It is a fate with its own poetry — the god the sagas forgot, remembered by everyone who waxes a pair of skis.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Ullr the god of?**
Skiing, archery, hunting, and single combat. Snorri calls him a bowman and ski-runner without equal, beautiful and warlike, the god to invoke before a duel; Atlakviða adds that solemn oaths were sworn on Ullr's ring.

**Q: Who are Ullr's parents?**
His mother is Sif, Thor's golden-haired wife, making him Thor's stepson. His father is never named in any surviving source — one of the more conspicuous blanks in the mythology.

**Q: Why are there so few myths about Ullr?**
He seems to belong to an older layer of Scandinavian religion whose stories had already faded by the time the Eddas were written in the thirteenth century. The dense spread of Ullr place-names across Sweden and Norway shows he was once a major cult god, whatever the texts forgot.

**Q: Is Ullr really the god of skiing?**
As near as the sources allow: Snorri names him a peerless ski-runner and archer, the classic equipment of the northern winter. Modern ski culture's adoption of him as patron — Ullr festivals, medallions, snow prayers — is a revival, but it rests on genuine Old Norse attestation.

**Q: What does the kenning "Ullr's ship" mean?**
It is a skaldic name for the shield, implying a lost myth in which Ullr crossed water on his shield — or glided on it like a ski or sledge. Saxo's tale of Ollerus crossing the sea on a spell-marked bone may echo the same story.
