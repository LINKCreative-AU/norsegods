Magni ("Strength") and Móði ("Courage") are [Thor](/gods/thor)'s virtues walking as his sons. Magni announced himself at three nights old — actually, some manuscripts say three years — by lifting the giant Hrungnir's leg off his pinned father when no other god could, earning the dead giant's golden-maned horse Gullfaxi as a prize, to [Odin](/gods/odin)'s visible annoyance.

Their real role waits past the end: after [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), Vafþrúðnismál says, "Móði and Magni shall have Mjölnir" — the hammer outlives the hand. Thunder is not abolished by the world's ending; it is inherited.

## Magni and the duel with Hrungnir

Magni's one recorded deed comes at the end of the mythology's greatest duel, told in Snorri's Skáldskaparmál. Hrungnir, strongest of the jötnar, had drunk himself into boasting in Asgard — he would kill all the gods and carry off [Freyja](/gods/freyja) and [Sif](/gods/sif) — until the gods called for Thor, and a formal duel was set at Grjótúnagarðar, on the border of the giants' land. Hrungnir stood waiting with a stone shield and a whetstone for a weapon; Thor came at him in a storm of lightning. The whetstone met Mjölnir mid-air and shattered — one piece lodging in Thor's head, where it remained — and the hammer went on to smash the giant's skull. But Hrungnir fell forward, and his enormous leg came down across Thor's neck, pinning him to the ground.

Then comes the scene the myth exists for. The gods gathered and heaved at the leg, and not one of them could shift it. Up came Magni, son of Thor and the giantess Járnsaxa — three nights old in Snorri's main text, though manuscripts disagree and some say three years (either way the point is his absurd infancy) — and threw the leg off alone, remarking that it was a shame he had come so late: had he met Hrungnir first, he would have knocked the giant into Hel with his bare fist. Thor, delighted, gave the boy Hrungnir's horse Gullfaxi, "Golden-mane," a steed nearly the equal of Sleipnir. Odin was displeased, Snorri notes, that Thor gave such a horse "to the son of a giantess" instead of to his own father — a flash of Allfather pettiness the story does not bother to resolve.

The episode is a compact essay on inheritance. Thor's strength does not merely continue in his son; it arrives already exceeding him, doing what the whole assembled pantheon could not. And Magni's mother is a jötunn — the defender of the gods against the giants fathers his heir on one, and the heir is stronger for it.

## Who is Móði?

Honesty first: Móði has no myth of his own. No source records his mother (Járnsaxa is named only as Magni's), no deed, no dwelling. He exists in three kinds of evidence: the prophecy in Vafþrúðnismál that pairs him with Magni as inheritor of Mjölnir; kennings — Thor is called "Móði's father" in Hymiskviða's account of the fishing for the World Serpent; and name-lists. That is all. His name, "the Angry One" or "Courage" — the battle-wrath — makes the brothers a matched pair of abstractions: Strength and Fury, the two components of Thor himself, walking as his sons. Some scholars think Móði was always only a poetic double, coined to alliterate beside Magni and Mjölnir; others assume lost stories. The texts permit either view.

## At Ragnarök: the hammer inherited

The brothers' true significance lies on the far side of the world's end. In Vafþrúðnismál, Odin asks the wise giant which of the Æsir will rule over the possessions of the gods "when Surtr's fire is slaked." The answer: [Víðarr](/gods/vidar) and [Váli](/gods/vali) will dwell in the gods' sanctuaries, "and Móði and Magni shall have Mjölnir, at the end of Vingnir's battle" — Vingnir being Thor. Snorri repeats it in Gylfaginning: on Iðavöllr, where Asgard once stood, the surviving young gods gather — Víðarr and Váli, Magni and Móði bringing the hammer, and [Baldr](/gods/baldr) and [Höðr](/gods/hodr) returned from [Hel](/gods/hel).

Thor himself does not survive; he kills the World Serpent and falls nine steps later to its venom. But Mjölnir — the weapon that hallowed weddings and warded Midgard — passes to Strength and Courage. It is one of the mythology's most deliberate consolations: the gods die, the world burns, and the thunder is not abolished but inherited. The generation of vengeance and defence gives way to a generation that simply carries the tools forward into a world that may not need them the same way.

## In the primary sources

Magni and Móði are attested in the Poetic Edda in Vafþrúðnismál (the inheritance of Mjölnir), with Magni also named in Hárbarðsljóð (Odin, disguised as the ferryman, taunts Thor while mentioning Magni) and Móði in a kenning in Hymiskviða; and in the Prose Edda in Skáldskaparmál (the Hrungnir duel and Magni's feat; both names in kennings for Thor) and Gylfaginning (the survivors on Iðavöllr). Neither brother has any known cult, place-name, or independent saga tradition; they live in the poetry as Thor's continuation.

## Legacy

The brothers' modern fame is mostly borrowed from their father — they appear wherever Thor's story is retold at length, from scholarly handbooks to comics and games that need an heir for the hammer. In Norse studies they serve as a textbook case of "transparent" gods: figures whose names are common nouns (magni, "might"; móðr, "wrath, courage"), suggesting how easily the mythology could personify a quality and set it walking. Magni's name survives as an ordinary male name in Iceland and the Faroes to this day.

## FAQ

**Q: Who are Magni and Móði in Norse mythology?**
They are the sons of the thunder-god Thor. Magni ("Strength") is his son by the giantess Járnsaxa; Móði ("Courage" or "Wrath") has no recorded mother. Together they are prophesied to inherit Thor's hammer Mjölnir after Ragnarök.

**Q: What did Magni do as an infant?**
When Thor killed the giant Hrungnir in a duel but was pinned beneath the dead giant's leg, none of the gods could free him. Magni — three nights old in Snorri's telling — lifted the leg off alone and said he could have felled Hrungnir with his fist. Thor rewarded him with the giant's golden-maned horse Gullfaxi.

**Q: Do Magni and Móði survive Ragnarök?**
Yes. Vafþrúðnismál and Gylfaginning both name them among the survivors who gather on Iðavöllr in the renewed world, where they possess Mjölnir. Thor himself dies killing the World Serpent, but his hammer passes to his sons.

**Q: Who is Magni's mother?**
The giantess Járnsaxa ("Iron-knife"), according to Skáldskaparmál. This makes Thor's heir half-jötunn — a detail that annoyed Odin when Thor gave the boy Hrungnir's prize horse — and it echoes how often the gods' strength is renewed from the giants' side of the family.

**Q: What do the names Magni and Móði mean?**
Magni means "Might" or "Strength," and Móði means "Wrath" or "Courage" — the two ingredients of Thor's own character personified as his sons. Many scholars read the brothers less as fully developed gods than as Thor's essential qualities given names and an inheritance.
