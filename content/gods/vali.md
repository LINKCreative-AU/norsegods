When [Baldr](/gods/baldr) fell, vengeance could not come from within the family circle that shared the guilt of the game, so [Odin](/gods/odin) begot a new son on the giantess Rindr. Váli was born, and at one night old — "he washed not his hands nor combed his hair" — he killed [Höðr](/gods/hodr), the blind brother whose hand [Loki](/gods/loki) had guided. He is a god who exists because grief demanded an instrument.

With his half-brother [Víðarr](/gods/vidar) he walks out of the fire of [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) into the renewed world — the avenger and the silent one, the two single-purpose sons of Odin, outliving everything they were made to answer for.

## The vengeance foretold

Váli's birth is prophesied before it happens, which tells you how the Norse understood it: not as a choice but as a mechanism. In Baldrs draumar, Odin rides to the edge of Hel's realm and wakes a dead seeress to ask who will kill Baldr — and who will avenge him. Her answer is precise: Rindr will bear Váli "in western halls," and that son of Odin will fight when one night old. He will not wash his hands nor comb his hair until he has borne Baldr's slayer to the funeral pyre. Völuspá repeats the image almost word for word. The unwashed hands and uncombed hair are a formula of sworn vengeance: the avenger suspends all ordinary life — even grooming — until the debt of blood is paid.

The logic behind his birth is stern Norse legal thinking. Baldr's killer, Höðr, was himself a son of Odin and a brother of the slain; the gods who had made a game of throwing weapons at the invulnerable Baldr all shared in the disaster. Vengeance from inside that circle would only compound the guilt. So a new agent had to be created — a son with no part in the game, no bond to the killer, no history at all. Váli was one night old when he did the only thing he was born to do. The blind instrument of Loki's malice was struck down by the newborn instrument of Odin's grief, and neither of them was the true author of his own act.

## Who was Rindr, Váli's mother?

The Eddas say almost nothing about her beyond her name and her role — a giantess, or in some tellings a goddess, whom Snorri lists among the Ásynjur. It is the Danish historian Saxo Grammaticus, writing around 1200, who preserves a fuller and far darker story. In his Gesta Danorum, Rinda is a human princess, daughter of the king of the Ruthenians, and Othinus (Odin) learns from seers that only her son can avenge the dead Balderus. She refuses him again and again; Odin resorts to disguises and finally to trickery and coercion in the guise of a healing-woman, fathering the avenger — called Bous in Saxo — against her will. For practising this shameful magic, Saxo says, the other gods drove Odin into exile for years. The kernel — a mother won by dishonourable means solely to produce an avenger — is echoed in a skaldic phrase attributed to the poet Kormákr: "Odin worked seiðr on Rindr." The myth remembers that Váli's begetting was not a love story.

## One god, two Vális

A source of endless confusion: the name Váli appears twice in the mythology. Besides Odin's son, Gylfaginning names a Váli among the sons of Loki. When the gods finally capture Loki after Baldr's death, they turn this Váli into a wolf, and he tears apart his own brother Narfi — whose entrails become Loki's fetters. Whether Snorri (or his sources) confused two figures, or the poets deliberately gave the avenging role a double echo inside Loki's own family, is a genuine scholarly puzzle. The prose conclusion of Lokasenna keeps the same grim scene but swaps the names' roles. Readers should simply know: Váli son of Odin avenges Baldr; the wolf-Váli of Loki's line belongs to Loki's punishment, and the two are distinct.

## At Ragnarök

Váli fights in the last battle and comes out the other side. Vafþrúðnismál, the wisdom-contest between Odin and the giant Vafþrúðnir, names him among the survivors: "Víðarr and Váli will dwell in the sanctuaries of the gods, when Surtr's fire is slaked." Snorri repeats it in Gylfaginning: the two half-brothers, joined by Thor's sons [Magni and Móði](/gods/magni-and-modi) bearing Mjölnir, meet on the field of Iðavöllr where Asgard once stood, and Baldr and Höðr return from [Hel](/gods/hel) to join them — the avenger and the man he killed, reconciled in a world where the old blood-debt no longer means anything.

## In the primary sources

Váli's attestations are brief but consistent. In the Poetic Edda he appears in Völuspá (the vengeance stanza), Baldrs draumar (the prophecy of his birth; the poem's final riddle may also allude to Rindr's refusal), Vafþrúðnismál (his survival), and Hyndluljóð, which names him among Odin's sons. In the Prose Edda, Gylfaginning describes him as bold in battle and a very sure shot, and counts him among the twelve Æsir; Skáldskaparmál gives kennings for him — "son of Odin and Rindr," "Baldr's avenging brother," "enemy and slayer of Höðr." Saxo Grammaticus tells the fullest version of his conception, with the avenger renamed Bous.

## Legacy

Váli left little trace outside the texts — no certain place-names, no known cult. His legacy is conceptual: he is the Norse ethic of vengeance distilled into a person, and his survival into the reborn world, standing beside the resurrected Höðr, is one of the mythology's quietest statements that the cycle of retribution ends only when the world that required it does. The claim sometimes met with that the month name "Valentine" or the Icelandic Váli-forms survive him is modern invention; only the poems keep his name.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is Váli in Norse mythology?**
Váli is a son of Odin and the giantess Rindr, conceived for a single purpose: to avenge the death of Baldr. He killed the blind god Höðr when he was only one night old, and he is one of the few gods who survives Ragnarök.

**Q: Why did Váli kill Höðr?**
Höðr had thrown the mistletoe dart that killed Baldr — unknowingly, with Loki guiding his hand. Norse custom demanded blood-vengeance, but the gods closest to Baldr were entangled in the deed, so Odin fathered a new son to carry it out. Váli slew Höðr, fulfilling the prophecy that he would neither wash nor comb his hair until Baldr's slayer burned on the pyre.

**Q: How old was Váli when he avenged Baldr?**
One night old. Both Völuspá and Baldrs draumar state that the son of Odin and Rindr fought at the age of one night — a deliberately superhuman detail marking him as vengeance personified rather than an ordinary child.

**Q: Does Váli survive Ragnarök?**
Yes. Vafþrúðnismál and Snorri's Gylfaginning both name Váli, with his half-brother Víðarr, among the gods who live on after Surtr's fire and inhabit the renewed world, where Baldr and Höðr also return from the dead.

**Q: Is Váli the same as Loki's son Váli?**
No. Gylfaginning also mentions a Váli who is a son of Loki, transformed by the gods into a wolf so that he kills his brother Narfi, whose entrails then bind Loki. The shared name has caused confusion since the Middle Ages, but Odin's avenging son and Loki's wolf-son are separate figures.
