Hel is the appointed queen of the unglorious dead. Daughter of [Loki](/gods/loki) and the giantess [Angrboða](/giants/angrboda), sister of [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) and [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), she was thrown by [Odin](/gods/odin) into [Niflheimr](/realms/niflheim) and given authority over nine worlds' worth of the dead — all who die of sickness, age, and mischance rather than battle. Half of her body is that of a living woman, half the blue-black of a corpse; her expression is described as grim and downcast.

Her hall is Éljúðnir ("Rain-damp"); her dish is called Hunger, her knife Famine, her threshold Stumbling-block, her bed Sick-bed. When [Hermóðr](/gods/hermodr) rode to her to ransom [Baldr](/gods/baldr), she set the terms — all creation must weep — and when one giantess refused, she kept him. At [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) the dead of her realm sail against the gods aboard the ship [Naglfar](/artifacts/naglfar), made of dead men's nails.

## The child the gods exiled

Hel's story begins with a prophecy and a preemptive strike. Gylfaginning tells how Loki fathered three children on the giantess Angrboða in [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim) — the wolf, the serpent, and Hel — and how the gods, learning from prophecy that great misfortune would come to them from these siblings, had them fetched to [Asgard](/realms/asgard). Odin dealt with each: the serpent was hurled into the sea that rings [Midgard](/realms/midgard), the wolf was raised at home until he grew too fearsome and was [bound](/events/binding-of-fenrir) — and Hel was cast down into Niflheim, the world of cold and mist, and given authority over nine worlds, to allot lodgings to all those sent to her: those who die of sickness and those who die of old age.

Read closely, the exile is also an enthronement. Alone of the three siblings, Hel was not chained or drowned but given a kingdom and a legal office — the queen of a jurisdiction the gods needed someone to run. Her monstrousness is administrative rather than violent: no myth shows Hel raging, hunting, or killing. She receives. And the gods' pre-emptive justice against Loki's children carries its usual Norse irony — by treating the three as enemies before they had acted, the gods guaranteed the enmity, and all three will be waiting at the world's end.

Snorri's description of her person is the most memorable portrait in his catalogue: she is half blue-black and half flesh-colour, so that she is easily known; rather drooping and grim she looks. Half a living woman, half a corpse — walking evidence of her office, the border between the living and dead drawn down the middle of one body. Whether her divided body is ancient myth or Snorri's own vivid systematising is debated; the skalds before him call her simply the goddess or giantess of the grave.

## The furnished house of death

No other Norse dwelling is inventoried like Hel's, and the inventory is a poem of attrition. Her hall is Éljúðnir, "sprayed with snowstorms" or "rain-damp"; her dish is Hungr, Hunger; her knife Sultr, Famine; her serving-man Ganglati and serving-maid Ganglöt, both meaning something like "slow-moving"; her threshold is called Fallanda forað, Stumbling-block; her bed is Kör, Sick-bed; and her bed-hangings Blíkjanda böl, Gleaming-bale. Everything in the house is the process of dying, furnished. It is bureaucratic horror rather than torture: the realm below is not punishment but continuation at the lowest ebb, hunger and sickness made into household objects.

Her realm — [Helheim](/realms/helheim), often simply "Hel," so that queen and country share a name — lies downward and northward, beneath one of the three roots of [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil), hard by [Hvergelmir](/concepts/hvergelmir), where the dragon [Níðhöggr](/creatures/nidhogg) gnaws the root and chews corpses. The road there, the Helvegr, runs nine nights through valleys dark and deep, over the river Gjöll on its golden-roofed bridge kept by the maiden [Móðguð](/creatures/modgud), and through a gate that shuts behind the dead. Within her wider territories lies the worst address in the cosmology: [Náströnd](/concepts/nastrond), the corpse-shore, its hall woven of serpents' backs dripping venom, where oath-breakers and murderers wade — though [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) assigns that horror to fate at large rather than to Hel's personal cruelty. Importantly, ordinary death in Hel carries no such judgment: the mythology's real scandal, to modern eyes, is that allocation runs not on virtue but on manner of death — battle sends you up to [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) or [Fólkvangr](/concepts/folkvangr), while the straw-death of age or fever sends even the good downward.

## Hel and Baldr: the queen's one scene

Hel speaks in exactly one story, and she owns it. When Baldr — best of the gods — was killed by the mistletoe and burned on [Hringhorni](/artifacts/hringhorni), the gods sent Hermóðr on [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir) down the nine-night road to buy him back. He leapt the gate itself, walked into Éljúðnir, and found Baldr enthroned in the high seat — death's queen had given the shining god the seat of honour. Hermóðr pleaded through the night: all the gods weep, all the world grieves; let Baldr ride home.

Hel's answer is the finest piece of cold statecraft in the mythology. It must be tested, she said, whether Baldr is as beloved as report makes him: if all things in the world, quick and dead, weep for him, he shall return to the Æsir — but he stays with Hel if any one thing speaks against it or will not weep. It is a condition that looks generous and is actually sovereign: she sets a standard that only a world without a single grudge could meet, and the world contains Loki. Everything wept — people, beasts, earth, stones, metals — until the messengers found a giantess in a cave, Þökk, who refused, and the [death of Baldr](/events/death-of-baldr) became permanent. Hel kept her guest, by the letter of her own law. Nothing in the text shows her cheating, gloating, or relenting; she is the one power in the mythology whose rulings even the gods cannot appeal. Odin himself, in [Baldrs draumar](/texts/baldrs-draumar), does not negotiate with Hel — he sneaks into her realm to necromance a dead seeress instead, and even so learns only that the benches are already decked for Baldr, the mead already brewed. Death, in the North, keeps an efficient front office.

## Is Hel a goddess or a place?

Both, and the doubling is old. In the poems, "Hel" is most often the location — to "go to Hel" or "fare to Hel" is simply to die, an idiom so worn that it survives in modern Scandinavian cursing. The personified queen with her divided face appears chiefly in Snorri, and some scholars therefore argue that Hel the person is largely a literary creation, a personification of the grave spun out of the place-name — pointing out that she has no known cult, no temples, no place-names, and no worshippers anywhere in the record. Others answer that kennings older than Snorri already treat her as a person — the skalds call death "Hel's embrace" and Baldr "Hel's companion," and Loki's daughter needed a name — and that a death-goddess beside the death-realm matches patterns across the Germanic world. The sources permit no verdict; what is certain is that the word came first in every reader's experience — English "hell" itself is her exact cognate, from the root "to cover, conceal" — and the queen condensed out of the cold of it.

## At Ragnarök

Hel does not fight — her subjects do. When the end comes, Naglfar, the ship built through the ages from dead men's uncut nails, comes loose on the flood, and Loki steers it; with him, in Snorri's account, come Hel's people — the loosed dead sailing against the gods their deaths never glorified. Völuspá's vision has the ship come from the east with Muspell's host, and the sources differ over who exactly crews and captains it; either way, the underworld empties upward, and the queen's father leads her subjects to the field of [Vígríðr](/concepts/vigrid). Of Hel herself at Ragnarök the texts say nothing: she neither marches nor falls. When the fires of [Surtr](/giants/surtr) have burned out and Baldr walks back into the green world, he comes "from Hel" — her gates open at last in the general amnesty of the world's rebirth, and the queen of the dead recedes, unkilled and unaccounted for, into the silence she came from.

## In the primary sources

Gylfaginning in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) carries nearly everything: her parentage and exile, the divided body, the furnished hall, and the Baldr negotiation. In the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Völuspá knows her realm and its horrors, Baldrs draumar takes Odin down her road, and [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal) places her under Yggdrasil's third root. Skáldskaparmál authorises the kennings — Loki is "father of Hel," death her embrace. Saxo's [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum) glimpses her Danish shadow: dying Balderus dreams of Proserpina, the Latin underworld-queen, come to claim him. The Old English cognate hell, meanwhile, shows how deep and pre-Viking the root is.

## Legacy

Hel's is the strangest afterlife of any Norse deity: her name, through the shared Germanic root, became the English word "hell," and Christian preaching in Scandinavia poured its fiery afterworld into her cold one until the borrowed name overwrote the older geography of mist. "Go to Hel" endures in every Scandinavian language as profanity with a thousand-year pedigree. Modern fantasy, comics, and games return to her constantly — the half-dead queen being one of mythology's most arresting designs — usually with more malice than the sources ever gave the punctilious, grim-faced administrator of the ordinary dead.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is Hel in Norse mythology?**
The daughter of Loki and the giantess Angrboða, sister of Fenrir and Jörmungandr. Odin cast her into Niflheim and made her ruler over the dead who die of sickness, age, and accident. Snorri describes her as half living woman, half blue-black corpse, grim and downcast.

**Q: Is Hel evil?**
The sources never show her doing evil. She rules a cold realm and drives a hard bargain — Baldr's release for the tears of all creation — but she keeps her own terms exactly. Her grimness is that of an office, not a villain; the malice in her story belongs to Loki.

**Q: What is the difference between Hel and Valhalla?**
Destination by manner of death, not merit. Those slain in battle go to Odin's Valhalla or Freyja's Fólkvangr; those who die of sickness, old age, or mishap go down to Hel's realm. Hel is not a punishment — though the corpse-shore Náströnd, for oath-breakers and murderers, lies in the same lower world.

**Q: Is the English word "hell" related to Hel?**
Yes — both come from the same Germanic root meaning "to cover or conceal." Old English hell and Old Norse Hel are cognates; when Scandinavia converted, the Christian afterlife took over the native name, which is why the goddess and the modern word shadow each other.

**Q: What happens to Hel at Ragnarök?**
The sources are silent about Hel herself. Her dead sail against the gods aboard Naglfar with Loki at the helm, and after the world is reborn Baldr and Höðr return "from Hel" — but the queen's own fate is never told. She neither fights nor falls in any surviving text.
