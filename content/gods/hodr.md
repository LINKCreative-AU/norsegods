Höðr is the tragedy of the pantheon: blind, strong, and used. When the gods amused themselves hurling weapons at the invulnerable [Baldr](/gods/baldr), [Loki](/gods/loki) placed a mistletoe dart in Höðr's hand and aimed his throw. Baldr fell dead, and Höðr became a fratricide without intent.

Vengeance came swiftly and strangely: [Odin](/gods/odin) fathered [Váli](/gods/vali) for the sole purpose of avenging Baldr, and Váli — one night old — slew Höðr. In the world after [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), the myth grants its gentlest image: Baldr and Höðr return together from [Hel](/realms/helheim), reconciled, to dwell in the new world. In Saxo Grammaticus's [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum), the story mutates entirely: Høtherus is a sighted human warrior who rivals the demigod Balderus for a princess.

## The weapon with a heartbeat

Snorri's introduction of Höðr in Gylfaginning is two sentences of foreboding: he is blind, but abundantly strong — and the gods would prefer that this god never needed naming, for his handiwork will long be remembered among gods and men. That is all the characterisation the tradition gives him before the catastrophe: a strong man in the dark, standing at the edge of other people's games.

The [death of Baldr](/events/death-of-baldr) turns on exactly that marginality. When [Frigg](/gods/frigg)'s oaths made Baldr proof against all things, the gods' favourite sport became hurling weapons at him and watching them fall away. Höðr stood outside the ring of laughter — he could not see to aim, and no one thought to include him. Loki, fresh from tricking the mistletoe's exemption out of Frigg, found him there and offered the two things nobody else had offered: a weapon and a target. Do honour to your brother like the others, he said; I will show your hand where he stands. The [mistletoe](/artifacts/mistilteinn) flew, Baldr fell, and the gods stood struck dumb inside their own sanctuary, where no vengeance could be taken on the spot.

The mythology never doubts where the guilt lies — [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) and Snorri alike treat Loki as the killer and Höðr as the instrument. But the archaic logic of blood-feud does not run on intent: a hand that kills owes a death, whatever the heart knew. Höðr's tragedy is that both things are true at once. He is innocent, and he must die; the myths hold the two without flinching, and his very name — höðr means "battle" — reads like a grim joke about a god who never chose a fight.

## Váli, the avenger one night old

Baldrs draumar and Völuspá both carry the prophecy: Odin will beget a son to avenge Baldr, and that son will neither wash his hands nor comb his hair until Baldr's slayer is on the pyre. The son is Váli, born of Odin and the giantess [Rindr](/giants), and at one night old he kills Höðr. The blind god's death gets no scene of its own in the surviving texts — no last words, no defence, no funeral. Having existed to strike one blow he could not see, Höðr exits with equal facelessness, dispatched by a child engineered for the purpose. Vengeance inside a family is still vengeance owed by the family: Odin could not kill one son for another with his own hand, so a new son was made for the deed — the feud-system's cold arithmetic applied to gods.

## Høtherus: Saxo's other Höðr

Around 1200, the Danish historian Saxo Grammaticus told the story again in Gesta Danorum, and almost nothing survives the retelling intact. His Høtherus is no blind god but a superbly gifted human prince — swimmer, archer, harper — who competes with the demigod Balderus for the hand of the princess Nanna. Guided by forest-maidens, Høtherus wins a magic sword from a satyr, battles Balderus repeatedly (even putting the gods to flight), and finally deals him a mortal wound. Odin then begets an avenger, Bous, who kills Høtherus in turn — the same skeleton of slaying and counter-slaying, wearing entirely different flesh. Mistletoe, blindness, and Loki are all absent.

The two versions are the classic case study in how differently the same myth could be told: Snorri's Icelandic tradition made Höðr a passive instrument in a divine tragedy, while Saxo's Danish sources — or Saxo's own rationalising pen — made him the capable hero of a courtly rivalry. Scholars have never fully agreed on which elements are older, and Höðr thus doubles as a permanent caution against treating any single medieval text as "the" Norse myth.

## After Ragnarök

Then comes the reversal no reader expects. In Völuspá's vision of the world reborn — green fields rising from the sea, eagles over the falls — the seeress says that Baldr will come, and Höðr with him, and both will dwell in Odin's victory-homesteads, gods reconciled where [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) stood. [Vafþrúðnismál](/texts/vafthrudnismal)'s giant confirms the pair among the survivors' company. Slayer and slain walk out of Hel together, the feud burned away with the world that required it. Of all the images Norse eschatology offers, this is the most quietly radical: the new age is founded not on the punishment of the guiltless killer but on his forgiveness — the end of the logic that demanded Váli's vengeance in the first place. The blind god's last appearance is his first happy one.

## In the primary sources

Höðr appears at the pivot of Völuspá and in the prophecy-frame of [Baldrs draumar](/texts/baldrs-draumar) in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda); Gylfaginning in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) tells his deed and names him among the returners, and Skáldskaparmál's kennings for him — "shooter of the mistletoe," "companion of Hel," "enemy of Váli" — compress his whole biography into three bitter phrases. Books III and IV of Saxo's Gesta Danorum carry the rival Høtherus version. He has no attested cult; he exists entirely inside one story and its long echo.

## Legacy

Höðr left no weekdays or place-names — only a figure that Western storytelling has never stopped finding useful: the innocent hand on the guilty weapon. His blindness beside Loki's guiding malice became, for later readers, an emblem of manipulated violence, cited wherever someone pulls a trigger aimed by someone else. In the reborn world of Völuspá he also left something rarer: Norse mythology's clearest image of forgiveness.

## FAQ

**Q: Did Höðr kill Baldr on purpose?**
No. Höðr was blind and did not know the mistletoe could harm his brother — Loki supplied the dart, proposed the throw, and aimed his hand. The sources treat Loki as the true contriver of Baldr's death, but under the old feud-logic Höðr's hand still owed the debt, and Váli killed him for it.

**Q: Who killed Höðr?**
Váli, a son Odin fathered on the giantess Rindr specifically to avenge Baldr. According to Völuspá and Baldrs draumar, Váli slew Höðr when he was one night old, neither washing his hands nor combing his hair beforehand.

**Q: Does Höðr come back after Ragnarök?**
Yes. Völuspá says Baldr and Höðr return together from Hel to the reborn world and dwell in peace in the gods' old sanctuaries — slayer and slain reconciled, one of the mythology's most striking images of a feud finally ended.

**Q: How is Saxo's version different?**
In Gesta Danorum, Høtherus is a sighted human warrior-prince who rivals the demigod Balderus for the princess Nanna and kills him with a magic sword in open war. There is no blindness, no mistletoe, and no Loki — evidence that the Baldr story circulated in very different forms.
