Freyja ("Lady") came to the Æsir with her father [Njörðr](/gods/njord) and twin brother [Freyr](/gods/freyr) as a hostage after the [Æsir–Vanir war](/events/aesir-vanir-war), and became the most honoured of goddesses. She drives a chariot drawn by two cats, wears a falcon-feather cloak that lets the wearer fly, and weeps tears of red gold for her wandering husband [Óðr](/gods/odr).

She is not merely a love goddess. Freyja is mistress of [seiðr](/concepts/seidr), the ecstatic magic of fate-weaving, which she taught to [Odin](/gods/odin) himself; and she is a battle-goddess who rides to war and claims half the slain for her field [Fólkvangr](/concepts/folkvangr) — Odin gets only the other half. The jötnar demand her constantly in ransom (for the wall of Asgard, for [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir)'s return), and the gods refuse at any cost.

Her necklace [Brísingamen](/artifacts/brisingamen), forged by four dwarves, is the greatest treasure of the goddesses. Her name survives in the Scandinavian word for "lady" (fru/frue).

## The Vanir bride of Asgard

Freyja's title is not really a name at all — Freyja simply means "Lady," as her twin's name Freyr means "Lord," suggesting that her true name was too holy to speak or was lost behind the honorific. She was born among the [Vanir](/realms/vanaheim), the gods of fertility and prosperity, and crossed to [Asgard](/realms/asgard) in the hostage-exchange that ended the first war in the world. Snorri, in Gylfaginning, calls her the most renowned of the goddesses: it was she who first taught the Æsir seiðr, "as was practised among the Vanir," and to her it is good to pray in matters of love.

She is married to Óðr, who is a mystery even to the Eddas — a husband perpetually away on long journeys, for whom Freyja weeps tears of red gold. That grief made her the mint of skaldic poetry: gold is "Freyja's tears," "the weeping of Mardöll," and her wandering search for Óðr among strange peoples earned her the string of names Snorri catalogues — Mardöll, Hörn, Gefn, Sýr. Her daughters by Óðr, Hnoss and Gersemi, are both named "Treasure," and both names became common nouns for precious things. Everything Freyja touches becomes a word for wealth.

## Goddess of the slain

The most startling line about Freyja comes from [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal): Fólkvangr is the ninth dwelling, where Freyja arranges the choosing of seats in the hall — and half the slain she chooses each day, while Odin has the other half. Half. The radiant Lady of love has equal shares with the Allfather in the harvest of every battlefield, and Snorri adds that wherever she rides to war, she takes her pick first. Her hall Sessrúmnir, "Seat-roomy," stands on that field as [Valhalla](/concepts/valhalla) stands on Odin's, though no source describes what her dead do there — one of the mythology's most tantalising silences. In Egils saga, a grieving woman says she will eat nothing until she sups with Freyja: for women, at least, Freyja's hall could be spoken of as death's destination.

The seiðr she commands belongs to the same dark register. It is the magic of fate — seeing it, twisting it, binding minds and loosening them — ecstatic, dangerous, and so strongly coded female that Odin's practice of it exposed him to insult. Ynglinga saga in [Heimskringla](/texts/heimskringla) names Freyja as its teacher and, after the euhemerised gods die, its last survivor, keeping the sacrifices alive; the wandering völva with her staff and chants, familiar from the sagas, works Freyja's craft. Some scholars see Freyja herself behind [Gullveig](/gods/gullveig)-Heiðr, the thrice-burned witch of [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) whose mistreatment ignited the Æsir–Vanir war — gold, seiðr, and unkillable persistence make the resemblance strong, though the poem never says so.

## Why does everyone want to marry Freyja?

Because she is the treasure of Asgard, and the giants know it. The pattern repeats until it becomes a structural joke: whenever a jötunn names a price, the price is Freyja. The builder of [Asgard's wall](/events/building-of-asgards-wall) demands Freyja plus the sun and moon, and the gods' scramble to cheat him drives that whole disastrous bargain. [Þrymr](/giants/thrym) buries Thor's stolen hammer eight leagues deep and will return it only for Freyja's hand. The giant [Hrungnir](/giants/hrungnir), drunk in Asgard, boasts he will carry off Freyja and [Sif](/gods/sif) — while Freyja alone of the gods dares keep pouring his ale until [Thor](/gods/thor) arrives.

Her refusal in [Þrymskviða](/texts/thrymskvida) is the best scene she gets. Told she must don a bridal veil and marry Þrymr, Freyja snorts with a rage that shakes the halls of the gods, and Brísingamen bursts from her throat: "You'd think I'd become the most man-crazy of women." The gods dress Thor in the veil instead — with Brísingamen borrowed to complete the disguise — and Freyja's necklace attends the wedding she refused. The insinuation she rebuffs there follows her to [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna), where [Loki](/gods/loki) accuses her of having bedded every god and elf in the hall, including her brother; Njörðr shrugs it off as slander of small consequence. Snorri's Skáldskaparmál preserves the flip side of the giants' desire: when Freyja wants something done, gods borrow her falcon cloak [Valshamr](/artifacts/valshamr) — Loki wears it to rescue [Iðunn](/gods/idun) — and in the late tale of Sörla þáttr she pays four dwarves an infamous price for Brísingamen, the necklace over which Loki and [Heimdallr](/gods/heimdall) later fight in seal-shape.

## The boar and the ancestors

In [Hyndluljóð](/texts/poetic-edda), Freyja rides by night to a giantess's cave on her boar Hildisvíni, "Battle-swine" — who is actually her human favourite Óttarr in disguise. She bullies and coaxes the reluctant giantess Hyndla into reciting Óttarr's entire genealogy so he can win an inheritance dispute, then demands a memory-ale so he will retain it. The poem shows Freyja's least-sung office: patroness of ancestry and inheritance, the goddess a devotee could ride to the underworld of memory with. Her boar links her, like her brother's [Gullinbursti](/creatures/gullinbursti), to the Vanir's oldest animal; her byname Sýr means "sow."

Freyja has no announced part in [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) — no source gives her a last battle or a death. She simply persists, as fertility does; Ynglinga saga makes her the last of the gods left sacrificing when the others have gone.

## In the primary sources

Freyja appears across the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) — refusing Þrymr in Þrymskviða, defending Frigg and enduring Loki's slurs in Lokasenna, riding Hildisvíni in Hyndluljóð, and standing behind Gullveig in Völuspá if that identification holds. In the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), Gylfaginning ranks her with Frigg and details Fólkvangr, her cats, and her gold-tears, while Skáldskaparmál builds kennings from every corner of her myth. Heimskringla's Ynglinga saga makes her the great priestess of the sacrifices. The late Sörla þáttr tells the purchase of Brísingamen and the endless battle Odin forces her to sustain — a story most scholars treat as Christian-era invention wrapped around old material.

## Legacy

Freyja's name became the everyday Scandinavian word for a lady — Danish frue, German Frau follows the same root — and Friday is claimed for her in a minority etymology, though [Frigg](/gods/frigg) holds the standard claim. Plants and place-names remember her across Sweden and Norway (Frövi, Frøihov), and after conversion many of her tenderest attributes were quietly transferred to the Virgin Mary in folk usage. She remains the most vivid female figure of Norse myth: the goddess strong enough to hold love, gold, magic, and death in the same two hands.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Freyja the goddess of?**
Love, beauty, and fertility — but equally seiðr magic, gold, and war. Grímnismál says she chooses half of all the battle-slain for her field Fólkvangr, sharing the dead equally with Odin, and Ynglinga saga names her the teacher of seiðr to the Æsir.

**Q: Are Freyja and Frigg the same goddess?**
The Norse sources keep them distinct — Frigg is Odin's wife and queen of the Æsir; Freyja is the Vanir "Lady" married to the absent Óðr. But their overlaps (and the Óðr/Óðinn name pair) lead some scholars to argue both descend from one older Germanic goddess. The question is unresolved.

**Q: Why does Freyja have a cat chariot?**
Snorri simply reports it: when she travels, she drives two cats yoked to her chariot. No surviving myth explains the cats, though they suit a goddess of love and seiðr; later folklore connected cats with her fondly. She also owns a falcon-feather cloak for flight, and rides the boar Hildisvíni.

**Q: What is Brísingamen?**
Freyja's golden necklace or torc, the greatest treasure of the goddesses, made by four dwarves. It bursts from her throat when she rages in Þrymskviða, adorns Thor's bridal disguise, and is the prize over which Loki and Heimdallr fight in the shape of seals in an old skaldic fragment.

**Q: Does Freyja take warriors to Valhalla?**
No — to Fólkvangr, her own "field of the host," where her hall Sessrúmnir stands. She chooses half the slain each day, and Odin's Valhalla receives the other half. The sources never explain how the halves are divided, which keeps the question alive among scholars.
