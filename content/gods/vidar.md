Víðarr, son of [Odin](/gods/odin) and the giantess [Gríðr](/giants/grid), is "the silent god" — strongest of the Æsir after [Thor](/gods/thor), dwelling in a hall of brushwood and high grass called Viði. He is a god held in reserve: his entire purpose waits at the end of the world.

When [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) swallows Odin at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), Víðarr steps into the wolf's mouth. He wears a shoe assembled through all of time from the scraps humans cut from the toes and heels of their shoes — pinning the wolf's lower jaw with it, he seizes the upper jaw and tears the beast apart. He is one of the few gods to survive Ragnarök, walking into the reborn world with his brother [Váli](/gods/vali).

## The god who waits

Snorri's sketch in Gylfaginning is brief and deliberate: Víðarr is the silent áss; he has a thick shoe; he is nearly as strong as Thor, and the gods rely on him greatly in all struggles. Nothing else — no adventures, no feuds, no loves. [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal) adds only his address, and makes even that eloquent: brushwood grows, and high grass, wide in Víðarr's land — an unfarmed, unbuilt country, land left fallow. The kenning-lists of Skáldskaparmál call him, along with Váli, one of the "vengeance-gods," and permit poets to call him "the possessor of the iron shoe" and "the slayer of Fenrir." That is the whole file. Víðarr is the mythology's most extreme case of a god defined by a single future act: everything about him — the silence, the fallow land, the ever-growing shoe — is a held breath.

His mother is the giantess Gríðr, a rare friendly jötunn who, in Skáldskaparmál, lends Thor her belt of strength, iron gloves, and staff for the journey to [Geirröðr](/giants/geirrod)'s hall. Odin's habit of fathering task-specific sons on giantesses — Váli on Rindr for one vengeance, Víðarr on Gríðr for another — gives the Allfather's family tree the look of a contingency plan.

## Why does Víðarr's shoe matter?

Because the whole human race helps make it. Snorri explains that Víðarr's shoe is assembled across all of time from the scraps of leather men cut from their own shoes at the toes and heels — and he adds a practical note with a wink: anyone who wants to help the Æsir should throw those scraps away. Every human cobbler and farmer trimming a shoe was, knowingly or not, contributing armour to the foot that would one day stand in Fenrir's jaw. It is the mythology's most intimate link between everyday life in [Midgard](/realms/midgard) and the last battle: the war at the end of the world is provisioned, scrap by scrap, from household waste. Older readers of the myth saw in it the trace of a real ritual habit — leather offcuts as offerings — but the sources give only Snorri's remark, and its charm needs no ritual to explain it.

## At Ragnarök

On the plain of [Vígríðr](/concepts/vigrid), the wolf Fenrir runs with jaws so wide that the upper touches the sky and the lower the earth, and he swallows Odin whole. That is the moment Víðarr exists for. [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa) gives it in a single hard stanza: then comes the great son of Sigfather, Víðarr, to strike at the beast of slaughter; with his hand he drives his sword into the heart of the giant's son, and his father is avenged. Snorri's Gylfaginning tells it with the shoe instead: Víðarr strides forward, sets one foot on the wolf's lower jaw, seizes the upper jaw with his hand, and rips the gape apart. Vafþrúðnismál agrees — asked who rules after Surtr's fire, the doomed giant answers that Víðarr will avenge Odin, cleaving the wolf's cold jaws in battle.

The two versions — sword through the heart, jaws torn asunder — sit side by side in the tradition, and a tenth-century stone seems to know the second: a panel of the Gosforth Cross in Cumbria shows a figure forcing open a monstrous wolf's jaws with foot and hand, carved while the old religion was still warm. Either way, the mythology's arithmetic is exact. Odin, who ordered Fenrir bound and fed the prophecies of his own doom, cannot be saved; but he can be answered, and the answer is instant. The silent god's one recorded act is also the last great deed of the old world — vengeance taken in the very jaws that ended it.

## After Ragnarök

Víðarr's reward is continuance. Vafþrúðnismál names him and Váli dwelling in the gods' holy places when Surtr's flame has died; Gylfaginning seats the brothers on the new green earth at Iðavöllr, where [Asgard](/realms/asgard) once stood, joined by Thor's sons [Magni and Móði](/gods/magni-and-modi) with [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir), and by [Baldr](/gods/baldr) and [Höðr](/gods/hodr) up from [Hel](/realms/helheim). The survivors sit in the grass and talk over the old mysteries, finding the golden gaming-pieces of the elder gods in the soil. The avenger and the boy-executioner, the two single-purpose sons of Odin, outlive everything they were made to answer for — and what they do with eternity afterward, no poem ever says. Perhaps, for the silent god, that is the point.

## In the primary sources

Víðarr's deed anchors Völuspá and Vafþrúðnismál in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda); Grímnismál gives his grassy land; in [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna) he has a small speaking part, when Odin bids the silent god rise and pour ale for [Loki](/gods/loki) — the peacemaker's cup before the storm of insults. Gylfaginning in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) supplies his portrait, the shoe, the jaw-rending, and his survival; Skáldskaparmál his kennings and his mother Gríðr. The Gosforth Cross in England likely carries his image in stone. No place-names or cult traces are known with confidence.

## Legacy

Víðarr left little on the map and everything in the shape of the story: he is the guarantee that Ragnarök is not pure annihilation. Poets and scholars have long paired the silent god's survival with the world's renewal — the strength held in reserve through the whole of history, spent once, and rewarded with morning. Modern retellings keep reaching for him whenever they need the quiet figure at the edge of the hall who turns out to be the reason anyone survives.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Víðarr the god of?**
Vengeance and silence, insofar as he has domains at all. The sources define him by attributes — nearly Thor's strength, the famous thick shoe, a grassy unfarmed land — and by one destiny: avenging Odin on the wolf Fenrir at Ragnarök.

**Q: How does Víðarr kill Fenrir?**
Snorri says he pins the wolf's lower jaw with his great shoe, grips the upper jaw, and tears the mouth apart; Völuspá instead has him drive a sword into the wolf's heart. Both traditions survive, and the Gosforth Cross appears to depict the jaw-rending version.

**Q: What is Víðarr's shoe made of?**
All the scraps of leather that people cut from the toes and heels of their shoes, gathered across the whole of time. Snorri adds that anyone wishing to help the gods should throw such scraps away — humanity quietly outfitting its avenger.

**Q: Does Víðarr survive Ragnarök?**
Yes. Vafþrúðnismál and Gylfaginning both count him among the survivors: he and his brother Váli dwell in the gods' sanctuaries on the renewed earth, alongside Magni, Móði, Baldr, and Höðr.

**Q: Why is Víðarr called the silent god?**
No source explains it. Snorri simply names him "the silent áss," and interpreters have guessed at ritual silence before vengeance, the muteness of held-back force, or a lost myth. His silence, like his fallow land, reads as purpose stored up for the world's last day.
