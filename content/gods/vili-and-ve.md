The world was made by a committee of three: [Odin](/gods/odin) and his brothers Vili ("Will") and Vé ("Sanctuary, the holy"), sons of Borr and the giantess Bestla. Together they killed Ymir, hauled his corpse into the void, and carpentered the cosmos from it; together they found the trees on the shore and made humans — in Snorri's version, Vili giving wit and motion, Vé giving face, speech, hearing and sight.

Then they evaporate from the record, leaving two traces: their names alliterate with Óðinn in the old triple pattern of cult, and [Loki](/gods/loki)'s nastiest taunt — that while Odin was once long in exile, Vili and Vé shared [Frigg](/gods/frigg). Creation's junior partners, remembered chiefly by a slander.

## The killing of Ymir and the building of the world

Snorri's Gylfaginning gives the brothers their hour. In the beginning was the void Ginnungagap, and in it the primeval giant Ymir. The cow Auðumla licked a man free of the salty ice: Búri, whose son Borr married Bestla, daughter of the giant Bölþorn — so the gods' mother is a giant's daughter, a fact the mythology never hides. Borr and Bestla had three sons: Odin, Vili, and Vé.

The three sons of Borr killed Ymir. The blood from the wound drowned nearly all the frost-giants — the first flood, with one giant family escaping to continue the race. Then the brothers dragged the corpse into the middle of Ginnungagap and built the world from it: earth from his flesh, mountains from his bones, the encircling sea from his blood, the sky from his skull — set up with a dwarf under each corner — and his brains thrown aloft as clouds. Völuspá remembers it more simply: "Borr's sons lifted the lands, they who shaped the famous Miðgarðr." Creation, in the North, is not speech over the waters; it is the biggest carpentry job ever undertaken, performed by three brothers on the body of their mother's kin — and Vili and Vé hold two-thirds of the credit.

The brothers appear once more at creation's second act. Walking by the sea, the sons of Borr found two trees or logs, and made from them the first human couple, Askr and Embla. Snorri distributes the gifts: the first brother gave breath and life; the second, wit and movement; the third, face, speech, hearing and sight — then they gave them clothes and names. In Völuspá the same scene is played by a different trio — Odin, [Hœnir](/gods/hoenir), and Lóðurr — and whether the two trios are rival traditions or the same gods under other names is a classic unsolved problem. Some scholars have pressed the equations Vili = Hœnir and Vé = Lóðurr; the texts neither confirm nor forbid it.

## What do the names mean?

The trio's names are a little sermon in themselves. Óðinn is built on óðr — frenzy, inspiration, mind in its ecstatic mode. Vili is the ordinary noun "will," desire and intention. Vé is the word for a holy place — the fenced sanctuary, the consecrated ground; Viking Age Scandinavia is scattered with place-names containing it (Odense, "Odin's vé," among them). Read as a set, the three brothers are the anatomy of a god, or of a mind: inspiration, will, and sanctity working as one agent, in the perfect alliteration Norse sacral formulas favoured. Whether Vili and Vé ever had cults and characters of their own, or were always Odin's aspects given legs for the creation story, is — like so much about them — beyond proof. What can be said is that the poets treated the names as fully real: Snorri lists "Vili's brother" among Odin's own kennings.

## Loki's taunt: the sharing of Frigg

The brothers' one other appearance is scandalous, and it is the reason their names survived in poetry at all. In Lokasenna, when Frigg objects to Loki's abuse at Ægir's feast, he rounds on her: "Be silent, Frigg... ever eager for men: you took both Vili and Vé, wife of Viðrir [Odin], to your embrace." The insult would be unintelligible unless it pointed at a story the audience knew, and Snorri's Ynglinga saga supplies it: Odin was once away so long on his far journeys that the Æsir despaired of his return; his brothers divided his inheritance between them, "but his wife Frigg they both shared. A little later Odin returned, and took his wife back." No consequences are recorded: no quarrel, no vengeance, nothing. Scholars read the episode variously — as a memory of royal succession customs, or as a mythic statement that Odin's brothers are Odin's substitutes, the wife of the sovereignty passing to whoever holds it; Saxo Grammaticus tells a parallel tale of Odin's exile and a substitute taking both his place and his wife. Whatever its origin, it is a strange fate for the world's co-creators: two-thirds of the makers of heaven and earth, remembered chiefly by a slander at a feast.

## In the primary sources

Vili and Vé appear together in the Prose Edda (Gylfaginning: sons of Borr and Bestla, the killing of Ymir, the building of the world, the making of Askr and Embla) and in the Poetic Edda only in Lokasenna's taunt; Völuspá speaks of "Borr's sons" without naming the other two. Ynglinga saga in Heimskringla tells the inheritance-and-Frigg episode. Hávamál's rune-section has Odin learn songs from "Bölþorn's famous son" — his mother Bestla's brother — placing the trio's giant kin in the chain of wisdom. Skáldskaparmál preserves the kennings ("Vili's brother" for Odin). Neither brother has any myth after the creation, any named hall, or any role at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok); place-name evidence exists for vé as sanctuaries, but none certainly for Vé as a worshipped person.

## Legacy

Vili and Vé's monument is the world of the myths itself — every later story happens inside the thing they built. Their linguistic legacy is oddly alive: vé survives in Scandinavian place-names marking ancient holy ground, and the common word vilje/vilja, "will," keeps Vili's name in everyday speech without anyone noticing. In modern retellings they surface occasionally as Odin's shadowy brothers — and in the scholarly literature they remain the standard example of the Indo-European creative triad, and of how completely a god can vanish once his one scene is played.

## FAQ

**Q: Who are Vili and Vé in Norse mythology?**
They are Odin's two brothers, sons of Borr and the giantess Bestla. With Odin they killed the primeval giant Ymir, built the world from his body, and gave the first humans their gifts — after which they almost entirely disappear from the mythology.

**Q: What did Vili and Vé create?**
With Odin they made the world from Ymir's corpse: earth from his flesh, sea from his blood, mountains from his bones, sky from his skull, clouds from his brains. They also helped make the first humans, Askr and Embla — Snorri credits the three brothers with giving breath, wit and movement, and the senses.

**Q: What do the names Vili and Vé mean?**
Vili means "Will"; Vé means "sanctuary" or "the holy" — the word for a consecrated place. With Óðinn ("master of inspiration"), the three alliterating brothers read like the anatomy of divine agency: inspiration, will, and holiness.

**Q: Did Vili and Vé really share Frigg?**
Two sources say so. Loki taunts Frigg in Lokasenna with having taken both brothers to her embrace, and Ynglinga saga explains that during one of Odin's long absences his brothers divided his inheritance and shared his wife, returning both when he came back. Scholars debate whether this reflects old ideas about sovereignty passing to a ruler's substitutes.

**Q: What happened to Vili and Vé after the creation?**
Nothing is recorded. They have no halls, no further myths, and no role at Ragnarök; after the making of the world and of humankind they vanish from the story, surviving only in kennings, Loki's insult, and the inheritance episode.
