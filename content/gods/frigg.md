Frigg is the queen of [Asgard](/realms/asgard) and the only one besides [Odin](/gods/odin) permitted to sit on the high seat [Hliðskjálf](/concepts/hlidskjalf). Her defining paradox is that she knows the fate of all beings yet never speaks it. She dwells in the marsh-hall Fensalir, attended by handmaidens such as [Fulla](/gods/fulla), Gná, and Hlín, and presides over marriage, the household, and childbirth.

Her great myth is her grief. Foreseeing danger to her son [Baldr](/gods/baldr), she exacted an oath from every thing in creation — fire, water, iron, stone, every beast and bird and plant — not to harm him. She overlooked only the young mistletoe, and through that gap [Loki](/gods/loki) aimed the dart. After Baldr's death she begged a rider to go to [Hel](/realms/helheim) to ransom him back, and all creation wept for him — save one giantess, Loki in disguise. Friday ("Frigg's day") preserves her name.

## The silent seeress

Lokasenna states her paradox in a single exchange. When Loki turns his venom on Frigg, it is [Freyja](/gods/freyja) who warns him off: Frigg knows all fates, she says, though she herself does not speak them. The queen of the gods is a seeress under a vow of silence the sources never explain. Where Odin buys knowledge of the future at ruinous prices — an eye, nine nights on the tree — Frigg simply has it, and carries it the way one carries an incurable condition. Read her myths with that in mind and they darken: when she takes oaths from all creation to protect Baldr, she is not acting on a vague worry. She has seen what is coming, and she tries to bargain with it anyway.

Her hall Fensalir, "Marsh Halls," sets her apart from the gleaming architecture of the other gods — a dwelling in the wet, liminal in-between land, which some scholars connect to the bog-offerings of earlier Scandinavian religion. From there she runs what amounts to Asgard's second court: Fulla carries her coffer and knows her secret counsel; Gná rides the horse Hófvarpnir through sky and sea on her errands; Hlín is charged with protecting those Frigg wishes to save. Snorri lists these figures as separate goddesses, though many read them as aspects or officers of Frigg herself — the queen's will, distributed.

## Frigg and Odin: a marriage of rivals

The Eddas' royal marriage is no idyll of deference. In the prose prologue to [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal), Frigg and Odin sit in Hliðskjálf comparing the fortunes of their respective human foster-sons. Odin gloats that Frigg's protégé is a king while hers — actually his own favourite, Geirröðr — rules well; Frigg retorts that Geirröðr is a miser who tortures guests. It is a slander, and it is bait: she sends her maid Gná ahead to warn the king to seize a suspicious traveller, and Odin walks in disguise into a trap of his wife's making, to hang between two fires for eight nights. Frigg wins the wager. The queen who never reveals the future is perfectly capable of arranging it.

In [Vafþrúðnismál](/texts/vafthrudnismal) the dynamic is tender instead: Odin asks her counsel before riding to duel wits with the giant [Vafþrúðnir](/giants/vafthrudnir), and she advises him against it, then blesses the journey when he will not be stopped — "safe may you go, safe return, safe be on the way." She knows how every road ends; she lets him take them anyway.

Loki, in Lokasenna, drags out the marriage's oldest scandal: that while Odin once wandered long in exile, Frigg took his brothers [Vili and Vé](/gods/vili-and-ve) to her bed. Ynglinga saga in [Heimskringla](/texts/heimskringla) repeats the tale as sober dynastic history — the brothers shared Odin's inheritance, and Frigg with it, until he returned. Whether this preserves an old myth of sovereignty (the queen as the kingship, held by whoever reigns) or merely a good slander, the sources let both readings stand.

## The mother of Baldr

Everything in Frigg's mythology bends toward one catastrophe, told fully in Gylfaginning. Baldr dreams of his death; Frigg answers with the most maternal act of magic in world myth — she makes the entire world swear. Fire and water, iron and every metal, stones, earth, trees, sicknesses, beasts, birds, venom, serpents: all take oath not to harm Baldr. The gods, delighted, make the invulnerability a party game, hurling weapons at the shining god while he stands unharmed.

Then an old woman comes to Fensalir. Frigg, unsuspecting, tells the visitor the one exception: a little sprout called mistletoe, growing west of Valhalla, too young for oath-taking. The old woman is Loki. The queen's single act of trust becomes the murder weapon, placed in the hand of blind [Höðr](/gods/hodr) (see the [death of Baldr](/events/death-of-baldr)). When Baldr falls, it is Frigg who rallies the ruined company: who will ride to Hel and offer ransom? [Hermóðr](/gods/hermodr) takes [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir), and Hel's terms — all things must weep — are so nearly met that the failure lands like a second death. One giantess, Þökk, keeps her eyes dry. In [Völuspá](/texts/voluspa)'s cold arithmetic, worse is still coming: at Ragnarök, Odin's fall to [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) is called the "second grief of Hlín" — Frigg's own protecting handmaiden, or Frigg herself under that name, losing husband after son. The goddess of foresight must weep for Fensalir twice, having seen both griefs from the beginning.

## Are Frigg and Freyja the same goddess?

It is one of the oldest open questions in Norse scholarship. The two greatest goddesses overlap suspiciously: both are associated with love and fate; Frigg's husband is Óðinn while Freyja weeps for a vanished husband named [Óðr](/gods/odr) — the same name-root, "frenzy, inspiration"; and Loki accuses both of comparable infidelities. Among the continental Germans there is only one great goddess in view, Frija — whose name lives in "Friday" — and some scholars argue she later split into the dignified wife Frigg and the passionate Freyja, while others hold they were always distinct, Æsir queen and [Vanir](/realms/vanaheim) mistress of [seiðr](/concepts/seidr). The sources preserve the puzzle without solving it; a careful reader keeps them apart, as the Eddas do, while noting the seam.

## In the primary sources

Frigg frames some of the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda)'s best poems: the wager in Grímnismál's prose, the farewell counsel in Vafþrúðnismál, the confrontation in [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna), and Völuspá's "grief of Hlín." In the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), Gylfaginning names her foremost of goddesses, catalogues her handmaidens, and narrates the Baldr tragedy at full length. Outside Scandinavia she appears centuries earlier as Frija in the Old High German Second Merseburg Charm, working healing magic beside Volla ([Fulla](/gods/fulla)), and in Langobard legend she tricks Wodan into granting the Lombards their name and victory — the same fate-steering wife under older skies. Saxo's [Gesta Danorum](/texts/gesta-danorum) preserves a hostile, scandalous version of her as a gold-hungry queen.

## Legacy

Friday carries her across the Germanic languages — Old English Frigedæg, "Frigg's day," matching the Roman day of Venus. Scattered place-names in Scandinavia and possibly England preserve her, and the plant orchid was once "Frigg's grass" in Swedish folk usage, tied to her role in childbirth. In modern culture she persists as the archetype she always was: the queen who knows how the story ends and loves her family through it regardless.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Frigg the goddess of?**
Marriage, motherhood, the household, and foresight. Snorri calls her the foremost of the goddesses, and Lokasenna adds her strangest attribute: she knows the fates of all beings but never reveals them.

**Q: Are Frigg and Freyja the same?**
The medieval sources treat them as two distinct goddesses — Frigg of the Æsir, Freyja of the Vanir. But their overlapping traits and paired husbands (Óðinn and Óðr) have led many scholars to argue they descend from a single earlier Germanic goddess, Frija. The question remains genuinely unsettled.

**Q: Why did Frigg's plan to save Baldr fail?**
She took oaths from everything in creation except the mistletoe, which she judged too young to swear. Loki, disguised as an old woman, coaxed that secret from her and made a dart of the plant, guiding blind Höðr's throw. The one gap in a mother's protection became the weapon.

**Q: Is Friday named after Frigg?**
Yes — Old English Frigedæg translates the Latin "day of Venus" with the name of Frigg (continental Frija). A minority view credits Freyja instead, but the standard etymology points to Frigg.

**Q: Who are Frigg's children?**
Baldr is her son with Odin, and Höðr is generally counted Odin's son and Baldr's brother, hence hers. Snorri also makes her the foster-context for Hermóðr's ride to Hel — it is Frigg who asks who dares make the journey to ransom her dead son.
