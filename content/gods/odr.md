Óðr exists mostly as an absence. Snorri says he "went away on long journeys," and [Freyja](/gods/freyja) weeps for him tears of red gold — which is why gold, in skaldic verse, is "Freyja's tears." She wanders among strange peoples seeking him, taking many names as she goes: Mardöll, Hörn, Gefn, Sýr.

His name is the noun behind [Odin](/gods/odin)'s own — óðr, "frenzy, inspiration, poetry" — and the oldest scholarly puzzle in Norse studies is whether Óðr and Óðinn were once one god, split by time into Freyja's vanished husband and [Frigg](/gods/frigg)'s wandering one. If so, the two great goddesses are shadows of one another, both married to the same restlessness.

## What the sources actually tell

Óðr's entire biography can be quoted in full, which is itself the most eloquent fact about him. Snorri, in Gylfaginning: "Freyja is the most renowned of the goddesses... She was married to the man called Óðr. Their daughter is Hnoss; she is so beautiful that from her name whatever is beautiful and precious is called hnossir (treasures). Óðr went away on long journeys, and Freyja weeps for him, and her tears are red gold. Freyja has many names, and the reason for that is that she gave herself various names when she travelled among unknown peoples seeking Óðr."

That is the whole story: a marriage, a daughter (Ynglinga saga adds a second, Gersemi — whose name also means "treasure"), a departure with no stated reason and no destination, and a grief that literally mints wealth. Nothing tells us why he left, whether he returns, or what he looks like. In the Poetic Edda he surfaces twice, both times obliquely. Völuspá calls Freyja Óðs mey, "Óðr's girl," in the episode where the giant-builder demands her as payment — her very identity in the poem is "the wife of the missing man." And in Hyndluljóð, the giantess Hyndla taunts Freyja: "you ran to Óðr, ever full of desire..." — a jibe implying stories of pursuit and passion the poet's audience knew and we do not.

## Why gold is Freyja's tears

Óðr's practical legacy in Old Norse literature is a whole family of kennings. Because Freyja weeps red gold for her absent husband, the skalds could call gold "Freyja's tears," "the rain of Freyja's eyes," "the eye-rain of Óðr's bedmate" — and every such phrase, glittering in a poem about some Norwegian chieftain's generosity, silently retells the myth: a wandering husband, a weeping wife, grief transmuted into treasure. Their daughters extend the metaphor — Hnoss and Gersemi, "Treasure" and "Jewel," as if everything this marriage produced turned to riches. Some scholars have noticed the pattern with a colder eye: the love-goddess's family reads like an allegory of gold itself — desire (óðr can also shade toward passion and frenzy), the woman won by it, and the precious things it begets.

## Is Óðr just Odin?

This is the question that has organised the scholarship for two centuries, and it deserves an honest map. The case for identity is strong on its face. The names are grammatically the same word: Óðinn is simply óðr — "frenzy, inspiration, ecstasy, poetry" — with the definite-article-like suffix -inn, "the master of óðr." Both figures are defined by absence: Óðr on his endless journeys, Odin forever wandering Midgard in disguise while Frigg keeps Asgard. And the wives mirror each other so closely that the suspicion arises of one original couple: Frigg and Freyja have both been accused (by [Loki](/gods/loki) in Lokasenna, and in Saxo's stories) of infidelities during a husband's absence; both are supreme goddesses associated with love, fate, and the domestic-yet-sovereign role. On this theory — argued in different forms by scholars from the nineteenth century onward — an older Germanic pair, the ecstatic wandering god and his goddess, was split by time and tribe into two couples: Óðinn/Frigg among the Æsir, Óðr/Freyja among the Vanir, and mythographers like Snorri inherited both halves without recombining them.

The case against is real, too. The Eddic sources themselves never identify the two; Snorri, who knew more lost poetry than we ever will, keeps them firmly distinct and marries them to different goddesses. Freyja is Vanir, Odin's relations with her are elsewhere those of colleague (she taught him seiðr) rather than husband; and doubling of names and roles is common enough in mythology without requiring identity. The sober conclusion most handbooks now offer: the names are certainly related, the figures are probably connected at some deep historical level, and the surviving mythology treats them as two. Óðr may be a fossil — the oldest layer of Odin, preserved inside Freyja's grief.

## In the primary sources

Óðr is attested in the Prose Edda (Gylfaginning's notice of the marriage, daughter, journeys, and golden tears; Skáldskaparmál, where Freyja may be called "Óðr's wife" and gold "Freyja's weeping," and where Hnoss's parentage recurs), and in the Poetic Edda (Völuspá 25, Freyja as "Óðr's girl"; Hyndluljóð 47, Hyndla's taunt). Heimskringla's Ynglinga saga adds the daughter Gersemi. He appears in no narrative, speaks no line, and performs no deed anywhere in the corpus. There is no evidence of cult, place-names, or images. He is, quite literally, the mythology's most important missing person.

## Legacy

Óðr's legacy is philological and poetic. The word he personifies — óðr, the divine frenzy of inspiration — is one of the keys to how the Norse understood poetry, prophecy, and battle-ecstasy alike, and through Odin's name it structures the entire pantheon's head. For students of religion he remains a standing lesson in how gods can fission and fade. And for poets he bequeathed one of literature's finest images of longing: a wife whose weeping is wealth, scattering gold across the world's poems in search of a husband the sources never let her find.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is Óðr in Norse mythology?**
Óðr is the husband of the goddess Freyja and father of her daughter Hnoss (and, in Heimskringla, Gersemi). Almost nothing else is recorded of him except that he leaves on long journeys, while Freyja weeps tears of red gold and travels the world under many names seeking him.

**Q: Is Óðr the same as Odin?**
Possibly — it is one of the oldest debates in Norse scholarship. The names share the same root (óðr, "frenzy, inspiration"), and both gods are defined by wandering, while their wives Freyja and Frigg mirror one another closely. But the medieval sources always treat them as two distinct gods, so the identification remains a theory, not a fact.

**Q: Why does Freyja weep gold?**
Because Óðr is gone. Snorri says Freyja weeps for her absent husband and her tears are red gold — the origin of skaldic kennings that call gold "Freyja's tears." Her daughters' names, Hnoss and Gersemi, both mean "treasure," extending the family's golden imagery.

**Q: Where did Óðr go?**
No source says. Gylfaginning states only that he "went away on long journeys," with no reason, destination, or return. His unexplained absence is the entire point of his myth: he exists in the tradition as the object of Freyja's searching and grief.

**Q: What does the name Óðr mean?**
It is the Old Norse noun for "frenzy, ecstasy, inspiration, poetry" — the same word Hœnir gave to the first humans as "mind," and the root of Odin's own name (Óðinn, roughly "master of óðr"). Óðr is that force of inspired passion personified.
