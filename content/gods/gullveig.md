Völuspá gives her six lines and two thousand years of argument. The Æsir hoisted Gullveig ("Gold-drink" or "Gold-draught") on spears in [Odin](/gods/odin)'s hall and burned her; three times burned, three times born, "yet she lives still." Reborn, she took the name Heiðr ("Bright") and went house to house working seiðr, "ever the delight of an evil bride."

Her mistreatment is the immediate cause of the Æsir–Vanir war — the first war in the world. Who was she? A Vanir emissary, say some scholars, perhaps [Freyja](/gods/freyja) herself (mistress of seiðr and gold, whose tears are gold); the corrupting lust for gold personified, say others. The poem refuses to settle it. What is certain: the gods' first act of violence against a woman who would not die properly cost them their innocence and their monopoly on the world.

## The two stanzas

Because everything about Gullveig hangs on so little text, the text itself deserves to be looked at squarely. Völuspá — the great prophecy-poem that opens the Poetic Edda — reaches, around its twenty-first stanza, the moment the seeress calls "the first war in the world," and remembers its cause:

She remembers when Gullveig was studded with spears, and in the hall of Hár they burned her; three times they burned her, three times born, often, not seldom — yet she lives still.

The next stanza follows her transformation: they called her Heiðr wherever she came to houses, a völva of good prophecy — she charmed wands, she practised seiðr where she could, seiðr with a playing (or raving) mind, and was "ever the delight of an evil bride" (or "of evil women" — even the grammar is contested). Then, immediately, the gods sit in council to debate whether the Æsir should pay tribute, Odin hurls his spear over the host — the ritual opening of battle — and the wall of the Æsir's stronghold is broken by the war-magic of the Vanir. That is the entire dossier. Gullveig appears in no other poem, no saga, no inscription; Snorri, retelling the Æsir–Vanir war in Ynglinga saga, does not mention her burning at all. Six lines, and the door of the world's first war swinging on them.

## What does the burning mean?

Read plainly, the scene is an execution that fails three times. Spears and fire in Hár's — the High One's, Odin's — own hall suggest something judicial or sacrificial rather than battlefield violence: the gods collectively try to destroy this woman, deliberately, at home, and cannot. Her survival is not endurance but rebirth — þrisvar borin, "three times born" — which has reminded many readers of the way gold itself is refined: put through the fire again and again, and coming out brighter each time. Her name points the same way: gull is gold; veig is a word for an alcoholic drink, and possibly for power or strength — "Gold-drink," "Gold-draught," "Gold-strength." Burn the intoxicating gold-woman thrice and she emerges as Heiðr — a name meaning "bright, gleaming" (and echoing the word for heath and for honour), the classic professional name of the wandering seeress: several saga-völvas are called Heiðr, as if Völuspá's witch had franchised.

On one reading, then, the stanzas are a moral allegory the poet's audience would have felt in their bones: gold-lust comes among the gods, they try to destroy it, and violence only refines and multiplies it — burned in the hall, it comes back door to door, retailing enchantment to eager women. The corruption the gods could not kill becomes ambient. And their resort to violence is itself the fall: within two stanzas the world is at war.

## Is Gullveig Freyja?

The other great reading identifies her. Freyja is the mistress of seiðr, the magic Heiðr practises, and the sources say she brought it to the Æsir; Freyja is the gold-goddess whose tears are red gold and whose bynames multiply as she moves among strange peoples; Freyja is Vanir. If the Æsir tortured a Vanir woman in Odin's hall, the Vanir's war — opened by their breach of Asgard's wall — has a motive the poem otherwise never states. Scholars such as Turville-Petre and many since have found the equation attractive: Gullveig-Heiðr would be Freyja's dark introduction, the moment the gold-and-magic goddess entered the Æsir's world through fire. Others object that the poem names neither Freyja nor the Vanir in the burning stanzas, that völva-figures and gold-personifications need not be goddesses, and that the identification stacks speculation on silence. There is also a minority view connecting her with Angrboða in Hyndluljóð's "Völuspá in skamma" material, or reading her simply as the archetypal witch whose persecution inaugurates the world's violence. Two thousand years of argument, as promised — and the honest summary is that every position is a reading, none a fact.

## In the primary sources

Gullveig exists in exactly one place: stanzas 21–22 of Völuspá in the Poetic Edda (with the war following in 23–24), preserved in the Codex Regius and, with variations, in Hauksbók. Snorri quotes neighbouring stanzas of the poem but passes over her in silence — his Ynglinga saga account of the Æsir–Vanir war gives it no cause at all, and his Skáldskaparmál tale of [Kvasir](/gods/kvasir), born of the peace-spittle, tells only the war's end. The name Heiðr recurs for seeresses in Hyndluljóð (where a Heiðr is Angrboða's kin) and in several sagas of wandering völvas, which may be echoes, borrowings, or coincidence of a common professional name. No image, cult, or place-name of Gullveig is known.

## Legacy

For a figure of six lines, Gullveig has done heavy modern work. She is a fixture of scholarship on the Æsir–Vanir war, on seiðr and Norse attitudes to magic-working women, and on Völuspá's design — where the burning of a woman stands at the head of the world's violence the way the theft of gold stands at the head of its greed. Feminist readers of the myths have found in her the tradition's primal witch-burning, uncomfortably prophetic; poets and metal bands have found in "three times burned, three times born" an image that needs no footnote. She remains what the seeress made her: the unkillable question at the root of the first war.

## FAQ

**Q: Who is Gullveig in Norse mythology?**
Gullveig is a mysterious female figure who appears only in the poem Völuspá. The Æsir pierced her with spears and burned her three times in Odin's hall, and three times she was reborn. She then took the name Heiðr and practised seiðr magic — and her mistreatment triggered the Æsir–Vanir war.

**Q: What does the name Gullveig mean?**
The first element is gull, "gold"; the second, veig, means a strong drink and possibly "power." Translations include "Gold-drink," "Gold-draught," and "Gold-strength" — leading many scholars to read her as gold, or the lust for it, personified and refined by fire.

**Q: Is Gullveig the same as Freyja?**
Many scholars have argued so: like Gullveig-Heiðr, Freyja is a Vanir figure defined by gold and by seiðr magic, and the identification would explain why the Vanir went to war. But Völuspá never names her as Freyja, and other scholars reject or doubt the equation. The question is genuinely unsettled.

**Q: Why did the Æsir burn Gullveig?**
The poem does not say — it records only the act and its failure. Interpretations range from an attempt to destroy the corrupting power of gold, to hostility toward seiðr witchcraft, to the torture of a Vanir emissary. Whatever the motive, Völuspá presents it as the crime that began the first war in the world.

**Q: Who is Heiðr?**
Heiðr ("Bright") is the name Gullveig bore after her rebirths, as a wandering völva who charmed wands and worked seiðr from house to house. The same name is used for seeresses elsewhere in Old Norse literature, suggesting it was the archetypal name of the travelling prophetess.
