Njörðr rules the wind, calms sea and fire, and grants wealth to those who call on him — the Icelandic phrase "as rich as Njörðr" remembers him. He dwells at Nóatún ("Ship-yard") by the sea, and came to [Asgard](/realms/asgard) with his children [Freyr](/gods/freyr) and [Freyja](/gods/freyja) as a hostage sealing the peace after the [Æsir–Vanir war](/events/aesir-vanir-war).

His marriage to the giantess [Skaði](/gods/skadi) is one of the myths' wry tragedies of incompatibility: she, of the mountains, chose him by his beautiful feet believing he was [Baldr](/gods/baldr). They agreed to alternate — nine nights in her snowy Þrymheimr, nine at his sunny harbour — and could stand neither: he could not sleep for the howling of wolves, she could not sleep for the screeching of gulls. They parted. After [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), Njörðr is said to return to the Vanir.

## The god a seafaring people needed

For a civilisation that lived by the keel, Njörðr was daily bread. Snorri's Gylfaginning defines his office precisely: he rules the course of the wind and stills sea and fire; on him men call for voyages and for fishing; and he is so wealthy that he can grant lands and goods to those who pray to him. He is not the sea's rage — that belongs to the giants [Ægir](/giants/aegir) and [Rán](/giants/ran), who brew storms and net drowned sailors — but the sea's profit: fair passage, full nets, safe harbour, and the wealth that comes home in the hold. His hall Nóatún, "Ship-enclosure," sits where the water meets the land, loud with gulls and swans.

He is the patriarch of the [Vanir](/realms/vanaheim) in Asgard. When the first war in the world ended in a truce, hostages were exchanged, and Njörðr came to the Æsir with his children — the peace made flesh. Ynglinga saga in [Heimskringla](/texts/heimskringla) adds a detail the Æsir found scandalous: among the Vanir, Njörðr had fathered Freyr and Freyja on his own sister, for such marriages were lawful there, though forbidden among the Æsir. [Lokasenna](/texts/lokasenna) drags the old story out — [Loki](/gods/loki) jeers at Njörðr as a hostage sent east and adds cruder insults involving the daughters of the giant [Hymir](/giants/hymir) — and Njörðr answers with unbothered pride: his comfort is that he got a son whom no one hates, foremost of the Æsir. Even the flyting cannot dent the sea-god's prosperity.

## Nine nights of wolves, nine of gulls

Njörðr's great story is a marriage settlement gone gently, permanently wrong. When the gods killed the giant [Þjazi](/giants/thjazi) — the eagle who stole [Iðunn](/gods/idun) and her apples — his daughter Skaði came to Asgard in full war-gear demanding redress. Part of her compensation was a husband, to be chosen by the feet alone. Scanning the row of divine feet, she picked the most beautiful pair, certain they belonged to shining Baldr. They belonged to Njörðr, whose feet the sea had washed smooth.

They tried honestly. The compromise — nine nights in Þrymheimr among her mountains, nine (some manuscripts say three winters) at Nóatún by his sea — collapsed on contact with geography. Coming down from the fells, Njörðr declared he was weary of mountains: the howling of wolves seemed ugly to him after the song of swans. Skaði answered that she could not sleep by the shore for the screeching of the birds; the gull woke her every morning. Gylfaginning preserves the couple's paired verses like two halves of a broken coin. She went back up to Þrymheimr to ski and hunt; he stayed with his ships. No blame is assigned. It is the mythology's most human divorce: two decent beings, each faithful to a landscape the other cannot bear.

## Was Njörðr once a goddess?

The oldest attestation of the name is a famous puzzle. Around 98 AD, Tacitus described the north-Germanic tribes worshipping Nerthus — "Mother Earth" — a goddess whose veiled wagon toured the countryside bringing peace and plenty, her name the exact earlier form of Njörðr's. How a first-century earth-goddess relates to a medieval sea-god is one of the longest debates in Germanic scholarship: a change of sex over the centuries, a divine brother–sister pair sharing one name (which would neatly explain Njörðr's unnamed sister-wife among the Vanir), or a coincidence of naming. The sources allow no verdict, but the continuity of the fertility-and-peace portfolio is hard to dismiss.

His cult, at any rate, was real and broad: place-names like Njarðvík in Iceland, Nærøy in Norway and Njärdhem in Sweden map his worship, and the Icelandic law-books preserve a legal oath sworn by Freyr, Njörðr, and "the almighty áss." Ynglinga saga, euhemerising, makes him a king of the Swedes after Odin, in whose reign peace and good seasons were so unbroken that the Swedes believed he governed the harvests and the wealth of men.

## At Ragnarök

Njörðr has no combat assigned at the world's end. Instead [Vafþrúðnismál](/texts/vafthrudnismal) gives him a quieter fate: created in [Vanaheim](/realms/vanaheim) by wise powers and given to the gods as a hostage, "at the doom of men he will come home again among the wise Vanir." The hostage's contract runs out when the world does. While Odin and Thor fall on the plain of [Vígríðr](/concepts/vigrid), the old sea-god simply goes home — the peace he embodied dissolving with the war that ends everything.

## In the primary sources

Gylfaginning in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) gives his portrait, his hall, and the Skaði marriage; Skáldskaparmál calls him "god of chariots" and "giver of rings" in kennings. In the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) he answers Loki in Lokasenna, is prophesied home to the Vanir in Vafþrúðnismál, and is named among the mighty in [Grímnismál](/texts/grimnismal), which assigns him Nóatún. Heimskringla's Ynglinga saga makes him Sweden's second euhemerised king, and Tacitus's Germania stands behind him at a distance of a millennium with the goddess Nerthus.

## Legacy

"As rich as Njörðr," the Icelandic saying went — the sea-god lingering as a byword for wealth long after his altars fell. His name lies scattered along the coasts of Scandinavia in harbour and island names, precisely where his worshippers needed him, and the Nerthus question keeps him permanently interesting to scholars: the god who may have crossed a thousand years, a language, and a gender on the way from Mother Earth to master of the ship-yard.

## FAQ

**Q: What is Njörðr the god of?**
The sea as livelihood: wind, seafaring, fishing, and the wealth they bring. Snorri says he controls the wind, calms sea and fire, and is rich enough to grant land and goods to those who invoke him — sailors' and fishermen's prayers were his domain.

**Q: Why did Njörðr and Skaði separate?**
Their homes were irreconcilable. They alternated between her mountain hall Þrymheimr and his seaside Nóatún, but he could not endure the wolves' howling in the fells and she could not sleep for the gulls at the shore. They parted without villainy — mountain and sea cannot share a house.

**Q: Who are Njörðr's children?**
Freyr and Freyja, the great Vanir twins, borne — according to Ynglinga saga — by his own sister, since sibling marriage was lawful among the Vanir. His later marriage to Skaði produced no children in the surviving sources.

**Q: Does Njörðr die at Ragnarök?**
No death is recorded for him. Vafþrúðnismál says instead that at the end of the world he returns home to the wise Vanir — the hostage of the gods' peace released when the gods' world ends.

**Q: What is the connection between Njörðr and Nerthus?**
Tacitus's first-century goddess Nerthus ("Mother Earth") bears the exact linguistic ancestor of Njörðr's name. Whether the deity changed gender over the centuries, or Nerthus and Njörðr were a paired sister and brother, remains one of the oldest unsolved questions in Germanic religion.
