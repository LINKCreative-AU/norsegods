Auðumbla is the second living thing in the cosmos and its first nurturer. Formed from the same dripping rime as Ymir, she fed the primordial giant with four rivers of milk — and fed herself by licking the salty ice-blocks of the void.

Her licking was a slow act of creation: on the first day a man's hair emerged from the ice, on the second a head, on the third the whole of Búri — beautiful, great, and strong — whose grandsons Odin, Vili, and Vé would slay the very giant Auðumbla nursed and build the world from his body. The cow who uncovered the gods appears only in a few sentences of Gylfaginning, yet whole cosmologies hang on her tongue.

## The cow at the beginning of the world

Norse creation begins not with light but with temperature. In the beginning, Snorri tells us in Gylfaginning, there was Ginnungagap, the yawning void, with the ice-world Niflheim to its north and the fire-world Muspelheim to its south. Where the rivers of Niflheim froze and the sparks of Muspelheim drifted, rime met warmth in the middle of the gap — and the melting drips quickened into the first living being: [Ymir](/giants/ymir), the primordial giant, ancestor of all the frost-giants, who sweated further giants from his armpits as he slept.

Then Gylfaginning's questioner asks the obvious, practical question — the kind the Prose Edda is so good at asking: what did Ymir live on? And the answer is Auðumbla. The melting rime dripped again, and next there stood a cow, from whose udders ran four rivers of milk; on these Ymir fed. The detail of the four rivers is worth pausing over: rivers, not streams — nourishment on the scale of geography, for a creature whose body would later become the world. The first relationship in the Norse cosmos is not a battle or a marriage but a feeding: a cow suckling a giant in the dark between fire and ice.

But what fed the cow? Snorri's questioner asks that too. Auðumbla licked the rime-stones, which were salty — the primordial cow sustained by the primordial mineral lick, an image any Scandinavian farmer would recognise from the byre, projected onto the void.

## What did Auðumbla's licking uncover?

Here the story turns from sustenance to creation, and Auðumbla becomes the strangest sculptor in mythology. As she licked the salty ice-blocks, something emerged under her tongue. By the evening of the first day, a man's hair stood out of the stone. On the second day, a head. On the third day, the whole man was free: Búri, "beautiful in appearance, big and powerful" — the first of the god-kind, uncovered from the ice as if he had been in it all along, waiting.

Búri begot a son, Borr, who married Bestla, daughter of the giant Bölþorn — the god-line and the giant-line crossing at the very first opportunity — and their sons were [Odin](/gods/odin), Vili, and Vé. Those three brothers killed Ymir, drowned nearly all the frost-giants in his blood, and built the world from his carcass: earth from his flesh, mountains from his bones, the sky from his skull, [Midgard](/realms/midgard) from his brows. The symmetry is stark, and Snorri does not soften it. The cow fed Ymir; the cow freed the line that would slaughter him. Both the victim of the first killing and its perpetrators' whole ancestry pass through Auðumbla. She nourishes the world's raw material and uncovers the world's makers, and then — her double work complete — the sources never mention her again. What became of the cow, no text says.

## What does the name Auðumbla mean?

The manuscripts themselves cannot quite agree on it — Auðumbla, Auðhumla, and Auðumla all appear — and the etymology is correspondingly debated. The first element is generally connected with auðr, "riches, abundance," and the second with a word for "hornless" or "polled" (humala), giving something like "the hornless cow rich in milk" — an abundance-cow, a wealth of nourishment on four legs. Whatever the precise parsing, the sense that tradition heard in the name is not in doubt: she is plenty itself, the inexhaustible udder at the base of everything.

## Why does creation need a cow?

Auðumbla appears only in Snorri, but she does not feel invented. A great primordial bovine at the root of creation is a genuinely ancient Indo-European idea — Iranian tradition has its primordial ox from whose body life springs, and cattle stand at the economic and ritual centre of every early Indo-European society we can see. For the Norse specifically, the cow was wealth: the word for cattle, fé, is also the word for money and property, and the first rune of the futhark bears its name. A cosmology built by cattle-farmers put a cow at the beginning of the world for the same reason it put a mineral lick in the void — because that is what abundance and survival actually looked like. Scholars therefore generally take Auðumbla to be a genuine fragment of old myth that only Snorri happened to preserve, rather than his invention; but readers should know the evidential situation plainly: no Eddic poem, no skaldic verse quoted by Snorri, and no carving shows her. She stands on a few sentences of the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) — supported by the name-lists appended to it, where she is remembered as the "noblest of cows."

## In the primary sources

Everything rests on Gylfaginning, chapters 5–6, where High answers Gangleri's questions about the beginning: the rime dripping into Ymir, the cow formed next from the melting drops, the four rivers of milk, the salt licking, and the three-day uncovering of Búri. The Nafnaþulur name-lists transmitted with the Prose Edda include her among cows. That is the complete corpus. Nothing describes her appearance beyond what a cow is; nothing records her end. For a figure on whom the entire genealogy of gods and the entire raw material of the world depend, the brevity is astonishing — and a useful reminder of how much Norse myth survives only because Snorri thought a question worth answering.

## Legacy

Auðumbla's afterlife is quieter than the monsters' but persistent. She presides, fittingly, over Icelandic dairy: Auðhumla is the name of Iceland's major dairy cooperative, and her image has served Nordic agriculture as emblem and brand for generations. She appears in modern retellings of the creation, in Marvel's cosmology, and in games such as God of War, usually as a figure of primeval calm amid the fire and ice. The picture that endures is Snorri's, and it needs no improvement: a cow licking a salty stone in the dark, and a god slowly appearing under her tongue.

## FAQ

**What is Auðumbla in Norse mythology?**
She is the primordial cow, the second living being in the cosmos, formed from the melting rime of Ginnungagap just after the giant Ymir. Four rivers of milk ran from her udders, feeding Ymir, while she fed herself by licking the salty rime-stones.

**Who did Auðumbla lick out of the ice?**
Búri, the ancestor of the gods — hair on the first day, head on the second, the whole man on the third. Búri's son Borr married the giantess Bestla, and their sons Odin, Vili, and Vé killed Ymir and made the world from his body.

**What does the name Auðumbla mean?**
Probably something like "hornless cow rich in milk": the first element is linked to auðr, "riches, abundance," the second to a word for a hornless cow. The manuscripts spell the name several ways — Auðumbla, Auðhumla, Auðumla — and the etymology remains debated.

**Where is Auðumbla mentioned in the sources?**
Only in Snorri's Prose Edda — Gylfaginning's account of the creation — plus the name-lists (Nafnaþulur) attached to it. No Eddic poem or skaldic verse mentions her, though most scholars think Snorri preserved a genuinely ancient myth rather than inventing one.

**What happened to Auðumbla?**
No source says. After she uncovers Búri, she disappears from the record entirely — the mythology never mentions her death, her fate at Ragnarök, or anything else. Her story is complete in a few sentences: she fed the first giant, freed the first god, and was gone.
