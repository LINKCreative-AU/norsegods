At the feast in Valhalla, Odin does not eat. Every dish set before him goes to the two wolves at his feet, Geri and Freki — both names meaning, more or less, 'the greedy one' — while the god takes only wine. It is a small tableau with a cold logic: the god of the battle-dead keeps wolves, the battlefield's cleanup crew, as his table companions.

With the ravens Huginn and Muninn above and the wolves below, Odin sits at the centre of a carrion ecosystem — thought, memory, and appetite. Warriors called wolves 'Viðrir's hounds' (Odin's hounds), and to feed them meant to make corpses. Wolf and raven following an army were Odin's outriders, and the poets never let anyone forget it.

## Odin's table

The scene comes from Grímnismál, where [Odin](/gods/odin) — disguised as Grímnir, tortured between two fires — describes his own household from the inside. Stanza 19 gives the wolves their moment: the war-accustomed, glorious Father of Hosts sates Geri and Freki, "but on wine alone the weapon-noble Odin ever lives." Snorri repeats the picture in Gylfaginning: Odin gives the food on his table to two wolves that he owns, called Geri and Freki, "and he himself needs no food: wine is to him both drink and meat."

The tableau rewards slow looking. [Valhalla](/realms/valhalla) is a hall organised entirely around eating and drinking — the einherjar feast nightly on the boar [Sæhrímnir](/creatures/saehrimnir) and drink the mead of the goat [Heiðrún](/creatures/heidrun) — and at its head sits a host who touches none of it. The lord of the feast passes his portion under the table, to wolves. Everything about it separates Odin from his guests: they are dead men being fattened for the last battle; he is something else, sustained by wine — the aristocratic, sacrificial drink — and by things subtler than meat. Elsewhere in the mythology Odin trades an eye for wisdom and hangs nine nights for the runes; a god who consumes knowledge has no great need of dinner.

## What do Geri and Freki's names mean?

Both names are adjectives for hunger walking on four legs. Geri derives from gerr, "greedy, gluttonous"; Freki from frekr, "avid, voracious, bold." They are barely names at all — more like the same idea stated twice, "Greedy and Greedy," a matched pair in the old poetic style that loves alliterating doublets ([Huginn and Muninn](/creatures/huginn-and-muninn), [Sköll and Hati](/creatures/skoll-and-hati)). Both words also serve, in Eddic poetry, as common nouns for "wolf": Völuspá calls the monstrous wolf of the end-time freki, and skaldic verse uses geri the same way. Odin's wolves, in other words, are named simply Wolf and Wolf — appetite in its purest grammatical form, seated at the foot of the god who supplies armies to feed it.

## Wolves of the battlefield

Geri and Freki have no adventures; their meaning lies in what wolves meant. In Norse poetry the wolf is one of the "beasts of battle" — with the raven and eagle, the professional attendee of every slaughter — and the poets bound the animal to Odin by name. In the first Helgi poem, wolves are called "Viðrir's hounds," Odin's hounds, running greedy over the field. To say a war-leader "fed the wolf" or "gladdened Geri" was the skalds' standing compliment: it meant he killed men and left them where they fell. The economy is exact and grim — the god of battle keeps wolves; battle feeds them; the prince who serves Odin best sets the god's own table.

The wolves also shadow Odin's human following. The úlfheðnar, "wolf-coats," appear in the sagas as ecstatic warriors of Odin fighting in wolf-skins, kin to the bear-shirted berserkers; a famous Vendel-era bronze die from Torslunda in Sweden shows a spear-dancer beside a warrior in a wolf's pelt. And archaeologists and iconographers have often read the animal figures accompanying Odin-like riders on migration-era gold bracteates and helmet plates as the god's wolf and ravens avant la lettre. None of these images comes labelled, and caution is owed; but the cluster — god, spear, wolf, raven — is old, coherent, and everywhere in the material.

## Why does Odin keep wolves at all?

The sources never explain, but the pattern speaks. Odin's animals divide his portfolio between them: the ravens are his mind abroad — thought and memory over all the worlds — while the wolves are his appetite at home, the devouring that the war-god's business ultimately is. The seating chart of Valhalla is a diagram of Odin himself: intelligence at the shoulders, hunger at the feet, and the god between them, drinking. Some scholars add an ironic footnote from the far end of the story: the god who tames wolves as pets will be swallowed at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) by the wolf he could not tame, [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir). The mythology never states the rhyme, but it is hard to believe the poets missed it — the wolf under Odin's table and the wolf with Odin in its belly are the two ends of one long, cold joke about what feeds on what.

## In the primary sources

The attestations are brief and consistent. Grímnismál 19, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), is the founding stanza: Geri and Freki fed by the Father of Hosts, who lives on wine alone. Gylfaginning 38, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), turns the stanza into prose and quotes it. Beyond these, the names live in the poets' word-hoard: geri and freki as common nouns for wolf in Eddic and skaldic verse, "Viðrir's hounds" in Helgakviða Hundingsbana I, and the whole beasts-of-battle convention in which every field of slain is a meal served to Odin's animals. The wolves have no parentage, no fate, and no story of their own — they are heraldry that eats.

## Legacy

Geri and Freki have padded quietly into the modern world wherever Odin's iconography goes: fantasy novels, Marvel's Asgard, video games, and a great deal of Norse-revival art in which the seated god is flanked by two wolves and two ravens. Their names are given to sled dogs, military units, and — in Freki's case — warships and wolf-packs of less honourable kinds; the imagery's appropriation by extremist groups is a modern misuse the medieval sources neither anticipate nor support. The original scene remains the best version: the greatest hall in the cosmos at dinner, and its host silently handing everything down to Greedy and Greedy, because a god who bought wisdom with an eye has no appetite left for meat.

## FAQ

**What are the names of Odin's wolves?**
Geri and Freki. Both names are formed from adjectives meaning "greedy" or "ravenous" — effectively "the Greedy One" twice over — and both words also serve as ordinary poetic terms for "wolf" in Old Norse verse.

**Why does Odin give his food to Geri and Freki?**
According to Grímnismál and Gylfaginning, Odin needs no food: wine is both meat and drink to him. Everything served to him at Valhalla's feast goes to the wolves at his feet. The sources offer no further explanation; the image marks Odin apart from the dead warriors he hosts.

**Do Geri and Freki appear in any myths?**
No narrative survives about them. They exist in the feast-scene of Grímnismál and Snorri's retelling, and in the poets' habit of calling battlefield wolves by their names or calling wolves "Odin's hounds." They have no recorded origin, deeds, or fate at Ragnarök.

**Are Geri and Freki connected to Fenrir?**
Not by any stated kinship — Fenrir is Loki's son, and the sources give Geri and Freki no parentage at all. The connection is thematic: Odin keeps two tame wolves at his feet, and is destined to die in the jaws of the one wolf that would not be tamed.
