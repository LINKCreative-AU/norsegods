At the bottom of the cosmos, beneath the third root of the world-tree by the spring Hvergelmir, Níðhöggr ('Malice-Striker') gnaws eternally at the root of everything. On the shore of corpses, Náströnd — a hall woven of serpents whose venom drips inward — he sucks the bodies of the worst dead: oath-breakers, murderers, and seducers of other men's wives. The name carries the force of níð, the Norse term for unforgivable dishonour.

He conducts a running feud by messenger: the squirrel Ratatoskr scurries the length of Yggdrasil carrying insults between the dragon below and the eagle at the crown. In the last lines of Völuspá, after the new world has risen, Níðhöggr flies up from the depths with corpses in his wings — either the old evil surviving into the new age, or its final departure; the poem leaves it dark.

## The dragon beneath the world-tree

Norse cosmology hangs everything on a tree, and then — with characteristic honesty — sets something at the bottom eating it. [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil), the ash that holds the nine worlds, stands on three great roots, and Snorri's Gylfaginning maps them: one among the Æsir, one among the frost-giants, and one over Niflheim, the primordial world of cold and dark. Beneath that third root lies the roaring spring Hvergelmir, source of the world's rivers — "and Níðhöggr gnaws the root from below." He is not alone down there. Grímnismál, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), says that more serpents lie under Yggdrasil "than any fool imagines," and names a whole nest of them — Góinn and Móinn, Grábakr and Grafvölluðr, Ófnir and Sváfnir — who "gnaw the tree's twigs" forever. Níðhöggr is the chief and the worst of an entire subterranean fauna of destruction.

The same poem steps back and takes in the whole suffering tree in one of the Edda's bleakest stanzas: "The ash Yggdrasil endures hardship more than men know: a hart bites it above, its side rots, and Níðhöggr tears it beneath." The tree that carries the cosmos is not serene; it is under attack at every level, and the dragon is the attack from below — decay itself, given jaws. Nothing in the sources suggests he can be stopped, bargained with, or even confronted; no god ever goes down to fight Níðhöggr. He is simply a condition of the world: whatever holds everything up is being eaten from underneath, always.

## Náströnd: the shore of corpses

Völuspá gives Níðhöggr his second office, and it is a grim one. Far from the sun, on Náströnd — "Corpse-Shore" — stands a hall whose doors face north. It is built of woven serpents, their backs plaited like wattle-work, and their heads point inward so the venom drips down through the smoke-hole; the dead who belong there wade heavy streams of it. And who belongs there is precise: perjurers — breakers of sworn oaths — murderers, and men who seduced the wives of others. "There Níðhöggr sucks the corpses of the dead," the poem says, "and the wolf tears men."

The selection of sins is a window into Norse values: not unbelief, not impiety, but oath-breaking, secret killing, and betrayal of another man's household — the three crimes that dissolve the trust a small, hard society runs on. Snorri repeats the description in Gylfaginning, quoting the stanzas, and places Náströnd among the fates awaiting the dead. Readers should know that scholars have long debated how much of this punishment-hall owes its shape to Christian ideas of hell filtering into a late pagan poem — Völuspá was composed around the conversion, and the venom-hall has a whiff of sermon about it. But the dragon and the níð are native: no missionary invented Níðhöggr.

## What does the name Níðhöggr mean?

The second element is plain: höggr, "striker," "hewer," from the verb to hew. The first element is the potent one. Níð in Old Norse is not ordinary insult; it is the formal term for contemptible infamy — the state of a man who has broken oaths, done shameful violence, or otherwise put himself beyond honour. Níð could be declared in verse or carved on a scorn-pole, and to be a níðingr was social death. So "Níðhöggr" is usually rendered "Malice-Striker" or "he who strikes with níð" — but the genitive relationship can also run the other way: the striker of the níðingar, the one who visits punishment on the irredeemably dishonoured. That is exactly what he does on Náströnd. The name allows both readings — the dragon as malice, and the dragon as what malice earns — and the myth needs both.

## The feud along the tree

Níðhöggr's one social relationship is an insult war. At the crown of Yggdrasil sits an unnamed [eagle](/creatures/eagle-of-yggdrasil), wise in many things, with the hawk Veðrfölnir perched between its eyes; at the root gnaws the dragon; and between them, endlessly, runs the squirrel [Ratatoskr](/creatures/ratatoskr), carrying — Snorri specifies — "slanderous words" from one to the other. Grímnismál states the route: the squirrel bears the eagle's words down to Níðhöggr. Top and bottom of the cosmos, wisdom and malice, connected by nothing except traffic in abuse. Commentators ancient and modern have noticed how the image completes the picture of the tree's suffering: Yggdrasil is gnawed from below, browsed from above, and quarrelled over along its whole length — held together, and held in tension, at once.

## At Ragnarök and after

Níðhöggr does not fight at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok); no source puts him on the field of Vígríðr. His moment comes after — and it is the last image of the greatest poem in the North. Völuspá ends with the new world risen: the earth green again from the sea, the eagle over the mountain-stream, the surviving gods on Iðavöllr, a hall fairer than the sun. And then, in the final stanza, "there comes the dark dragon flying, the gleaming serpent, up from Niðafjöll" — the Dark Mountains — "Níðhöggr bears corpses in his feathers as he flies over the plain. Now she will sink."

What it means has been argued for centuries. Is the dragon's flight the last departure of evil from a cleansed world — malice carrying its dead away for good? Or is he the one thing that survives every apocalypse, rising into the new age with the old corpses still in his wings — the poem's final refusal of a clean ending? The seeress does not say; she sinks. The ambiguity is almost certainly deliberate, and honest retellings leave it exactly as dark as the poet did.

## In the primary sources

Everything known about Níðhöggr comes from three texts. Völuspá gives Náströnd and the corpse-sucking (stanzas 38–39 in most editions) and the dragon's final flight from Niðafjöll (the closing stanza). Grímnismál gives the cosmic geography: the squirrel-borne feud with the eagle (st. 32) and the gnawing of Yggdrasil from beneath (st. 35), amid its catalogue of the tree's serpents. Snorri's Gylfaginning, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), systematises it all — the root over Hvergelmir, the serpent-hall, the feud — quoting the Eddic stanzas as he goes. There is no saga appearance, no skaldic adventure, no physical description beyond "dark" and "gleaming," and no explanation of his origin. Níðhöggr simply is, at the bottom, from the beginning to past the end.

## Legacy

Níðhöggr's descendants are legion wherever fantasy needs a dragon at the root of the world. He appears under his own name in video games (Final Fantasy XIV, God of War, Fire Emblem), in Magic: The Gathering and Dungeons & Dragons bestiaries, and in metal lyrics beyond counting; the "dragon gnawing at the foundations" has become shorthand, in political essays as much as novels, for corrosion working unseen at the base of any great structure. Few figures from any mythology have supplied so durable a metaphor while remaining, in the sources, so briefly and darkly sketched.

## FAQ

**What does Níðhöggr do?**
Two things, forever: he gnaws the root of the world-tree Yggdrasil from beneath, by the spring Hvergelmir, and he feeds on the corpses of the worst of the dead — oath-breakers, murderers, and seducers — on Náströnd, the Shore of Corpses. He also trades insults with the eagle at Yggdrasil's crown via the squirrel Ratatoskr.

**What does the name Níðhöggr mean?**
Roughly "Malice-Striker." The element níð denotes the deepest Norse category of dishonour — the infamy of oath-breakers and cowards — and höggr means "striker" or "hewer." The name can be read as "he who strikes with malice" or "he who strikes the dishonoured," and the dragon does both.

**Does Níðhöggr fight at Ragnarök?**
No source places him in the battle. His appearance comes in Völuspá's final stanza, after the new world has risen, when he flies up from the Dark Mountains with corpses in his wings. Whether this means evil departing the renewed world or evil surviving into it is left deliberately unresolved.

**Is Níðhöggr a dragon or a serpent?**
The sources use both images: Völuspá calls him "the dark dragon" and "the gleaming serpent" in the same stanza, and he flies with feathered wings while elsewhere gnawing among a nest of serpents. Norse tradition drew no firm line between the great serpent and the dragon — Fáfnir and Jörmungandr straddle it too.

**Where does Níðhöggr live?**
Beneath the third root of Yggdrasil, the one that reaches over Niflheim, beside the seething spring Hvergelmir from which the world's rivers flow — and on Náströnd, the corpse-shore with its hall woven of venomous serpents, far from the sun, its doors facing north.
