Every dawn Odin sends out his ravens Huginn ('Thought') and Muninn ('Memory'), and by breakfast they are back on his shoulders, whispering the news of the nine worlds. They are why Odin is called Hrafnaguð, the raven-god, and why nothing happens far from his knowing.

In Grímnismál, Odin admits an anxiety: 'I fear for Huginn, that he come not back, yet more anxious am I for Muninn' — thought can be risked; memory, once lost, is lost. Ravens followed real armies to real battlefields, and skalds called the raven 'Huginn's...' anything as a kenning for the carrion bird; to feed the raven was to kill men. The pair appear on helmet plates and brooches centuries older than the written myths.

## The god's daily intelligence service

Snorri describes the arrangement in Gylfaginning with the brisk clarity of a job posting. Two ravens sit on [Odin's](/gods/odin) shoulders and say into his ear all the tidings they see or hear. At dawn he sends them out to fly over the whole world, and by day-meal — the morning meal — they are back. "From this he gets knowledge of many things," Snorri writes, and from this men call him the raven-god. Whatever else Odin is — war-god, poetry-god, god of the hanged — he is above all the god who knows, and the ravens are how the knowing gets done.

The picture deserves a moment's attention, because it is stranger than familiarity makes it. The chief of the gods, seated in his high seat Hliðskjálf from which he can see into every world, still runs birds. Sight, apparently, is not enough; Odin wants report — things heard as well as seen, news carried back and spoken into the ear. The mythology consistently shows Odin acquiring wisdom at terrible prices: an eye pawned in [Mímir's](/gods/mimir) well, nine nights hanged on the windswept tree for the runes. Against those purchases, the ravens are his renewable income — the daily interest on a lifetime of buying knowledge dear. And in Heimskringla's Ynglinga saga, where Snorri retells Odin as a wizard-king of old, the same birds appear rationalised: Odin had two ravens he had trained to speak, and they flew far over the lands and brought him tidings. Even euhemerised into history, Odin keeps his ravens.

## What do the ravens' names mean?

Huginn is formed from Old Norse hugr — "thought," "mind," the thinking, intending part of a person. Muninn is usually derived from the verb muna, "to remember," giving "memory," though the related noun munr can also mean "mind" or "desire," and scholars have noted the overlap: the two names may once have been near-synonyms, a doubled figure of the mind itself. Either way, the tradition that reached the Eddas heard them as Thought and Memory, and the allegory was surely audible to the poets too: every morning the god's mind goes out over the world, and every morning what he knows comes home to roost.

That the god's mental faculties take the shape of birds is not a casual choice. Old Norse tradition is full of minds that travel — seers and shape-changers whose spirit goes out in animal form (the hugr faring abroad while the body lies still) — and Odin is the master of exactly such arts; Ynglinga saga says his body would lie as if asleep while he fared in a bird's or beast's shape to distant lands. Huginn and Muninn look very much like this belief given permanent, feathered form: the god's own mind, flying.

## Why does Odin fear losing Muninn more?

The one flash of feeling in the sources comes in Grímnismál, stanza 20, where Odin — disguised, tortured between two fires — recites the ravens' routine and then confides: "Huginn and Muninn fly each day over the wide earth. I fear for Huginn, that he come not back; yet I am more anxious for Muninn."

The poem does not explain the asymmetry, and its restraint has made the stanza famous. The most natural reading is the one that stings: thought can be sent out again — new thoughts are cheap — but memory, once it fails to return, is gone with everything it carried. For a god who has paid an eye and a hanging for what he knows, the nightmare is not ignorance of today's news but the loss of everything already learned. Some scholars read the stanza more technically, as the seer's fear that the mind sent out in trance might not find its way back to the body — the occupational hazard of Odin's own magic. The two readings are not really rivals. Both say: the god of wisdom lives with the daily possibility that his mind will not come home.

## Ravens of the battlefield

The myth grew from observation. Ravens follow armies; they always have. In skaldic poetry the raven is the constant attendant of battle, and the kennings bind the birds to Odin by name: warriors "feed Huginn," a battle "reddens Huginn's claws," blood is the raven's drink, and a generous prince is generous above all to the birds of the slain. To say a king fed the raven well was the poets' hardest compliment — it meant he made corpses. The wolf and the raven together are Odin's beasts of battle, the same pairing the god keeps at home in the hall: ravens at his shoulders, the wolves [Geri and Freki](/creatures/geri-and-freki) at his feet, the god of the battle-dead seated at the centre of the battlefield's ecosystem.

The birds are also among the oldest datable pieces of Odin's cult. Helmet plates from Vendel-era Sweden — the sixth and seventh centuries, hundreds of years before the Eddas were written — show a spear-bearing rider attended by two birds, and bird-pairs flank figures on brooches and bracteates earlier still. A famous seventh-century helmet plaque die from Uppland shows the rider with two birds so prominent they can hardly be anything but the ravens. On such objects Huginn and Muninn — or their ancestors — were already at the god's shoulders while the poems were still centuries from parchment. Later tradition kept the association martial: Norse armies carried raven banners, and the sagas and chronicles credit such flags to leaders from the sons of Ragnarr Loðbrók to the earls of Orkney and Cnut's armies in England.

## In the primary sources

The core attestations are two. Grímnismál 20, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), gives the ravens' names, their daily flight over the world, and Odin's fear for their return. Gylfaginning 38, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), gives the prose account — shoulders, dawn dispatch, breakfast-time return, the whispering of tidings — and derives from it Odin's title Hrafnaguð. Around these fixed points lies a wide scatter: Skáldskaparmál teaches that the raven may be called Huginn in kennings and preserves skaldic verse doing exactly that; Ynglinga saga in Heimskringla rationalises the birds as trained messengers; and the Third Grammatical Treatise and various sagas keep the association alive. Notably, no myth survives in which the ravens do anything except their job — they have no adventure, no speech of their own, no fate at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok). They are pure function: the going-out and coming-back of the god's mind, every day until the end.

## Legacy

Huginn and Muninn may be the most-reproduced image Norse myth ever made. The paired ravens are everywhere in the modern Norse revival — the logos of sports clubs and breweries, the insignia of military units, tattoos beyond counting, Marvel's films, video games from God of War (where a certain green-eyed pair of ravens hide in every corner of the map) to Assassin's Creed Valhalla. Ravens named Thought and Memory have proved irresistible to writers on cognition and computing alike. And at the Tower of London, ravens are still kept against the kingdom's fall — a different legend, but the same old instinct: where the ravens are, the knowing power still holds.

## FAQ

**What do Huginn and Muninn's names mean?**
Huginn means "Thought," from Old Norse hugr, the mind. Muninn is usually translated "Memory," from the verb muna, "to remember" — though the root can also shade toward "mind" or "desire." Together they personify the faculties Odin sends out over the world each day.

**What do Odin's ravens actually do?**
According to Gylfaginning, Odin sends them out at dawn to fly over all the world, and they return by the morning meal to sit on his shoulders and whisper everything they have seen and heard. They are the source of much of Odin's knowledge and the reason he is called Hrafnaguð, "raven-god."

**Why is Odin more worried about Muninn than Huginn?**
In Grímnismál Odin says he fears Huginn may not return, but fears more for Muninn. The poem gives no reason. The usual reading is that lost thought can be replaced but lost memory cannot; some scholars also see the seer's fear that a mind sent out in trance might fail to return to the body.

**Are Huginn and Muninn mentioned outside the Eddas?**
Yes. Skaldic poetry uses "Huginn" in kennings for the raven of battle, and Ynglinga saga in Heimskringla describes Odin's two speaking ravens as news-bringers. Visually, paired birds attend an Odin-like figure on Vendel-era helmet plates from the sixth and seventh centuries — evidence the motif predates all surviving texts.

**Do the ravens fight at Ragnarök?**
No source gives Huginn and Muninn any role at Ragnarök, or indeed any story beyond their daily flight. Their entire myth is the routine itself — and Odin's quiet fear that one morning it will break.
