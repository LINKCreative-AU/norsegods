At the very top of the world-tree sits an eagle whom the sources, maddeningly, never name — only credit with 'knowledge of many things.' Between its eyes perches a hawk, Veðrfölnir ('storm-pale'), a lookout upon a lookout, one of the strangest and least explained images in the cosmology.

The eagle's one recorded activity is the feud: the squirrel Ratatoskr runs the trunk carrying insults between the eagle above and Níðhöggr gnawing below. Wisdom at the crown, malice at the root, and a rodent monetising the traffic — the world-tree's ecosystem, and possibly the first flame war.

## The watcher at the crown

The great ash [Yggdrasil](/realms/yggdrasil) is the armature of the Norse cosmos, and its inhabitants read like a moral diagram of everything: serpents and the dragon [Níðhöggr](/creatures/nidhogg) gnawing at the roots, four harts browsing the branches, the goat [Heiðrún](/creatures/heidrun) and the hart Eikþyrnir on Valhalla's roof at its heart — and at the very summit, above all of it, the eagle. Snorri places him in Gylfaginning with a single loaded clause: "An eagle sits in the branches of the ash, and it is knowing of many things." That is the entire characterisation. The highest creature in the cosmology, holding the best vantage point in all the worlds, is defined by one attribute — knowledge — and given no name to hold it under.

The anonymity is genuinely strange, because Norse tradition names everything. The squirrel on the trunk has a name; the four harts have names; the serpents under the root are catalogued individually; even the cook and kettle of Valhalla are named. Yet the wise eagle at the top of everything is simply "an eagle." Whether a name was lost in transmission, or the figure was always a nameless emblem — wisdom as such, surveying the worlds — the sources give no way to decide. The gap has at least the virtue of honesty: at the top of the tree of everything sits something that knows much and says little, and we do not even know what to call it.

## Who is Veðrfölnir, the hawk between its eyes?

Snorri adds the detail that makes the image unforgettable: "and between its eyes sits the hawk called Veðrfölnir." The name is usually rendered "storm-pale," "wind-bleached" — weathered pale by the winds of the summit. Nothing else is said: not what the hawk does, not why it sits there, not whose eyes see for whom.

Interpretation has been busy ever since. The most common reading takes the arrangement functionally — the hawk as the eagle's scout, sharper-eyed still, a lookout mounted upon a lookout, so that the tree's summit sees everything twice. Others have compared it to the way knowledge is layered elsewhere in the mythology: [Odin](/gods/odin) has his ravens [Huginn and Muninn](/creatures/huginn-and-muninn) as detachable eyes and mind, and Mímir's well holds sight beyond the eye that was pledged for it; a hawk perched on the seat of vision itself fits a cosmology fascinated by seeing and its instruments. But these are readings, not sources. The texts give us only the picture: a pale hawk on an eagle's brow, at the top of the world, forever.

## Is the eagle Hræsvelgr?

Norse tradition knows a second great eagle, and the two are easily and often confused. Vafþrúðnismál tells of Hræsvelgr, "Corpse-Swallower," a giant in eagle's shape who sits at the edge of heaven — at the north of the sky, Snorri says — and from whose beating wings all winds come. Some readers, medieval and modern, have wondered whether the wind-eagle and the tree-eagle are one figure seen from two angles; the summit of the world-tree and the rim of heaven are not far apart, and both perches suit a maker of weather. But the sources treat them separately: Hræsvelgr has a name, a nature (a jötunn in eagle form), and a job (the winds); the tree-eagle has no name, an attribute (wisdom), and a feud. Snorri describes both in Gylfaginning without connecting them, and careful summaries keep them apart. The overlap remains an open question — one more thing about the crown of Yggdrasil that the tradition left unresolved.

## The feud with the dragon

The eagle's one recorded activity is a quarrel it never conducts in person. Grímnismál 32 sets the mechanism: the squirrel [Ratatoskr](/creatures/ratatoskr) runs up and down the ash, carrying "the eagle's words" from above down to Níðhöggr below; Snorri specifies the cargo as slander and spite, and makes the service two-way. About the feud's origin, content, and stakes, the sources are silent. What survives is pure structure, and the structure is eloquent: the wisest creature in the cosmos and the most malicious are locked in permanent, mediated hostility, connected by nothing but a courier of insults. The tree that holds all the worlds together also carries, along its whole length, an argument that cannot end — which may be as good a one-line description of the world as the mythology ever produced.

## In the primary sources

The complete textual basis is two passages: Grímnismál stanza 32 in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), which names the squirrel's route and the eagle's words but not the eagle; and Gylfaginning chapter 16 in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), which adds the eagle's wisdom and the hawk Veðrfölnir. The wind-eagle Hræsvelgr, for comparison, appears in Vafþrúðnismál 37 and Gylfaginning 18. No other poem, saga, or carving can be securely connected with the tree-eagle. Everything else — the hawk's function, the feud's cause, the eagle's identity — is interpretation, and the honest article says so.

## Legacy

An all-seeing bird at the top of the world-tree proved one of the mythology's most portable images. Eagle-topped trees appear in cosmologies and folk traditions well beyond Scandinavia, and comparative mythologists have long set the Yggdrasil eagle beside the eagle-and-serpent trees of other traditions — the bird above and snake below locked in enmity is one of the oldest picture-ideas humanity keeps. In modern culture the unnamed eagle appears wherever Yggdrasil is drawn — fantasy novels, games such as God of War and Assassin's Creed Valhalla, and a thousand tattooed world-trees — usually with the little hawk faithfully rendered between its eyes, still unexplained, exactly as Snorri left it.

## FAQ

**What is the name of the eagle on top of Yggdrasil?**
It has none — the sources never name it. Gylfaginning says only that an eagle sits in the ash's branches and "knows many things." The hawk between its eyes is named (Veðrfölnir), and the dragon it feuds with is named (Níðhöggr), but the eagle itself remains anonymous.

**What is Veðrfölnir?**
A hawk that perches between the eagle's eyes, according to Snorri's Gylfaginning. The name means something like "storm-pale" or "wind-bleached." No source explains its function; the common guess is a lookout for the lookout — vision doubled at the summit of the world.

**Is the eagle of Yggdrasil the same as Hræsvelgr?**
Probably not, though they are often confused. Hræsvelgr ("Corpse-Swallower") is a giant in eagle form who sits at heaven's edge and makes the winds with his wings, attested in Vafþrúðnismál. The tree-eagle is unnamed, wise, and feuds with Níðhöggr. The sources describe them separately and never merge them.

**Why do the eagle and Níðhöggr hate each other?**
No source says. The feud exists only as traffic: the squirrel Ratatoskr carries insulting words between the eagle at the crown and the dragon at the root. Its origin and subject are never given — the quarrel between wisdom above and malice below is simply a permanent feature of the world-tree.
