After Loki sheared Sif's hair, it was to the sons of Ivaldi he crawled for a fix — and they over-delivered: hair of living gold, and, unasked, the spear Gungnir that never misses its thrust and the ship Skíðblaðnir that folds like a napkin and always finds its wind. Three of the pantheon's six great treasures came off their bench in one commission.

Then Loki's boasting about them triggered the wager with Brokkr and Sindri that produced the other three — so the brothers are, indirectly, responsible for Mjölnir too. The sources never name them individually or say what became of them: the mythology's greatest artisans are a job title, which is roughly how the Norse treated smiths — indispensable, uncanny, and anonymous.

## The commission that started everything

The story that introduces the sons of Ivaldi is told in **Skáldskaparmál** as the origin of several skaldic kennings at once. [Loki](/gods/loki), for no better reason than mischief, cut off all the hair of [Sif](/gods/sif), Thor's wife, while she slept. When [Thor](/gods/thor) discovered his wife shorn and understood who had done it, Loki's life was plainly on the line, and he swore an oath to make the dwarves craft Sif hair of gold that would take root and grow like her own. He went to the sons of Ivaldi, and they delivered exactly what was promised — but did not stop there. Alongside the golden hair they forged, unrequested, [Gungnir](/artifacts/gungnir), a spear that would strike true against any target it was thrown at, and [Skíðblaðnir](/artifacts/skidbladnir), a ship large enough to carry every god fully armed yet able to be folded up like a piece of cloth and tucked into a pouch, always finding a favorable wind the moment its sail was raised. Three of the finest objects in the mythology, made in a single sitting, by smiths whose commission had asked for none of it beyond the hair.

## Where the treasures went

Loki carried the three gifts back to Asgard and distributed them precisely: the golden hair restored Sif, satisfying the debt that had sent him to the dwarves in the first place; Gungnir went to [Odin](/gods/odin), who would carry it as his signature weapon and, in time, use it to swear the oaths and rulings that made a spear-point the guarantee of a king's word; and Skíðblaðnir went to [Freyr](/gods/freyr), the god of ships and fair weather, for whom a vessel that obeyed the wind on command and vanished into a pocket when not needed was a fitting emblem of exactly the powers he governed. **Grímnismál**, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), independently confirms the ship's pedigree, calling Skíðblaðnir the best of ships and stating outright that it was crafted "with skill, by Ivaldi's sons" — one of the few places outside Snorri's prose where the family is named at all, and proof the tradition was not Snorri's invention alone.

## The boast that built Mjölnir

Loki, flush with the success of the commission, could not resist telling everyone that no dwarves anywhere could match what the sons of Ivaldi had made — a boast overheard by another dwarf, Brokkr, who staked Loki's own head on his brother's ability to do better. That wager produced the forge-scene that made [Gullinbursti](/creatures/gullinbursti), [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir), and, at the cost of a short handle, [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) itself — meaning the sons of Ivaldi, without lifting another hammer, are the indirect cause of Thor's weapon existing at all. Between the two commissions, six of the mythology's most important objects trace back to a single afternoon's rivalry, sparked by nothing more than one dwarf's wounded pride at hearing another praised. See [Brokkr and Sindri](/creatures/brokkr-and-sindri) for the full account of that second forging.

## Who was Ivaldi?

The father named in "sons of Ivaldi" never appears in his own right in either Edda, and identifying him is one of the mythology's more tantalizing loose threads. Some scholars have noted that a figure called Ívaldi is named as the father of the master smith [Völundr](/heroes/volund) — Wayland the Smith — in later Norse and broader Germanic legendary tradition, and have wondered whether the two Ivaldis, or their sons, were originally connected: a family of smiths spanning both the divine forge-myths of the Eddas and the heroic, half-elvish smith-legend of Völundarkviða. No primary source states this identification directly, and it remains a proposed connection rather than an attested fact. Whether elf, dwarf, or something the sources simply never bothered to specify, Ivaldi himself is a blank the tradition never filled in.

## Anonymous, indispensable, and everywhere

What is most striking about the sons of Ivaldi is how little the sources cared to distinguish them as people. They are never given individual names, descriptions, or personalities — unlike Brokkr, who gets a name, a grudge, and an awl to express it with. They simply produce three of the finest objects in the cosmos and vanish from the narrative the instant the objects are handed over, matching a broader pattern with the [dwarves](/creatures/dvergar) throughout Norse myth: craft without characterization, genius treated as a trade rather than a personality. The gods depend on dwarf-work for nearly everything that keeps the world running, and repay that dependence, again and again, with barely a name.

## In the primary sources

**Skáldskaparmál**, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), tells the full story of the commission, from Sif's shorn hair through the delivery of all three treasures. **Grímnismál**, in the Poetic Edda, independently credits "Ivaldi's sons" with Skíðblaðnir's construction, confirming the family name predates or exists outside Snorri's prose retelling. No source anywhere gives the sons individual names, a count of how many there were, or any story of their own beyond this one commission.

## FAQ

**Who were the sons of Ivaldi?**
Unnamed dwarf smiths commissioned by Loki to replace Sif's shorn hair with gold. They fulfilled that request and, unprompted, also forged the spear Gungnir for Odin and the ship Skíðblaðnir for Freyr — three of the mythology's great treasures from one job.

**What did the sons of Ivaldi make?**
Sif's golden hair, the unerring spear Gungnir, and the folding, ever-fair-winded ship Skíðblaðnir. Their work, and Loki's bragging about it, also indirectly caused the forging of Mjölnir, Draupnir, and Gullinbursti by the rival smiths Brokkr and Sindri.

**Is Ivaldi related to Völundr, the smith of Norse legend?**
Possibly, though it is not confirmed by any primary source. A figure named Ívaldi appears as Völundr's father in later legendary tradition, and some scholars have wondered whether this is the same Ivaldi, but the Eddas never make the connection explicit.

**Why don't the sons of Ivaldi have individual names?**
The sources never say. It fits a broader pattern in Norse mythology where dwarf smiths are frequently treated as a craft or a family rather than as individuals — Brokkr is a notable exception, but even his brother's name is uncertain between the sources and later tradition.

**Which is better, the sons of Ivaldi's treasures or Brokkr and Sindri's?**
The myth stages this as a direct contest and rules in Brokkr and Sindri's favor, on the strength of Mjölnir alone — but the sons of Ivaldi's Gungnir and Skíðblaðnir remained among the gods' most prized possessions regardless, and the "contest" framing is really Snorri's device for explaining why the gods own six treasures instead of three.
