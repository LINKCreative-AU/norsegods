Gullinbursti ('Golden-Bristles') came out of Sindri's forge in the great wager against Loki: a pigskin went into the fire, and a living boar of gold came out. He outruns any horse, crosses air and water alike, and his bristles light the darkest night — a vehicle, a lamp, and an emblem in one animal.

Freyr rides him, or drives him harnessed to his chariot — he carried the god to Baldr's funeral. The boar was sacred to the Vanir generally: warriors wore boar-crested helmets for protection, the Yule boar was sworn on and sacrificed at midwinter, and today's Swedish Christmas ham is, at the far end of a very long thread, Gullinbursti's table portion.

## Forged from a pigskin

Gullinbursti's birth is industrial. Skáldskaparmál tells how [Loki](/gods/loki), having cut off Sif's hair and made it good with treasures from the sons of Ivaldi, then wagered his own head with the dwarf [Brokkr](/creatures/brokkr-and-sindri) that Brokkr's brother Sindri could not forge anything to equal them. Sindri laid a pigskin in the forge and told Brokkr to pump the bellows without pause until he returned. Loki, hedging his bet, turned into a fly and bit Brokkr's hand — but the dwarf pumped on, and when Sindri came back and drew the work from the fire, it was a living boar with bristles of gold.

Two more heats produced the ring [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir) and the hammer [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir), and the gods judged the hammer worth more than everything else on the table — so the brothers won, Loki kept his head on a technicality, and Brokkr sewed the trickster's lips shut. Gullinbursti thus belongs to the greatest single haul of treasures in the mythology: six wonders in one story, three of them from a forge where a fly kept biting the bellows-hand. And of the six, the boar is the only one alive — the dwarves' craft crossing, just once, from artifact into animal.

## What can Gullinbursti do?

Snorri itemises the boar's specifications with the satisfaction of a maker's catalogue. He can run through air and over water, by night or day, better than any horse — and no night is so dark, no murky region so black, that it is not bright where he travels, so much light shines from his bristles. The boar is transport and torch at once: a mount for a god whose business, as lord of sunshine and growth, is precisely the defeat of darkness. Skáldskaparmál also records a second name, Slíðrugtanni — something like "Fearsome-Tusk" or "Cutting-Tooth" — a reminder that under the golden light the animal is still a boar, the most dangerous game animal northern Europe had.

His one attested outing is a funeral. When [Baldr](/gods/baldr) was burned, Gylfaginning says, [Freyr](/gods/freyr) drove to the pyre in his chariot drawn by the boar called Gullinbursti or Slíðrugtanni — and this detail is older than Snorri, for the late-tenth-century skaldic poem Húsdrápa, composed by Úlfr Uggason about mythological scenes carved in an Icelandic hall, already describes Freyr riding the gold-bristled boar to Baldr's balefire. A god of light, drawn by a shining boar, arriving at the mythology's darkest occasion: the carvers of that hall knew what an image was.

## Why is the boar sacred to Freyr and the Vanir?

Gullinbursti is the mythological face of a much wider cult fact: the boar belonged to the [Vanir](/concepts/vanir), the gods of fertility, wealth, and increase. [Freyja](/gods/freyja), Freyr's sister, has her own dwarf-forged boar — Hildisvíni, "Battle-Swine," which she rides in the poem Hyndluljóð — and one of her names is simply Sýr, "Sow." The associations are not hard to read: the pig was the North's great meat animal, prolific, fattening, the very engine of plenty; and the wild boar was courage incarnate, the beast that charges the spear. Fertility and ferocity in one animal — a compact summary of the Vanir themselves.

The cult left fingerprints across the historical record. Warriors wore boar-figures on their helmets for protection: Beowulf mentions boar-crests guarding the lives of the helmeted, and archaeology has produced them — boar-crested helmets like the one from Benty Grange in England, boar-ornamented plates from Vendel-era Sweden. Kings of the Swedes, the dynasty that claimed descent from Freyr himself, appear in legendary sagas with boar-named heirlooms. And at midwinter, the sonargöltr — the "sacrifice-boar" or atonement-boar — was led out at Yule so that men might lay hands on its bristles and swear their solemn oaths upon it before it was offered; the prose of Helgakviða Hjörvarðssonar and Hervarar saga both remember the custom. Sworn on, sacrificed, and eaten: the Yule boar fed gods and household alike.

## In the primary sources

Gullinbursti's dossier is compact. Skáldskaparmál, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), gives the forging in the wager-story and the specifications — speed over air and water, the light of the bristles, the alternative name Slíðrugtanni — and teaches poets to call Freyr the boar's owner. Gylfaginning names him drawing Freyr's chariot at Baldr's funeral. Húsdrápa, quoted in Skáldskaparmál, independently attests Freyr on the gold-bristled boar at the funeral, and shows the scene was carved in a pagan-age hall. Around the named boar stands the wider evidence: Hyndluljóð for Freyja's Hildisvíni, the saga prose for the Yule oath-boar, and the helmets in the ground. For a creature with no adventures of his own, Gullinbursti is unusually well corroborated — text, verse, and archaeology all glinting the same gold.

## Legacy

The golden boar has trotted a long way. Scandinavian Yule kept its boar through conversion and reformation — as the oath-hallowed sonargöltr became the julgalt, the Yule boar of straw and pastry, and eventually the Swedish julskinka, the Christmas ham still central to the holiday table. Gullinbursti himself appears in Marvel comics and the God of War games, in fantasy fiction, and as a favourite of modern heathen iconography, where the shining boar serves as Freyr's emblem exactly as it did on Viking-age arm-rings and helmet crests. Few mythological creatures can claim so unbroken a supply chain: from a dwarf's forge, through a thousand years of midwinter, to dinner.

## FAQ

**Who made Gullinbursti?**
The dwarf-smith Sindri forged him, with his brother Brokkr working the bellows, during the wager in which Loki staked his head that the brothers could not match the treasures of the sons of Ivaldi. Sindri put a pigskin into the forge, and the living golden boar came out — alongside Draupnir and Mjölnir in the same session.

**What powers does Gullinbursti have?**
He runs through air and over water, faster than any horse, by day or night, and his golden bristles shed so much light that darkness cannot exist around him. Snorri records both capabilities in Skáldskaparmál; the boar's second name, Slíðrugtanni, points to formidable tusks besides.

**Where does Gullinbursti appear in the myths?**
His one narrative appearance is Baldr's funeral, where he draws (or carries) Freyr to the pyre — attested both in Snorri's Gylfaginning and in the tenth-century skaldic poem Húsdrápa. Otherwise he exists as Freyr's mount and emblem rather than as an actor in stories.

**Is the Christmas ham really connected to Freyr's boar?**
By a long but genuine thread. The pagan Yule featured the sonargöltr, a boar sworn upon and sacrificed at midwinter — a custom remembered in Hervarar saga and the Helgi poems. The Yule boar survived christianisation in Scandinavian folk custom and food, and Sweden's julskinka, the Christmas ham, is its modern descendant.
