Every day the cook Andhrímnir boils the boar Sæhrímnir in the great cauldron Eldhrímnir, and every einherji in Valhalla eats his fill; every evening the boar is whole again. 'It is the best of meats,' Grímnismál notes, then adds the mythology's driest aside: 'few know what the einherjar feed on.'

The boar, the cook, and the kettle all share the element -hrímnir, 'the sooty one' — a matched kitchen set for eternity. Sæhrímnir is the food-supply answer to Heiðrún's drink supply: the afterlife of warriors runs on one immortal pig and one unkillable goat, resurrection applied first of all to dinner.

## The kitchen of Valhalla

[Valhalla](/realms/valhalla) is a hall with a staggering headcount. [Odin](/gods/odin) gathers to it every warrior worth having who falls in battle, against the day he will need an army at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok); Grímnismál counts five hundred and forty doors, each wide enough for eight hundred einherjar to march out abreast. Gylfaginning's questioner, Gangleri, does the arithmetic every listener does, and asks the practical question: that is "a very mighty multitude" — what do they all eat?

The answer is a single pig. Every day Andhrímnir, the cook of the gods, seethes the boar Sæhrímnir in the cauldron Eldhrímnir, and there is always enough; every evening the boar stands whole again, ready for tomorrow's slaughter. Grímnismál compresses the whole system into one stanza — cook, kettle, boar, "best of pork" — and then appends the sly line that has delighted readers ever since: "but few know what the einherjar feed on." The poem winks: it is telling a secret, and the secret is a kitchen. High, in Snorri's retelling, allows himself the same dry note — few are wise enough to tell of it truly.

The staffing deserves its moment. The three names rhyme by design: Andhrímnir the cook, Eldhrímnir the kettle, Sæhrímnir the meat, all built on hrímnir, "the sooty one" — soot from the fire (eldr) being the honest condition of any working kitchen. The mythology names the pot and the cook with the same care it gives swords and horses: in a warrior culture, the man who feeds the army is part of the war machine, and eternity's cook gets his stanza alongside eternity's soldiers.

## What does the name Sæhrímnir mean?

The second element is settled — "the sooty one," like his kitchen-mates. The first element is the puzzle: sæ- looks for all the world like "sea," which would make the beast something like "sooty sea-animal" — an odd name for a boar. Scholars have chewed on it. Some suggest the name is old and the creature was not originally a boar at all, but some sea-beast or a "sooty" beast of sacrifice that the later tradition reclassified as pork; others derive the first element differently and keep the pig. Snorri, for his part, has no doubts: Sæhrímnir is a göltr, a boar, and Grímnismál's fleska bezt — "best of pork" — agrees. The honest position is that the texts serve pork while the etymology smells faintly of the sea, and no one has fully reconciled the two.

The boar, in any case, is the right animal for the job. The pig was the North's great feast-meat, and the boar was the sacred animal of plenty — [Freyr's](/gods/freyr) shining [Gullinbursti](/creatures/gullinbursti) and the midwinter sacrifice-boar belong to the same complex. An eternal feast built on eternal pork is Norse abundance thought through to its logical end.

## Why does the boar come back to life?

Because everything in Valhalla does; the boar just does it on a schedule. The einherjar themselves fight to the death every morning and sit down whole together every evening — the hall's entire economy is resurrection, applied to soldiers and supper alike. The same magic runs through the mythology's other famous meal: [Thor's](/gods/thor) chariot-goats, [Tanngrisnir and Tanngnjóstr](/creatures/tanngrisnir-and-tanngnjostr), can be slaughtered and eaten at night and hammer-blessed back to life at dawn, provided the bones are unbroken. Scholars have connected this cluster of myths to old ideas about sacrifice — the beast given to the gods is not destroyed but renewed — and to the simple, potent fantasy of the herding cultures that told them: the animal you can eat forever. Sæhrímnir is that fantasy given a name, a cook, and a cauldron. He is also, though no poem pauses to pity him, the hardest-working creature in the mythology: the only inhabitant of paradise for whom every single day ends the same way.

## In the primary sources

The complete dossier is two texts. Grímnismál stanza 18, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), names cook, cauldron, and boar, rates the meat, and drops its aside about how few know the einherjar's fare. Gylfaginning 38, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), expands the stanza into prose — the daily seething, the nightly wholeness, the sufficiency for any multitude — quotes the verse, and adds Gangleri's follow-up about whether Odin shares the menu (he does not: his portion goes to the wolves [Geri and Freki](/creatures/geri-and-freki), for the Allfather lives on wine alone). The name-lists attached to the Prose Edda remember Sæhrímnir among swine. Beyond that, nothing — no origin, no personality, no fate. He is provisioning, perfected and personified.

## Legacy

Sæhrímnir's modern career is mostly culinary, as befits him. He turns up in fantasy fiction and games wherever Valhalla needs catering — the regenerating feast-beast is a stock marvel from Dungeons & Dragons to video games — and his name has been borrowed by Nordic restaurants, food festivals, and at least one line of academic jokes about sustainable meat production, for which he remains the theoretical ideal. The stanza's wink still lands after a thousand years: everyone imagines paradise; the Norse also remembered to ask what was for dinner, and who was doing the cooking.

## FAQ

**What is Sæhrímnir?**
Sæhrímnir is the boar of Valhalla, whose flesh feeds the einherjar, Odin's host of chosen warriors. Each day the cook Andhrímnir boils him in the cauldron Eldhrímnir; each evening the boar is whole and alive again, ready to be cooked anew. Grímnismál calls his flesh the best of meats.

**What do the names Andhrímnir, Eldhrímnir, and Sæhrímnir mean?**
All three share -hrímnir, "the sooty one," from the soot of the cooking fire — a deliberately matched set of cook, kettle, and meat. The first elements are less certain; Sæ- looks like "sea," which puzzles scholars, since the texts plainly describe a boar.

**Does Sæhrímnir ever run out?**
No. Snorri says the boiled boar suffices for any number of einherjar, however vast the host grows, and Valhalla's doors imply a host of hundreds of thousands. The renewal is nightly and perpetual — the mythology's answer to the logistics of an ever-growing army of the dead.

**Does Odin eat Sæhrímnir?**
No. According to both Grímnismál and Gylfaginning, Odin gives all the food set before him to his wolves Geri and Freki and lives on wine alone. Sæhrímnir feeds the warriors; the god at the head of the table touches none of it.
