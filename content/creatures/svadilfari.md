The unnamed builder who offered to wall Asgard in one winter had a secret asset: his stallion Svaðilfari, who hauled boulders by night that doubled the day's work. Three days from the deadline the wall was all but done, and the gods faced paying a giant the sun, the moon, and Freyja — and turned, as ever, on the advisor whose fault it was.

Loki's solution was thorough. A mare ran from the forest, whinnying; Svaðilfari snapped his traces and followed; the builder's deadline died in the underbrush. The builder raged into his giant's shape and met Mjölnir. Some months later Loki returned from an awkward absence with a grey, eight-legged foal — Sleipnir, best of horses, whom Odin rides between the worlds. The greatest steed in mythology exists because a horse got distracted at work.

## The bargain for Asgard's wall

The story belongs to the early days of the gods, and Snorri tells it in Gylfaginning as a cautionary tale about contracts. [Asgard's](/realms/asgard) old fortification lay broken after the war with the Vanir, and a builder — a smith, Snorri calls him, arriving alone with his horse — offered to raise a wall in three half-years so strong that no hill-giant or frost-giant could ever storm it. His price was not modest: [Freyja](/gods/freyja) as his wife, and the sun and the moon into the bargain — the gods' most beloved goddess and the lights of the world, handed over the mountain to Jötunheimr.

The gods conferred and counter-offered, on [Loki's](/gods/loki) advice: one single winter, no help from any man, and if anything remained unfinished on the first day of summer, no payment at all. They meant it to be impossible; the builder asked only that he might use his stallion, and Loki argued the concession through. Oaths were sworn with many witnesses — the builder, prudently, would not work among gods without safe-conduct, [Thor](/gods/thor) being abroad killing trolls in the east — and work began on the first day of winter.

Then the gods discovered what they had signed. By day the builder set the stones; by night Svaðilfari hauled them, and the horse dragged rocks so enormous that the gods sat up marvelling — the stallion, Snorri says plainly, did twice the work of the master. The wall climbed. Three days before summer it stood high and strong, all but the gateway. The Æsir convened in something close to panic and asked one another the questions committees ask: who had advised marrying Freyja into giant-land, who had proposed spoiling the sky itself by giving away sun and moon? The answers all pointed one way, and the gods informed Loki that unless he found a way out, he would die — an incentive structure Loki always understood perfectly.

## The mare in the wood

That evening, as the builder drove Svaðilfari out for stone, a mare ran out of a wood and whinnied at the stallion. Svaðilfari went — frantic, Snorri says, snapping his traces — and the two horses ran in the wood all night, the builder crashing after them. The night's haulage was lost, and the next day's work stalled; the builder saw the deadline die and flew into jötunmóðr, giant's fury, dropping all disguise. The gods, seeing a giant confirmed, felt their oaths of safe-conduct expire on the spot and called Thor, who arrived hammer-first. [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) paid the builder's wage, as Snorri puts it, by shattering his skull and sending him down below Niflhel — a settlement the poem Völuspá remembers with an edge: oaths were broken, words and promises, when Thor struck in wrath.

As for the mare: "somewhat later" Loki bore a foal — grey, eight-legged, the best horse among gods and men. Svaðilfari's son is [Sleipnir](/creatures/sleipnir), [Odin's](/gods/odin) own mount, who carries the Allfather over sea and sky and leapt the gates of [Hel](/realms/hel). It is the mythology's most backhanded genealogy: the gods' greatest treasure of horseflesh is the by-product of a sabotage, sired by a giant's draught-animal on the god of mischief. Loki never lived it down; centuries of poems keep the receipt. Hyndluljóð counts the foaling among Loki's outrages, and in Lokasenna the accusation of having borne children is among the insults traded — shape-shifting across sex being, in Norse terms, a charge of the gravest kind. The horse, at least, emerged with his reputation intact.

## What does the name Svaðilfari mean?

The name is usually parsed with -fari, "traveller, farer," as its second element; the first is connected by most scholars with words for a slippery place or a disastrous, laborious journey (svaðill), giving something like "unlucky traveller" or "he who makes a disastrous journey." It is hard not to hear the irony, whichever way the etymology runs: the stallion's one recorded journey — the night-run into the wood — was disastrous precisely for his master. Certainty is not available; the name appears only in this story and in Hyndluljóð's allusion, and the parsing rests on comparative philology rather than any explanation in the sources.

## In the primary sources

Svaðilfari's story is told once, in Gylfaginning 42 of the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), where Snorri anchors it to Völuspá's stanzas about broken oaths and Thor's wrath — evidence that some version of the tale is older than Snorri, though the poem never names horse or builder. Hyndluljóð 40, in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), independently confirms the essential scandal: Loki bore Sleipnir "to Svaðilfari." That is the entire corpus. The builder is never named; the mare's months in the wood are passed over in one clause; and Svaðilfari himself vanishes from the record the moment his traces snap. What became of him — whether he ran back to a dead master's camp or stayed wild in the woods of Midgard — no source says.

## Legacy

Svaðilfari survives in modern culture mostly as the answer to a famous question — "who is Sleipnir's father?" — and as the straight man in the mythology's best-known joke about Loki. He appears in retellings, comics, and games wherever the wall-builder story is told, and the tale itself has become a touchstone for writers on contract law and project deadlines, who find in it everything they need: scope creep, an impossible schedule, a subcontractor with hidden capacity, and a client who liquidates the vendor rather than pay. The horse, as ever in that literature, is the only party who behaved naturally.

## FAQ

**Whose horse was Svaðilfari?**
He belonged to the unnamed builder — a giant in disguise — who contracted to build Asgard's wall in a single winter for the price of Freyja, the sun, and the moon. The stallion hauled the wall's stones by night and did, by Snorri's account, twice the work of his master.

**Is Svaðilfari Sleipnir's father?**
Yes. When Loki, in the shape of a mare, lured Svaðilfari from his work to wreck the builder's deadline, the encounter had consequences: Loki later gave birth to the grey, eight-legged foal Sleipnir, who became Odin's horse. Both Gylfaginning and Hyndluljóð attest the parentage.

**What does the name Svaðilfari mean?**
Probably something like "unlucky traveller" or "one who makes a disastrous journey" — the second element means "farer," and the first is linked to an Old Norse word for a slippery spot or calamitous trip. The etymology is scholarly reconstruction; no source explains the name.

**What happened to Svaðilfari after the wall story?**
The sources never say. He disappears from the record at the moment he breaks his traces and follows the mare into the wood. His master was killed by Thor when the deadline failed, and the wall was left with its gateway unfinished — completed, presumably, by the gods themselves.
