Odin hurled the infant serpent, child of Loki and Angrboða, into the sea that rings Midgard — where it grew until it encircled the whole world and gripped its own tail. When it lets go, the end has begun.

Thor and the serpent meet three times. In Útgarða-Loki's castle, the 'cat' Thor could barely lift off the floor was Jörmungandr in disguise — and the giants paled to see it. On Hymir's fishing boat, Thor baited a hook with an ox-head and pulled the serpent's venom-spitting head above the waves, and only Hymir cutting the line saved the world an early apocalypse. At Ragnarök they finish it: the serpent comes ashore flooding the land and spraying venom; Thor crushes its skull with Mjölnir, strides nine paces, and falls dead of its poison.

## The serpent thrown into the sea

Jörmungandr — the name means something like "mighty monster" or "great wand" — is the middle child of [Loki](/gods/loki) and the giantess [Angrboða](/giants/angrboda) of Jötunheimr, sibling to the wolf [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) and to [Hel](/gods/hel). When prophecy warned the gods that this brood would be their ruin, [Odin](/gods/odin) had the children fetched to Asgard and disposed of each in turn: Hel down to the world of the dead, Fenrir kept under the gods' eyes, and the serpent flung into the deep sea that surrounds all lands.

The disposal failed as thoroughly as a disposal can. In the ocean the serpent found the one environment with no ceiling on growth, and it grew until it lay around the whole of [Midgard](/realms/midgard) and could bite its own tail. That circle is the creature's defining image and its cosmic function in one: the World Serpent is the boundary of the human world, the living rim of the map. Sailors' horizons everywhere ran to Jörmungandr. And because the myths are honest about what boundaries are, the serpent is also a coiled deadline — the day it releases its tail and comes ashore is the day the world ends.

## Thor and the serpent: three meetings

The feud between [Thor](/gods/thor) and the Midgard Serpent runs through the mythology like a fault line, and Snorri arranges it as three encounters of rising stakes.

The first is a humiliation in disguise. In the hall of the giant-king [Útgarða-Loki](/giants/utgarda-loki), Thor was challenged to a series of rigged contests, one of which was to lift the king's grey cat off the floor. Straining with all his god's strength, Thor managed only to raise one paw. Only at his departure did Útgarða-Loki confess the illusion: the cat was Jörmungandr, and when the giants saw Thor stretch the World Serpent so far that its back arched toward heaven, they were terrified. The joke told at Thor's expense was, decoded, a boast about him.

The second meeting is the most famous fishing trip in world literature. Rowing out with the giant [Hymir](/giants/hymir) — far beyond the grounds where Hymir dared go — Thor baited a colossal hook with the head of Hymir's best ox and let it sink. The serpent took the bait, the hook bit into its palate, and it jerked so hard that Thor's fists slammed against the gunwale. Furious, the god braced himself with such force that his feet went through the bottom of the boat to the sea-floor, and hauled the serpent's head up out of the waves. The Eddic poem Hymiskviða and Snorri's Gylfaginning both preserve the stare that followed — Thor glaring down, the serpent glaring up and spitting venom — one of the great frozen moments of Norse art. In Snorri's telling, the terrified Hymir cut the line as Thor raised his hammer, and the serpent sank back into the sea; Thor knocked the giant overboard and waded ashore. Snorri adds, scrupulously, that some accounts claimed Thor struck the serpent's head off at the sea-bottom — but that he himself believes the truth to be that the Midgard Serpent still lives, waiting in the ocean that rings the world.

## Why are Thor and Jörmungandr enemies?

The sources never explain it; they simply assume it, the way one assumes thunder and sea-storm are opposites. Thor is the defender of Midgard, and Jörmungandr is the thing Midgard needs defending from — the chaos of the outer ocean given a body. Their enmity is structural: the hallower of boundaries against the boundary that will one day come ashore. Scholars have long compared the pair to other Indo-European thunder-god-versus-serpent myths, and the Norse poets clearly relished the symmetry of a duel too big to finish early: twice the fight is interrupted — by illusion, by a cut line — because fate has reserved it for the last day. Kennings preserve the relationship from both directions: the serpent is Thor's "enemy," and Thor is the serpent's "sole slayer."

## At Ragnarök

When [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) comes, the serpent lets go of its tail. Jörmungandr writhes in giant fury and heaves itself onto the land, driving the sea over its banks in the flood on which the ship Naglfar floats free. It advances beside its brother Fenrir — the wolf's jaws spanning earth and sky, the serpent blowing venom over air and ocean alike — to the plain of Vígríðr, where the last battle is joined.

There Thor and the World Serpent finally finish what the fishing line interrupted. Thor kills it — Völuspá and Gylfaginning agree — crushing the monster with [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir). Then he steps back from the carcass, walks nine paces, and falls dead from the venom the serpent blew over him. Völuspá gives the scene its lasting shape: the son of Earth staggers dying from the serpent, having slain it, and the poem does not say which of them won. Neither does anyone since.

## In the primary sources

Jörmungandr is unusually well attested, in stone as well as parchment. In the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Völuspá prophesies the serpent thrashing in the sea at Ragnarök and Thor's death by its venom, and Hymiskviða devotes its centre to the fishing expedition. In the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), Gylfaginning tells the serpent's origin, the cat-lifting at Útgarða-Loki's, the fishing trip, and the Ragnarök duel, while Skáldskaparmál preserves skaldic verse on the subject — including fragments of Úlfr Uggason's Húsdrápa and Bragi Boddason's Ragnarsdrápa, poems describing the fishing scene as it was carved and painted in Viking-age halls. And the carvings themselves survive: the Altuna runestone in Sweden shows Thor with the line, ox-head bait, and his foot through the hull; the Hørdum stone in Denmark and a panel long associated with the Gosforth Cross in England show the same scene. Few Norse myths can be checked against pictures made by believers; this one can.

## What does the World Serpent mean?

Interpretation has circled the serpent as steadily as the serpent circles the world. The tail-in-mouth posture invites comparison with the ouroboros, the world-ringing snake of ancient Mediterranean symbolism, though the Norse sources themselves attach no doctrine of eternity to it — for them the point is tension, not cycle: the serpent holds the world together the way a hoop holds a barrel, and its release is collapse. Others read Jörmungandr as the mythic face of the North Atlantic itself — the encircling, ship-swallowing ocean at the edge of every map the Norse drew. What the texts license without speculation is this much: the serpent marks the limit of the ordered world, and the Norse imagined that limit as something alive, patient, and hostile.

## Legacy

Jörmungandr's afterlife is as long as its body. The Midgard Serpent coils through modern novels, comics, and screens — Marvel's Thor mythos, video games from God of War to Assassin's Creed Valhalla and Smite — and its name has been borrowed by everything from sea-monster cryptozoology to a notorious computer worm's family tree of nicknames. The image that endures is the one the skalds already loved: a god in a small boat, an ox-head on a hook, and the rim of the world rising to the bait.

## FAQ

**What does the name Jörmungandr mean?**
It is usually rendered "mighty monster" or "huge serpent" — jörmun- is an intensifying element meaning "vast, mighty" (the same element as in one of Odin's names, Jörmunr). The creature is equally often called Miðgarðsormr, "the Midgard Serpent" or "World Serpent."

**How big is Jörmungandr?**
Big enough to encircle the entire inhabited world and bite its own tail — that is the only measurement the sources give. In the Útgarða-Loki episode, when Thor lifts the disguised serpent as a "cat," raising a single paw off the floor stretches the creature's back up toward the sky.

**Did Thor ever almost kill Jörmungandr before Ragnarök?**
Yes — on the fishing trip with Hymir, Thor hauled the serpent's head above the waves and raised Mjölnir to strike, but Hymir panicked and cut the line. Snorri notes that some old accounts said Thor struck the serpent's head off then and there, but he judges the truer version to be that it survived to fight at Ragnarök.

**Who kills whom at Ragnarök?**
Both kill each other. Thor crushes the serpent with Mjölnir, then walks exactly nine steps before the venom it sprayed over him brings him down dead. The mutual destruction is attested in both Völuspá and Gylfaginning.

**Are Jörmungandr and Fenrir related?**
They are brothers — both children of Loki and the giantess Angrboða, along with their sister Hel. At Ragnarök the two advance side by side, the wolf on land with gaping jaws, the serpent spewing venom, and between them they account for Odin and Thor.
