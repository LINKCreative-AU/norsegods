Andvari kept his wealth the sensible way: as a fish. Living in pike-shape beneath his waterfall, he was safe from everyone except a god with Rán's drowning-net and a debt to pay. Loki, needing gold to ransom the gods for the killing of Otr, netted the pike, and squeezed the dwarf for every ounce — including the small ring Andvaranaut with which Andvari hoped to "breed back" his fortune, for it was a ring that begets gold.

Denied even that, the dwarf spoke the curse that powers the entire Völsung tragedy: the ring and the gold shall be the death of whoever owns them. He then vanishes from the story, the only holder of the hoard to survive it — proof that in Norse economics, the best position in a cursed asset is early and involuntary divestment.

## The debt that sent Loki to the waterfall

Andvari's story begins as someone else's crisis. [Odin](/gods/odin), Hœnir, and [Loki](/gods/loki) were traveling together when Loki killed an otter fishing by a waterfall with a well-thrown stone, not knowing it was Otr, son of the sorcerer Hreiðmarr, who took that shape by day. The three gods lodged that night at Hreiðmarr's house and, showing off the pelt, found themselves seized: Hreiðmarr and his surviving sons recognized the skin and demanded a ransom — enough gold to fill the otter-skin and cover it completely on the outside, until not a single hair showed. Loki was sent to raise the sum, and knew exactly where to look: a dwarf named Andvari, famous for a hoard he kept in a form no one could easily rob.

## A dwarf who lived as a fish

Andvari had taken the shape of a pike and made his home in the water beneath a waterfall that would later carry his name — Andvarafors, "Andvari's falls" — safe there from ordinary theft in a way no vault on land could match. It took [Rán](/giants/ran)'s net, borrowed by Loki from the sea-giantess who dragged the drowned into her own hall, to catch him: a net built to take anything the water tried to hide. Once hauled out and returned to dwarf-shape, Andvari had no way to resist. Loki stripped him of the entire hoard, gold enough to fill and cover an otter-skin many times over, leaving the dwarf with nothing.

## The ring, and the curse

Andvari tried to keep back one small thing: a plain ring, later called Andvaranaut, "Andvari's gift" or "Andvari's loss," which held the power to breed more gold from itself — the seed capital, in effect, from which he could have rebuilt his entire fortune given time. Loki noticed the dwarf's hand closing around it and demanded that too, taking the very last coin of a fortune he had already emptied out. It was this final theft, past the point of any practical need, that broke Andvari's patience. He spoke a curse over the ring and the gold together: whoever came to hold them, he swore, would be brought to ruin and death by the possessing of them — a doom binding to every future owner, gods and mortals alike, with no exceptions written in.

## The debt paid, and the curse set loose

Loki delivered the gold to Hreiðmarr and, by most accounts with something close to relish, passed the warning along as he handed it over. The ransom was piled onto the otter-skin and covered until Hreiðmarr, checking closely, found one whisker still showing — and Odin himself covered that last hair with the ring Andvaranaut, completing the payment and, in the same motion, completing the curse's delivery into the family that would suffer it first. Within days Hreiðmarr's own son [Fáfnir](/creatures/fafnir) killed his father in his sleep for a full share of the hoard, then hoarded it alone on Gnita-heath until greed and a helm of terror turned him into a dragon. From there the gold and the ring pass to Fáfnir's brother Reginn, to Reginn's foster-son [Sigurd](/heroes/sigurd) after the dragon-slaying, and on through [Brynhild](/heroes/brynhild), [Gunnar](/heroes/gunnar), and [Gudrun](/heroes/gudrun) — the entire Völsung cycle, one death after another, tracing the path of exactly the object Andvari swore would kill everyone who held it.

## The one owner who survived

What makes Andvari unusual among the figures caught up in his own curse is that he is the only one of them to walk away. He loses everything — his hoard, his ring, his years of hidden accumulation — but he keeps his life, and he curses the gold only after it has already left his hands for good, meaning the doom he speaks can never touch him. Every mortal and every god who later possesses so much as a fraction of that fortune pays for it; the dwarf who built it pays only in poverty. It is a bleak but consistent piece of the mythology's logic: the safest relationship to cursed wealth is to lose it early, and involuntarily, before it has the chance to want you back.

## In the primary sources

**Reginsmál** in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) gives Andvari's own words, including the curse, in verse — a rare case of a dwarf speaking directly rather than being described. The **[Völsunga saga](/texts/volsunga-saga)** retells the episode in prose as the opening act of the entire Völsung and Niflung tragedy, tracing the gold's path through every later death. Snorri's **Skáldskaparmál**, in the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), summarizes the same events to explain why gold is called "otter's ransom" or "Andvari's inheritance" in skaldic verse. Across all three, the details agree closely enough that this appears to be one of the more stable, well-preserved stories in the corpus, rather than a case of the sources pulling in different directions.

## FAQ

**Who is Andvari in Norse mythology?**
A dwarf who hoarded gold while living as a pike beneath a waterfall. Loki caught him with a net borrowed from the sea-giantess Rán and stripped him of his entire fortune, including a self-multiplying ring, to pay a ransom for the gods.

**Why did Andvari curse his gold?**
Loki took even the one ring Andvari begged to keep — Andvaranaut, which could regenerate wealth on its own — leaving him with nothing at all. In response, Andvari cursed the ring and the gold to bring death to every future owner.

**Did the curse actually work?**
According to the sources, yes, repeatedly. The gold and ring pass through Hreiðmarr, Fáfnir, and on through the whole Völsung saga, and nearly every person who holds them dies violently, culminating in the tragedies of Sigurd, Brynhild, and the Niflung kings.

**What happened to Andvari after the curse?**
He disappears from the story entirely. Unlike everyone who later possesses his gold, Andvari survives — having lost his fortune before speaking the curse, he is the one figure in the whole chain the doom cannot reach.

**Is Andvari connected to Wagner's Ring cycle?**
Yes, closely. Richard Wagner drew his dwarf Alberich and the cursed Rhinegold directly from the Andvari material, transplanting the waterfall hoard, the theft, and the curse into the opening of Der Ring des Nibelungen.
