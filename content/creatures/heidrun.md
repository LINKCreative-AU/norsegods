Valhalla's bar has one supplier: a goat. Heiðrún stands on the hall's roof browsing the foliage of the tree Læraðr, and from her udders runs not milk but shining mead — a vatful so large that all the einherjar drink their fill and it never runs dry. The Norse afterlife solved logistics before it solved anything else.

Beside her the hart Eikþyrnir grazes the same tree, and from his antlers water drips into Hvergelmir, feeding all the world's rivers — the roof of the warriors' hall quietly irrigating the cosmos. Practical note from the myths: paradise is a place where the drink pours itself.

## The goat on the roof

The picture comes from Grímnismál, the great catalogue-poem in which [Odin](/gods/odin), disguised and suffering between two fires, describes the architecture of the divine world — and pauses, amid rafters made of spears and a roof thatched with shields, for the livestock. "Heiðrún is the goat's name, who stands on the Father of Hosts' hall and bites from Læraðr's limbs; she shall fill a vat of shining mead — that drink can never diminish." Snorri repeats it in Gylfaginning with his usual enjoyment of quantities: the mead she yields daily fills a tun so great that all the einherjar drink themselves full from it.

The einherjar are [Valhalla's](/concepts/valhalla) whole point: the battle-slain, chosen by Odin and gathered by the valkyries, who fight each other to pieces every day, stand whole again at evening, and feast every night until they march out at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok). An army that dies daily works up a thirst, and the mythology, with the unsentimental practicality that makes Norse paradise so believable, thought the catering through. Meat is handled by the boar [Sæhrímnir](/creatures/saehrimnir), boiled every day and whole again by night; drink is Heiðrún's department. One immortal pig, one unkillable goat, and eternity is provisioned.

She does not work alone up there. Grímnismál's next stanza gives her a roof-mate: the hart Eikþyrnir, who browses the same tree, and from whose antlers moisture drips down into Hvergelmir, the seething spring beneath the world — whence fall all the rivers of the world, which the poem then names at length. It is one of the mythology's quietest marvels: the gutter-runoff of the warriors' hall is the source of every river in the cosmos. The roof of Valhalla is a watershed, and its livestock are hydrology.

## What is the tree Læraðr?

Læraðr, whose foliage feeds both goat and hart, is named only in these stanzas and Snorri's retelling, and its identity is a small standing puzzle. Many scholars take it to be [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil) itself under another name — the world-tree does stand at the centre of the gods' world, and a tree overhanging Valhalla whose browsing sustains the cosmos's drink and rivers sounds like nothing smaller. Others note that the sources simply do not say, and leave Læraðr as its own tree. The etymology is disputed too. Honest summary: a great tree stands over Odin's hall; the goat eats its leaves and gives mead; whether it is the world-tree wearing a local name, no text confirms.

## Why does the goat give mead?

Because mead is what the hall runs on. In Norse culture mead and ale were not refreshments but institutions — the drink of oaths, of poetry, of the lord's generosity to his retainers; a chieftain's hall was, functionally, a roof over a drinking-bond. Valhalla is the chieftain's hall perfected, so its drink must be perfect: not brewed in batches that run out, but flowing from a living, self-renewing source, the way the whole hall is a machine for making death temporary. There may be a joke folded in as well — goat's milk was the homestead's ordinary drink, and Heiðrún is the homestead goat promoted to divine service, her produce upgraded from milk to shining mead. Paradise, Norse-style, is the farm working impossibly well.

Note what Heiðrún's vat is not: it is not the [Mead of Poetry](/events/mead-of-poetry), the blood-brewed drink of inspiration that Odin stole from the giant Suttungr. The two meads are different myths doing different work — Heiðrún's fills warriors, Kvasir's blood fills poets — though they testify to the same conviction that mead is the liquid form of everything worth having.

## The other Heiðrún

The goat makes one more appearance in the poems, and it is not flattering to anyone. In Hyndluljóð, the giantess Hyndla, quarrelling with [Freyja](/gods/freyja), sneers that the goddess runs out at night "as Heiðrún runs among the he-goats." The goat on Valhalla's roof was evidently also proverbial for being in heat — a reminder that the Norse could revere and mock in the same breath, and that a creature's dignity in one poem was fair game in the next. It is the only glimpse of Heiðrún's character the sources allow, and it is a flyting insult; readers may weigh it accordingly.

## In the primary sources

Heiðrún's attestation is brief and clean: Grímnismál stanza 25 in the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) (with Eikþyrnir in 26), retold and quoted in Gylfaginning 39 of the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), plus the comparative insult in Hyndluljóð 46–47. No origin is given for her, no fate, no further deeds; she is a fixture, not a character. Like much of Valhalla's furniture, she exists to answer a question a listener would actually ask — what do the chosen dead drink, and why does it never run out? — and having answered it perfectly, the tradition needed nothing more from her.

## Legacy

For a goat with two stanzas to her name, Heiðrún has kept busy. She is the namesake of Norway's national goat-cheese heritage marketing more than once, of a Faroese supermarket chain, of research vessels and breweries, and — inevitably — of taprooms and meaderies across the Nordic world and beyond; modern mead-makers could hardly resist the patron animal of the unfailing vat. She appears in fantasy fiction and games wherever Valhalla is furnished properly. The image lasts because it is perfect of its kind: a goat on the roof-tree of heaven, udders running with mead, eternity's simplest supply chain.

## FAQ

**What is Heiðrún in Norse mythology?**
She is the goat who stands on the roof of Valhalla, feeding on the foliage of the tree Læraðr. From her udders flows clear mead instead of milk, filling a vat so large that all the einherjar — Odin's chosen battle-dead — can drink their fill every day, and it never runs dry.

**Is the tree Læraðr the same as Yggdrasil?**
Possibly. Læraðr is named only in connection with Heiðrún and the hart Eikþyrnir, and many scholars identify it with the world-tree Yggdrasil overhanging Odin's hall. The sources themselves never make the equation, so it remains a plausible but unproven identification.

**Is Heiðrún's mead the Mead of Poetry?**
No. Heiðrún's mead is the everyday (if inexhaustible) drink of the warriors in Valhalla. The Mead of Poetry is a separate myth: the drink brewed from the blood of Kvasir, hidden in a mountain, and stolen by Odin, which grants the gift of poetry.

**Where is Heiðrún mentioned?**
In Grímnismál stanza 25 of the Poetic Edda, in Snorri's Gylfaginning (which quotes the stanza and adds the enormous daily vat), and in a passing insult in Hyndluljóð, where Freyja's night-roaming is compared to Heiðrún running among the he-goats.
