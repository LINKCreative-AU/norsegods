Gjallarhorn exists for one note. Snorri says its blast is heard in all worlds, and the myths hold it in reserve for the morning the jötnar come: [Heimdallr](/gods/heimdall), seeing the enemy across all distances, will stand and blow, and every being in nine worlds will know at once that the end has started. Völuspá gives the moment its drumbeat: "Loud blows Heimdallr, the horn is aloft."

No other object in the mythology is so purely an instrument of the future. [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) is swung constantly; Gungnir opens every war. Gjallarhorn waits — and the sources spend more words on where it is kept than on what it does.

## Heimdallr, the watchman of the gods

The horn belongs to the god built to use it. Heimdallr dwells at Himinbjörg, "Heaven-mountain," beside the rainbow bridge Bifröst at the edge of the sky, guarding the one bridge into [Asgard](/realms/asgard) against the mountain-giants. Snorri catalogues his equipment: he needs less sleep than a bird; he sees a hundred leagues before him, by night as well as by day; and he hears the grass growing on the earth and the wool growing on sheep — and everything louder than that.

Gjallarhorn completes the sentinel. Eyes that see everything and ears that hear everything are useless without a voice that reaches everyone; the horn is that voice, at cosmic scale. The name says so — gjallr means "ringing, resounding" — Gjallarhorn is "the loud-sounding horn," the alarm the whole world-order relies on.

## Is Gjallarhorn a war-horn or a drinking horn?

The sources glimpse a second use, or a doubled tradition. In Gylfaginning's tour of [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil), Snorri says that under the root reaching toward the frost-giants lies Mímir's well, where wisdom and understanding are hidden — and that Mímir, its keeper, is full of wisdom because he drinks from the well out of Gjallarhorn, every morning. The alarm of doomsday doubles as the cup of wisdom: the vessel that will one day announce the end is meanwhile used, quietly, to sip the meaning of things.

Völuspá deepens the puzzle. The seeress says she knows where Heimdallr's hljóð is hidden — under the bright, holy tree, in the same waters where [Odin](/gods/odin) left his eye in pledge for a single drink. The word hljóð can mean "horn," "sound," or "hearing," and scholars have long argued over it. On one reading, the horn itself lies banked beneath Yggdrasil until it is needed. On another, what is hidden is Heimdallr's hearing — an ear deposited in the well of wisdom exactly as Odin's eye was. Some scholars hold both readings together: the god of vigilance banks his instrument, the god of wisdom his sight, both pledges to be redeemed at the end of the world. The traditions do not resolve; the well keeps them both.

## When will Heimdallr blow Gjallarhorn?

At the hinge of the last day. Snorri lays out the sequence: first the Fimbulvetr, three winters with no summer between them; then the wolf [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) breaks free of his fetter, and [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr) writhes ashore in giant's rage, driving the sea over the land. The flood floats [Naglfar](/artifacts/naglfar), the ship of dead men's nails. The sky splits, and the sons of Muspell ride out behind [Surtr](/giants/surtr) with his flaming sword, breaking Bifröst — Heimdallr's own bridge — beneath them as they cross.

Then Heimdallr stands up and blows Gjallarhorn with all his might, waking all the gods to council. Odin rides to Mímir's well and takes counsel of Mímir for himself and his people — a last visit to the place where the horn was kept. Yggdrasil shakes, and everything in heaven and earth is afraid. Völuspá compresses it into a few lines of thunder: the old Gjallarhorn sounding, Heimdallr blowing loud with the horn aloft, Odin speaking with Mímir's head as the world-tree groans. One note, and every being in nine worlds knows the same thing at the same instant.

## At Ragnarök

The blast is Gjallarhorn's whole war-service; the fighting that follows belongs to its owner. The gods and their chosen warriors arm themselves and ride to the plain of Vígríðr, where the last battles are paired off — and at the end of the field, watchman meets saboteur. Heimdallr fights [Loki](/gods/loki), the god who mocked him in Lokasenna for a life spent standing with a stiff back in the rain, and each kills the other. It is the oldest feud in the sources: an archaic poem already had the two fighting in seal-shape over Brísingamen at the skerry Singasteinn. The horn-blower and the arsonist cancel each other out, and the signal Heimdallr gave outlives him into the world's renewal.

## In the primary sources

The horn's entire dossier is two texts. In [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Völuspá contributes both ends of its story: the stanza with Heimdallr's hljóð hidden under the holy tree beside Odin's pledge, and the doomsday stanza where the old Gjallarhorn finally sounds. Gylfaginning in [the Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) supplies everything else: the statement that Heimdallr owns the horn and that its blast is heard in all worlds; the report that Mímir drinks from Gjallarhorn each morning at his well beneath Yggdrasil; and the Ragnarök narrative in which the horn wakes the gods. That is the whole attestation — a few dozen lines, all bent toward one moment.

## Legacy

The name's root survives all around the horn: gjallr, "resounding," also sounds in Gjöll, the river of the underworld, and Gjallarbrú, the echoing bridge Hermóðr crossed to reach Hel — a family of loud words at the borders of worlds. In modern culture the horn keeps its job as the ultimate alarm: Heimdall and his horn anchor Ragnarök storylines in comics, films, and games, and Gjallarhorn is famous to gamers as the most coveted weapon in Destiny. Every air-raid siren is its descendant: one sound, heard everywhere, meaning the same thing to everyone.

## FAQ

**What is Gjallarhorn?**
Gjallarhorn, "the loud-sounding horn," is the horn of the watchman-god Heimdallr. Snorri says its blast can be heard in all worlds, and it is reserved for the moment Ragnarök begins, when Heimdallr blows it to wake the gods.

**Where is Gjallarhorn kept?**
Völuspá says Heimdallr's hljóð — horn, or hearing — lies hidden under the holy tree Yggdrasil, in the waters of Mímir's well, beside the eye Odin pledged there. Gylfaginning adds that Mímir drinks from Gjallarhorn every morning, so the sources place the horn at the well until the end draws near.

**When does Heimdallr blow Gjallarhorn?**
At the outbreak of Ragnarök, when Fenrir is loose, the sea has flooded the land, Naglfar has sailed, and the sons of Muspell are breaking Bifröst. Heimdallr stands, blows with all his might, and the gods wake and take counsel for the last battle.

**Is Gjallarhorn a drinking horn?**
In one tradition, yes. Gylfaginning says Mímir drinks wisdom from his well using Gjallarhorn each morning, while the same book makes it Heimdallr's alarm-horn. Scholars debate whether these are two functions of one horn or two traditions imperfectly joined; the texts simply present both.

**What happens to Heimdallr after he blows the horn?**
He fights his old enemy Loki on the plain of Vígríðr, and they kill each other. The sources say nothing about the horn's fate after its one great note.
