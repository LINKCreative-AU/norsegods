Hringhorni was the greatest ship ever built, and it is remembered for one voyage that went nowhere: out from the shore, burning. When [Baldr](/gods/baldr) died — killed by a sprig of [mistletoe](/artifacts/mistilteinn) that [Loki](/gods/loki) put in blind Höðr's hand — the gods resolved to give him a ship-funeral aboard his own vessel. And then they found that grief, or the ship's greatness, had rooted it: no god could shift Hringhorni down the rollers.

That single image is the ship's whole biography. Hringhorni exists in the sources for one scene only, the darkest morning the gods ever knew — the finest hull in the cosmos, built to carry a god exactly once.

## The funeral of Baldr

Snorri Sturluson tells the story in Gylfaginning. The gods carried Baldr's body down to the sea where Hringhorni stood, meaning to build the pyre on board and launch the burning ship. It would not move. So they sent to [Jötunheimr](/realms/jotunheim) for the giantess Hyrrokkin, who arrived riding a wolf, with vipers for reins; [Odin](/gods/odin) called up four berserkers to mind her mount, and even they could not hold it until they had wrestled it to the ground.

Hyrrokkin walked to the prow and shoved the ship to sea in one heave — a push so violent that fire sprang from the rollers and all the lands trembled. [Thor](/gods/thor), his grief already burning, took the earthquake as an insult and raised [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) to crush her skull; the assembled gods had to beg peace for her. At the funeral of the gentlest god, the strongest god nearly starts a killing.

Then the pyre was loaded. Onto it went Baldr's body; his wife Nanna, whose heart burst with grief when she saw him carried aboard; his horse, led into the flames fully harnessed; and the gold ring [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir), which Odin laid on the fire and which dead Baldr would later send back from Hel. Odin bent over his son and whispered something into his ear — a word no source ever reports. Thor hallowed the pyre with Mjölnir, the hammer's oldest and holiest office; and in the myth's one flash of ugly grief, when the dwarf Litr ran underfoot, Thor kicked him into the flames.

## Whose funeral was on Hringhorni?

The ship carried Baldr, Odin's son and the best-loved of the gods, together with his wife Nanna Nepsdóttir, his horse, and his treasures. But the funeral was attended by the whole cosmos, and the sources take a roll-call. Gylfaginning says Odin came with [Frigg](/gods/frigg), his valkyries, and his ravens; [Freyr](/gods/freyr) drove behind the boar Gullinbursti; [Heimdallr](/gods/heimdall) rode his horse Gulltoppr; [Freyja](/gods/freyja) drove her cats. And — remarkably — a great host of frost-giants and mountain-giants came too: even Baldr's enemies stood on the shore and mourned him. Only Loki did not.

The skaldic poem Húsdrápa, composed around 985 by Úlfr Uggason, confirms the procession from outside Snorri's prose: it describes the gods riding to the burning as scenes carved into the wall-panels of a real Icelandic hall. The funeral of Baldr was, quite literally, interior decoration in tenth-century Iceland.

## What does the name Hringhorni mean?

The name is usually read as "ring-horned" or "ring-prowed" — a ship with a ring or circular ornament on its stem. No source explains it, so anything further is interpretation, but the picture fits what archaeology shows of high-status Viking ships: elaborately carved, spiral-tipped prows like the Oseberg ship's serpent-coil. The name tells us the ship was beautiful before it tells us anything else. Snorri adds only the superlative — greatest of all ships — a rank that puts Hringhorni alongside Skíðblaðnir (best of ships) and [Naglfar](/artifacts/naglfar) (biggest of ships) in the mythology's odd little fleet of record-holders.

## In the primary sources

Hringhorni's attestation is narrow and deep. Gylfaginning (chapter 49) of [the Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) is the only full account: the ship's name and rank, the failed launch, Hyrrokkin and her wolf, the fire from the rollers, Nanna, the horse, Draupnir, Litr, and Thor's hallowing all come from that one chapter. Húsdrápa, quoted by Snorri in Skáldskaparmál, independently attests the funeral procession two centuries earlier, which matters: it shows the burning of Baldr was already a fixed, famous image in the pagan period, not a medieval invention.

One detail has a second, older witness. In Vafþrúðnismál in [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Odin defeats the wise giant with an unanswerable question: what did Odin say into his son's ear before Baldr was borne to the pyre? The poem never gives the answer — no one but Odin knows it — but it proves the pyre-side whisper belongs to the old tradition. The ship itself appears nowhere else: no other voyage, no builder, no origin. What the sources do not say about Hringhorni is nearly everything; the honest reading is that the ship existed in the myth for the sake of the funeral, not the other way around.

## Legacy

The real Norse buried their great dead in real ships. The Oseberg and Gokstad ships, excavated from Norwegian burial mounds, still exist; Denmark's Ladby ship and the ship-grave at Sutton Hoo belong to the same world of ideas, and the Arab traveller Ibn Fadlan watched a Rus chieftain burned in his vessel on the Volga in 922. Hringhorni is that rite at mythological scale, and it has repaid the debt: almost every "Viking funeral" in modern film and fiction — the flaming longship pushed out onto dark water — is really a memory of Baldr's ship, since archaeology mostly shows ships burned or buried on land. And because Baldr's death is the first tremor of [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), Hringhorni's one voyage is also the mythology's point of no return: the ship that carried the world's innocence out to sea.

## FAQ

**What was Hringhorni?**

Baldr's own ship, called the greatest of all ships in the Prose Edda. It appears in the sources for a single event: after Baldr's death, the gods built his funeral pyre on board and sent the ship out to sea in flames.

**Why couldn't the gods launch Hringhorni?**

Snorri doesn't explain it — he simply says the gods could not move the ship. They sent to Jötunheimr for the giantess Hyrrokkin, who arrived riding a wolf bridled with vipers and shoved the ship to sea in a single push that struck fire from the rollers and shook the earth.

**Who was burned on Hringhorni's pyre?**

Baldr; his wife Nanna, dead of grief; and his horse in full harness. Odin laid the ring Draupnir on the fire and whispered an unrecorded word in his son's ear, and Thor hallowed the flames with Mjölnir — kicking the dwarf Litr into the fire when he ran underfoot.

**Did Vikings really have burning-ship funerals?**

Ship-burial was real — the Oseberg, Gokstad, and Ladby ships all come from graves — and Ibn Fadlan witnessed a ship cremation on land in 922. But the burning ship launched onto open water is essentially Hringhorni's myth; real ships were buried or burned ashore, not set adrift.

**Does Hringhorni appear at Ragnarök?**

No — its story ends with the funeral. But that funeral is the beginning of the end: Baldr's death sets the doom of the gods in motion, and when the world returns renewed after Ragnarök, Baldr comes back from Hel — without his ship.
