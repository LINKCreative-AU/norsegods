Gungnir ('the Swaying One') came out of the first great dwarf-commission: after [Loki](/gods/loki) sheared Sif's hair, the sons of Ivaldi forged the golden hair, the ship [Skíðblaðnir](/artifacts/skidbladnir), and the spear that never halts in its thrust, with runes carved on its point. [Odin](/gods/odin)'s oaths are sworn on it, which makes it the constitutional document of the cosmos: what is sworn on Gungnir binds even gods.

A hammer is for defense; a spear is for claiming. [Thor](/gods/thor)'s [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) guards the enclosures against the giants, but Gungnir does subtler and darker work — it opens wars, seals oaths, marks victims, and once pinned its own owner to a tree. If Mjölnir is the mythology's shield wall, Gungnir is its signature: wherever the spear points, something has been irrevocably decided.

## Why did Loki cut Sif's hair?

The sources give no motive beyond Loki's own nature — Skáldskaparmál says simply that he did it *til lævísi*, out of malice or love of mischief. What matters is the fallout. Thor, discovering his wife shorn, laid hands on Loki and promised to break every bone in him unless the damage was more than undone. So Loki went down to the dark smithies of [Svartálfaheimr](/realms/svartalfheim) and commissioned the sons of Ivaldi, who forged three marvels: hair of real gold that would grow on Sif's head like living hair; Skíðblaðnir, the folding ship that always finds a fair wind; and Gungnir, the spear that never stops in its thrust. It was on the way home from this errand that Loki, unable to leave well enough alone, wagered his head with the dwarf Brokkr that his brother Sindri could not forge three treasures as good — the bet that produced the ring Draupnir, the boar Gullinbursti, and Mjölnir itself. Every irreplaceable object in [Asgard](/realms/asgard) descends from one act of vandalism against a hairstyle. Gungnir went to Odin, and Sigrdrífumál's rune-lore remembers that runes were cut on the spear's point — fitting equipment for the god who won the runes themselves.

## Why did Odin stab himself with Gungnir?

The strangest and deepest of Gungnir's attestations is in Hávamál, where Odin speaks of his own ordeal: nine nights he hung on the windswept tree — understood to be [Yggdrasil](/concepts/yggdrasil) — "wounded by a spear, given to Odin, myself to myself," without food or drink, peering into the depths, until he grasped the runes, screaming, and fell. The spear is his own; the sacrifice follows the logic of his cult, in which victims dedicated to Odin were hung and stabbed with a spear — the god performs on himself the rite performed for him, and pays with agony for the alphabet of power. Gungnir is thus present at the mythology's central act of self-transcendence: the same point that marks armies for death marked their god for wisdom.

## The spear-cast that starts wars

Völuspá preserves the moment war entered the world. In the conflict between the Æsir and the Vanir — the gods of war-craft and the gods of fertility and [seiðr](/concepts/seidr) — the poem says: "Odin hurled his spear, shot it over the host; that was still the first war in the world." The gesture became a rite men actually performed. Casting a spear over an enemy army dedicated every man in it to Odin — *Óðinn á yðr alla*, 'Odin owns you all' — turning battle into mass sacrifice and victory into liturgy. The sagas and heroic poetry remember commanders opening battle this way, and in Völsunga saga's world it is with a spear that Odin appears on battlefields to collect the heroes he has marked, as when the old one-eyed man raises his spear against Sigmundr and the sword Gram shatters on it. What is thrown over you belongs to the thrower: Gungnir is the deed of ownership for every field of the slain, and the valkyries who carry the dead to Valhalla are executing its paperwork.

## In the primary sources

Gungnir's file is short but weighty. In the [Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda): Völuspá 24 gives the spear-cast over the Vanir that begins the first war; Hávamál 138 gives the self-sacrifice, "wounded by a spear... myself to myself"; Sigrdrífumál 17 lists Gungnir's point among the places runes were carved. In the [Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda), Skáldskaparmál tells the forging by the sons of Ivaldi after Loki's shearing of Sif, and records that the spear "never stops in its thrust"; Gylfaginning arms Odin with it at Ragnarök. Skaldic poetry knows Gungnir as a fixture — kennings call battle "Gungnir's din" — and Snorri adds in Ynglinga saga's euhemerized history that Odin, dying in his bed, had himself marked with a spear-point to claim the dead man's road he had promised to warriors. The oath-swearing on the spear is part of the broader attested practice of swearing on weapons and on Odin's names; the sources agree that what is sworn by the spear is not undone.

## At Ragnarök

At the world's end, Odin rides first onto the plain of Vígríðr — golden helmet, mail-coat, and Gungnir in his hand, Snorri specifies — against the wolf [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir). It is the last thing the spear does not win. The thrust that never halts meets the mouth that was prophesied to swallow the god, and the wolf takes Odin, spear and all; vengeance falls not to Gungnir but to Víðarr's silent boot and hands. The poems do not say what becomes of the spear itself, and it is not among the treasures — Mjölnir, the golden game-pieces — that the sources carry over into the renewed world. The instrument of decision goes down with the decider, which has its own justice: a world being born again does not need the old signature. [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) is, in a sense, the one engagement Gungnir ever lost.

## Legacy

The spear-god left marks outside the manuscripts. Viking Age spearheads survive with inlaid ornament and, in a few cases, runes on the blade — impossible to prove as "Gungnir-imitation," but entirely in the spirit of Sigrdrífumál's rune-carved point — and tenth-century picture stones and bracteates show a rider or striding figure with a spear that iconographers identify as Odin. The dedication-cast echoes in accounts of Germanic and Norse battle-custom, and Wagner gave the spear a second career in the Ring cycle, where Wotan's spear is engraved with the treaties that uphold the world and splinters when the free hero breaks it — an operatic reading of the oath-spear idea that has colored every retelling since. In modern games and comics Gungnir is a staple, usually as a lightning-throwing super-lance; the older and stranger truth, that it was the instrument by which a god notarized reality and once crucified himself, tends to be too interesting to film.

## FAQ

**What does Gungnir mean?**
The name is usually rendered 'the Swaying One' or 'the Swaying', evoking either the quiver of a spear-shaft in flight or the tremble of a poised weapon. It fits a spear defined by its thrust: the sources say Gungnir never stops or fails in its stroke.

**Who made Gungnir?**
The sons of Ivaldi, dwarven smiths of Svartálfaheim, forged it as part of the commission Loki extracted after cutting Sif's hair — the same commission that produced Skíðblaðnir and Sif's living golden hair. It came to Odin, and runes were carved on its point.

**Did Odin kill himself with Gungnir?**
Not quite — Hávamál describes a sacrificial ordeal, not a death. Odin hung nine nights on the world-tree, spear-wounded, "given to Odin, myself to myself," and through that suffering won the runes. It mirrors his cult's sacrificial rite of hanging and spear-marking, turned by the god upon himself.

**Why did Odin throw his spear over the Vanir?**
The cast dedicated the enemy host to himself — the act that, Völuspá says, began the first war in the world. Historically, hurling a spear over an opposing army was a known rite consecrating them to Odin; the myth makes the god the first performer of his own liturgy.

**What happens to Gungnir at Ragnarök?**
Odin carries it into the last battle against the wolf Fenrir, and loses: the wolf swallows the god. The sources never mention the spear again — unlike Mjölnir, which passes to Thor's sons, Gungnir has no stated place in the world after Ragnarök.
