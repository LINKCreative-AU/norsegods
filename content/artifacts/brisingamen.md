Brísingamen ('necklace of the Brísings', or perhaps 'of fire') is the emblem of [Freyja](/gods/freyja)'s power and beauty — an artifact so fused with its owner that the poets could name her simply by naming it. The late tale Sörla þáttr tells its price: Freyja found four dwarves forging it in a stone, and each demanded a night with her; she paid, wore the necklace — and [Odin](/gods/odin), learning of it from Loki, ordered it stolen in disgust, forcing Freyja into an endless war-charm as ransom. Older sources know the necklace without the scandal.

The theft, at least, is old. [Loki](/gods/loki) as 'thief of Brísingamen' is a fixed epithet, and one archaic poem preserves [Heimdallr](/gods/heimdall) and Loki fighting in seal-shape at the skerry Singasteinn over the jewel, Heimdallr winning it back. When [Thor](/gods/thor) disguised himself as Freyja to recover his hammer in Þrymskviða, Brísingamen on his chest was the finishing touch of the costume. And in Beowulf, the 'Brosinga mene' appears as a treasure of legend — the necklace was famous across the whole Northern world, centuries before anyone wrote down a story of how it was made.

## What does Brísingamen mean?

The second element is plain: men is Old Norse for a neck-ornament, a torc or necklace. The first is a genuine puzzle. Read as 'necklace of the Brísingar', it names a family or clan of whom the sources tell us nothing — a whole tribe surviving only in the possessive of a jewel. The rival reading connects the name to a Norse word for fire, making Brísingamen the 'flaming necklace' — which suits both worked gold at a hearth and the goddess who wears it. The sources never describe the necklace itself: no gem, no clasp, no weight of gold. Its splendour is entirely reputation, and the reputation was evidently enough.

That fusion of owner and ornament runs through the poetry. In Skáldskaparmál, Snorri lists 'possessor of Brísingamen' among the proper ways to name Freyja in verse, and the necklace anchors kennings for others too: Loki is its thief, Heimdallr its seeker. A single jewel giving poetic titles to three gods is a jewel at the centre of a story everyone was expected to know.

## Who stole Brísingamen?

Loki — and the fact that this needed no explanation is itself the evidence. In Skáldskaparmál, Snorri gives 'thief of Brísingamen' as a standard kenning for Loki, an epithet as fixed as Thor's hammer or Odin's ravens. Behind it lies a full story of the theft, of which we hold only the two endpoints: Loki took the necklace, and Heimdallr got it back.

The recovery survives in one precious fragment. Around the 980s the Icelandic skald Úlfr Uggason composed Húsdrápa, describing the mythological scenes carved into the hall timbers at Hjarðarholt, and one surviving stanza — quoted by Snorri in Skáldskaparmál — shows the renowned watchman of the gods contending with Loki, 'the wily son of Fárbauti', at a place called Singasteinn. Snorri, who could still read the allusion, supplies the key: the two fought in the form of seals, the prize was Freyja's necklace, and this is why Heimdallr can be called the necklace's seeker and the visitor of the skerries Vágasker and Singasteinn.

It is a startling image — the far-sighted sentinel of [Asgard](/realms/asgard) and the shape-shifting trickster, wrestling as sea-beasts on a wave-washed rock over a jewel. Why seals, why a skerry, how Loki came to have the necklace there: all of that was carved on a tenth-century Icelandic wall and spoken in a poem of which we keep one stanza. What remains is the essential structure, older than any complete story we have: Loki steals what is Freyja's, and Heimdallr wins it back.

## The wedding at Þrymr's hall

Brísingamen's most vivid surviving scene is a comedy. In Þrymskviða, Thor wakes to find [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir) gone. Loki borrows Freyja's [falcon cloak](/artifacts/valshamr), flies to Jötunheimr, and learns that the giant [Þrymr](/giants/thrym) has buried the hammer eight leagues deep — and will return it for one price: Freyja as his bride.

Thor and Loki go to Freyja and tell her, with remarkable tactlessness, to put on a bridal veil. Her answer shakes the world: she snorts with such fury that the halls of the Æsir tremble, and the great Brísinga men bursts from her neck. The snapping necklace is the poem's measure of her rage — the emblem of her power flung off at the suggestion that her body is a bargaining chip. The gods convene, and it is Heimdallr, 'whitest of the Æsir', who proposes the solution: dress Thor as the bride. Bind the bridal linen on him, hang housewife's keys at his waist — and set the Brísinga men about his neck.

So the necklace that leapt off Freyja in fury is fastened onto Thor in fraud. The disguised god rides to Jötunheimr, devours an ox and eight salmon at his own wedding feast, and terrifies the groom with burning eyes through the veil — Loki, as the quick-witted bridesmaid, explains that 'Freyja' has neither eaten nor slept for eight nights out of longing. Then Þrymr calls for the hammer to be laid in the bride's lap to hallow her. Thor's heart laughs in his chest; he takes Mjölnir and slaughters Þrymr and all his hall. Brísingamen, worn by the wrong god, is what makes the trap credible: no giant could doubt a bride wearing Freyja's own necklace.

## How did Freyja get Brísingamen?

Only one source answers, and it must be handled with care. Sörla þáttr, a short tale preserved in the Flateyjarbók manuscript of the late fourteenth century, embedded in a saga of the missionary king Óláfr Tryggvason, tells the famous story: Freyja — here reduced to Odin's mistress — came upon four dwarves, Alfrigg, Dvalinn, Berlingr and Grérr, forging a gold necklace in their stone. She offered gold and silver; they had no need of it, and each named the same price: a night with her. She weighed the necklace against the cost and paid. When Loki reported the bargain, Odin ordered him to steal the jewel; Loki slipped into Freyja's locked bower as a fly, bit her as a flea until she turned in her sleep, and unclasped it. To have it back, Freyja had to accept Odin's terms: she must set two kings, each with twenty kings beneath him, at eternal war, the slain rising each night to fight again — the everlasting battle of Heðinn and Högni, which grinds on until one of King Óláfr's Christian retainers dares to end it.

This is a good story and a late one, and honesty requires saying which is which. Sörla þáttr was written down some four centuries after Iceland's conversion, inside a text whose explicit programme is the triumph of Christianity over the old gods: its Freyja is a king's concubine, its Odin a scheming sorcerer, its miracle belongs to the new faith. The older tradition — the Húsdrápa stanza of the 980s, Þrymskviða, Snorri's kennings, the Beowulf poet across the sea — knows Brísingamen as Freyja's famous necklace and Loki as its thief, but breathes no word of four dwarves or a price paid in nights. Even the everlasting battle existed independently: Snorri tells the Hjaðningavíg in Skáldskaparmál with no necklace and no Freyja in sight. The four-dwarves story may echo a genuine lost myth of the necklace's origin — the theft it explains is certainly ancient — but as it stands it is medieval literature about paganism, not paganism itself.

## In the primary sources

The written trail runs from tenth-century verse to fourteenth-century prose. Húsdrápa, by Úlfr Uggason, is the oldest Norse witness: its surviving stanza on the Heimdallr–Loki contest at Singasteinn, preserved in Skáldskaparmál. [The Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) supplies the interpretive frame — Snorri's explanation of the seal-fight, and the kennings naming Freyja as the necklace's possessor, Loki its thief, Heimdallr its seeker. In [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Þrymskviða gives the necklace its two great dramatic moments: bursting from Freyja's neck in rage, and adorning Thor's bridal disguise. Sörla þáttr, in Flateyjarbók, contributes the late origin story of the four dwarves. And Beowulf — perhaps the earliest text of all — praises a golden collar by saying none better had been seen since Hama carried off the Brosinga mene, fleeing the enmity of Eormenric the Goth: proof that the necklace was already proverbial treasure in early England, part of a store of legend shared across the North Sea.

## At Ragnarök

The necklace itself has no part in the end of the world — no source gives it a fate. But the two who fought over it at Singasteinn meet once more. At [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok), Snorri says, Heimdallr and Loki face each other in the last battle and each is the other's death. The sources never connect the two encounters, and we should not pretend they do — yet the symmetry was surely audible: the watchman and the thief, who once wrestled as seals over Freyja's jewel, close their long account at the world's ending, and this time neither swims away.

## Legacy

Brísingamen's fame outran its stories. Its appearance in Beowulf as the Brosinga mene makes it one of the very few Norse mythological treasures independently attested in Old English — a legend already old when both literatures were young. In the modern era the necklace keeps its grip: Alan Garner's classic fantasy The Weirdstone of Brisingamen (1960) borrowed its name for an enchanted jewel, and it remains a fixture of retellings, comics, and games wherever Freyja appears. The great gold and silver neck-rings of the Viking Age inevitably invite the comparison — for a culture that counted wealth and honour in worn metal, the supreme goddess naturally wore the supreme treasure at her throat.

## FAQ

**Who owns Brísingamen?**
Freyja. The necklace is so firmly hers that skaldic poetry could name her simply as its possessor, and in Þrymskviða its presence on 'the bride' is what convinces the giants that Thor in his veil is really the goddess.

**Who made Brísingamen?**
Only the late Sörla þáttr says: four dwarves — Alfrigg, Dvalinn, Berlingr and Grérr — who each demanded a night with Freyja as the price. The older sources know the necklace without any origin story, so the four-dwarves tale may be a lost myth's echo or a medieval invention; the earliest tradition never tells us.

**Who stole Brísingamen, and who got it back?**
Loki stole it — 'thief of Brísingamen' is his fixed poetic epithet — and Heimdallr recovered it, fighting Loki in seal-shape at the skerry Singasteinn. The full story is lost; one stanza of Húsdrápa, with Snorri's explanation, is all that survives.

**Does Brísingamen have magical powers?**
No surviving source attributes any power to it. Unlike Mjölnir or Draupnir, Brísingamen does nothing: its force is beauty, fame, and its bond with Freyja. Modern retellings often make it an amulet of love or war, but that is embroidery on silence.

**What is the Brosinga mene in Beowulf?**
The same legendary necklace, named in Old English. The Beowulf poet, praising a gift-collar, says no finer treasure had been known since Hama carried off the Brosinga mene while fleeing Eormenric's enmity — the necklace as the gold standard of all necklaces.
