Of all the treasures the dwarves ever made for the gods, Gleipnir is the strangest: not a weapon, not gold, but a fetter. After the wolf [Fenrir](/creatures/fenrir) snapped the great chains Læðing and Drómi, the gods understood that nothing hammered from metal would ever hold him, and sent to the dwarves of Svartálfaheimr for something beyond metal. The dwarves forged Gleipnir ('Open One') from six things that do not exist: the sound of a cat's footfall, the beard of a woman, the roots of a mountain, the sinews of a bear, the breath of a fish, and the spittle of a bird. That is why, Snorri notes with a straight face, these things cannot be found now — they were all used up.

The ribbon is soft and smooth as silk, and that is the trap: strength that looks like weakness. Fenrir, smelling deceit, demanded a god's hand in his mouth as surety; only [Týr](/gods/tyr) paid. The wolf strained, the ribbon hardened, and the gods laughed — 'all except Týr.' Fenrir lies bound by Gleipnir on the island Lyngvi, a sword propping his jaws, until the fetter breaks at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok). Gleipnir is the mythology's meditation on binding evil with cleverness rather than force — and on what the cleverness costs the one honest party to it.

## Why did the gods bind Fenrir?

The wolf was born trouble. Snorri's Gylfaginning tells how [Loki](/gods/loki) fathered three children on the giantess [Angrboða](/giants/angrboda): the serpent [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), the half-dead girl Hel, and the wolf Fenrir. Warned by prophecy of the ruin this brood would bring, [Odin](/gods/odin) hurled the serpent into the sea that rings the world and cast Hel down into [Niflheim](/realms/niflheim) — but the wolf, strangely, the Æsir kept, and raised at home in their own halls.

It did not go well. Fenrir grew at a frightening rate, and every prophecy named him the gods' bane. Soon only one god dared to feed him: Týr. That repeated act of nerve is the foundation of everything that follows — when the day came to deceive the wolf, the wolf trusted exactly one face in the crowd.

The gods decided the wolf must be bound; yet they would not simply kill him. Gylfaginning explains why not: the gods so respected their sanctuaries and places of peace that they refused to defile them with the wolf's blood, even knowing he would one day be Odin's death. The binding of Fenrir is not mercy. It is a killing deferred out of scruple, and the whole cosmos will pay the interest.

## Læðing and Drómi: the fetters that failed

The gods tried force first. They forged a great chain called Læðing and brought it to the wolf, proposing — as if it were a game — that he test his strength against it. Fenrir judged it within his power, let them bind him, and burst it at the first strain. So they made a second fetter, twice as strong, called Drómi, and coaxed him again: break this, and your fame will be immense. Fenrir weighed it — the chain was mighty, but he had grown, and a wolf must risk something to be renowned. He let them lay it on, kicked, strained, and shattered it so violently the fragments flew far. Snorri adds that both breakings passed into proverb: to 'loose out of Læðing' or 'dash out of Drómi' was to win free by tremendous effort.

Two chains, two failures, and a wolf now certain nothing the gods forged could hold him. Which was true. So they stopped forging.

## Who made Gleipnir?

Odin sent Skírnir — the messenger of [Freyr](/gods/freyr), the same servant who rides to woo Gerðr in Skírnismál — down into Svartálfaheimr, the world of the black-elves, to certain dwarves. (Snorri's Svartálfaheimr overlaps confusingly with Niðavellir, the dwarves' 'dark fields' in Völuspá; the sources never untie that knot.) The dwarves took the commission the smiths of metal could not fill, and sent back Gleipnir: a band smooth and soft as a silken ribbon, stronger than any chain. They had understood what the gods had not — Fenrir could not be out-muscled, only out-thought.

## What is Gleipnir made of?

Snorri lists the six ingredients precisely:

- the sound of a cat's footfall
- the beard of a woman
- the roots of a mountain
- the sinews of a bear
- the breath of a fish
- the spittle of a bird

Six impossible things — and, Snorri says, the proof is all around you: cats move in silence and women have no beards because the dwarves used those things up. It is one of the finest jokes in the Prose Edda, an origin story for the world's absences, with a darker logic beneath: what does not exist cannot be broken. Fenrir's strength is real, so only the unreal can defeat it.

## The binding on Lyngvi

The gods took the wolf out to an island called Lyngvi, in the lake Ámsvartnir, and showed him the silken band. They passed it around, tested it, declared it somewhat stronger than it looked, and offered Fenrir the challenge: surely the wolf who shattered Drómi could snap a ribbon.

Fenrir smelled the trap. Little fame, he said, in tearing so slender a thing — and if it was made with craft and cunning, it was not coming near his feet. The gods pressed: if he could not break so thin a band, he was no threat, and they would loose him afterwards. The wolf answered with cold clarity: if they bound him so that he could not free himself, he did not expect to be waiting long for their help. But rather than be called a coward, he named his terms — let one of the gods lay a hand in his mouth as a pledge of good faith.

The gods looked at one another. Here was the price of the plan, suddenly itemised, and no one reached for it — until Týr stretched out his right hand and laid it in the wolf's jaws.

Fenrir kicked. The band hardened. The harder he strained, the sharper it bit, and a loop of silk held the strongest jaws in the cosmos. Then all the gods laughed — all except Týr. He lost his hand.

They took the cord Gelgja, which hung from the fetter, threaded it through a great stone slab called Gjöll, fixed the slab deep in the earth, and drove the boulder Þviti deeper still as an anchoring peg. Fenrir gaped and snapped, so they thrust a sword into his mouth — hilt against the lower jaw, point against the upper — propping his jaws apart forever. He howls dreadfully, and the slaver running from his mouth forms the river Ván, 'Hope' — the bitterest place-name in the mythology. There he lies until Ragnarök.

## Why did Týr lose his hand?

Because the pledge was real even though the game was rigged. Fenrir demanded surety against treachery; treachery was precisely the plan; and a pledge given in bad faith still has to be paid when the fraud is discovered. Týr, the god most associated with oaths and law, is the only Ás willing to guarantee the lie with his own body — the only one who treats the wolf's terms as binding. The gods win the wolf; Týr alone pays; and Snorri's dry line that they all laughed 'except Týr' carries the story's whole moral weight.

The Norse kept the transaction in their own bodies: Gylfaginning notes that the wrist was called úlfliðr, the 'wolf-joint' — every arm a small monument to where Fenrir's teeth closed. And the Poetic Edda shows the wound still raw. In Lokasenna, Loki jeers that Týr can never again carry the right hand of justice among men; Týr answers that he may lack a hand, but Loki lacks the wolf — and the wolf waits in his shackles until the doom of the gods.

## In the primary sources

The full story of Gleipnir belongs to [the Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda): Snorri's Gylfaginning tells the raising of the wolf, the failed fetters, Skírnir's errand, the six ingredients, the binding on Lyngvi, Týr's hand, and the sword in Fenrir's jaws — as well as the gods' reason for binding rather than killing him.

In [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Lokasenna preserves the aftermath: the flyting in which Loki mocks Týr's missing hand and Týr retorts that Fenrir lies bound until Ragnarök, the accompanying prose glossing the players. Völuspá never names Gleipnir, but its refrain that 'the fetter will break and the wolf run free' is usually read as this fetter and this wolf. Gleipnir's complete narrative we owe to Snorri, resting on older poetry that alludes to the binding as a thing already known.

## At Ragnarök

Gleipnir holds until the end — and its breaking is one of the end's signatures. When Ragnarök comes, amid earthquakes and the unmooring of all bonds, Fenrir tears free at last. He advances with his lower jaw against the earth and his upper against the sky, fire burning from his eyes and nostrils, and on the plain of Vígríðr he swallows Odin whole — the doom promised on the day the gods chose binding over blood. Odin's son Víðarr avenges him, tearing the wolf's jaws apart. The fetter of impossible things was never a solution; it was a clock. All Gleipnir bought was time.

## Legacy

Gleipnir's afterlife is the afterlife of its idea: that the subtlest bond is the strongest. The proverbs Snorri records — loosing out of Læðing, dashing out of Drómi — show the story embedded in everyday Norse speech, as does úlfliðr, the wrist as 'wolf-joint.' Readers of Tolkien have long noticed that The Silmarillion pays the myth direct homage: Beren's hand, bitten off at the wrist by the great wolf Carcharoth, restages Týr's sacrifice in Middle-earth. And the name Gleipnir surfaces across modern fantasy and games wherever an author needs a bond that cannot break — a silk ribbon still holding better than any chain.

## FAQ

**What does the name Gleipnir mean?**
It is usually rendered 'Open One' — a name as deceptive as the fetter itself, since Gleipnir exists precisely so that it will never open.

**Who made Gleipnir?**
Dwarves in Svartálfaheimr, the world of the black-elves, forged it after Odin sent Freyr's messenger Skírnir to commission it. The sources never name the individual smiths; the credit belongs to the dwarves collectively, the makers the gods turned to whenever they needed the impossible.

**Why didn't the gods simply kill Fenrir?**
Gylfaginning answers directly: the gods so respected their sanctuaries that they would not defile them with the wolf's blood — even knowing Fenrir would be Odin's death. At Ragnarök the deferred killing comes due.

**Does Gleipnir break at Ragnarök?**
Yes. The fetter no strength could snap fails when the world itself does: amid the ruin of Ragnarök Fenrir comes free, swallows Odin, and is killed in turn by Odin's son Víðarr.
