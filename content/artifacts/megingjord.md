Snorri Sturluson inventories [Thor](/gods/thor)'s equipment in three items: the hammer [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir), the iron gloves [Járngreipr](/artifacts/jarngreipr) without which the hammer's shaft cannot be gripped, and Megingjörð, the "main-girdle" or belt of power — when he clasps it, his ásmegin, his god-strength, doubles. The strongest being in the cosmos owns a strength multiplier: the Norse never saw a superlative they wouldn't stack.

Unlike the hammer, the belt has no forging-story, no dwarf-smiths, no wager, no theft. It simply appears in Gylfaginning, buckled around the waist of the god who defends [Asgard](/realms/asgard) and Midgard. And yet the one tale in which the belt truly matters is the tale where Thor doesn't have it.

## Thor's threefold kit

In Gylfaginning, Snorri introduces Thor as the strongest of all gods and men, then lists his three treasures in order. First Mjölnir, which the frost-giants and mountain-giants know well when it comes flying. Second the megingjarðar — the word is actually plural in Old Norse, "main-girdles" — which double his divine strength when he buckles them on. Third the iron gloves, which he cannot do without when he grips the hammer's shaft.

The listing is telling. Norse gods are not omnipotent; they are equipped. [Odin](/gods/odin) has his spear and his ring, [Freyja](/gods/freyja) her necklace and her falcon-skin — and Thor, whose whole function is applied force, carries force-application gear. The belt is the middle term of a system: the gloves grip the hammer, the belt powers the arm, the hammer delivers the blow.

## How strong does Megingjörð make Thor?

Twice as strong as the strongest being that exists — which invites the obvious question of why he needs it. The sources never ask, but the myths answer by example: Thor's opponents are not fair fights. He hooks [Jörmungandr](/creatures/jormungandr), the serpent that circles the world, on a fishing line and hauls its head to the gunwale; he is the designated answer to beings the size of landscapes. The word at the heart of the belt's name, megin, means main-strength, the deep reserve of power — the same word hiding in the English phrase "might and main." Ásmegin is that reserve in its divine form, and the belt is the tap that opens it fully.

There is also a quieter reading. In a warrior culture, a man girded himself for work and for war; the belt was the thing you put on when the day turned serious. That the mythology's mightiest arm still buckles up before work says something the poets surely enjoyed: even for a god, strength is not a mood but a discipline.

## The journey to Geirröðr's courts

The belt matters most in the tale where Thor lacks it. In Skáldskaparmál, [Loki](/gods/loki) — caught flying in a borrowed falcon-skin and starved in a chest by the giant [Geirröðr](/giants/geirrod) — buys his freedom with a promise: he will deliver Thor to Geirröðr's courts without hammer or belt of strength. Thor, trusting Loki for reasons the sources leave between the lines, sets out unarmed.

He survives because of a giantess. On the road he lodges with Gríðr, mother of Odin's silent son [Víðarr](/gods/vidar), and she tells him the truth about Geirröðr — a cunning enemy, bad to deal with — and lends him her own gear: a belt of strength, iron gloves, and her staff, Gríðarvölr. Girded in borrowed power, Thor crosses the torrential river Vimur as Geirröðr's daughter Gjálp swells it against him, and dams the flood at its source with a well-thrown stone. In Geirröðr's hall the giant's daughters try to crush him against the roof beams by lifting his chair; Thor pushes back with the staff and breaks both their backs. Then comes the molten iron — but that is [Járngreipr](/artifacts/jarngreipr)'s story.

## Who made Megingjörð?

Nobody, as far as the sources say — and the silence is genuinely interesting. Mjölnir, Gungnir, [Draupnir](/artifacts/draupnir), and Sif's golden hair all come from named dwarf-smiths in documented commissions; [Brísingamen](/artifacts/brisingamen) has its four makers; even the fetter [Gleipnir](/artifacts/gleipnir) has a supplier. Megingjörð has no maker, no origin, no price. And the Geirröðr episode complicates the picture: Gríðr, a giantess, owns a functionally identical belt. Belts of strength seem to be a category of thing in the mythology's background economy rather than a unique treasure — power you can wear, own, and crucially lend. Whether Snorri inherited an origin-tale now lost, or whether the belt was always simply part of Thor's silhouette, we cannot know. The sources give us the object in use, not its biography.

## In the primary sources

Everything rests on [the Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda). Gylfaginning (chapter 21) provides the inventory: the belt's name, its place among Thor's three treasures, and the doubling of his ásmegin. Skáldskaparmál (chapter 18) supplies the Geirröðr adventure, with its pointed condition that Thor come without belt or hammer, and Gríðr's loan of her own megingjarðar. Snorri anchors that story in old verse, quoting at length from Þórsdrápa, Eilífr Goðrúnarson's dense skaldic poem of the 990s — evidence the adventure was pagan-age tradition, not Snorri's invention. [The Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda) never names the belt; no saga does either. Megingjörð is, textually speaking, Snorri's gift to us — two mentions, one of them an absence.

## Legacy

The belt has had a healthy afterlife wherever Thor goes. Marvel's comics adopted the Belt of Strength by name alongside hammer and gauntlets, and modern games and retellings routinely include it in Thor's kit. Linguistically it hides in plain sight: Old Norse megin is cognate with Old English mægen, and anyone who works "with might and main" is invoking the word Thor buckles on. And as a story-pattern it stands early in a long line running to the power-belts of tabletop and video-game fantasy, where the "girdle of giant strength" is a genre fixture. The idea is Megingjörð's: strength as equipment, worn at the waist, doubled at the buckle.

## FAQ

**What is Megingjörð?**

Megingjörð (Old Norse megingjarðar, "power-girdle") is Thor's belt of strength. According to Snorri's Prose Edda, when Thor clasps it his ásmegin — his divine strength — doubles. It is one of his three great possessions, with the hammer Mjölnir and the iron gloves Járngreipr.

**How strong does the belt make Thor?**

Snorri says Thor is already the strongest of gods and men, and the belt doubles that. The sources never quantify further; the point is that Thor's enemies — world-circling serpents, mountain-sized giants — demand more than even the greatest natural strength.

**Who forged Megingjörð?**

No source says. Unlike Mjölnir, which came from the dwarves Brokkr and Sindri, the belt has no origin story at all. The giantess Gríðr owns a belt of strength of her own, which suggests such belts were a kind of thing in the mythology rather than a single unique treasure.

**When did Thor use the belt?**

Snorri describes it as standing equipment, but the defining story is one of absence: Loki promised Geirröðr to deliver Thor without hammer or belt, and Thor survived the trip only because Gríðr lent him her belt, iron gloves, and staff.

**Does Megingjörð appear at Ragnarök?**

The accounts of Thor's last battle with Jörmungandr at [Ragnarök](/events/ragnarok) mention neither belt nor gloves — only the hammer, and the nine steps Thor takes before the serpent's venom fells him. Whether he wears the belt at the end, the tradition never records.
