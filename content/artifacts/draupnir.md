Draupnir, "the Dripper," is the economy of kingship cast in gold: every ninth night, eight rings of the same weight drip from it, each the equal of the mother ring. In a world where lords were "ring-givers" and loyalty was paid out in arm-gold, a self-replicating ring is not a trinket — it is the bottomless treasury of the Allfather, forged by the dwarf brothers Brokkr and Sindri in the same wager that produced [Mjölnir](/artifacts/mjolnir).

Among [Odin's](/gods/odin) possessions — the spear [Gungnir](/artifacts/gungnir), the horse Sleipnir, the far-flying ravens — Draupnir is the quietest: it kills nothing, sees nothing, wins no battles; it generates. And yet its two great appearances in the myths are stories about the limits of gold — laid on a pyre it could not quench, and offered for a love it could not buy.

## How was Draupnir made?

It began, as so much does, with [Loki](/gods/loki). After the sons of Ivaldi forged Sif's golden hair, the ship [Skíðblaðnir](/artifacts/skidbladnir), and the spear Gungnir, Loki wagered his own head with the dwarf Brokkr that Brokkr's brother Sindri could not match those three treasures. Sindri laid gold in the forge, told Brokkr to pump the bellows without a single pause, and went out. Loki, in the shape of a fly, settled on Brokkr's neck and bit twice as hard as before — but the bellows never faltered, and Sindri drew from the fire a gold ring he named Draupnir, declaring its virtue: every ninth night, eight rings of equal weight would drip from it.

At the judging in Asgard, Brokkr presented the ring to Odin. The gods ruled Mjölnir the greatest of all the treasures, "the best defence against the frost-giants," so Draupnir entered the mythology as runner-up in the finest craft-contest ever held — a flawless second place, since Loki's fly-bite marred only the hammer's handle. Loki kept his head on a technicality: the dwarves had won his head, but no part of his neck.

## What does Draupnir do?

To feel the ring's weight, remember what gold was for. Norse poets call a generous lord a "ring-giver": rulers wore their wealth as rings on arm and finger, to be broken off and handed out for loyalty. Viking Age hoards from Ireland to Russia are full of standardised arm-rings that passed as currency. Draupnir compresses that whole political economy into one object: a lord who owns it can never exhaust his treasury, and so never runs out of followers — fitting equipment for the god gathering an army of the dead against Ragnarök.

Even the rhythm is significant. Nine is Odin's number — he hung nine nights on the world-tree, and Hermóðr would ride nine nights toward Hel — and Draupnir drips wealth to the same sacred beat.

## Draupnir on Baldr's pyre

The ring's most moving appearance is on a funeral ship. When [Baldr](/gods/baldr), killed by the mistletoe in blind Höðr's hand, was laid on his own great vessel [Hringhorni](/artifacts/hringhorni), Odin bent over his son. He whispered something into the dead god's ear that no source records — and he laid Draupnir on the pyre. The richest being in the cosmos gave his inexhaustible treasury to the flames.

Then Hermóðr rode. Nine nights on Sleipnir through valleys so deep and dark he saw nothing, over the gold-thatched bridge across the river Gjöll, and down into [Hel](/realms/helheim), where he found Baldr seated in the high seat of the dead. Hermóðr pleaded for his brother's release, and Hel set her famous condition — that all things in the world weep for him. As Hermóðr turned to go, Baldr gave him Draupnir to carry back to Odin as a keepsake, and Nanna sent a linen robe to Frigg and a gold finger-ring to Fulla. A gift exchanged across the border of death, twice: father to dead son, dead son to father. Whatever Odin whispered on the pyre, the returned ring was part of the answer.

## Why did Gerðr refuse Draupnir?

Draupnir surfaces once more, in the strangest of places: a courtship. In Skírnismál, [Freyr](/gods/freyr) sits in Odin's high seat, catches sight of the giantess Gerðr far off in Jötunheimr, and falls into lovesick despair. His messenger Skírnir rides to win her, carrying the best bribes Asgard can stake: eleven golden apples, and then the ring itself — which, the poem notes, "was burned with Odin's young son." Gerðr refuses both, flatly. She has no lack of gold, she answers; in her father Gymir's courts, wealth is never scarce. Even the ring that makes rings cannot buy everything, and Skírnir must fall back on threats and a terrible curse before she consents to meet Freyr.

The poem's aside about the pyre is a famous knot. If Draupnir was burned with Baldr, then the wooing of Gerðr happens after the funeral and the ring's return from Hel — or else the myths simply refuse to run on a single timeline. Either way, the poets knew the ring's biography: this was the gold from the fire.

## In the primary sources

Skáldskaparmál in [the Prose Edda](/texts/prose-edda) preserves the forging: Loki's wager, the fly at the bellows, and Brokkr's presentation of the ring to Odin — part of Snorri's account of where the gods' great treasures come from. Gylfaginning carries the funeral: Draupnir on Baldr's pyre, Hermóðr's ride to Hel, and Baldr sending the ring back as a keepsake. In [the Poetic Edda](/texts/poetic-edda), Skírnismál holds the offer to Gerðr and her refusal, with its passing reference to the pyre. The name echoes elsewhere too: Völuspá's catalogue of dwarfs includes a dwarf called Draupnir — a separate figure, but a reminder that ring and ring-makers belong to the same imaginative world.

## Legacy

Draupnir stands behind every later dream of self-multiplying treasure. The ring-giver culture it crystallises runs through Beowulf, and its modern descendants are easy to spot: rings of power that command loyalty and generate wealth are a fantasy staple from Wagner to Tolkien — though the curse in those stories belongs to [Andvaranaut](/artifacts/andvaranaut), the other great ring of Norse myth. Draupnir is the benign twin: gold without a body count, wealth as the glue of a society rather than its solvent.

## FAQ

**What does the name Draupnir mean?**
It means "the Dripper," from the Old Norse verb drjúpa, "to drip." The name describes exactly what the ring does: every ninth night, eight new gold rings of identical weight drip from it.

**How many rings does Draupnir produce?**
Eight rings every ninth night, each equal in weight to Draupnir itself. Snorri states this virtue both at the forging and when Brokkr hands the ring to Odin.

**Was Draupnir destroyed when Baldr was burned?**
No. Odin laid it on Baldr's pyre, but the ring passed with Baldr into Hel, and Baldr sent it back to his father as a keepsake with the messenger Hermóðr. It is one of the very few objects in the myths to cross the border of death in both directions.

**Are Draupnir and Andvaranaut the same ring?**
No. Draupnir is Odin's dwarf-forged ring that multiplies gold and carries no curse; Andvaranaut is the ring Loki extorted from the dwarf Andvari, which breeds gold and destroys every owner. Both are golden, dwarf-made, and wealth-generating, but their stories run in opposite moral directions.
